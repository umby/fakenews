currently has an impressive rating<a href=\"https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0399167439/interregnumnow-20\"><span style=\"color: #0000ff;\"> on <em>Amazon</em></span></a>, of 4.6 out of 5 stars, from 74 customer reviews. 74% of the reviews gave the book a maximum 5-stars rating.</p>\n<p>The book is actually an English translation of a Spanish-language book that was published in 2010, titled <span style=\"color: #0000ff;\"><em><a style=\"color: #0000ff;\" href=\"https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/9501524507/interregnumnow-20\" target=\"_blank\">El Jesuita: Conversaciones con Catholicism is a Crucified Christ dripping blood . . .\u201d, to which Bergoglio replied:</p>\n<blockquote><p>\u201cThe exaltation of suffering in the Church depends a great deal on the era and The Church has represented Christ according to the cultural environment of the time. If you look at Eastern icons, Russian, for example, you realize they have very few images of a sorrowful crucifixion. It\u2019s more common to see the resurrection. On the other hands, if we look at the Spanish Baroque period or the icons of Cuzco, Peru, we find images of Christ with His patience torn to shreds, because the Baroque era emphasized Jesus\u2019 passion. <i>White Crucifixion</i>, by Marc Chagall, who was a Jewish believer, is not cruel but hopeful. Pain is depicted there with serenity. To my mind, it\u2019s one of the most beautiful things he painted.\u201d</p></blockquote>\n<p>Below are scans of pp. 26-27 of <em>Pope Francis: His Life in His Own Words</em>, provided by <a href=\"https://callmejorgebergoglio.blogspot.com/2014/03/jorge-jokes-about-our-lords-crucifixion.html\"><span style=\"color: #0000ff;\"><em>Call Me Jorge</em></span></a> blog.
