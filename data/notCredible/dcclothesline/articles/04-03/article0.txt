that governments would absolutely love to have the capability of wide scale geoengineering. It could be the nuclear <span id=\"more-71211\"></span>weapons of the 21st century. And more and more scientists are conducting <a href=\"http://www.dcclothesline.com/2017/03/29/harvard-engineers-plan-new-real-world-geoengineering-experiment/\" target=\"_blank\">geoengineering experiments</a>, with the latest being Harvard scientists, who will be conducting the world\u2019s largest and most expensive solar geoengineering project within a few weeks.</p>\n<p>In the following video, Right Wing News questions the true motives behind such an experiment. Is it really to combat the threat of climate change? Of course, climate change is a useful and convenient cover that will for sure allow them to bypass deeper scrutiny. While the experiment might be completely innocent and well-intentioned, it still involves spraying particles into the stratosphere, into the very air we breathe. Did they ever think that perhaps the public would want to be informed before breathing whatever chemicals they are spraying?</p>\n<p><iframe src=\"https://www.youtube.com/embed/15ZRkTpZrNc\" width=\"660\" height=\"460\" frameborder=\"0\" scrolling=\"no\" allowfullscreen=\"allowfullscreen\" data-mce-fragment=\"1\"></iframe></p>\n<p><a href=\"http://yournewswire.com/harvard-scientists-chemtrails-experiment/\"><strong>Your News Wire Reports: </strong></a></p>\n<p><strong>A $20 million Harvard University project will send aerosol injections up into the earth\u2019s stratosphere, in the world\u2019s biggest chemtrails experiment on the public to date. </strong></p>\n<p>Two US scientists say they hope to solve the problem of global warming by conducting the world\u2019s biggest and most expensive solar geoengineering project within the next few weeks.</p>\n<p>The aim of the program is to establish whether chemtrail technology can simulate the atmospheric cooling effects of a volcanic eruption, in a desperate attempt to halt climate change.</p>\n<p><strong>\u201c<em>This is not the first or the only university study</em>,\u201d said Gernot Wagner, one of the project\u2019s co-founders, \u201c<em>but it is most certainly the largest, and the most comprehensive</em>.\u201d</strong></p>\n<p><strong><a href=\"https://www.technologyreview.com/s/603974/harvard-scientists-moving-ahead-on-plans-for-atmospheric-geoengineering-experiments/\">Technologyreview.com</a></strong><strong><a href=\"https://www.technologyreview.com/s/603974/harvard-scientists-moving-ahead-on-plans-for-atmospheric-geoengineering-experiments/\"> reports:</a></strong></p>\n<p><strong><em>The basic idea is that spraying certain types of particles into the stratosphere could help reflect more heat back into space. </em></strong>Scientists believe it could work because nature already does it. Large volcanic eruptions in the past have blasted tens of millions of tons of sulfur dioxide into the sky, which contributed to lower global temperatures in subsequent months.</p>\n<p><strong><em>What\u2019s less clear is how precisely the technique could control worldwide temperatures, what materials would work best, and what the environmental side effects might be.</em></strong> Notably, previous volcanic eruptions have also decreased precipitation levels in parts of the world, and sulfur dioxide is known to deplete the protective ozone layer.</p>\n<p>Keith has previously used computer modeling to explore the possibility of using other materials that may have a neutral impact on ozone, including diamond dust and alumina.<em><strong>Late last year, he, Keutsch, and others <a href=\"http://www.pnas.org/content/113/52/14910.abstract\" target=\"_blank\">published a paper</a> that found using calcite, a mineral made up of calcium carbonate, \u201cmay cool the planet while simultaneously repairing the ozone layer.\u201d</strong></em></p>\n<p>The balloon tests could provide additional insight into how these chemicals actually interact with precursors to ozone in the real world and offer additional information that could help refine their understanding of solar geoengineering, he says: <strong><em>\u201cYou have to go measure things in the real world because nature surprises you.\u201d</em></strong></p>\n<p>Keith stresses that it\u2019s too early to say whether any geoengineering technologies should ever be deployed. But he has <a href=\"http://www.recode.net/2014/12/11/11633680/harvards-david-keith-knows-how-to-dial-down-the-earths-thermostat-is\" target=\"_blank\">argued for years</a> that research should move ahead to better understand their capabilities and dangers, because it\u2019s possible they could significantly reduce the risks of climate change. <strong><em>He stressed that the experiments would have negligible environment impacts, as they will involve no more than a kilogram of materials.</em></strong></p>\n<p>Funding for the initial experiments would come from grants that Harvard provided Keith and Keutsch as new professors. Additional funds may come from Harvard\u2019s Solar Geoengineering Research Program, a multidisciplinary effort launching this spring to study feasibility, risks, ethics, and governance issues surrounding geoengineering. <strong><em>As of press time, it had raised more than $7 million from Microsoft cofounder Bill Gates, the Hewlett Foundation, the Alfred P.
