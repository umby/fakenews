underground city completed, fully staffed by 1960: Project Iceworm</a></h1>\n\t\t\t\n\t\t\t<div class=\"entry-meta\">\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<span class=\"author-link\">\n\t\t\t\t\t\tPosted by .entry-meta -->\n\t\t</header><!-- .entry-header -->\n\n\t\t<div class=\"entry-content\">\n\t\t\t\t\t<h3 class=\"td-post-sub-title\" style=\"text-align: center;\"><em><strong>&#8220;Camp Century&#8221; was used as a cover story for a secret underground Arctic city built by the U.S. Army</strong></em></h3>\n<p>N.W. GREENLAND (<a href=\"https://www.intellihub.com/u-s-army-built-nuclear-powered-underground-city-completed-fully-staffed-by-1960/\" target=\"_blank\">INTELLIHUB</a>) \u2014 Project Iceworm was the U.S. Army\u2019s code name for the top-secret installation of mobile nuclear-capable missile launching pads beneath a well surveyed and completely flat ice sheet in Greenland.<span id=\"more-71157\"></span></p>\n<p>Using a cover story, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers kept Iceworm secret from the Danish government by publicizing the construction of a planned \u201cresearch\u201d facility named Camp Century.</p>\n<p>The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) initiated the construction of Camp Century in 1959 after over a year of methodical planning and preparation.</p>\n<p>During the camp\u2019s construction, a bogus propaganda video was filmed to appease certain government figureheads and members of the general public. Although the video itself was real, it existed as a cover story to what was really being constructed \u2014 a large-scale underground city.</p>\n<p>The city\u2019s location was carefully chosen to be in a spot where the ice sheet deviated less than one degree in slope. The foundation for which the city would be built upon was perfectly flat which would minimize mistakes and speed up the construction process.</p>\n<p>Workers would work around the clock and rotate in 10-hour shifts.</p>\n<p>Supplies were transported 150-miles from Thule Air Force Base to a team of skilled Officers and a good size group of engineers and construction workers via aircraft and on 50 and 100-ton sleds that were pulled across the ice sheet by convoys of large Caterpillar tractors. The treks across the winter wonderland were known to Army personnel as \u201cheavy swings.\u201d</p>\n<p>Working a heavy swing was brutal for most due to the freezing temperatures, wind shear, and the potential for sunburn, but men were fed well as rations were plentiful.</p>\n<p>A three-mile-long road was constructed close to the camp in order to speed up the delivery of incoming supplies.</p>\n<p>In one trip alone, before the nuclear reactor \u201cwent critical,\u201d twenty-five thousand gallons of diesel fuel was brought in which would power all of the local equipment for months to come.</p>\n<p>All-in-all over 6000 tons of supplies were used during the construction process of Camp Century before it\u2019s completion in 1959.</p>\n<p>One of the most impressive features of the camp at the time was its nuclear reactor that would supply infinite power to the city\u2019s future residents.</p>\n<p>95 percent of the city was constructed underground. Using a tunneling process and then overlaying 40-foot arched steel sections of roofing which rested on a steel beam frame, the reactor building and other areas of the city were completed in record time.</p>\n<p>Bricks were made out of the ice in some cases which would form non-structural walls in parts of the camp.</p>\n<figure id=\"attachment_159523\" class=\"wp-caption aligncenter\" style=\"width: 487px;\"><img class=\" wp-image-159523\" src=\"https://i0.wp.com/www.intellihub.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/PM2Anuclearpowerplant-1.jpg?resize=477%2C352&#038;ssl=1\" sizes=\"(max-width: 999px) 100vw, 999px\" srcset=\"https://www.intellihub.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/PM2Anuclearpowerplant-1-300x221.jpg 300w, https://www.intellihub.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/PM2Anuclearpowerplant-1-768x567.jpg 768w, https://www.intellihub.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/PM2Anuclearpowerplant-1-696x513.jpg 696w, https://www.intellihub.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/PM2Anuclearpowerplant-1-569x420.jpg 569w, https://i0.wp.com/www.intellihub.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/04/PM2Anuclearpowerplant-1.jpg?resize=477%2C352&#038;ssl=1 999w\" alt=\"camp century\" data-recalc-dims=\"1\" /><figcaption class=\"wp-caption-text\">Camp Century layout (U.S. Army)</figcaption></figure>\n<p>In total 16 fire escapes were installed in the city\u2019s tunnel systems allowing residents to flee to the surface through hatches in case of an emergency.</p><div class=\"wpInsert wpInsertInPostAd wpInsertMiddle\" style=\"margin: 5px; The city had the world\u2019s largest deep freezer which held a fat 3-month supply of food for residents.</p>\n<p>Living quarters were \u201cmodern, spacious, and comfortable \u2014 not lacking in any way.\u201d</p>\n<p><iframe src=\"https://www.youtube.com/embed/1Ujx_pND9wg?feature=oembed\" width=\"600\" height=\"360\" frameborder=\"0\" allowfullscreen=\"allowfullscreen\" data-mce-fragment=\"1\"></iframe></p>\n<p>Once the cover \u201cCamp Century\u201d was complete it was turned over to \u201cresearchers\u201d who were allegedly part of the \u201cPolar Research and Development Program.\u201d</p>\n<p>However, that may not have been the case because under Project Iceworm missile silos were to be constructed.</p>\n<p>Wikipedia reports:</p>\n<blockquote><p>According to the documents published by Denmark in 1997, the U.S. Army\u2019s \u201cIceworm\u201d missile network was outlined in a 1960 Army report titled \u201cStrategic Value of the Greenland Icecap\u201d. If fully implemented, the project would cover an area of 52,000 square miles (130,000\u00a0km<sup>2</sup>), roughly three times the size of Denmark. The launch complex floors would be 28 feet (8.5\u00a0m) below the surface, with the missile launchers even deeper, and clusters of missile launch centers would be spaced 4 miles (6.4\u00a0km) apart. New tunnels were to be dug every year, so that after five years there would be thousands of firing positions, among which the several hundred missiles could be rotated. The Army intended to deploy a shortened, two-stage version of the U.S. Air Force\u2019s Minuteman missile, a variant the Army proposed calling the <b>Iceman</b>.</p></blockquote>\n<p>Was the funding for Project Iceworm used on nuclear capable underground missile launchpads or something entirely different?</p>\n<p>Were \u201cresearchers\u201d really researching?
