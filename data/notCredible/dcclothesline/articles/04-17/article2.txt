#0000ff;\">Shroud of Turin</span></a> is a rectangular 14 ft 5 in \u00d7 3 ft 7 in piece of woven flax cloth, believed by Christians to be the burial cloth of Jesus. Poor Clare nuns repaired the damage with patches; and the carbon-14 dating was performed precisely on a repaired patch, thereby skewing the test result.</p>\n<p><span style=\"color: #333399;\"><strong>Two discoveries in recent years lend support to</strong> </span>that explanation, as well as to<span style=\"color: #333399;\"> <strong>the authenticity of the Shroud as the burial cloth of our Lord Jesus Christ.</strong></span></p>\n<h3>(1) Pollen</h3>\n<div>\n<p>Doug Stanglin reports for <a href=\"http://www.usatoday.com/story/news/world/2013/03/30/shroud-turin-display/2038295/\"><em><span style=\"color: #0000ff;\">USA Today</span></em></a>, March 30, 2013, that a new test by scientists at the University of Padua in northern Italy, using the same fibers from the 1988 tests, dates the Shroud to between 300 BC and 400 AD, which would put it in the era of Christ.</p>\n<p>The latest findings are contained in a new Italian-language book, <i>Il Mistero Della Sindone</i> (<i>The Mystery of the Shroud</i>), by Giulio Fanti, a professor of mechanical and thermal measurement at Padua University, and journalist Saverio Gaeta.</p>\n</div>\n<p>The new test:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>Determined that the 1988 carbon-14 results may have been skewed by contamination from fibers used to repair the cloth when it was damaged by fire in the Middle Ages.</li>\n<li>Supports earlier findings of traces of dust and pollen on the Shroud which could only have come from the Holy Land in the time of Christ.</li>\n</ol>\n<div style=\"clear:both;
