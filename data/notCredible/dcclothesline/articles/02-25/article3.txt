SSA Defends it!</h1>\n\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"entry-meta\">\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<span class=\"meta-prep meta-prep-author\">Posted on</span> <a href=\"http://www.dcclothesline.com/2017/02/25/1-billion-was-paid-to-people-without-a-social-security-number-and-ssa-defends-it/\" title=\"5:00 am\" rel=\"bookmark\"><span class=\"entry-date\">February 25, 2017</span></a> <span class=\"meta-sep\">by</span> <span class=\"author vcard\"><a class=\"url fn n\" href=\"http://www.dcclothesline.com/author/tim-brown/\" title=\"View all posts by Tim Brown\">Tim Brown</a></span>\t\t\t\t\t</div><!-- .entry-meta -->\n\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"entry-content\">\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<p>According to a new audit, the Social Security Administration paid $1 billion in benefits to people who don&#8217;t even possess a Social Security Number (SSN).<span id=\"more-69931\"></span></p>\n<p>According to the <a href=\"http://oig.ssa.gov/sites/default/files/audit/full/pdf/A-09-16-50159.pdf\">audit</a>, which was released Friday, there were thousands of cases where the administration paid money to people who had no SSN on file.</p>\n<p>&#8220;SSA needs to improve controls to ensure it (a) records individual representative payees\u2019 SSNs in its payment records and (b) retains the application for representative payees who do not have an SSN,&#8221; reads the report&#8217;s findings. &#8220;Based on our random sample, we estimate that 150,257 beneficiaries had an individual representative payee who had a valid SSN that SSA should have recorded on the MBR/SSR. Of these, 26,912 beneficiaries had representative payees whom, according to eRPS, SSA had terminated or not selected. From October 2004 to September 2016, SSA paid these representative payees about $853.1 million. Furthermore, unless it takes corrective action, we estimate SSA will pay these representative payees about $189.6 million in benefits annually.</p>\n<p>&#8220;In addition, we estimate that 22,426 beneficiaries had an individual representative payee who did not have an SSN, and SSA had not followed its policy to retain the paper application,&#8221; the report continues. &#8220;These representative payees were not in eRPS. From April 2006 to September 2016, SSA paid these representative payees about $1 billion. Furthermore, unless it takes corrective action, we estimate SSA will pay these representative payees about $182.5 million in benefits annually.&#8221;</p>\n<p>Through random sampling, the Office of the Inspector General estimated that &#8220;150,257 beneficiaries had an individual representative payee who had a valid SSN that SSA should have recorded on the MBR/SSR. Of these, 26,912 beneficiaries had representative payees whom, according to eRPS, SSA had terminated or not selected. From October 2004 to September 2016, SSA paid these representative payees about $853.1 million.</p>\n<p>The OIG estimated that the SSA will pay about $189.6 million in benefits, annually, to representative payees whom it had terminated or not selected.</p>\n<p>The sampling also indicated that &#8220;22,426 beneficiaries had an individual representative payee who did not have an SSN, and SSA had not followed its policy to retain the paper application. These representative payees were not in eRPS. From April 2006 to September 2016, SSA paid these representative payees about $1 billion.&#8221;</p>\n<p>The OIG stressed that unless the SSA takes corrective action, they will &#8220;pay about $182.5 million in benefits, annually, to representative payees who do not have an SSN or paper application supporting their selection.&#8221;</p>\n<p>So, why did this happen? According to the OIG report, there were three reasons. The SSA did not:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>record the representative payee\u2019s SSN on the MBR/SSR or retain a paper application supporting its selection of the representative payee and;</li>\n<li>remove terminated or non-selected representative payees from the MBR/SSR or update the information in eRPS.</li>\n<li>SSA\u2019s systems did not always generate alerts when employees did not record a representative payee\u2019s SSN on the MBR/SSR or when eRPS and the MBR/SSR had discrepant payee information.</li>\n</ol>\n<p>Here are the results of the random sampling of 100 beneficiaries.</p><div class=\"wpInsert wpInsertInPostAd wpInsertMiddle\" style=\"margin: 5px; When appointing representative payees, we adhere to guidance in the <em>Social Security Act</em> (the Act).\u201d</p>\n<p>\u201cSpecific to this audit, the Act permits us to appoint, in certain circumstances, an undocumented alien, or applicant who resides outside the United States without a Social Security number (SSN) to serve as payee,\u201d the agency said. \u201cSpecifically, the Act states we should verify a person\u2019s SSN (or employer identification number) in our investigation of the payee applicant. However, the Act does not state that the applicant must have an SSN to serve as a payee.\u201d</p>\n<p>The \u201cabsence of an SSN is not a criterion preventing an individual from serving as payee,\u201d the agency added.</p>\n<p>The OIG issued the following recommendations:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>Take appropriate action for the 77 beneficiaries whose representative payees\u2019 SSNs are not on the MBR/SSR, as identified by our audit.</li>\n<li>Evaluate the results of its actions for the 77 beneficiaries and determine whether it should review the remaining population of 224,164 beneficiaries we identified.</li>\n<li>Improve controls to ensure (a) it records representative payees\u2019 SSNs on the MBR/SSR and (b) its systems generate alerts when there is a discrepancy between representative payee information in eRPS and the MBR/SSR.</li>\n</ol>\n<p><em>Courtesy of <a href=\"http://freedomoutpost.com/1-billion-was-paid-to-people-without-a-social-security-number-and-ssa-defends-it/\" target=\"_blank\">Freedom Outpost</a></em></p>\n<p><em>Tim Brown is an author and Editor at <a href=\"http://freedomoutpost.com/\">FreedomOutpost.com</a>, <a href=\"http://sonsoflibertymedia.com/\">SonsOfLibertyMedia.com</a>, <a href=\"http://gunsinthenews.com/\">GunsInTheNews.com</a> and <a href=\"http://thewashingtonstandard.com/\">TheWashingtonStandard.com</a>.
