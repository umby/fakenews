alt=\"bubblepop1\" width=\"560\" height=\"322\" /></p>\n<p>The Dow Jones continues to hit record highs and it reached a milestone not seen since January of 1987.<span id=\"more-69942\"></span></p>\n<p>Back then, the band Starship was at the top of the charts with their hit song <em>Nothing\u2019s Gonna Stop Us Now</em>, which appears to also be the rallying cry of Wall Street, Main Street and 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue today.</p>\n<p>Of course, for those who are familiar with their history, that year didn\u2019t end up so well for investors. On \u2018Black Monday\u2019, October 19th, 1987, the U.S. stock market suffered the largest crash in history with the Dow Jones losing 22.6% in value, amounting to roughly $500 billion in losses by the end of the trading day.</p>\n<p>And if the exuberance of 1987 is any guide, we may be looking at a similar set of events over the course of 2017:</p>\n<blockquote><p>The Dow finished with a more than 30 point gain Thursday and hit its tenth record closing high in a row.</p>\n<p>\u2026</p>\n<p>But what makes this rally truly historic is the fact that the market is also continuing to hit new highs during this epic run. That hasn\u2019t happened since Ronald Reagan\u2019s second term.</p>\n<p>The Dow wound up hitting 12 consecutive records in January of 1987, and it went up 13 straight days overall.</p>\n<p>Of course, market historians might ominously note that 1987 was also the year that the stock market suffered its worst one-day drop ever. (<strong><a href=\"http://money.cnn.com/2017/02/23/investing/stock-market-dow-record-winning-streak/\" target=\"_blank\">CNN Money</a></strong>)</p></blockquote>\n<p>The records in and of themselves aren\u2019t necessarily an indicator of a coming crash, but they should certainly be raising warnings signs, especially considering that many experts, including traditional bulls and bears, are noting that markets have gone up too high, too fast.</p>\n<blockquote><p>\u201cWe\u2019re due for at least a rest, if not a correction. We\u2019re way overbought,\u201d said Steve Massocca, managing director with Wedbush Securities. Massocca said the market will continue to focus on data, with a truck load of reports next week on everything from fourth-quarter GDP to inflation and auto sales. The other focus, of course will be on Washington. (<a href=\"http://www.cnbc.com/2017/02/23/dow-pulls-off-a-stunt-it-hasnt-done-in-30-years.html\" target=\"_blank\">CNBC</a>)</p></blockquote>\n<p>But don\u2019t take our word for it. Here is <a href=\"http://www.zerohedge.com/news/2016-06-21/fed-warns-stocks-are-vulnerable-forward-valuations-well-above-norms\" target=\"_blank\">what the Federal Reserve had to say</a> about stocks just a few months ago, before we started breaking historical records like we did on Thursday:</p>\n<blockquote><p><strong>Finally, the chart below shows the median price/revenue ratio of S&amp;P 500 component stocks, which recently pushed to the highest level in history, exceeding both the 2000 and 2007 market peaks.</strong> In recent quarters, the broad market has deteriorated, even in the most reasonably valued decile of stocks, but the most richly valued decile has held up for a last hurrah, as it did near the peaks of previous bubbles. This dispersion has created a headwind for hedged-equity strategies in U.S. stocks, particularly value-conscious strategies, but <strong>investors should understand that beneath the surface of this short-term outcome is singularly the most extreme point of overvaluation for the median stock in history.<br />\n</strong></p></blockquote>\n<p>As the <a href=\"http://www.zerohedge.com/news/2017-02-19/how-major-bank-calculated-20x-pe-now-fair-value\" target=\"_blank\">analysts at Zero Hedge</a>\u00a0highlight, we are literally at a point where major banks and<strong> analysis firms are justifying corporate valuations</strong> at 20+ price/earnings ratios<strong> with \u201canimal spirits\u201d as a driving force</strong>.</p><div class=\"wpInsert wpInsertInPostAd wpInsertMiddle\" style=\"margin: 5px; padding: 0px;\"><div id=\"ld-2901-1196\"></div><script>(function(w,d,s,i){w.ldAdInit=w.ldAdInit||[];w.ldAdInit.push({slot:8985198184511335,size:[0, 0],id:\"ld-2901-1196\"});if(!d.getElementById(i)){var j=d.createElement(s),p=d.getElementsByTagName(s)[0];j.async=true;j.src=\"//cdn2.lockerdome.com/_js/ajs.js\";j.id=i;p.parentNode.insertBefore(j,p);}})(window,document,\"script\",\"ld-ajs\");</script></div>\n<p>No, seriously, we\u2019re not kidding:</p>\n<blockquote><p>Carbon-based traders of a certain vintage \u2013 which excludes today\u2019s 20-year-old hedge fund managers \u2013 may recall a time when a 15x P/E was considered \u201cfair.\u201d Not any more. In fact, according to a new analysis by Barclays\u2019 equity strategist Keith Parker, which tries to factor in so-called \u201canimal spirits\u201d as a driver of valuation has found that 20x P/E is perfectly normal and fair for the current market, further demonstrating just how deep into the goalseeking rabbit hole US capital markets have fallen.</p>\n<p>First, to prove we are not joking, here is Barclays explaining why it is important to quantify animal spirits as a input factor of \u201cpermanently high plateaued\u201d P/E multiples:</p>\n<blockquote><p><strong>Core drivers of the P/E multiple and animal spirit indicators</strong></p>\n<p>In order to estimate the effects of \u201canimal spirits\u201d, or the potential effects of some of President Trump\u2019s agenda, we first model the S&amp;P 500 P/E using the core fundamental drivers of equity valuations. What it \u201cfinds\u201d is that what was once a \u201cfair\u201d 15x P/E is now really 20x P/E thanks to, drumroll, animal spirits</p>\n<p><em>Full report:\u00a0<a href=\"http://www.zerohedge.com/news/2017-02-19/how-major-bank-calculated-20x-pe-now-fair-value\" target=\"_blank\">How A Major Bank \u201cCalculated\u201d That 20x P/E Is Now \u201cFair Value\u201d</a></em></p></blockquote>\n<p>We\u2019re not going to attempt to predict what happens next, but history suggests that extreme valuations, consecutive\u00a0all-time record highs, and media exuberance often lead to the same end result.</p>\n<p>But this time it\u2019s different, right?</p>\n<p>Perhaps.
