itemprop=\"name\">William Smith</span></a></span>  </p></header><div class=\"entry-content\" itemprop=\"text\"><div class='code-block code-block-5' style='margin: 8px auto; text-align: center;'>\n<script type=\"text/javascript\" src=\"//ap.lijit.com/www/delivery/fpi.js?z=403625&#038;u=chesclyde&#038;width=300&#038;height=250\"></script></div>\n<p><strong>When President Obama left, I stayed on at the National Security Council in order to serve my country. I lasted eight days.</strong></p>\n<p>In 2011, I was hired, straight out of college, to work at the White House and eventually the National Security Council. My job there was to promote and protect the best of what my country stands for. I am a hijab-wearing Muslim woman\u2013\u2013I was the only hijabi in the West Wing\u2013\u2013and the Obama administration always made me feel welcome and included.</p>\n<p>Like most of my fellow American Muslims, I spent much of 2016 watching with consternation as Donald Trump vilified our community. Despite this\u2013\u2013or because of it\u2013\u2013I thought I should try to stay on the NSC staff during the Trump Administration, in order to give the new president and his aides a more nuanced view of Islam, and of America&#8217;s Muslim citizens.</p>\n<p>I lasted eight days.</p>\n<p>When Trump issued a ban on travelers from seven Muslim-majority countries and all Syrian refugees, I knew I could no longer stay and work for an administration that saw me and people like me not as fellow citizens, but as a threat.</p>\n<p>The evening before I left, bidding farewell to some of my colleagues, many of whom have also since left, I notified Trump\u2019s senior NSC communications adviser, Michael Anton, of my departure, since we shared an office. His initial surprise, asking whether I was leaving government entirely, was followed by silence\u2013\u2013almost in caution, not asking why. I told him anyway.</p>\n<p>I told him I had to leave because it was an insult walking into this country\u2019s most historic building every day under an administration that is working against and vilifying everything I stand for as an American and as a Muslim. I told him that the administration was attacking the basic tenets of democracy. I told him that I hoped that they and those in Congress were prepared to take responsibility for all the consequences that would attend their decisions.</p>\n<p>He looked at me and said nothing.</p>\n<p>It was only later that I learned he authored an essay under a pseudonym, extolling the virtues of authoritarianism and attacking diversity as a \u201cweakness,\u201d and Islam as \u201cincompatible with the modern West.\u201d</p>\n<p>My whole life and everything I have learned proves that facile statement wrong.</p>\n<p>My parents immigrated to the United States from Bangladesh in 1978 and strove to create opportunities for their children born in the states. My mother worked as a cashier, later starting her own daycare business. My father spent late nights working at Bank of America, and was eventually promoted to assistant vice president at one of its headquarters. Living the American dream, we\u2019d have family barbecues, trips to Disney World, impromptu soccer or football games, and community service projects. My father began pursuing his Ph.D., but in 1995 he was killed in a car accident.</p>\n<p>I was 12 when I started wearing a hijab. It was encouraged in my family, but it was always my choice. It was a matter of faith, identity, and resilience for me. After 9/11, everything would change. On top of my shock, horror, and heartbreak, I had to deal with the fear some kids suddenly felt towards me. I was glared at, cursed at, and spat at in public and in school. People called me a \u201cterrorist\u201d and told me, \u201cgo back to your country.\u201d</p>\n<p>My father taught me a Bengali proverb inspired by Islamic scripture: \u201cWhen a man kicks you down, get back up, extend your hand, and call him brother.\u201d Peace, patience, persistence, respect, forgiveness, and dignity. These were the values I\u2019ve carried through my life and my career.</p>\n<p>I never intended to work in government. I was among those who assumed the government was inherently corrupt and ineffective. Working in the Obama White House proved me wrong. You can\u2019t know or understand what you haven\u2019t been a part of.</p>\n<p>Still, inspired by President Obama, I joined the White House in 2011, after graduating from the George Washington University. I had interned there during my junior year, reading letters and taking calls from constituents at the Office of Presidential Correspondence. It felt surreal\u2013\u2013here I was, a 22-year-old American Muslim woman from Maryland who had been mocked and called names for covering my hair, working for the president of the United States.</p>\n<p>In 2012, I moved to the West Wing to join the Office of Public Engagement, where I worked with various communities, including American Muslims, on domestic issues such as health care. In early 2014, Deputy National Security Advisor Ben Rhodes offered me a position on the National Security Council (NSC). For two and a half years I worked down the hall from the Situation Room, advising President Obama\u2019s engagements with American Muslims, and working on issues ranging from advancing relations with Cuba and Laos to promoting global entrepreneurship among women and youth.</p>\n<p>A harsher world began to reemerge in 2015. In February, three young American Muslim students were killed in their Chapel Hill home by an Islamophobe. Both the media and administration were slow to address the attack, as if the dead had to be vetted before they could be mourned. It was emotionally devastating. But when a statement was finally released condemning the attack and mourning their loss, Rhodes took me aside to to tell me how grateful he was to have me there and wished there were more American Muslims working throughout government. America\u2019s government and decision-making should reflect its people.</p>\n<p>Later that month, the evangelist Franklin Graham declared that the government had \u201cbeen infiltrated by Muslims.\u201d One of my colleagues sought me out with a smile on his face and said, \u201cIf only he knew they were in the halls of the West Wing and briefed the president of the United States multiple times!\u201d I thought: Damn right I\u2019m here, exactly where I belong, a proud American dedicated to protecting and serving my country.</p>\n<p>Read More @<a
