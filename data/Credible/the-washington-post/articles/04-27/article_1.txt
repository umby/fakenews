itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p>Despite pressure from the White House, House GOP leaders determined Thursday night that they didn\u2019t have the votes to pass a rewrite of the Affordable Care Act and would not seek to put their proposal on the floor on Friday.</p> <p>A late push to act on health care had threatened the bipartisan deal to keep the government open for one week while lawmakers crafted a longer-term spending deal. Now, members are likely to approve the short-term spending bill when it comes to the floor and keep the government open past midnight on Friday.</p> <p>The failure of Republicans to unite behind the new health-care measure was a blow to White House officials, who were eager to see a vote ahead of President Trump\u2019s 100-day mark. Congressional leaders were more focused this week on securing a spending agreement, according to multiple people involved in the discussions who spoke on condition of anonymity because they were not allowed to talk publicly.</p> <p>It was also evidence of just how divided Republicans are about how to overhaul Obamacare, despite seven years of GOP promises to repeal and replace the 2010 law. Conservatives and moderates have repeatedly clashed over the contours of such a revamp, most sharply over bringing down insurance premiums in exchange for limiting the kind of coverage that is required to be offered.</p> <p>As many as 15 or so House Republicans have publicly said they will not support the latest GOP proposal, which was crafted among the White House, the hard-line House Freedom Caucus and a leading moderate lawmaker. That leaves House Speaker Paul D. Ryan (R-Wis.) and the White House an incredibly narrow path for passage. The speaker can lose only 22 Republicans on a health-care vote because Democrats have fiercely opposed any attempt to repeal the ACA.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"analysis\" data-duration=\"113536\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Will the federal government avoid a shutdown?\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"59010acde4b0a104b8689420\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/04-26-2017/t_1493240513355_name_shutdown_0916.jpg\" data-uuid=\"9d284ea6-2ac3-11e7-9081-f5405f56d3e4\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"FQfE9N4IjfU\"> <script src=\"https://d1pz6dax0t5mop.cloudfront.net/sandbox/powaBoot.js\"></script> <script src=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-stat/posttv/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Peter Stevenson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>Exiting a roughly 90-minute meeting in Ryan\u2019s office late Thursday night, House Majority Leader Kevin McCarthy (R-Calif.) said there would be no health-care vote Friday and that the main focus of the impromptu huddle was to ensure that the leadership had the votes to pass the one-week funding bill. </p> <p>\u201cWe are not voting on health-care tomorrow,\u201d McCarthy said Thursday, denying that leaders had ever wanted to vote by Friday. </p> <p>\u201cWe\u2019re still educating members,\u201d McCarthy said, adding: \u201cWe\u2019ve been making great progress. As soon as we have the votes, we\u2019ll vote on it.\u201d</p> <p> </p> <p>Trump weighed in on the spending negotiations on Thursday, tweeting that Democrats wanted to shut down the government to \u201c<a href=\"https://twitter.com/realDonaldTrump/status/857604709829529601\" shape=\"rect\">bail out insurance companies</a>.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cAs families prepare for summer vacations in our National Parks \u2014 Democrats threaten to close them and shut down the government. Terrible!\u201d Trump <a href=\"https://twitter.com/realDonaldTrump/status/857605104417026049\" shape=\"rect\">tweeted</a>.</p> <p>But the failure to make progress on health care is a good sign for smooth passage of the government funding bill \u2014 at least the version that will keep the government\u2019s lights on through May 5. Lawmakers are still finishing negotiations on a longer-term spending deal to fund the government through September. Republicans have stated that they need Democratic support to pass that measure, which they expect to consider next week.</p><div></div> <p>The Senate stands ready to approve the one-week spending bill, but only once the broader spending agreement is complete. Senators in both parties told reporters they were instructed not to leave Washington on Thursday night.</p> <p>Senate Minority Leader Charles E. Schumer (D-N.Y.) on Thursday blocked a measure to allow the Senate to approve the stopgap budget without a formal vote. He has indicated that he will drop his objections once he is assured that a long-term budget agreement is in place, according to Senate Democratic aides.</p> <p>\u201cInstead of rushing through health care,\u201d Schumer told reporters, \u201cthey first ought to get the government funded for a full year \u2014 plain and simple.\u201d</p> <p>The White House tried to jump-start talks on health care after House Republicans failed to pass a previous attempt at an ACA rewrite at the end of March. </p> <p>But Democrats fiercely oppose any effort to repeal the ACA and threatened to pull their support from the short-term bill if Republicans moved forward with that effort. </p> <p id=\"U12002202146946a4\">\u201cIf Republicans pursue this partisan path of forcing Americans to pay more for less and destabilizing our county\u2019s health-care system,\u201d said House Minority Whip Steny H. Hoyer (D-Md.), then \u201cRepublicans should be prepared to [keep the government open] on their own.\u201d</p> <p>House Minority Leader Nancy Pelosi (D-Calif.) told a meeting of Democratic whips on Thursday that she had called Ryan and told him there were two conditions for Democratic support of the short-term funding bill, according to aides in the room. Democrats would only sign off on the emergency spending measure to allow lawmakers time to pass the longer-term spending deal, and they would not back the measure if doing so would allow Ryan time to set up a vote on a GOP rewrite of the Affordable Care Act. </p> <p>The sudden turmoil was yet another sign of Congress\u2019s inability to meet deadlines for its most basic function: keeping the government\u2019s lights on. And it presages fights among Congress, the White House and both parties over spending priorities, despite the one-party rule that gave some observers hope that the gridlock would cease.</p> <p>But it was Republicans who this week jettisoned money for Trump\u2019s border wall because of widespread agreement that it should not be tied to the spending deal. Trump has also agreed to pay the cost-sharing subsidies for low-income people who get their insurance under the ACA \u2014 something he threatened to withhold if he did not get money for the wall.</p> <p>Ryan on Thursday also blamed Democrats for \u201cdragging their feet\u201d on negotiations in an apparent preparation to<b> </b>blame Democrats if their deal falls through.</p> <p>\u201cI would be shocked if they would want to see a government shutdown, that the Democrats would want to do that,\u201d Ryan told reporters at his weekly press briefing. \u201cThe reason this government funding bill is not ready is because Democrats have been dragging their feet.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U120022021469467LE\">The standoff is the first in what could be several budget battles between Congress and the White House this year. Trump has called for massive hikes to defense spending and harsh cuts to domestic agencies in his 2018 budget, a proposal that many Republicans have rejected out of hand. He is also likely to revive calls for money to begin constructing the border wall \u2014 which by some estimates would cost as much as $21 billion \u2014 in future budget negotiations.</p> <p>Ryan and Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell (R-Ky.) were forced to negotiate with Democrats<b> </b>on the budget<b> </b>after it became clear that Republicans lacked enough votes to pass a long-term spending bill on their own. As a result, the GOP leaders have had the uncomfortable task of writing a measure that ignores nearly all of Trump\u2019s priorities, including money for the border wall.</p> <p>Schumer also sought to refocus blame on the GOP, arguing that the only thing standing in the way of a long-term agreement was Trump himself. Congressional leaders were nearing a final deal several weeks ago, but the talks were derailed when Office of Management and budget director Mick Mulvaney announced that Trump would demand that money for the border wall be included in the funding bill.</p> <p>\u201cUnfortunately the president stood in the way for quite a long time,\u201d Schumer said. \u201cThat\u2019s why we\u2019re a little delayed.\u201d</p> <p>Congressional leaders had hoped to finalize a spending deal by midweek, but the talks were stuck on a small number of unrelated policy provisions, known as riders. Democrats complained that GOP leaders were trying to use the spending bill to cut abortion access and scale back Wall Street reforms passed under President Barack Obama.</p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p The speaker can lose only 22 Republicans on a health-care vote because Democrats have fiercely opposed any attempt to repeal the ACA.\n\n(Peter Stevenson/The Washington Post)\n\nExiting a roughly 90-minute meeting in Ryan\u2019s office late Thursday night, House Majority Leader Kevin McCarthy (R-Calif.) said there would be no health-care vote Friday and that the main focus of the impromptu huddle was to ensure that the leadership had the votes to pass the one-week funding bill.\n\n\u201cWe are not voting on health-care tomorrow,\u201d McCarthy said Thursday, denying that leaders had ever wanted to vote by Friday.\n\n\u201cWe\u2019re still educating members,\u201d McCarthy said, adding: \u201cWe\u2019ve been making great progress. As soon as we have the votes, we\u2019ll vote on it.\u201d\n\nTrump weighed in on the spending negotiations on Thursday, tweeting that Democrats wanted to shut down the government to \u201cbail out insurance companies.\u201d\n\n\u201cAs families prepare for summer vacations in our National Parks \u2014 Democrats threaten to close them and shut down the government. Terrible!\u201d Trump tweeted.\n\nBut the failure to make progress on health care is a good sign for smooth passage of the government funding bill \u2014 at least the version that will keep the government\u2019s lights on through May 5. Lawmakers are still finishing negotiations on a longer-term spending deal to fund the government through September. Republicans have stated that they need Democratic support to pass that measure, which they expect to consider next week.\n\nThe Senate stands ready to approve the one-week spending bill, but only once the broader spending agreement is complete. Senators in both parties told reporters they were instructed not to leave Washington on Thursday night.\n\nSenate Minority Leader Charles E. Schumer (D-N.Y.) on Thursday blocked a measure to allow the Senate to approve the stopgap budget without a formal vote. He has indicated that he will drop his objections once he is assured that a long-term budget agreement is in place, according to Senate Democratic aides.\n\n\u201cInstead of rushing through health care,\u201d Schumer told reporters, \u201cthey first ought to get the government funded for a full year \u2014 plain and simple.\u201d\n\nThe White House tried to jump-start talks on health care after House Republicans failed to pass a previous attempt at an ACA rewrite at the end of March.\n\nBut Democrats fiercely oppose any effort to repeal the ACA and threatened to pull their support from the short-term bill if Republicans moved forward with that effort.\n\n\u201cIf Republicans pursue this partisan path of forcing Americans to pay more for less and destabilizing our county\u2019s health-care system,\u201d said House Minority Whip Steny H. Hoyer (D-Md.), then \u201cRepublicans should be prepared to [keep the government open] on their own.\u201d\n\nHouse Minority Leader Nancy Pelosi (D-Calif.) told a meeting of Democratic whips on Thursday that she had called Ryan and told him there were two conditions for Democratic support of the short-term funding bill, according to aides in the room. Democrats would only sign off on the emergency spending measure to allow lawmakers time to pass the longer-term spending deal, and they would not back the measure if doing so would allow Ryan time to set up a vote on a GOP rewrite of the Affordable Care Act.\n\nThe sudden turmoil was yet another sign of Congress\u2019s inability to meet deadlines for its most basic function: keeping the government\u2019s lights on. And it presages fights among Congress, the White House and both parties over spending priorities, despite the one-party rule that gave some observers hope that the gridlock would cease.\n\nBut it was Republicans who this week jettisoned money for Trump\u2019s border wall because of widespread agreement that it should not be tied to the spending deal. Trump has also agreed to pay the cost-sharing subsidies for low-income people who get their insurance under the ACA \u2014 something he threatened to withhold if he did not get money for the wall.\n\nRyan on Thursday also blamed Democrats for \u201cdragging their feet\u201d on negotiations in an apparent preparation to blame Democrats if their deal falls through.\n\n\u201cI would be shocked if they would want to see a government shutdown, that the Democrats would want to do that,\u201d Ryan told reporters at his weekly press briefing. \u201cThe reason this government funding bill is not ready is because Democrats have been dragging their feet.\u201d\n\nThe standoff is the first in what could be several budget battles between Congress and the White House this year. Trump has called for massive hikes to defense spending and harsh cuts to domestic agencies in his 2018 budget, a proposal that many Republicans have rejected out of hand. He is also likely to revive calls for money to begin constructing the border wall \u2014 which by some estimates would cost as much as $21 billion \u2014 in future budget negotiations.\n\nRyan and Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell (R-Ky.) were forced to negotiate with Democrats on the budget after it became clear that Republicans lacked enough votes to pass a long-term spending bill on their own. As a result, the GOP leaders have had the uncomfortable task of writing a measure that ignores nearly all of Trump\u2019s priorities, including money for the border wall.\n\nSchumer also sought to refocus blame on the GOP, arguing that the only thing standing in the way of a long-term agreement was Trump himself. Congressional leaders were nearing a final deal several weeks ago, but the talks were derailed when Office of Management and budget director Mick Mulvaney announced that Trump would demand that money for the border wall be included in the funding bill.\n\n\u201cUnfortunately the president stood in the way for quite a long time,\u201d Schumer said. \u201cThat\u2019s why we\u2019re a little delayed.\u201d\n\nCongressional leaders had hoped to finalize a spending deal by midweek, but the talks were stuck on a small number of unrelated policy provisions, known as riders.
