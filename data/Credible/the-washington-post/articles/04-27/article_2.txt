itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p>President Trump was set to announce Saturday, on the 100th day of his presidency, that he was withdrawing from the North American Free Trade Agreement \u2014 the sort of disruptive proclamation that would upend both global and domestic politics and signal to his base that he was keeping his campaign promise to terminate what he once called\u00a0\u201ca total disaster\u201d and\u00a0\u201cone of the worst deals ever.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI was all set to terminate,\u201d Trump said in an Oval Office interview Thursday night. \u201cI looked forward to terminating. I was going to do it.\u201d</p> <p>There was just one problem: Trump\u2019s team \u2014 like on so many issues \u2014 was deeply divided.</p> <p>\u00a0As news of the president\u2019s plan reached Ottawa and Mexico City in the middle of the week and rattled the markets and Congress, Commerce Secretary Wilbur Ross, Agriculture Secretary Sonny Perdue and others huddled in meetings with Trump, urging him not to sign a document triggering a U.S. withdrawal from NAFTA.</p> <p>Perdue even brought along a prop to the Oval Office: A map of the United States that illustrated the areas that would be hardest hit, particularly from agriculture and manufacturing losses, and highlighting that many of those states and counties were\u00a0\u201cTrump country\u201d communities that had voted for the president in November.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"wonkblog\" data-duration=\"146304\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"The North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), explained\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>\u201cIt shows that I do have a very big farmer base, which is good,\u201d Trump recalled. \u201cThey like Trump, but I like them, and I\u2019m going to help them.\u201d</p> <p>By Wednesday night, Trump \u2014 who spent nearly two years as a candidate railing against the trade agreement \u2014 had backed down, saying that conversations with advisers and phone calls with the leaders of Canada and Mexico had persuaded him to reconsider.</p> <p>Recalling his late Wednesday conversation with Mexican President Enrique Pe\u00f1a Nieto, Trump said, \u201cHe said to me, \u2018I would really appreciate if we could negotiate instead of you terminating, because terminating sets a lot of things in motion that could be pretty devastating for a lot of people.\u2019\u2009\u201d</p> <p>Trump\u2019s declaration to withdraw from NAFTA, followed by his abrupt turnabout, was the latest in a series of sudden policy shifts and outright reversals in the frenzied lead-up\u00a0to his 100th day in office, reflecting a president desperate to notch tangible victories and to offer the impression of forward momentum. It was also another example of the inherent tension between the fiery populist, who ran on a promise to upend Washington, and the pragmatic businessman, who is eager to score wins and is easily influenced by a cadre of chief executive friends and top advisers, many with Wall Street pedigrees.</p> <p>Trump announced that he was not labeling China a currency manipulator, after months of promising to do so on Day One of his presidency. And he declared NATO\u00a0\u201cno longer obsolete,\u201d after months of saying it was.</p> <p>He threatened to shut down the government over the border wall, only to retreat on funding for an actual brick-and-mortar structure. And he tasked his team with drafting a complex overhaul of the nation\u2019s tax code only to suddenly announce, surprising even his own aides and advisers, that he expected a proposal to be rolled out within days.</p> <p>In some ways, as Trump nears the 100-day mark of his presidency, he is arguably beginning to find his footing, concentrating on core issues that have always animated him (trade) and others that captivate his business side (taxes). By refocusing on questions of trade and the economy, Trump has returned to more familiar and comfortable territory \u2014 the nationalistic populism that has defined him since the 1980s.</p><div></div> <p>\u201cThe president has put himself in a perfect position on NAFTA because folks know he\u2019s inclined [to be] negative on NAFTA, yet he\u2019s open to negotiating,\u201d said Chief of Staff Reince Priebus. \u201cIt\u2019s a good spot to be. The leverage is all with the president.\u201d</p> <p>Unlike his first failed attempt at passing a Republican replacement for President Barack Obama\u2019s health-care law, Trump and his team are directing as much activity as possible from within the West Wing, relying on executive orders rather than the more unwieldy but durable process of legislation.\u00a0</p> <p>But the approach has worried and alienated many of his closest allies, on Capitol Hill, on K Street and abroad. And it has showcased a president who often seems more interested in short-term accomplishments \u2014 and positive cable news headlines \u2014 than longer-term policy goals guided by ideology, and who can be swayed by unfolding events or compelling arguments from whomever he talks to last.</p> <p>\u201cI think they\u2019re just going to act whenever they can on executive action,\u201d said Chris Ruddy, the chief executive of\u00a0Newsmax, a conservative media company, and a longtime friend of Trump\u2019s. \u201cThe problem is it\u2019s very\u00a0temporary, but he wants to get things done, and trade has always been one of his big issues.\u201d</p> <p>Trump, Ruddy added, \u201cis a business guy. He thinks that America gets a bad part of these deals, and he wants to renegotiate them.\u201d</p> <div class=\"subhead\">\u2018All over the map\u2019</div> <p>With Saturday\u2019s 100-day marker fast approaching, and eager for a win, the president turned his attention to taxes.\u00a0</p> <p>Trump had privately groused that he wished he had tackled taxes before trying to push through health care, a view magnified by some outside friends and confidants. Even something more modest than the full overhaul for which he hoped, such as cutting corporate tax rates, they said, would provide the president and his base with an energizing triumph.</p> <p>But it was <a href=\"https://www.nytimes.com/2017/04/19/opinion/why-are-republicans-making-tax-reform-so-hard.html?_r=0\" target=\"_self\">an April 19 op-ed</a> in the New York Times, titled\u00a0\u201cWhy Are Republicans Making Tax Reform So Hard?\u201d and penned by Steve Forbes, Larry Kudlow, Arthur Laffer and Stephen Moore, that helped propel Trump to act.\u00a0</p> <p>The op-ed, written by conservatives who have strong influence within the White House, said an overhaul of the tax code would give Trump a much-needed \u201clegislative victory\u201d and complained that the White House \u201cseems to be all over the map on the subject.\u201d It called on the administration to move quickly on a tax proposal, not to overthink it and to push forward \u201cwith some degree of urgency.\u201d\u00a0</p> <p>Trump saw the op-ed right as he was becoming restless with the success of his economic agenda.</p> <p>The White House rushed to engage the op-ed\u2019s authors and reassure the economic conservatives who have privately complained about Trump\u2019s nationalistic streak on trade and the lack of action of taxes.</p> <p>When Kudlow and Moore gathered a group of conservatives Tuesday evening at Cafe Berlin, a white-tablecloth German restaurant on Capitol Hill, Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin stopped by, even though he was not scheduled to attend.\u00a0</p> <p>\u201cWe texted him and said, \u2018Come by if you\u2019d like,\u2019\u2009\u201d Kudlow said. \u201cWell, he did, and he spoke for two or three minutes and took questions.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cEveryone looked around and said, \u2018This is the Steve we knew during the campaign,\u2019\u2009\u201d Kudlow added, referring to Mnuchin\u2019s enthusiasm for sweeping tax cuts.</p> <p>On April 21, two days after the op-ed ran,\u00a0Trump announced in <a href=\"https://apnews.com/c810d7de280a47e88848b0ac74690c83\" target=\"_self\">an interview with the Associated Press</a> that his advisers would be releasing a tax plan by the following Wednesday, or \u201cshortly thereafter.\u201d</p> <p>Some aides working on the plan were stunned, caught unaware of the expedited timeline.</p> <p>Still, they reasoned, maybe\u00a0\u201cshortly thereafter\u201d meant they could unveil the plan a week or two later. But hours after the AP interview, during an appearance at the Treasury Department, Trump stood beside Mnuchin and told reporters that the tax plan would come out Wednesday.\u00a0</p> <p>The proposal unveiled that day<b>\u00a0</b>offered some specifics \u2014 cutting the corporate tax rate to 15 percent and collapsing seven tax brackets down into three \u2014 but was vague in other areas, including just how the government would pay for it. Critics seized on the one-page printout the White House distributed Wednesday with details of the tax plan as the flimsy embodiment of its lack of depth.\u00a0</p> <p>\u201cIt was a restatement of bullet points that Larry Kudlow and Stephen Moore drafted on the back of a cocktail napkin at the 21 Club,\u201d quipped one longtime Washington Republican in contact with the White House.\u00a0</p> <p>But the tax proposal \u2014 unveiled with great fanfare in a midday briefing with reporters \u2014 achieved several of Trump\u2019s key objectives, garnering him a day of largely positive headlines; laying at least a baseline marker for a top policy goal; and reassuring Americans, many of whom voted for the promise of a businessman commander in chief, that pocketbook relief would be arriving by next tax season.\u00a0</p> <p>\u201cThe president was being ill-advised that he had to repeal Obamacare before passing taxes,\u201d said Sam Nunberg, a former Trump adviser.\u00a0\u201cThe two are mutually exclusive.\u201d<br/> </p> <p>Kudlow spent Wednesday at the White House, chatting with top officials following the rollout of a plan that many credited him with helping to spur.</p> <p>\u201cIf we helped, I\u2019m very pleased,\u201d Kudlow said. \u201cI think the president has set a terrific tax reform, economic growth marker.\u201d</p> <p>Still, he dismissed the suggestion that he was the impetus for Trump\u2019s swift action on taxes.<br/> </p> <p>The president, Kudlow said,\u00a0\u201cjust wants to move. He\u2019s been tied in knots on health care, and he had an impulse to get it together on taxes.\u201d<b></b> </p> <div class=\"subhead\">\u2018Ambush\u2019</div> <p>Canada and Mexico were blindsided Wednesday as news of Trump\u2019s planned NAFTA withdrawal spread north and south of the border.\u00a0</p> <p>Arturo Sarukhan, a former Mexican ambassador to the United States, described the news as a \u201cmy way or the highway ambush\u201d from the White House, especially coming amid what has been weeks of steady and amicable discussions among the three countries about revamping the trade agreement.\u00a0</p> <p>But Mexico \u2014 which was already on edge following Trump\u2019s brief flirtation with attaching border-wall funding to a must-pass, short-term spending bill \u2014 quickly leapt to action. Two cabinet-level officials in Mexico reached out to their U.S. counterparts to deliver a blunt message: If Trump officially announced the U.S. intention to withdraw from NAFTA, Mexico would not return to the negotiating table.</p> <p>Mexico would not, the officials warned, negotiate with \u201ca gun to its head.\u201d</p> <p>The president, meanwhile, was hearing a similar message from some of his own senior advisers. Ross, the commerce secretary, and Jared Kushner, the president\u2019s son-in-law, scrambled to persuade Trump to back down.</p> <p>The United States can only trigger the six-month clock to withdraw from NAFTA once, they said. They told the president that he had strong leverage to renegotiate the trade deal but that once he publicly signaled his intent to leave, the situation would become so politically fraught for Canada and Mexico that they would not be able to return to negotiations, even if they wanted to.</p> <p>In the Oval Office interview, however, Trump repeatedly insisted that he was ready to pull out of NAFTA. At one point, he turned to Kushner, who was standing near his desk, and asked, \u201cWas I ready to terminate NAFTA?\u201d</p> <p>\u201cYeah,\u201d Kushner said, before explaining the case he made to the president: \u201cI said, \u2018Look, there\u2019s plusses and minuses to doing it,\u2019 and either way he would have ended up in a good place.\u201d</p> <p>Perdue, the agriculture secretary, and Secretary of State Rex Tillerson also cautioned Trump against moving ahead, while two of the White House\u2019s populists,\u00a0trade adviser Peter Navarro and chief strategist Stephen K. Bannon, urged him to stay the course, announcing his intention to withdraw in a splashy prime-time rally Saturday night in Harrisburg, Pa.</p> <p>The administration\u00a0also received pressure from hundreds of business executives from around the country, many of whom called the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, asking what was going to happen, before calling the White House directly, urging Trump not to sign the order.</p> <p>Tom Donohue, the chamber\u2019s chief executive, also relayed the message to senior White House officials.\u00a0</p> <p>The complaints pouring in from agriculture groups were even more apoplectic,\u00a0warning White House officials that withdrawing from NAFTA could devastate the U.S. agriculture industry, allowing Mexico to reinstate high tariffs against U.S. exports.</p> <p>The Mexican government, meanwhile, had\u00a0\u201cseveral\u201d contacts with its Canadian counterparts throughout the day Wednesday to share reactions and map out a joint strategy, including separate phone calls between their countries\u2019\u00a0leaders and Trump that evening.\u00a0</p> <p>\u201cWe had the same position,\u201d said Mexican Foreign Minister Luis Videgaray on Thursday in an interview on Mexican television.\u00a0</p> <p id=\"U12002212546631iL\">Trump publicly claimed Thursday that his phone calls with Pe\u00f1a Nieto and Canadian Prime Minister\u00a0Justin Trudeau persuaded him to give negotiations a chance.</p> <p>But a senior administration official said Trump had already decided to hold off on signing the NAFTA termination letter before his phone calls with Mexico and Canada.</p> <p>In the interview, Trump recounted his internal deliberations: \u201cIn one way, I like the termination. In the other way, I like them \u2014 a lot, both of them. We have a very good relationship. And it\u2019s very hard when you have a relationship, it\u2019s very much something that would not be a nice act. It would not be exactly a friendly act.\u201d</p> <p>But, the president added, he reserves the right to change his mind. \u201cI can always terminate,\u201d Trump said. \u201cThey called me up, they said, \u2018Could we try negotiating?\u2019 I said, \u2018Absolutely, yes.\u2019 If we can\u2019t come to a satisfactory conclusion, we\u2019ll terminate NAFTA.\u201d</p> <div class=\"subhead\">Promises unfulfilled</div> <p>A few doors from the Oval Office, Bannon works out of what he calls \u201cthe war room,\u201d a West Wing hideaway adjoining the chief of staff\u2019s spacious suite, from which Karl Rove and David Axelrod once worked.</p> <p>\u00a0Upon moving in, Bannon cleared out most of the furniture, save for a standing desk and chairs, and plastered the walls with lists of Trump\u2019s campaign promises \u2014 scores, even hundreds of them, with green check marks or giant red X\u2019s over those that have been met.</p> <p>A list of 10 major pieces of legislation that Trump promised in his \u201cContract with Voters\u201d hangs from near the ceiling down to the floor. None is crossed out yet.</p> <p>\u00a0On Wednesday evening, around the time Trump was talking with his Canadian and Mexican counterparts, one whiteboard contained an ominous marking: \u201cNAFTA\u201d and\u00a0\u201cApril 29.\u201d It underscored Bannon\u2019s hope that on Saturday night, Trump would sign the paperwork initiating the withdrawal from NAFTA.\u00a0</p> <p>Indeed, that same whiteboard contained other trade-specific actions, many boasting already checked-off promises.\u00a0</p> <p>Withdrawing from the Trans-Pacific Partnership? Check. Action on the aluminum and steel industries? Check and check.</p> <p>One of the few unfulfilled, so far, was NAFTA.\u00a0</p> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>Robert Costa and Karen Tumulty contributed to this report.</p> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\"></p>
