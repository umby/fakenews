Here's what (little) it shows. (Gillian Brockell/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>President Trump paid $38 million in federal taxes in 2005 on income of $153\u00a0million and reported a $105\u00a0million write-down in business losses, according to a copy of his <a href=\"http://www.msnbc.com/sites/msnbc/files/trump2005tax.pdf\">tax return first revealed Tuesday night on MSNBC</a>.</p> <p>Trump paid an effective tax rate of 24 percent and saved millions of dollars in additional taxes by claiming the losses, according to the document, the first two pages of which were obtained by Pulitzer Prize-winning journalist David Cay Johnston and first shared on \u201cThe Rachel Maddow Show.\u201d</p> <p>The return shows that Trump paid $36.5\u00a0million in income tax, of which about $31\u00a0million was in the form of an \u201calternative minimum tax,\u201d a supplemental tax designed to cut down on filers with excessive deductions. He also paid about $1.5\u00a0million in Medicare and Social Security taxes.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"http://www.msnbc.com/sites/msnbc/files/trump2005tax.pdf\">See the tax documents from Trump's 2005 return</a>] </i> </p> <p>The document offers a rare snapshot of Trump\u2019s personal finances, considering he has refused to disclose his tax returns to the public. The White House issued a statement chastising MSNBC for reporting on Trump\u2019s taxes \u2014 \u201ctotally illegal,\u201d read the statement \u2014 but also confirming top-line numbers from the return and defending Trump.</p> <p>Trump\u2019s taxes were a major flash point in last year\u2019s campaign and continue to be a subject of intense scrutiny from Democrats and others who believe they would show whether he has financial relationships with any Russian entities.</p> <p>The newly revealed pages from his 2005 return do not detail his financial ties, but they do seem to disprove the theory that some Democrats advanced in last year\u2019s campaign that Trump avoided paying any federal income taxes during that period.</p> <p>In their first general-election debate, Democratic nominee Hillary Clinton speculated about Trump, \u201cmaybe he doesn\u2019t want the American people \u2014 all of you watching tonight \u2014 to know that he\u2019s paid nothing in federal taxes.\u201d At that, Trump quipped, \u201cThat makes me smart.\u201d</p> <p>Donald Trump Jr., the president\u2019s oldest son, tweeted, \u201cThank you Rachel Maddow for proving to your #Trump hating followers how successful @realDonaldTrump is &amp; that he paid $40mm in taxes! #Taxes\u201d</p> <p>Last fall, the New York Times revealed that Trump reported a massive $916\u00a0million loss in 1995, which he could have carried over to allow him to offset taxable income and avoid paying income taxes for up to 18 years. Though Trump was able to use those net losses as a deduction in 2005, the returns show, they did not zero out his tax obligation altogether.</p> <p>Trump used real estate deductions to pay no federal income taxes in 1978 and 1979, according to Trump tax returns submitted years ago to New Jersey regulators. Much of those losses came from a deduction offered to real estate developers, like Trump, allowing them to subtract from their income on the basis that buildings depreciate in value over time.</p> <p>Trump repeatedly has refused to release his income tax returns \u2014 breaking with four decades of tradition for presidential nominees \u2014 claiming that he has been under audit and that his lawyers have counseled him not to release them.</p> <p>The Internal Revenue Service has not confirmed that it is auditing Trump\u2019s taxes.</p> <p>The Washington Post could not independently verify the 2005 return, but a statement from the White House indicated that it is authentic. The statement, issued to reporters anonymously to be attributed to a White House spokesperson, accused MSNBC of unlawfully releasing Trump\u2019s tax returns.</p> <p>\u201cYou know you are desperate for ratings when you are willing to violate the law to push a story about two pages of tax returns from over a decade ago,\u201d the statement said. It added that Trump, as \u201cone of the most successful businessmen in the world,\u201d paid \u201cno more tax than legally required.\u201d</p> <p>The statement went on to say: \u201cMr. Trump paid $38\u00a0million dollars even after taking into account large scale depreciation for construction, on an income of more than $150\u00a0million dollars, as well as paying tens of millions of dollars in other taxes such as sales and excise taxes and employment taxes and this illegally published return proves just that. Despite this substantial income figure and tax paid, it is totally illegal to steal and publish tax returns. The dishonest media can continue to make this part of their agenda, while the President will focus on his, which includes tax reform that will benefit all Americans.\u201d</p> <p>The tax documents were sent to Johnston, a veteran investigative reporter who specializes in taxes and formerly worked at the New York Times. He speculated on air to Maddow that Trump himself may have sent him the returns.</p> <p>The return shows Trump and wife Melania paid most of their income taxes as an alternative minimum tax. Taxpayers requesting many itemized deductions must pay the AMT in certain circumstances. The AMT rejects specific deductions, including for dependents and assorted real estate write-downs. In his tax-reform plan, Trump has called for eliminating the AMT.</p> <p>The return shows that Trump\u2019s business income in 2005 totaled roughly $42\u00a0million, with capital gains from investments totaling roughly $32\u00a0million and income from partnerships, trusts and so-called \u201cS corporations\u201d totaling roughly $67\u00a0million.</p> <p>Trump used roughly $103\u00a0million in net operating losses, carried over from previous years, to greatly lower that tax obligation. Trump also used such losses, likely from a series of costly business failures and Atlantic City bankruptcies, to lower his tax bill in 1995.</p> <p>Trump has routinely taken pride in his aggressive working of the nation\u2019s tax laws.</p> <p>\u201cI have brilliantly used those laws,\u201d Trump said at a campaign event in October. \u201cI was able to use the tax laws of this country, and my business acumen, to dig out of the real estate mess \u2014 you would call it a depression \u2014 when few others were able to do what I did.\u201d</p> <p>Trump\u2019s reported income in 2005 may call into question his net worth. Trump says he is now worth more than $10\u00a0billion, but independent estimators have said that figure may be overstated. In 2005, Trump said he was worth more than $5\u00a0billion.</p> <p>Trump sued author Timothy O\u2019Brien after his 2005 book, \u201cTrumpNation,\u201d cited anonymous sources suggesting Trump was instead worth $150 to $250\u00a0million. In depositions from that case, O\u2019Brien\u2019s attorneys revealed estimates from two lenders to Trump, North Fork Bank and Deutsche Bank, who suggested Trump\u2019s net worth was between $800\u00a0million and $1.2\u00a0billion.</p> <p>Trump\u2019s current finances are set to face a new test with the coming April 18 tax deadline. He has steadfastly refused to release his returns, pointing both to ongoing audits and to his belief that Americans do not care to see them. However, a Pew Research Center survey in January found that 60 percent of Americans believe that Trump has a responsibility to release his returns.</p> <p>All U.S. presidents are automatically audited every year, as prescribed by a guideline in the Internal Revenue Service manual in place for presidents and vice presidents since the 1970s. Trump could urge Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin to change that provision, legal experts said.
