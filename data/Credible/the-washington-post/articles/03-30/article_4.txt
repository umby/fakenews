office on the seventh floor of the State Department building, where sightings of him are rare on the floors below.</p> <p>On many days, he blocks out several hours on his schedule as \u201creading time,\u201d when he is cloistered in his office poring over the memos he prefers ahead of in-person meetings.</p> <p>Most of his interactions are with an insular circle of political aides who are new to the State Department. Many career diplomats say they still have not met him, and some have been instructed not to speak to him directly \u2014 or even make eye contact.</p> <p>On his first three foreign trips, Tillerson skipped visits with State Department employees and their families, embassy stops that were standard morale-boosters under other secretaries of state.</p> <p>Eight weeks into his tenure as President Trump\u2019s top diplomat, the former ExxonMobil chief executive is isolated, walled off from the State Department\u2019s corps of bureaucrats in Washington and around the world. His distant management style has created growing bewilderment among foreign officials who are struggling to understand where the United States stands on key issues. It has sown mistrust among career employees at State, who swap paranoid stories about Tillerson that often turn out to be untrue. And it threatens to undermine the power and reach of the State Department, which has been targeted for a 30\u00a0percent funding cut in Trump\u2019s budget.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"83242\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Trump\u2019s Transition: Who is Rex Tillerson?\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"584c7ba6e4b0d21f321acb49\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/12-10-2016/t_1481407286436_name_223190248_1919.jpg\" data-uuid=\"77b0e576-bf24-11e6-ae79-bec72d34f8c9\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"KjichxxJGek\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Thomas Johnson,Victoria Walker,Danielle Kunitz/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>Many have expressed alarm that Tillerson has not fought harder for the agency he now leads.</p> <p>Rep. Eliot L. Engel (N.Y.), the top Democrat on the House Foreign Affairs Committee, said Tillerson called him after the proposed cuts were announced. Engel said Tillerson seemed to share Engel\u2019s concern that the cuts are \u201cdraconian\u201d and counterproductive. But Engel said Tillerson seemed to signal his acquiescence when he called them \u201ca glide path to what was about to happen.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI\u2019m chagrined by what\u2019s happening, or <i>not</i> happening,\u201d Engel said.</p> <p>\u201cWhen you put it all together, it certainly seems they\u2019re trying to downsize the State Department and make it irrelevant. I\u2019m at a loss for words. Why would Tillerson take the job if he was not going to defend his agency?\u201d</p> <p>Tillerson\u2019s low profile reflects his desire to do his job without fanfare, said a senior aide who spoke on the condition of anonymity to comment frankly.</p> <p>As an oil executive, Tillerson traveled the world negotiating deals behind closed doors, with just one or two aides accompanying him. Tillerson\u2019s current aide said the secretary thinks that model served him well.</p> <p>British Ambassador to the United States Kim Darroch brushed off the concerns about staff vacancies, confusion and a clamp on information. His country\u2019s dealings with the Trump administration have gone well starting with Prime Minister Theresa May\u2019s visit to the White House <span>just </span>days after Trump took office, Darroch said.</p><div></div> <p>\u201cWe are having absolutely no problem, I promise you, with access or accessibility\u201d at the State Department or White House, Darroch said.</p> <p>Still, the secretary of state is visibly uncomfortable with the vast infrastructure and expectations of public diplomacy that come with his new role.</p> <p>Tillerson\u2019s slow start has rattled other foreign diplomats. Some complain that with assistant secretary of state positions occupied only by \u201cacting\u201d deputies, they have no one of authority to contact. Tillerson remains the only Senate-confirmed official selected by Trump anywhere inside the State Department building. Weeks after the White House embarrassed Tillerson by rejecting the seasoned foreign policy hand he had selected for a deputy, Republican lawyer John J. Sullivan is the leading candidate. Sullivan held senior jobs in the George W. Bush administration but has no direct experience in the State Department.</p> <p>Some diplomats have begun meeting <span>with each other </span>to swap notes on how to decipher the fledgling administration\u2019s policies.</p> <p>\u201cWe\u2019re rowing against the current, and the current has a Twitter account,\u201d a foreign diplomat posted in Washington said about how information relayed by State Department diplomats can be undercut by a presidential tweet.</p> <p>Current and recently departed State Department officials \u2014 all of whom spoke on the condition of anonymity to offer candid assessments of what one called the \u201cbenching\u201d of the oldest Cabinet department \u2014 said Tillerson is paying a price.</p> <p>Tillerson\u2019s political advisers have little foreign policy experience and little pull at the White House, current and former officials said. Their dealings with the department staff have sometimes been testy and unpleasant.</p> <p>\u201cPart of it is a deep distrust of bureaucracy,\u201d said a senior Senate Democratic aide. \u201cIt sets a command climate that makes people cautious and paranoid. These folks, in their political-commissar roles, take that to an extreme. Everything we have heard is about how small the aperture is for information coming in and going out of the secretary\u2019s office. That is not a recipe for success.\u201d</p> <p>For weeks, a rumor circulated in Foggy Bottom <span>\u2014 an informal name for the department \u2014 </span>that Tillerson was ripping up a grand adjacent office on \u201cMahogany Row\u201d to install a warren of cubicles for White House-approved political aides who could bypass department employees. According to the senior Tillerson aide, the story was untrue. The secretary is merely converting the office into a conference room, the aide said, intended to be a place where he can convene the sort of strategy sessions he found useful when gaming out oil deals and profit plans at Exxon.</p> <p>\u201cThe man loves his whiteboards. He wanted to build out a spot, a working room, to engage with colleagues and map things out,\u201d the aide said.</p> <p>Tillerson charmed employees on his first day on the job with a pledge to listen and learn \u2014 \u201cHi, I\u2019m the new guy,\u201d he said then \u2014 but the ensuing weeks suggest that the former executive\u2019s boardroom sensibilities are an awkward fit for the diplomatic salon.</p> <p>Career employees might have helped Tillerson avoid embarrassing gaffes such as the initial decision not to attend a NATO foreign ministers\u2019 meeting.</p> <p>The 28-member session Friday in Brussels was rescheduled to accommodate Tillerson, who reversed course amid criticism that by his planned absence he had offered proof of the new administration\u2019s indifference to the transatlantic military alliance.</p> <p id=\"U12001201914769STC\">\u201cRookie error, plain and simple,\u201d one former State Department employee said, noting that department officials in charge of dealings with NATO and Europe were cut out of many planning discussions.</p> <p>The debacle may serve as an example of how Tillerson\u2019s corporate insistence on efficient time management did not serve him well, another official said.</p> <p>But Darroch, the British ambassador, said the dust-up over the NATO meeting ended well, with Tillerson making room for it<span> in his schedule</span>.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s great that he\u2019s coming over to Europe pretty much just for this meeting, racking up the air miles already, and so it\u2019s all fine,\u201d Darroch said with a smile.</p> <p>Tillerson has opted to scrap at least two senior jobs formerly housed on the seventh floor, including that of department counselor, the Tillerson aide said. Some secretaries have used that job as a kind of in-house truth-teller, someone empowered to tell the boss she or he is making a mistake. Other secretaries <span>of state </span>have used the counselor to act as a surrogate, or like Tillerson, opted not to fill the position at all.</p> <p id=\"U120012019147691RF\">Rumors that Tillerson does not plan to fill the many vacancies at the assistant-secretary level are not true, the aide said. But a lack of guidance from Tillerson since he arrived Feb.\u00a02 has fostered a sense among career diplomats that they are considered an obstacle to change, one department official said.</p> <p>\u201cWe\u2019re rooting for our secretary of state to come around, and trying to figure out a way to convince him we do work for administrations of both parties,\u201d the official said.</p> <p>Tillerson has told employees that he will travel less than previous secretaries did and will take a smaller, faster plane<span> that is more like the corporate jets of his former life</span>. The government plane he is using this week in Europe has room for fewer than a dozen staff members, perhaps half the contingent that customarily traveled with recent predecessors.</p> <p>No official note-taker accompanied him on a recent trip, so senior aides did the job to have a record of his talks with foreign ministers, according to a congressional aide.</p> <p>On Thursday, Tillerson held his first visit with State Department employees abroad, at the U.S. Embassy in Ankara, where he appeared to acknowledge some dissent in the ranks when he urged \u201chonest\u201d confrontation of differing opinions.</p> <p>\u201cThat\u2019s how we come to a better decision in all that we do. And only if we do that can we then be honest with all of our partners and allies around the world as well. And still, I mean, we\u2019re going to have our differences, but we\u2019re going to be very honest and open about those, so at least we understand them.\u201d</p> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>Julie Tate (BRENDAN SMIALOWSKI/AFP/Getty Images)\n\nSecretary of State Rex Tillerson takes a private elevator to his palatial office on the seventh floor of the State Department building, where sightings of him are rare on the floors below.\n\nOn many days, he blocks out several hours on his schedule as \u201creading time,\u201d when he is cloistered in his office poring over the memos he prefers ahead of in-person meetings.\n\nMost of his interactions are with an insular circle of political aides who are new to the State Department. Many career diplomats say they still have not met him, and some have been instructed not to speak to him directly \u2014 or even make eye contact.\n\nOn his first three foreign trips, Tillerson skipped visits with State Department employees and their families, embassy stops that were standard morale-boosters under other secretaries of state.\n\nEight weeks into his tenure as President Trump\u2019s top diplomat, the former ExxonMobil chief executive is isolated, walled off from the State Department\u2019s corps of bureaucrats in Washington and around the world. His distant management style has created growing bewilderment among foreign officials who are struggling to understand where the United States stands on key issues. It has sown mistrust among career employees at State, who swap paranoid stories about Tillerson that often turn out to be untrue. And it threatens to undermine the power and reach of the State Department, which has been targeted for a 30 percent funding cut in Trump\u2019s budget.\n\n(Thomas Johnson,Victoria Walker,Danielle Kunitz/The Washington Post)\n\nMany have expressed alarm that Tillerson has not fought harder for the agency he now leads.\n\nRep. Eliot L. Engel (N.Y.), the top Democrat on the House Foreign Affairs Committee, said Tillerson called him after the proposed cuts were announced. Engel said Tillerson seemed to share Engel\u2019s concern that the cuts are \u201cdraconian\u201d and counterproductive. But Engel said Tillerson seemed to signal his acquiescence when he called them \u201ca glide path to what was about to happen.\u201d\n\n\u201cI\u2019m chagrined by what\u2019s happening, or not happening,\u201d Engel said.\n\n\u201cWhen you put it all together, it certainly seems they\u2019re trying to downsize the State Department and make it irrelevant. I\u2019m at a loss for words. Why would Tillerson take the job if he was not going to defend his agency?\u201d\n\nTillerson\u2019s low profile reflects his desire to do his job without fanfare, said a senior aide who spoke on the condition of anonymity to comment frankly.\n\nAs an oil executive, Tillerson traveled the world negotiating deals behind closed doors, with just one or two aides accompanying him. Tillerson\u2019s current aide said the secretary thinks that model served him well.\n\nBritish Ambassador to the United States Kim Darroch brushed off the concerns about staff vacancies, confusion and a clamp on information. His country\u2019s dealings with the Trump administration have gone well starting with Prime Minister Theresa May\u2019s visit to the White House just days after Trump took office, Darroch said.\n\n\u201cWe are having absolutely no problem, I promise you, with access or accessibility\u201d at the State Department or White House, Darroch said.\n\nStill, the secretary of state is visibly uncomfortable with the vast infrastructure and expectations of public diplomacy that come with his new role.\n\nTillerson\u2019s slow start has rattled other foreign diplomats. Some complain that with assistant secretary of state positions occupied only by \u201cacting\u201d deputies, they have no one of authority to contact. Tillerson remains the only Senate-confirmed official selected by Trump anywhere inside the State Department building. Weeks after the White House embarrassed Tillerson by rejecting the seasoned foreign policy hand he had selected for a deputy, Republican lawyer John J. Sullivan is the leading candidate. Sullivan held senior jobs in the George W. Bush administration but has no direct experience in the State Department.\n\nSome diplomats have begun meeting with each other to swap notes on how to decipher the fledgling administration\u2019s policies.\n\n\u201cWe\u2019re rowing against the current, and the current has a Twitter account,\u201d a foreign diplomat posted in Washington said about how information relayed by State Department diplomats can be undercut by a presidential tweet.\n\nCurrent and recently departed State Department officials \u2014 all of whom spoke on the condition of anonymity to offer candid assessments of what one called the \u201cbenching\u201d of the oldest Cabinet department \u2014 said Tillerson is paying a price.\n\nTillerson\u2019s political advisers have little foreign policy experience and little pull at the White House, current and former officials said. Their dealings with the department staff have sometimes been testy and unpleasant.\n\n\u201cPart of it is a deep distrust of bureaucracy,\u201d said a senior Senate Democratic aide. \u201cIt sets a command climate that makes people cautious and paranoid. These folks, in their political-commissar roles, take that to an extreme. Everything we have heard is about how small the aperture is for information coming in and going out of the secretary\u2019s office. That is not a recipe for success.\u201d\n\nFor weeks, a rumor circulated in Foggy Bottom \u2014 an informal name for the department \u2014 that Tillerson was ripping up a grand adjacent office on \u201cMahogany Row\u201d to install a warren of cubicles for White House-approved political aides who could bypass department employees. According to the senior Tillerson aide, the story was untrue. The secretary is merely converting the office into a conference room, the aide said, intended to be a place where he can convene the sort of strategy sessions he found useful when gaming out oil deals and profit plans at Exxon.\n\n\u201cThe man loves his whiteboards. He wanted to build out a spot, a working room, to engage with colleagues and map things out,\u201d the aide said.\n\nTillerson charmed employees on his first day on the job with a pledge to listen and learn \u2014 \u201cHi, I\u2019m the new guy,\u201d he said then \u2014 but the ensuing weeks suggest that the former executive\u2019s boardroom sensibilities are an awkward fit for the diplomatic salon.\n\nCareer employees might have helped Tillerson avoid embarrassing gaffes such as the initial decision not to attend a NATO foreign ministers\u2019 meeting.\n\nThe 28-member session Friday in Brussels was rescheduled to accommodate Tillerson, who reversed course amid criticism that by his planned absence he had offered proof of the new administration\u2019s indifference to the transatlantic military alliance.\n\n\u201cRookie error, plain and simple,\u201d one former State Department employee said, noting that department officials in charge of dealings with NATO and Europe were cut out of many planning discussions.\n\nThe debacle may serve as an example of how Tillerson\u2019s corporate insistence on efficient time management did not serve him well, another official said.\n\nBut Darroch, the British ambassador, said the dust-up over the NATO meeting ended well, with Tillerson making room for it in his schedule.\n\n\u201cIt\u2019s great that he\u2019s coming over to Europe pretty much just for this meeting, racking up the air miles already, and so it\u2019s all fine,\u201d Darroch said with a smile.\n\nTillerson has opted to scrap at least two senior jobs formerly housed on the seventh floor, including that of department counselor, the Tillerson aide said. Some secretaries have used that job as a kind of in-house truth-teller, someone empowered to tell the boss she or he is making a mistake. Other secretaries of state have used the counselor to act as a surrogate, or like Tillerson, opted not to fill the position at all.\n\nRumors that Tillerson does not plan to fill the many vacancies at the assistant-secretary level are not true, the aide said. But a lack of guidance from Tillerson since he arrived Feb. 2 has fostered a sense among career diplomats that they are considered an obstacle to change, one department official said.\n\n\u201cWe\u2019re rooting for our secretary of state to come around, and trying to figure out a way to convince him we do work for administrations of both parties,\u201d the official said.\n\nTillerson has told employees that he will travel less than previous secretaries did and will take a smaller, faster plane that is more like the corporate jets of his former life. The government plane he is using this week in Europe has room for fewer than a dozen staff members, perhaps half the contingent that customarily traveled with recent predecessors.\n\nNo official note-taker accompanied him on a recent trip, so senior aides did the job to have a record of his talks with foreign ministers, according to a congressional aide.\n\nOn Thursday, Tillerson held his first visit with State Department employees abroad, at the U.S. Embassy in Ankara, where he appeared to acknowledge some dissent in the ranks when he urged \u201chonest\u201d confrontation of differing opinions.\n\n\u201cThat\u2019s how we come to a better decision in all that we do. And only if we do that can we then be honest with all of our partners and allies around the world as well. And still, I mean, we\u2019re going to have our differences, but we\u2019re going to be very honest and open about those, so at least we understand them.\u201d\n\nJulie Tate contributed to this
