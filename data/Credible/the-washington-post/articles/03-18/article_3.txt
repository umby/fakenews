But that\u2019s not how it works.\u201d</p> <p>Despite these critiques, this line of argument has been a near-constant refrain for Trump and his administration.\u00a0Since the campaign, he\u2019s argued that other countries aren't contributing what they should for the defense alliance. NATO members are urged to contribute <a href=\"http://talkingpointsmemo.com/livewire/trump-germany-nato-spending\">2 percent of their GDP to defense spendin</a>g. Germany pays 1.2 percent; the United States kicks in more than 3 percent. Four other countries \u2014 Greece, Estonia, Poland and Britain \u2014 also meet their obligation.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"021f475384\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/wp-apps/imrs.php?src=https://img.washingtonpost.com/blogs/worldviews/files/2017/03/Botsford170317Trump12941-1024x682.jpg&amp;w=1484\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/wp-apps/imrs.php?src=https://img.washingtonpost.com/blogs/worldviews/files/2017/03/Botsford170317Trump12941-1024x682.jpg&amp;w=480\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/blogs/worldviews/files/2017/03/Botsford170317Trump12941-1024x682.jpg\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/wp-apps/imrs.php?src=https://img.washingtonpost.com/blogs/worldviews/files/2017/03/Botsford170317Trump12941-1024x682.jpg&amp;w=480\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">President Trump and German Chancellor Angela Merkel participate in a joint news conference in the East Room of the White House on Friday. (Jabin Botsford/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>Those numbers, though, don\u2019t tell the whole story.</p> <p>Since World War II, Germany has intentionally kept its military small. The country defines itself by its pacifism and its commitment to the idea of \u201cnever again.\u201d Germany\u2019s defense spending \u2014 or lack thereof \u2014 has frequently been criticized and mocked in the past. In 2014, for instance, German forces made headlines when they were forced to use broomsticks instead of machine guns during a NATO exercise, exposing the state of its underequipped military.</p> <p>But, Germans argue, they make up for this in other ways. As Merkel argued in a speech last month, mutual security goes beyond military spending. International development aid on things like hospitals and schools does as much for peace as warheads in Europe.\u00a0\u201cWhen we help people in their home countries to live a better life and thereby prevent crises, this is also a contribution to security,\u201d Merkel said <a data-reactid=\"263\" href=\"https://www.securityconference.de/en/media-library/munich-security-conference-2017/video/statement-by-angela-merkel/\" target=\"_blank\">in Munich</a>. \u201cSo I will not be drawn into a debate about who is more military-minded and who is less.\u201d</p> <p>She and other German leaders also point out that they\u2019re bearing the brunt of the Syrian refugee crisis, spending 30 to 40 billion euros a year. If that was included in the tally, they say, they\u2019d be putting more than 2 percent of their budget a year toward security. (They\u2019re also <a href=\"http://time.com/4676097/donald-trump-nato-spending-germany-merkel/\">quick to note</a> that U.S. military interventions are one reason there are so many displaced people from the Middle East.)</p> <p>Not everyone agrees. For the countries on Europe\u2019s eastern border \u2014 places like Poland, Latvia and Lithuania \u2014 military might isn\u2019t an abstract idea but an insurance policy against Russian invasion. (Russia, for its part, keeps tanks and missiles stocked right up against the NATO border.) <a href=\"http://time.com/4676097/donald-trump-nato-spending-germany-merkel/\">At last month\u2019s conference</a>, Artis Pabriks, a former defense minister of Latvia, responded curtly: \u201cFor me, as a Latvian, it sounds a little bit bitter that support for my borders and the security of my country will be challenged because some other European nations will not pay their share.\u201d</p> <p>And Jens Stoltenberg, the secretary general of the alliance, told Time magazine last month that, \u201cit\u2019s not either development or security. We need both.\u201d \u201cWhen we live in more challenging times, we need to invest more in defense,\u201d he went on. \u201cWe need peace and security to facilitate development.\u201d</p> <p>Germany\u00a0says it will increase its military spending by about 3 billion euros per year for the next eight years. By 2024, the country will be meeting its NATO commitment. That plan though, still needs to be approved\u00a0by\u00a0the German government and parliament.</p> <p>And Trump may be making that harder. As\u00a0Marcel Dirsus, a\u00a0<span lang=\"EN-US\">German security politics scholar, argued,\u00a0the president\u2019s\u00a0</span><span lang=\"EN-US\">public criticism of German defense spending could backfire, making it harder for Merkel to increase the country\u2019s defense budget, particularly just months before a tough reelection campaign.</span></p> <p>An \u201cincrease in defense spending is unpopular, and so is Donald Trump. By \u2018ordering\u2019 Merkel to increase spending, he will make it harder for her to sell that increase at home,\u201d he said.\u00a0\u201c<span lang=\"EN-US\">Nothing would be worse for Merkel than being seen as taking orders from Trump. On Twitter, former U.S. ambassador to NATO Ivo Daalder wrote that increased defense budgets by Germany aren\u2019t transferred to the United States. He also pointed out that NATO decided to make the 2 percent requirement mandatory just a couple of years ago. The alliance gave all member states until 2024 to reach that goal, and Germany is on track.\n\n\u201cTrump\u2019s comments misrepresent the way NATO functions,\u201d Daalder told us. \u201cThe President keeps saying that we need to be paid by the Europeans for the fact that we have troops in Europe or provide defense there. But that\u2019s not how it works.\u201d\n\nDespite these critiques, this line of argument has been a near-constant refrain for Trump and his administration. Since the campaign, he\u2019s argued that other countries aren't contributing what they should for the defense alliance. NATO members are urged to contribute 2 percent of their GDP to defense spending. Germany pays 1.2 percent; the United States kicks in more than 3 percent. Four other countries \u2014 Greece, Estonia, Poland and Britain \u2014 also meet their obligation.\n\n\n\nPresident Trump and German Chancellor Angela Merkel participate in a joint news conference in the East Room of the White House on Friday. (Jabin Botsford/The Washington Post)\n\nThose numbers, though, don\u2019t tell the whole story.\n\nSince World War II, Germany has intentionally kept its military small. The country defines itself by its pacifism and its commitment to the idea of \u201cnever again.\u201d Germany\u2019s defense spending \u2014 or lack thereof \u2014 has frequently been criticized and mocked in the past. In 2014, for instance, German forces made headlines when they were forced to use broomsticks instead of machine guns during a NATO exercise, exposing the state of its underequipped military.\n\nBut, Germans argue, they make up for this in other ways. As Merkel argued in a speech last month, mutual security goes beyond military spending. International development aid on things like hospitals and schools does as much for peace as warheads in Europe. \u201cWhen we help people in their home countries to live a better life and thereby prevent crises, this is also a contribution to security,\u201d Merkel said in Munich. \u201cSo I will not be drawn into a debate about who is more military-minded and who is less.\u201d\n\nShe and other German leaders also point out that they\u2019re bearing the brunt of the Syrian refugee crisis, spending 30 to 40 billion euros a year. If that was included in the tally, they say, they\u2019d be putting more than 2 percent of their budget a year toward security. (They\u2019re also quick to note that U.S. military interventions are one reason there are so many displaced people from the Middle East.)\n\nNot everyone agrees. For the countries on Europe\u2019s eastern border \u2014 places like Poland, Latvia and Lithuania \u2014 military might isn\u2019t an abstract idea but an insurance policy against Russian invasion. (Russia, for its part, keeps tanks and missiles stocked right up against the NATO border.) We need both.\u201d \u201cWhen we live in more challenging times, we need to invest more in defense,\u201d he went on. \u201cWe need peace and security to facilitate development.\u201d\n\nGermany says it will increase its military spending by about 3 billion euros per year for the next eight years. By 2024, the country will be meeting its NATO commitment. That plan though, still needs to be approved by the German government and parliament.\n\nAnd Trump may be making that harder.
