<span class=\"pb-caption\">White House Office of Management and Budget Director Mick Mulvaney. (Kevin Lamarque/Reuters)</span> </div> <p>Top Trump administration\u00a0officials are giving out differing accounts of a\u00a0tax plan that the president has promised will be released Wednesday, calling into question whether they have ironed out some of the most difficult components of any proposal.</p> <p>Among the major decisions President Trump and his party face on taxes is whether to go for a short-term tax cut\u00a0that keeps much of the existing tax code intact but reduces rates or whether to go for a more fundamental\u00a0shift that makes long-term changes to how the government collects revenue.\u00a0Putting in place long-term changes to the tax code would give businesses certainty to help guide their investment decisions, but these sorts of changes to the tax code are much more difficult politically, as they would require bipartisan support.</p> <p>On Saturday, administration officials offered confusing signals on which route Trump would pursue.</p> <p>Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin on Saturday suggested Wednesday\u2019s announcement would pursue a long-term overhaul of the tax code.</p> <p>Mnuchin said one of the White House\u2019s top priorities was to complete \u201ccomprehensive economic tax reform,\u201d dramatically simplifying how people file their annual returns. He said he wants to allow many people to file their taxes on a \u201cpostcard.\u201d</p> <p>But on Sunday, Mick Mulvaney, director of the Office of Management and Budget, appeared to cast doubt on Mnuchin\u2019s statements, saying the White House hadn\u2019t decided whether to pursue a long-term or short-term tax overhaul.</p> <p>He also said they haven\u2019t decided whether to offset the rate cuts with other changes that would reduce the budget deficit. \u201cI don\u2019t think we\u2019ve decided that part yet,\u201d Mulvaney said on Fox News Sunday. \u201cYou can either have a small tax cut that\u2019s permanent or a large tax cut that\u2019s short-term.\u201d</p> <p>Mulvaney said the tax plan the White House will release Wednesday will include \u201csome specific governing principles, some guidance, also some indication of what the rates are going to be.\u201d</p> <p>For his part, Trump on Saturday wrote on Twitter that\u00a0\u201cBig TAX REFORM AND TAX REDUCTION will be announced next Wednesday.\u201d</p> <p>Trump surprised Capitol Hill \u2014 and even some people within the White House \u2014\u00a0when he first announced\u00a0the Wednesday deadline at an event last week. But even while he has promised the announcement will prove to be the starting point of tax reform, advisers have cautioned that it will offer broad outlines and leave some key questions unanswered.</p> <p>The president has said an overhaul of the tax code is one of his biggest priorities, but top advisers have refused to provide details so far of how he will usher in the biggest tax cut in U.S. history \u2014 which he has promised \u2014\u00a0without piling on trillions of dollars in new federal debt.</p> <p>He has said cutting taxes will grow the economy, lead to more hiring and bring trillions of dollars back into the United States that companies are now holding overseas to avoid taxation.\u00a0Among the administration\u2019s stated aims are to simplify the tax code for individuals and families, lower corporate tax rates and provide a major tax cut for the middle class.</p> <p>Mnuchin, one of the key architects of the White House plan, also said that some ways of measuring the impact of the tax cuts would find that it would grow the debt in the \u201cshort term.\u201d</p> <p>Budget experts have forecast that Trump\u2019s proposal could boost the economy but also dramatically expand the deficit because trillions of dollars in revenue would be lost over 10 years.</p> <p>When asked about the deficit impact of the tax plan, Mnuchin said at an International Monetary Fund event Saturday that when accounting for the economic growth that would occur because of the tax changes, the tax overhaul \u201cwill pay\u201d for itself.</p> <p>But he also said that if these macroeconomic changes aren\u2019t factored in, the tax plan would grow the deficit. An analysis that doesn\u2019t factor in economic growth forecasts is called a \u201cstatic\u201d assessment.</p> <p>\u201cUnder static scoring, there will be some short-term issues,\u201d Mnuchin said.</p> <p>Factoring in the macroeconomic impact of tax cuts is very controversial because it is very difficult to do. A number of conservative budget experts \u2014 including key congressional aides who write tax law \u2014 have said that the White House has an overly rosy view of what the economic impact of its tax cuts would be.</p> <p>The deficit impact of tax cuts is vitally important because any plan that grows the deficit over the long term is much more difficult to pass through Congress. It would take a number of Democrats to support such a plan because Republicans only have 52 seats in the Senate. They would need at least 60 votes to get such a tax measure through. A tax plan that increases the deficit only in the short term needs only a majority Senate vote.</p> <p>During the campaign, Trump proposed to reduce the corporate tax rate from 35 percent to 15 percent. He also proposed collapsing the seven personal income tax brackets into three, with rates of 12 percent, 25 percent and 33 percent, based on income levels. The proposal would also increase the standard tax deduction for\u00a0individuals and married couples filing jointly.</p> <p>He also wants to eliminate the estate tax and the alternative minimum tax. Trump has also proposed some sort of \u201creciprocity\u201d tax that would hit imports from countries that have taxes or tariffs on\u00a0U.S. exports.</p> <div class=\"inline-content
