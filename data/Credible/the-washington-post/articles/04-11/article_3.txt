data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/11/National-Politics/Images/Kansas-Congress-Special_Election_87226-51e76-3919.jpg?uuid=ZPMH1h8SEeegp4sqRePchA\" (Jaime Green/AP)</span> </div> <div class=\"pb-sig-line hasnt-headshot has-0-headshots hasnt-bio is-not-column\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/david-weigel/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">David Weigel</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-byline-tool-mobile\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/david-weigel/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">David Weigel</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:david.weigel@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Republicans hang on to win Kansas House seat'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> </span> <span class=\"pb-timestamp\" itemprop=\"datePublished\" content=\"2017-04-11T10:46-500\">April 11 at 10:46 PM</span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:david.weigel@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Republicans hang on to win Kansas House seat'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> <span class=\"tweet-authors\"><span class=\"pb-twitter-follow unprocessed\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/daveweigel\" class=\"twitter-follow-button\" data-show-count=\"false\" data-lang=\"en\">Follow daveweigel</a></span> </span> </div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p> <span class=\"dateline\">WICHITA \u2014</span> Republicans\u00a0fended\u00a0off<b>\u00a0</b>a surprising Democratic challenge on Tuesday in the first special election of the year for a House seat vacated by a Republican lawmaker who became part of President Trump\u2019s administration.</p> <p>Ron Estes, the GOP state treasurer, was buoyed by an 11th-hour intervention from national Republicans,\u00a0Vice President Pence,\u00a0and Trump himself\u00a0in his bid to retain the seat of former Rep. Mike Pompeo, who is now the CIA director. With 78\u00a0percent of ballots counted,\u00a0he was set to defeat\u00a0Democrat James Thompson, an attorney making his\u00a0first bid for office,\u00a0by at least 7\u00a0points. Pompeo won reelection by 31 points last year.</p> <p>The Republican\u2019s resilience dashed Democratic hopes \u2014 which surged over the weekend \u2014 of slicing into House Speaker Paul Ryan\u2019s (R-Wis.) majority. It also may dampen that party\u2019s hopes of performing well in the three House seats vacated by Republicans \u2014 most significantly, in Georgia where Democrat Jon Ossoff is currently leading in the April 18 contest to replace Health and Human Services Secretary Tom Price in a crowded field of GOP candidates.</p> <p>Yet holding the 4th Congressional District \u2014 which Trump captured by 27 points in November, and is the home to Koch Industries \u2014 took more work, and money, than Republicans expected. Thompson easily won voters who\u2019d cast their ballots early, and was poised to win Wichita\u2019s Sedgwick County, which Trump won by 18 points.</p> <p>In the campaign\u2019s final weekend, the National Republican Congressional Committee spent close to $100,000 on the race, and the GOP-allied Congressional Leadership Fund super PAC paid for tens of thousands of get-out-the-vote phone calls. President Trump even recorded a call for the Republican and sent an Election Day tweet calling Estes a \u201cwonderful guy\u201d who would help him on \u201cHealthcare &amp; Tax Cuts (Reform).\u201d</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-left\" style=\"width:300px;\"> <a name=\"1cb61a7f3ed6d2613c982dcf0e5a433967e27434\"></a> <img alt=\"\" class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1024w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/11/National-Politics/Images/Kansas_Congress_Special_Election_35922-5d055.jpg?uuid=bp1z_B8SEeegp4sqRePchA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/11/National-Politics/Images/Kansas_Congress_Special_Election_35922-5d055.jpg?uuid=bp1z_B8SEeegp4sqRePchA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/11/National-Politics/Images/Kansas_Congress_Special_Election_35922-5d055.jpg?uuid=bp1z_B8SEeegp4sqRePchA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/11/National-Politics/Images/Kansas_Congress_Special_Election_35922-5d055.jpg?uuid=bp1z_B8SEeegp4sqRePchA\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Democrat James Thompson, a candidate for Kansas' 4th Congressional District, speaks during a debate in Wichita, Kan. (Bo Rader/AP)</span> </div> <p>The victory helped\u00a0House Republicans\u00a0retain a 23-seat advantage\u00a0over Democrats, despite entering the two-week recess short on their major legislative goals. Estes did not focus on Trump in his race, and though he is expected to be a reliable vote for Republicans in the House\u00a0-- he said on Monday he would not join either right-wing or moderate caucuses --\u00a0he had\u00a0criticized\u00a0the GOP\u2019s health-care bill that was pulled before the congressional recess.</p> <p>Thompson, a 46-year old lawyer who had caucused for Bernie Sanders, waged a stronger-than-expected race that has inspired national Democrats to compete more aggressively ahead of 2018\u2019s midterm elections.</p> <p>But progressives were critical of the party for letting the race play out without a major investment.</p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p><div></div> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p>The close race reflected the unique dynamics in the 4th District. It also suggested that the GOP\u2019s eight-year ride in the opposition was smoother than what was coming next. Without the boogeyman of the Obama administration, and without the threat of Hillary Clinton filling an open Supreme Court seat, Republicans resorted to arguing that Thompson would use their tax money to fund abortions. Until it finally spent some money on get-out-the-vote calls, the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee stayed away for fear that it would nationalize the race.</p> <p>The contest began with Pompeo\u2019s Jan.\u00a023 departure from Congress, which attracted little national attention until the start of April. The Cook Political Report marked the race as \u201csafe\u201d for Republicans, downgrading the party\u2019s chances only after the final weekend\u2019s scramble.</p> <p>Inside the district, however, both parties saw early potential for an upset. Estes, a businessman who entered politics in 2004, had only ever won landslides in the 4th District for state office.</p> <p>But in Topeka, the state capital, he became associated with a Republican governing team that has presided over a weak economic recovery and a series of budget deficits. Democrats \u2014 and increasingly, voters \u2014 came to blame Gov. Sam Brownback\u2019s supply-side tax cuts, which cut into the state\u2019s revenue.</p> <p>\u201cI know doctors, lawyers, who pay no taxes and watch their secretaries sweat their own tax bills,\u201d said Chuck Schmidt, 67, a retired schools superintendent who had driven 113\u00a0miles to Wichita to campaign for Thompson. \u201cIt\u2019s a disaster. It\u2019s just not fair.\u201d</p> <p>In 2016, as Republicans won across the country, the party lost ground in Kansas. Democrats gained 12 seats in the state House and one seat in the state Senate, after moderate anti-Brownback Republicans defeated conservatives in a series of primaries. On Election Day, Trump carried the 4th District easily \u2014 but Republicans lost three of the party\u2019s state House seats.</p> <p>The Democratic Party, meanwhile, underwent an an evolution of its own. Sanders crushed Hillary Clinton here in the 2016 caucuses, and many Sanders activists stayed inside the party to win leadership positions. When Pompeo\u2019s seat opened, former state treasurer Dennis McKinney \u2014 who was defeated in 2010 by Estes \u2014 was initially seen as the Democratic front-runner. But the antiabortion McKinney lost a tight party contest to the pro-abortion rights, economically populist Thompson.</p> <p>While Estes was tied up in Topeka, working on the controversial state budget, Thompson barnstormed the district. He chided the Republican for skipping public forums or for meeting voters only at ticketed events. In campaign literature, he called Estes a \u201cBrownbacker,\u201d associating him with a governor whose approval rating statewide was mired in the 20s after he declined to expand Medicaid as part of the Affordable Care Act. In television ads, Thompson called himself a \u201cfighter for Congress\u201d and was filmed firing an AR-15 rifle.</p> <p>In the final 24 hours of the race, the Democrat attended a Seder with Jewish voters, recorded a \u201cthank you\u201d video for volunteers, shook hands at a breakfast for Learjet retirees, and (through a translator) sat for an interview with Wichita\u2019s Spanish-language radio station.</p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/powerpost/\" shape=\"rect\"
