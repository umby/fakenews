establishment appeared Wednesday to fend off a challenge from anti-Muslim firebrand Geert Wilders, according to initial vote counts, a victory in a closely watched national election<b>\u00a0</b>that heartened centrist leaders across Europe who are fearful of populist upsets in their own nations.</p> <p>The result was embraced by other leaders inside and outside the Netherlands as a major blow to anti-immigrant populism, breaking a streak of disruption that started with the Brexit vote and continued with the election of Donald Trump, a skeptic of European integration. Instead, as the Netherlands\u2019 famed tulip season gets underway, Prime Minister Mark Rutte will remain in office as he tries to form a coalition.</p> <p id=\"U1200559295274EhC\">The vote in the prosperous trading nation was seen as a bellwether for France and Germany, which head to the polls in the coming months and have also been shaken by fierce anti-immigrant sentiment.\u00a0</p> <p> <b></b>Wilders nose-dived in recent weeks after topping opinion polls for most of the past 18\u00a0months, as Dutch voters appeared\u00a0to turn away from an <b>\u00a0</b>election message that described some Moroccans as \u201cscum\u201d and called for banning the Koran and shuttering mosques.\u00a0</p> <p>Wednesday is \u201can evening where the Netherlands, after \u00adBrexit, after the American elections, said no to the wrong kind of populism,\u201d Rutte told a cheering crowd in The Hague. He said he had already spoken to other European leaders to accept their congratulations.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"analysis\" data-duration=\"181034\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"A populist surge is pushing some progressive Dutch toward the right\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58c2cf2ce4b059a9e4249eba\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-10-2017/t_1489168825759_name_gen_far_right.jpg\" data-uuid=\"9f7d4b12-05ab-11e7-9d14-9724d48f5666\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"LKspdDgm4MY\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Video: Anna-Maria Magnusson / Full Story Media for The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>But Wilders vowed to continue his fight, saying that his fierce message had resonated with Dutch voters and that he would prevail as the victor of the next elections.</p> <p>\u201cWe\u2019ll have to wait for the next time to make this happen,\u201d Wilders told reporters early Thursday. \u201cI guarantee you, this patriotic spring will start. It has already started.\u201d</p> <p>Despite the worse-than-expected result, Wilders was still on track to gain seats, reconfirming his role as a sharp and persistent thorn in the side of the nation\u2019s mainstream leaders.</p> <p>Outside Dutch borders, many leaders offered sighs of relief<b></b>at the dike that voters may have erected against populist tides.</p> <p>\u201cCongratulations to the Dutch for stopping the rise of the extreme right,\u201d wrote French Foreign Minister Jean-Marc Ayrault on Twitter. \u201cWillingness to work for a stronger Europe.\u201d His nation faces the first round of a presidential election on April 23.</p> <p id=\"U1200559295274Mw\">There, National Front leader Marine Le Pen has vowed to try to lead France out of the European Union if she wins the presidency, a step that would likely shatter the bloc. German leaders also face a challenge as the anti-immigrant Alternative for Germany party stands to capture seats in Parliament.</p> <p>German Chancellor Angela Merkel also sent a relieved valentine.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-left\" style=\"width:300px;\"> <a name=\"79a06ae76200008cf598069973991c0d2a5f6979\"></a> <img alt=\"\" class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1024w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/15/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/653610286.jpg?uuid=M6DsdAnYEeehX6WNSpiEdA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/15/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/653610286.jpg?uuid=M6DsdAnYEeehX6WNSpiEdA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/15/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/653610286.jpg?uuid=M6DsdAnYEeehX6WNSpiEdA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/15/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/653610286.jpg?uuid=M6DsdAnYEeehX6WNSpiEdA\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Dutch Prime Minister Mark Rutte is greeted by supporters as he arrives to make a speech in The Hague after his election victory. (Carl Court/Getty Images)</span> </div> <p>\u201cI look forward to continued good cooperation as friends, neighbors, Europeans,\u201d Merkel told Rutte in a phone call, her spokesman said.</p> <p>Taken together, the initial results appeared to show a nation that agreed that it disliked the current political situation \u2014\u00a0but diverged sharply about an alternative direction. Turnout was high \u2014 more than 80 percent \u2014 and there were 28 parties to choose from.</p> <p>With 84 percent of the voting districts reporting results early Thursday,\u00a0Rutte\u2019s center-right People\u2019s Party for Freedom and Democracy\u00a0remained the largest party, but it was on track to lose nearly a quarter of its seats in parliament, forcing the prime minister to form a new, broader coalition across the political spectrum. His coalition partner, the center-left\u00a0Labor Party, was wiped out as a political force,\u00a0a punishing blow in response to cooperation with a longtime rival that had a sharply different approach to the core issues of working citizens.</p> <p>Even as Wilders confronted limits to his ballot-box appeal \u2014 he captured about 13 percent of the vote \u2014 his agenda-setting power remained evident after many mainstream politicians tacked rightward during the campaign to advocate for stricter limits on immigrants.\u00a0</p> <p>\u201cAll the politicians of the main parties have been debating his issues, more than they\u2019ve been debating other issues such as climate change,\u201d said Sarah de Lange, a professor of political science at the University of Amsterdam.</p> <p>Wilders\u2019s Party for Freedom was forecast to build slightly on its current 15 seats in the lower house of parliament, tying it with the centrist Democrats 66 party and the center-right Christian Democratic Appeal. The center-left Green-Left party, led by Jesse Klaver, a 30-year-old upstart who embraced Barack Obama-style campaign tactics, also appeared to do well, potentially quadrupling its seats.</p> <p>\u201cWith Brexit and Trump and with the elections in France and Germany on their way, all those journalists we\u2019ve spoken with in the last weeks wanted to know: Will populism break through in the Netherlands?\u201d Klaver said at a jubilant evening rally. \u201cThis is the answer we have for Europe. Populism didn\u2019t break through.\u201d</p> <p>Still, the likely formation of a broad, weak coalition across the political spectrum could give extra ammunition to Wilders even though he will be barred from power. Rutte has repeatedly said he would not work with the peroxide-haired campaigner.\u00a0Wilders may be able win more voters in the future by saying that mainstream parties are conniving across political lines to ignore the will of the Dutch people.</p> <p>But it remained to be seen how enduring the sharp tone on immigrants would be.\u00a0</p> <p id=\"U12801218645JCJ\">Rutte significantly toughened his stance on immigrants during the campaign in a bid to capture Wilders\u2019s supporters, telling immigrants in January to \u201cact normal or go away.\u201d</p> <p>With many of the prime minister\u2019s potential coalition partners in favor of a more accepting approach to refugees, he may moderate his tone to build partnerships. And Wilders\u2019s underwhelming election-day performance will further ease pressure to take a hard line on immigration.</p> <p>\u201cThe key effect of it is international,\u201d said Cas Mudde, a professor of political science at the University of Georgia who focuses on political extremism in Europe. \u201cIt determines what issues we talk about and how we talk about them.\u201d </p> <p>He said that mainstream Dutch political leaders would probably feel less pressure to take a hard line against immigration and may actually become significantly more moderate depending on the outcome of coalition negotiations.</p> <p>The tone of the Dutch campaign dispirited some citizens who want a welcoming attitude toward refugees and immigrants.</p> <p>\u201cThey\u2019re not making the point what they want to do. They\u2019re just saying what they\u2019re against,\u201d said Arieke Maljaars, 32, a teacher at an elementary school in the heavily immigrant Schilderswijk area of The Hague, where Turkish kebab stands are close to Surinamese grocery stores. She said she planned to vote for the small, centrist Christian Union party. She said some of her 8- and 9-year-old students, most of whom are immigrants or the children of immigrants, \u201cwere really scared.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cOne of them said, \u2018Maybe I\u2019ll have to go to Turkey, and I really don\u2019t want to go there.\u2019 For children in the neighborhood, it can feel frightening,\u201d Maljaars said.</p> <p>Muslim immigrants also said they felt threatened by the message.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s normal that someone thinks about his own country. But don\u2019t do it with discrimination,\u201d said Nawaz Nawaz, 62, who emigrated from Pakistan to The Hague 40 years ago and said he voted for a small party that caters to older voters.</p> <p id=\"U1200559295274XJE\">Many Wilders supporters said Wednesday that they resented that refugees who came to their country were provided housing and health care even as Dutch people struggled to make ends meet.</p> <p>\u201cI understand they don\u2019t have anything, but I have to pay for all that,\u201d said Bep van Beele, 66, who lives in the working-class Duindorp area of The Hague, a bastion of Wilders\u2019s support. \u201cIt creates jealousy. There\u2019s not much left for the Dutchman.\u201d</p> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>Annabell Van den Berghe in The Hague and Isaac Stanley-Becker in Maastricht contributed to this report.</p> </p>
