Ryan said Wednesday that his health-care proposal must change to pass the House, marking a significant retreat from his earlier position that the carefully crafted legislation would fail if substantially altered.</p> <p>Ryan acknowledged that changes would be made two days after an analysis issued by the Congressional Budget Office prompted a fresh round of criticism of his proposal. Among the report\u2019s projections was that 14\u00a0million fewer Americans would be insured after one year under the Republican plan.</p> <p>Speaking after a private meeting of GOP lawmakers, Ryan said that leaders would \u201cincorporate feedback\u201d from the rank-and-file in response to the CBO findings. He did not repeat his previous comments calling support for the bill a \u201cbinary choice\u201d for Republican lawmakers.</p> <p>\u201cNow that we have our score we can make some necessary improvements and refinements to the bill,\u201d he said, referring to the CBO\u2019s estimate of the effect on the number of those covered by health insurance and what the GOP proposal would cost.</p> <p id=\"U1200637134286FcF\">Ryan did not detail what changes are under consideration to his plan.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-graphic-linked-296\"> <div class=\"photo-wrapper\"> <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/politics/obamacare-replacement-next-steps/\"><img src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/14/National-Politics/Graphics/obamacare-replacement-next-steps-diagram-promo.jpg?uuid=8_1wgAjCEee9Gf06-g9-Kg\"/></a> <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/politics/obamacare-replacement-next-steps/\"> <span class=\"overlay\"> <span class=\"view-graphic\">View Graphic <span class=\"fa fa-arrow-circle-right\"></span></span></span> </a> </div> <span class=\"pb-caption\">What\u2019s next for the Obamacare replacement bill</span> </div> <p id=\"U120063713428611C\">Vice President Pence also spoke to House Republicans in the meeting, acknowledging that the White House was open to changes in the legislation, which heads to the House Budget Committee for approval Thursday. President Trump has offered his support for Ryan\u2019s measure, while still meeting with conservative lawmakers who have expressed serious doubts about the\u00a0plan.</p> <p>\u201cThis president is ready to put the full weight of his bully pulpit and all of his tools\u201d behind the bill, Pence told Republicans, according to Rep. Kevin Cramer (R-N.D.). \u201cIt was very important for us to hear that, because there are a lot of people who need that shoring up.\u201d</p> <p>Earlier in the day, Pence told conservatives at a private lunch meeting of the Republican Study Committee, a large caucus of conservative House Republicans, that the plan was still under negotiation, according to several \u00adattendees.</p> <p id=\"U1200637134286UWG\">\u201cAs he said, it\u2019s not often that we get an opportunity to undo such a big piece of legislation that had negative consequences on the American people,\u201d said Rep. Markwayne Mullin (R-Okla.), paraphrasing Pence\u2019s message. \u201cHe\u2019s open to make improvements. .\u2009.\u2009. Anything that can get 218 votes and make the bill better, we\u2019re all about it.\u201d</p> <p>Pence\u2019s visit to Capitol Hill \u2014 which included meetings with influential blocs of Republicans as well as individual members \u2014 came as part of a White House effort to salvage support for the embattled American Health Care Act, even as Trump\u2019s conservative allies told him the bill <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/trump-loyalists-sound-alarm-over-ryancare-endangering-health-bill/2017/03/14/cfc187e6-08dc-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html?hpid=hp_hp-top-table-main_trumpryan-0748pm%3Ahomepage%2Fstory&amp;utm_term=.4cb6ffff382c\" shape=\"rect\">could be a political trap</a>.</p> <p>Ryan aides played down his remarks, saying that the speaker had long acknowledged further changes were possible as the bill moved through a multistep legislative process. </p> <p>\u201cAs the speaker has repeatedly said, this bill will work its way through regular order and while the structure will remain intact, modifications will happen during the legislating process,\u201d spokeswoman AshLee Strong said. \u201cBut the reality remains: Members will face a binary choice to either vote for a bill that can pass or vote for Obamacare status quo.\u201d</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"165034\" class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Bastien Inzaurralde/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>The legislation will face an important test Thursday, when the Budget Committee will decide whether to advance it to the House floor. The committee may not directly amend the bill but may make nonbinding recommendations. Any substantive changes would be made by the House Rules Committee, which controls how the bill is presented and debated on the floor.</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">House Budget Committee Chairman Diane Black (R-Tenn.) insisted that GOP leaders are not \u201cdug in\u201d when it comes to concerns about the measure.</p> <p> <span>\u201cWe\u2019re listening; we\u2019re definitely listening,\u201d she said Wednesday to the conservative radio host Hugh Hewitt. </span> <span>\u201cAnd I will tell you: Tune in to our Budget Committee tomorrow, and you will hear these conversations.\u201d</span> </p> <p>The blitz came two days after the <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/powerpost/obamacare-revision-would-reduce-insured-numbers-by-24-million/2017/03/13/ea4c860a-0829-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html?hpid=hp_hp-top-table-main_cbo-430pm%3Ahomepage%2Fstory&amp;utm_term=.fe3b96bb6eb7\" shape=\"rect\">CBO estimated</a> that 24\u00a0million fewer Americans would be insured over the next 10 years under the GOP plan. The falling coverage numbers and estimates of higher premiums for older and low-income Americans raised concerns among many lawmakers, particularly those representing states that expanded eligibility for Medi<span>caid under the Affordable Care Act.</span> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\" id=\"U1200637134286VtD\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/powerpost/paloma/daily-202/2017/03/14/daily-202-cbo-bombshell-is-going-to-force-changes-to-health-care-bill/58c73ea6e9b69b1406c75d65/?utm_term=.9701ee6c697f\" shape=\"rect\">Budget office bombshell is going to force changes to health-care bill</a>]</i> </p> <p id=\"U1200637134286bvF\">Ryan plans to hold a House floor vote next week, and Trump administration officials have spent the past two days in near-constant discussions with Republican lawmakers as they scramble to find enough votes to pass the measure. Leaders were set to take a preliminary count of members supporting the bill Wednesday\u00a0night.</p> <p>Critics of the legislation warned Wednesday that the measure remains far short of the 216 votes needed for passage. Typically, 218 votes are needed to pass a bill in the 435-member House, but five pending vacancies have reduced that threshold. No Democrats are expected to support the bill, and with 237 Republicans, party leaders can afford no more than 21 \u00addefections.</p> <p>\u201cThey will whip it, and they will find 40 nos and another 30 or 40 undecideds,\u201d said Rep. Mark Meadows (R-N.C.), chairman of the hard-right House Freedom Caucus, who has called for \u00adchanges to mollify conservatives.</p> <p>One member of the Budget Committee who belongs to the Freedom Caucus, Rep. Dave Brat (R-Va.), said he will vote against the bill in committee. Two other Freedom Caucus members, as well as three others on the panel, have not said how they will vote.</p> <p>One conservative Budget Committee member who has voiced concerns about the bill said Wednesday that he had been persuaded to advance it.</p> <p>Rep. Glenn Grothman (R-Wis.) said he would support the legislation in the panel based on an \u201cimplied promise that things are going to be different on the floor, and therefore we can keep the process moving.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U1200637134286uPH\">He declined to say exactly who had made that promise: \u201cPeople higher up on the food chain than me.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI\u2019ll say this, if the bill that comes to the floor is the exact same one in Budget, I\u2019ll feel a little bit that I\u2019d been had,\u201d he\u00a0said.</p> <p>House Majority Whip Steve \u00adScalise (R-La.) acknowledged in an interview with MSNBC on Wednesday that the bill remains under \u00adnegotiation.</p> <p>\u201cWatching sausage being made is not the prettiest thing,\u201d he said. \u201cWhat can we do on those final pieces that are being negotiated with President Trump and a number of our members in our conference that ultimately want to get to a yes vote and send this bill over to the Senate?\u201d</p> <p>Ryan spent much of Tuesday and Wednesday giving interviews in an attempt to drum up support for the plan. <b></b>On the Fox Business Network, he defended the plan as a collaboration with Trump and the Senate and said that \u201cthe major components are staying intact\u201d while opening the door to \u201crefinements and \u00adimprovements.\u201d</p> <p>Later, in an interview with the conservative radio host Laura \u00adIngraham, he countered reports that the White House\u2019s support for the bill might be slipping. Ryan said he had spoken to White House Chief of Staff Reince \u00adPriebus and Trump\u2019s chief strategist, Stephen K. Bannon, \u201ca number of times\u201d about the bill, and he noted that Trump had brought lawmakers to the White House multiple times to make the case for the legislation.</p> <p>\u201cWe are on the same page as the White House,\u201d Ryan said. \u201cI think there are those who would love to wedge us for one reason or another, but that\u2019s just not the case.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/powerpost/conservative-lawmakers-continue-to-push-back-on-obamacare-plan/2017/03/14/f7331e70-08aa-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html?utm_term=.efac5af41a50\" shape=\"rect\">White House tries to salvage GOP health-care plan as criticism mounts</a>]</i> </p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">Hours later, at a rally for Affordable Care Act opponents outside the Capitol, Sen. Ted Cruz (R-Tex.) told activists that the House plan \u201chas a lot of problems\u201d but could possibly be modified into a \u201creal repeal\u201d of the ACA.</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">\u201cI believe we can get it done,\u201d Cruz said. \u201cI can tell you, the last week, four days, I\u2019ve been at the White House meeting with the president, with the vice president, saying, \u2018We\u2019ve got to get it done.\u2019\u2009\u201d</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">Various changes have been floated since the bill was unveiled this month. Conservatives have pushed for an earlier phaseout of the ACA\u2019s Medicaid expansion, moving it from the end of 2019 to the beginning of 2018, but GOP moderates in the House and Senate have pushed back on that notion.</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">\u201cDoing that would be a nonstarter and would be enormously and hugely problematic for many of our members,\u201d said Rep. Charlie Dent (R-Pa.), a co-chairman of the centrist Tuesday Group.</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">Meadows said Wednesday that the Freedom Caucus is closely focused on repealing more of the ACA\u2019s mandates governing the extent of insurance coverage. \u201cThat is probably the No. 1 priority,\u201d he said. \u201cIf we can get those, it would make the rest of it a whole lot easier.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">GOP leaders, however, have kept those provisions out of the pending legislation, arguing that their inclusion could threaten the bill\u2019s passage in the Senate and that many of those mandates can be undone through Trump administration actions.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com,!Daily\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> </p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">In another sign of the effect of the CBO report, Sen. John Thune (R-S.D.) floated a proposal to recalibrate the tax credits established under the House bill to offer more substantial help to low-income Americans. Ryan said Wednesday that his health-care proposal must change to pass the House, marking a significant retreat from his earlier position that the carefully crafted legislation would fail if substantially altered.\n\nRyan acknowledged that changes would be made two days after an analysis issued by the Congressional Budget Office prompted a fresh round of criticism of his proposal. Among the report\u2019s projections was that 14 million fewer Americans would be insured after one year under the Republican plan.\n\nSpeaking after a private meeting of GOP lawmakers, Ryan said that leaders would \u201cincorporate feedback\u201d from the rank-and-file in response to the CBO findings. He did not repeat his previous comments calling support for the bill a \u201cbinary choice\u201d for Republican lawmakers.\n\n\u201cNow that we have our score we can make some necessary improvements and refinements to the bill,\u201d he said, referring to the CBO\u2019s estimate of the effect on the number of those covered by health insurance and what the GOP proposal would cost.\n\nRyan did not detail what changes are under consideration to his plan.\n\nView Graphic What\u2019s next for the Obamacare replacement bill\n\nVice President Pence also spoke to House Republicans in the meeting, acknowledging that the White House was open to changes in the legislation, which heads to the House Budget Committee for approval Thursday. President Trump has offered his support for Ryan\u2019s measure, while still meeting with conservative lawmakers who have expressed serious doubts about the plan.\n\n\u201cThis president is ready to put the full weight of his bully pulpit and all of his tools\u201d behind the bill, Pence told Republicans, according to Rep. Kevin Cramer (R-N.D.). \u201cIt was very important for us to hear that, because there are a lot of people who need that shoring up.\u201d\n\nEarlier in the day, Pence told conservatives at a private lunch meeting of the Republican Study Committee, a large caucus of conservative House Republicans, that the plan was still under negotiation, according to several \u00adattendees.\n\n\u201cAs he said, it\u2019s not often that we get an opportunity to undo such a big piece of legislation that had negative consequences on the American people,\u201d said Rep. Markwayne Mullin (R-Okla.), paraphrasing Pence\u2019s message. \u201cHe\u2019s open to make improvements. . . . Anything that can get 218 votes and make the bill better, we\u2019re all about it.\u201d\n\nPence\u2019s visit to Capitol Hill \u2014 which included meetings with influential blocs of Republicans as well as individual members \u2014 came as part of a White House effort to salvage support for the embattled American Health Care Act, even as Trump\u2019s conservative allies told him the bill could be a political trap.\n\nRyan aides played down his remarks, saying that the speaker had long acknowledged further changes were possible as the bill moved through a multistep legislative process.\n\n\u201cAs the speaker has repeatedly said, this bill will work its way through regular order and while the structure will remain intact, modifications will happen during the legislating process,\u201d spokeswoman AshLee Strong said. \u201cBut the reality remains: Members will face a binary choice to either vote for a bill that can pass or vote for Obamacare status quo.\u201d\n\n(Bastien Inzaurralde/The Washington Post)\n\nThe legislation will face an important test Thursday, when the Budget Committee will decide whether to advance it to the House floor. The committee may not directly amend the bill but may make nonbinding recommendations. Any substantive changes would be made by the House Rules Committee, which controls how the bill is presented and debated on the floor.\n\nHouse Budget Committee Chairman Diane Black (R-Tenn.) insisted that GOP leaders are not \u201cdug in\u201d when it comes to concerns about the measure.\n\n\u201cWe\u2019re listening; we\u2019re definitely listening,\u201d she said Wednesday to the conservative radio host Hugh Hewitt. \u201cAnd I will tell you: Tune in to our Budget Committee tomorrow, and you will hear these conversations.\u201d\n\nThe blitz came two days after the CBO estimated that 24 million fewer Americans would be insured over the next 10 years under the GOP plan. The falling coverage numbers and estimates of higher premiums for older and low-income Americans raised concerns among many lawmakers, particularly those representing states that expanded eligibility for Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act.\n\n[Budget office bombshell is going to force changes to health-care bill]\n\nRyan plans to hold a House floor vote next week, and Trump administration officials have spent the past two days in near-constant discussions with Republican lawmakers as they scramble to find enough votes to pass the measure. Leaders were set to take a preliminary count of members supporting the bill Wednesday night.\n\nCritics of the legislation warned Wednesday that the measure remains far short of the 216 votes needed for passage. Typically, 218 votes are needed to pass a bill in the 435-member House, but five pending vacancies have reduced that threshold. No Democrats are expected to support the bill, and with 237 Republicans, party leaders can afford no more than 21 \u00addefections.\n\n\u201cThey will whip it, and they will find 40 nos and another 30 or 40 undecideds,\u201d said Rep. Mark Meadows (R-N.C.), chairman of the hard-right House Freedom Caucus, who has called for \u00adchanges to mollify conservatives.\n\nOne member of the Budget Committee who belongs to the Freedom Caucus, Rep. Dave Brat (R-Va.), said he will vote against the bill in committee. Two other Freedom Caucus members, as well as three others on the panel, have not said how they will vote.\n\nOne conservative Budget Committee member who has voiced concerns about the bill said Wednesday that he had been persuaded to advance it.\n\nRep. Glenn Grothman (R-Wis.) said he would support the legislation in the panel based on an \u201cimplied promise that things are going to be different on the floor, and therefore we can keep the process moving.\u201d\n\nHe declined to say exactly who had made that promise: \u201cPeople higher up on the food chain than me.\u201d\n\n\u201cI\u2019ll say this, if the bill that comes to the floor is the exact same one in Budget, I\u2019ll feel a little bit that I\u2019d been had,\u201d he said.\n\nHouse Majority Whip Steve \u00adScalise (R-La.) acknowledged in an interview with MSNBC on Wednesday that the bill remains under \u00adnegotiation.\n\n\u201cWatching sausage being made is not the prettiest thing,\u201d he said. \u201cWhat can we do on those final pieces that are being negotiated with President Trump and a number of our members in our conference that ultimately want to get to a yes vote and send this bill over to the Senate?\u201d\n\nRyan spent much of Tuesday and Wednesday giving interviews in an attempt to drum up support for the plan. On the Fox Business Network, he defended the plan as a collaboration with Trump and the Senate and said that \u201cthe major components are staying intact\u201d while opening the door to \u201crefinements and \u00adimprovements.\u201d\n\nLater, in an interview with the conservative radio host Laura \u00adIngraham, he countered reports that the White House\u2019s support for the bill might be slipping. Ryan said he had spoken to White House Chief of Staff Reince \u00adPriebus and Trump\u2019s chief strategist, Stephen K. Bannon, \u201ca number of times\u201d about the bill, and he noted that Trump had brought lawmakers to the White House multiple times to make the case for the legislation.\n\n\u201cWe are on the same page as the White House,\u201d Ryan said. \u201cI think there are those who would love to wedge us for one reason or another, but that\u2019s just not the case.\u201d\n\n[White House tries to salvage GOP health-care plan as criticism mounts]\n\nHours later, at a rally for Affordable Care Act opponents outside the Capitol, Sen. Ted Cruz (R-Tex.) told activists that the House plan \u201chas a lot of problems\u201d but could possibly be modified into a \u201creal repeal\u201d of the ACA.\n\n\u201cI believe we can get it done,\u201d Cruz said. \u201cI can tell you, the last week, four days, I\u2019ve been at the White House meeting with the president, with the vice president, saying, \u2018We\u2019ve got to get it done.\u2019 \u201d\n\nVarious changes have been floated since the bill was unveiled this month. Conservatives have pushed for an earlier phaseout of the ACA\u2019s Medicaid expansion, moving it from the end of 2019 to the beginning of 2018, but GOP moderates in the House and Senate have pushed back on that notion.\n\n\u201cDoing that would be a nonstarter and would be enormously and hugely problematic for many of our members,\u201d said Rep. Charlie Dent (R-Pa.), a co-chairman of the centrist Tuesday Group.\n\nMeadows said Wednesday that the Freedom Caucus is closely focused on repealing more of the ACA\u2019s mandates governing the extent of insurance coverage. \u201cThat is probably the No. 1 priority,\u201d he said. \u201cIf we can get those, it would make the rest of it a whole lot easier.\u201d\n\nGOP leaders, however, have kept those provisions out of the pending legislation, arguing that their inclusion could threaten the bill\u2019s passage in the Senate and that many of those mandates can be undone through Trump administration actions.\n\nIn another sign of the effect of the CBO report, Sen. John Thune (R-S.D.) floated a proposal to recalibrate the tax credits established under the House bill to offer more substantial help to low-income Americans. The House bill would offer assistance to individuals making as much as $115,000 a year; Thune\u2019s amendment would phase out aid for those making about $75,000 or more.\n\n\u201cWe want to build on what the House has done, and we think that this amendment does that,\u201d Thune said. \u201cIt just makes it more defensible both in terms of the policy implications and the politics.\u201d\n\nKelsey
