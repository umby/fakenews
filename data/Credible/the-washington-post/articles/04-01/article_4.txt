class=\"span8 col\">\n<p><b>MARIA ELENA, Chile</b></p>\n<p class=\"firstgraf\">On the solar farms of the Atacama Desert, the workers dress like astronauts. They wear bodysuits and wraparound sunglasses, with thick canvas headscarves to shield them from the radiation.</p>\n<p>The sun is so intense and the air so dry that seemingly nothing survives. Across vast, rocky wastes blanched of color, there are no cactuses\u00a0or other visible signs of life. It\u2019s Mars, with better cellphone reception.</p>\n<p>It is also\u00a0the world\u2019s best place to produce solar energy, with the most potent sun power\u00a0on the planet.</p>\n<p>So powerful, in fact, that something extraordinary happened last year when the Chilean government invited utility companies to bid on\u00a0public contracts. Solar producers dominated the auction, offering to supply electricity at about half the cost of coal-fired plants.</p>\n<p>It wasn\u2019t because of a government subsidy\u00a0for alternative energy. In Chile and a growing list of nations, the price of solar energy has fallen so much that it is increasingly <a href=\"http://global-climatescope.org/en/summary/\" target=\"_blank\">beating out</a> conventional sources of power. Industry experts and government regulators hail this moment\u00a0as a turning point in the\u00a0history of human electricity-making.</p>\n<p>\u201cThis is the beginning of a trend that will only class=\"span8 marT30 col\">\n<p>President Trump ordered U.S. regulators this week to reverse Obama-era policies aimed at curbing greenhouse gas emissions, and he has promised to \u201cbring back\u201d the U.S. coal industry. But construction of coal-fired power plants <a href=\"http://www.vox.com/energy-and-environment/2017/3/21/14988436/global-coal-boom-decline\" target=\"_blank\">dropped 62\u00a0percent</a> over the past year worldwide, according to a survey by the Sierra Club and other activist groups. In China last year, the number of new permits for coal-fired plants fell by 85\u00a0percent.</p>\n</div></div><div class=\"row\">            <div class=\"span4 col pull-right right-content  bTB1S-phone padTB30\">\n                <!--BOX AD 0 BEGIN-->\n                <div id=\"slug_bigbox\" data-ad-type=\"bigbox\" style=\"display:none;\" class=\"padTB10 advertisement\"></div>\n                <!--BOX AD 0 END-->\n            </div>\n                <div class=\"span8 col\">\n<p>More worldwide generating capacity is now being added from clean sources than coal and natural gas combined, according to a December <a href=\"https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2016-12-15/world-energy-hits-a-turning-point-solar-that-s-cheaper-than-wind\" target=\"_blank\">report</a> by Bloomberg New Energy Finance, which closely tracks investment in renewables.</p>\n<p>An investor in Chile wanting to build a hydroelectric dam or coal-fired plant potentially faces years of costly political battles and fierce resistance from nearby communities. In contrast, a solar company can lay out acres of automated sun-tracking panels across an isolated stretch of desert and have them firing quiet, clean electricity in less than a year, with no worries about fluctuating fuel prices or droughts. The sunlight is free and shows up for work on time, every morning.</p>\n<p>Long dependent on energy imports, Chilean officials now envision their country turning into a \u201csolar Saudi Arabia.\u201d Chile\u2019s solar energy production has increased sixfold since 2014, and last year it was the top-scoring clean-energy producer in the Americas, and second in the world to China, according to the Bloomberg rankings. (China is the world\u2019s\u00a0largest producer of greenhouse gases but also the <a href=\"https://qz.com/641993/the-worlds-biggest-polluter-is-now-the-world-leader-in-renewable-energy-spending/\" target=\"_blank\">leading investor</a> in renewable energy.)</p>\n<p>Driving the global shift to cheap sun power is a dramatic decline in the cost of the photovoltaic (PV) panels that can be used to create giant desert solar farms or rooftop home installations. China produces more than two-thirds of the world\u2019s PV panels, and their price has fallen more than <a href=\"https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/are-we-entering-the-photovoltaic-energy-era/\" target=\"_blank\">80\u00a0percent</a> since 2008.</p>\n</div></div><div class=\"row\">                <div class=\"span12 bTB1S marT10 no-border graphic bg-none graphicPadTB30\" id=\"raw\">\n        <div class=\"franklin-thin-quote\">\r\n    <p><span>\u201cThis is the beginning of a trend that will only accelerate.</span> We\u2019re talking about an infinite fuel source.\u201d</p>\r\n    <p><b>Andr\u00e9s Rebolledo</b><span>, Chilean energy minister</span></p>\r\n</div>        </div>\n                <div class=\"span8 marT30 col\">\n<p>If the trend does indeed symbolize\u00a0a turning point, that doesn\u2019t mean\u00a0every country is on board with the global green-energy conversion.</p>\n<p>Trump\u2019s executive order this week <a href=\"https://www.whitehouse.gov/the-press-office/2017/02/16/president-trump-putting-coal-country-back-work\" target=\"_self\">rolled back</a> restrictions on the coal industry and struck down a slew of other measures intended to limit carbon emissions, including requirements that federal officials take climate change into account when making regulatory decisions.</p>\n<p>Electricity production is the largest source of U.S. greenhouse gas emissions, and more than two-thirds is generated from fossil-fuel sources, according to <a href=\"https://www.epa.gov/ghgemissions/sources-greenhouse-gas-emissions\">the most recent data</a> from the Environmental Protection Agency. The United States last year <a href=\"https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2016/may/27/g7-nations-pledge-to-end-fossil-fuel-subsidies-by-2025\" target=\"_self\">pledged</a> with\u00a0other members\u00a0of the Group of 7 nations to phase out\u00a0subsidies for oil, gas and coal by 2025.</p>\n<p>But meeting such goals remains politically contentious in the United States, with an existing network of conventional power plants and jobs that depend on them. Nations like Chile can take a different path.</p>\n<p>Chile is adding solar plants because they fulfill the country\u2019s\u00a0goal of 60 percent clean energy by the year 2035. But mostly it is adding them because no other energy source can compete with the awesome sun power\u00a0of the Atacama Desert.</p>\n</div></div><div class=\"row\">                                        <div class=\"span12 bTB1S marT10 padTB30 bTB1S\">\n            \n                        <div class=\"photo-full temp-pad\">\n                    <div class=\"spinner\">\n            <img class=\"lazy-image image \"data-original=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/sf/wp-content/themes/wapo-blogs/inc/imrs.php?src=http://www.washingtonpost.com/sf/world/wp-content/uploads/sites/15/2017/03/TamaraMerinoCalama02-web1.jpg&authapi-mob-redir=0\" src=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/sf/world/wp-content/themes/enterprise-theme-nav/images/pixel.png?authapi-mob-redir=0\"  />\n                    </div>\n                    \n            \n        </div>\n\n                                                <div class=\"marT10\">\n                                            <p class=\"marTB0 light-grey size-fourteen franklin-light\">\n                                                    Local workers clean insulators at the Cerro Dominador solar plant's booster substation. With a heavy dependence on imported fuel, Chileans have been paying some of the highest electricity rates in the region, but prices are falling as\u00a0renewable sources come online.</p>\n<p>The Atacama is well-suited to solar energy production for the same reasons astronomers put high-powered <a href=\"http://www.almaobservatory.org/\" target=\"_self\">telescopes</a> in northern Chile for the clearest possible Earth-based views of the cosmos.</p>\n<p>In nations such as Japan and Germany, which\u00a0are some of the world leaders in solar energy production, the sun\u2019s rays are partly diffused by water molecules floating in the air, even on days when it isn\u2019t cloudy.</p>\n<p>But in the super-dry Atacama, where it virtually never rains, the photons beam straight down. Put a solar panel beneath them and it\u2019s\u00a0like plugging\u00a0into the sun.</p>\n<p>At the Finis Terrae solar plant near the tiny town of Maria Elena,\u00a0more than 500,000 PV panels blanket the desert. The 160-megawatt plant was the largest solar installation in Latin America when it went online last summer, capable of powering nearly 200,000 homes. Since then, another\u00a0Chilean\u00a0plant has surpassed it.</p>\n</div></div><div class=\"row\">            <div class=\"span4 col pull-right right-content  bTB1S-phone padTB30\">\n                <!--BOX AD 0 BEGIN-->\n                <div id=\"slug_bigbox_2\" data-ad-type=\"bigbox|2\" style=\"display:none;\" class=\"padTB10 advertisement\"></div>\n                <!--BOX AD 0 END-->\n            </div>\n                <div class=\"span8 col\">\n<p>Outside the plant\u2019s operations center, a worker-safety chart rated the day\u2019s ultraviolet radiation levels on a scale of one to 10.\u00a0\u201cEleven,\u201d it read. \u201cExtreme.\u201d</p>\n<p>\u201cCompared with the Arabian, Sahara and Australian deserts, the Atacama Desert has the highest levels of direct normal irradiation, the key component of the sun\u2019s rays for energy production,\u201d said Salvatore Bernabei, the head of renewable energies for Latin America at Enel, an Italian multinational that owns the plant.</p>\n<p>The radiation is so intense that no one is really sure how long standard-issue PV panels \u2014 which are designed to last 25 years \u2014 will be able to withstand it. One solar plant here had to replace most of its exposed cables after six months because the radiation fried their insulation.</p>\n<p>Another problem solar companies are facing is the desert soil. In the afternoons,\u00a0writhing dust devils zigzag across the desert floor, sprinkling particles onto the panels and reducing their output. Plant managers try to keep them clean using specially outfitted tractors with long wiper-arms, but there\u2019s little water available.</p>\n<p>Chilean energy officials say these challenges are relatively minor. The country derives about 6 percent of its energy from solar, but the potential of the Atacama is so great that Chile could generate all of its electricity\u00a0with about 4\u00a0percent of the desert\u2019s surface area, if there were a way to efficiently store and distribute that energy.</p>\n<p>\u201cWe\u2019ve been thinking for so long that we\u2019re poor in energy resources, but we\u2019re really rich,\u201d said Rodrigo Mancilla, who leads a commission on solar power at Chile\u2019s Ministry of Energy.</p>\n</div></div><div class=\"row\">        <div class=\" marT10\">\n So many companies have raced to set up solar plants\u00a0that they\u2019ve essentially outpaced the electrical grid\u2019s ability to absorb their electricity output. At times of peak radiation in recent months, they\u2019ve had to\u00a0give away sun power virtually <a href=\"https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2016-06-01/chile-has-so-much-solar-energy-it-s-giving-it-away-for-free\" target=\"_self\">free</a>.</p>\n<p>Chilean officials say the problem will be overcome this year when the country\u2019s northern power grid will finally be linked to the larger central grid, which serves most of the country\u2019s residential consumers.\u00a0In theory, that will allow the system to draw more heavily on sun power during the day and save sources like hydropower and coal for the evenings, when the solar plants are useless.</p>\n<p>Still, this remains the biggest knock on\u00a0renewable energy sources: They cannot produce reliable, round-the-clock power.</p>\n</div></div><div class=\"row\">            <div class=\"span4 col pull-right right-content  bTB1S-phone padTB30\">\n                <!--BOX AD 0 BEGIN-->\n                <div id=\"slug_bigbox_3\" data-ad-type=\"bigbox|3\" style=\"display:none;\" class=\"padTB10 advertisement\"></div>\n                <!--BOX AD 0 END-->\n            </div>\n                <div class=\"span8 col\">\n<p>Conventional plants using coal or other fuels can\u2019t be turned on and off like a light switch. So they have to keep running during the day, when they\u2019re far less competitive, and try to make up the difference at night.</p>\n<p>A company looking to bridge this gap in Chile is building Latin America\u2019s first solar thermal plant. You can see its solitary tower rising from the desert for miles around,\u00a0like some sort of\u00a0alien religious shrine. At nearly 700 feet, it is the second-tallest building in Chile.</p>\n<p>Instead of PV panels, the solar thermal plant will have 10,000 giant, rotating mirrors set in concentric circles around the tower. They will\u00a0concentrate the sun\u2019s rays on a huge boiler at the top, filled with molten salts, that reaches more than 1,000 degrees and glows like the Eye of Sauron in \u201cThe Lord of the Rings.\u201d</p>\n<p>The superheated salts ooze downward to steam turbines at the base of the tower, retaining enough energy to generate electricity all night. It\u2019s essentially a giant, rechargeable $1.4 billion battery.</p>\n<p>The plant\u2019s owner, Cerro\u00a0Dominador, a subsidiary of U.S.-based EIG Global Energy Partners, says it will be completed\u00a0in 2019. Larger solar thermal facilities based on the technology are in operation in California, and Chile has issued permits for others.</p>\n<p>Ivan Araneda, the company\u2019s top executive, said such solar thermal facilities can transform the industry.</p>\n<p>\u201cThe attack on renewables is that they\u2019re too expensive, but this is efficient, proven technology,\u201d Araneda said. \u201cOn an even
