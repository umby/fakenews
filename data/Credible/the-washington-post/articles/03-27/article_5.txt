But he will take it. \u00a0</p> <p>Since his electoral triumph last summer, the man famous for\u00a0<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/worldviews/wp/2016/09/06/the-son-of-whore-story-is-about-so-much-more-than-dutertes-dirty-mouth/?utm_term=.06bf9b2a2b3a\" target=\"_self\">cursing foreign leaders</a> and\u00a0<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/worldviews/wp/2016/09/29/duterte-hitler-killed-3-million-jews-i-will-kill-3-million-drug-dealers/?utm_term=.013e1a18cee7\" target=\"_self\">calling for mass killing</a> seems to be raking in the cash for Manila. A tidy $24\u00a0billion in deals with China. Fresh\u00a0<a href=\"http://www.philstar.com/headlines/2017/01/12/1662066/abe-pledges-8.7b-investments-speedboats-philippines\" target=\"_self\">billions</a> from Japan. Not to mention the tens of millions in military and development aid the United States sends each year \u2014 despite his call for a\u00a0\u201cseparation.\u201d</p> <p>Indeed, eight months into his tenure, with President Trump in power and Asian affairs in flux, Duterte\u2019s devil-may-care diplomacy and relentless talk of \u201cslaughter\u201d seem to be paying off, propping up his domestic popularity even as an International Criminal Court prosecutor\u00a0<a href=\"http://af.reuters.com/article/worldNews/idAFKCN12D2SD\" target=\"_self\">warns</a> of a possible war crimes investigation against him.</p> <p>Courting the president of the Philippines are new friends such as China, which last<b>\u00a0</b>week sent a vice premier to Duterte\u2019s home town, and Russia, which recently\u00a0<a href=\"http://edition.cnn.com/2017/01/03/asia/russia-philippines-exercises-south-china-sea/\" target=\"_self\">dispatched</a> two warships to Manila on a goodwill visit. Both see Duterte as an ally against the U.S. military\u2019s Asian ambitions.</p> <p>Old partners such as the United States and Japan might bristle at Duterte\u2019s rhetoric and rights record, but they are willing to speak softly because they need his help countering Chinese claims to most of the South China Sea.\u00a0</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-left\" style=\"width:300px;\"> <a name=\"972551f90005fe5b56175cc28491baf3b70d99e2\"></a> <img alt=\"\" class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1024w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/16/Foreign/Images/AFP_M25DQ.jpg?uuid=fr9WZgpdEeehX6WNSpiEdA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/16/Foreign/Images/AFP_M25DQ.jpg?uuid=fr9WZgpdEeehX6WNSpiEdA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/16/Foreign/Images/AFP_M25DQ.jpg?uuid=fr9WZgpdEeehX6WNSpiEdA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/16/Foreign/Images/AFP_M25DQ.jpg?uuid=fr9WZgpdEeehX6WNSpiEdA\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Duterte supporters attend an overnight vigil to demonstrate backing for his drug crackdown in Manila. (Noel Celis/AFP/Getty Images)</span> </div> <p>Duterte, meanwhile, seems happy to flirt with his various suitors, alternating between swearing and sweet talk, backtracking as required.</p> <p>As a presidential candidate, the longtime mayor of Davao City promised Filipinos an \u201cindependent\u201d foreign policy, vowing to stand up to the Americans and make money from everyone else. With deals and dignitaries streaming in, Duterte can credibly say he delivered \u2014 at least for now.</p> <p>But much of the Philippines prefers the United States to China; Duterte may want to align himself with Beijing\u2019s \u201cideological flow,\u201d as he put it, but swaths of the country\u2019s establishment do not.</p> <p>Duterte\u2019s defense secretary, Maj. Gen. Delfin Lorenzana, recently\u00a0<a href=\"http://www.philstar.com/headlines/2017/03/13/1680750/duterte-concern-over-chinese-ships-benham-rise-exaggerated\" target=\"_self\">expressed concern</a> about Chinese survey ships lingering in waters off the Philippine coast. Faced with questions from reporters, Duterte seemed\u00a0<a href=\"http://www.rappler.com/newsbreak/inside-track/164116-duterte-confusion-benham-rise\" target=\"_self\">confused</a>; he eventually asserted that he would ask the military to tell Beijing to back off \u2014 but in a friendly way.</p> <p>There is a growing sense that his foreign policy is a short-term fix, said Herman Kraft, a political scientist at the University of the Philippines.\u00a0\u201cWe have a tiny window when we can still play both sides.\u201d</p> <p> <b></b>Duterte has a flair for the dramatic, and his entrance to the foreign policy stage was nothing short of spectacular.</p> <p>Casting insults at President Barack Obama, he made a show about finding new \u201cbest friends\u201d in Moscow and Beijing \u2014 although his calls to curtail the decades-old U.S.-Philippine military partnership were quickly played down by members of his own cabinet.</p><div></div> <p>China, seeing an opportunity to curry favor with a key U.S. ally, invited him to the Chinese capital, where he signed billions in deals. Duterte thanked his hosts by <a href=\"http://www.philstar.com/headlines/2016/10/21/1635923/full-text-dutertes-keynote-address-philippines-china-trade-and\" target=\"_self\">railing against</a> the United States.\u00a0</p> <p>Not a month after his speech in Beijing, Donald Trump\u2019s triumph had Duterte singing a different tune. The two countries could now stop feuding, he said \u2014 a turnaround that gave him room to quietly reach out to the United States.\u00a0</p> <p>While Trump prepared for his inauguration, a U.S. ally stepped in. Japanese Prime Minister Shinzo Abe paid a visit. He toured Duterte\u2019s home in the southern Philippines, reportedly\u00a0<a href=\"http://news.abs-cbn.com/news/01/13/17/japans-abe-enjoys-dutertes-folksy-diplomacy-in-philippines\" target=\"_self\">admiring his bed and mosquito net</a>, and\u00a0<a href=\"http://www.scmp.com/news/asia/southeast-asia/article/2061597/japan-pm-shinzo-abe-becomes-first-foreign-leader-visit\" target=\"_self\">announced</a> $8.7 billion in aid.</p> <p>Duterte\u2019s diplomatic maneuvering allowed him to press ahead with state-led killings\u00a0of alleged drug dealers and \u00adusers<b>\u00a0</b>while securing billions of dollars\u2019 worth of deals. \u201cDespite all his shenanigans, he hit a strategic sweet spot,\u201d said\u00a0Richard Javad Heydarian, an assistant professor of political science at Manila\u2019s De La Salle University.</p> <p>\u201cBut,\u201d Heydarian added,\u00a0\u201cthis may be a bit of strategic beginner\u2019s luck. If he keeps at this for a few years, he will be seen as a flip-flopper.\u201d</p> <p>Filipino and foreign experts are skeptical about whether big promises from China and Russia will actually materialize and, if they do, whether the money will keep coming.</p> <p>China will eventually make a move in the South China Sea that Filipinos find unpalatable, said\u00a0Jose L. Cuisia Jr., who, until June, was the Philippine ambassador to the United States.\u00a0When that happens, it will be hard for Duterte to do as China pleases, and those Chinese pledges could dry up, Cuisia said.</p> <p>\u201cI am not sure that we will see a strong relationship with China and Russia in the long term,\u201d he said.</p> <p>For now, Duterte seems likely to woo as many allies and investors as possible, said\u00a0Aileen S.P. Baviera, a China expert at the University of the Philippines\u2019 Asian Center.</p> <p>\u201cBecause of Trump, most countries want to hedge their bets and remain as flexible as possible,\u201d she Not to mention the tens of millions in military and development aid the United States sends each year \u2014 despite his call for a \u201cseparation.\u201d\n\nIndeed, eight months into his tenure, with President Trump in power and Asian affairs in flux, Duterte\u2019s devil-may-care diplomacy and relentless talk of \u201cslaughter\u201d seem to be paying off, propping up his domestic popularity even as an International Criminal Court prosecutor warns of a possible war crimes investigation against him.\n\nCourting the president of the Philippines are new friends such as China, which last week sent a vice premier to Duterte\u2019s home town, and Russia, which recently dispatched two warships to Manila on a goodwill visit. Both see Duterte as an ally against the U.S. military\u2019s Asian ambitions.\n\nOld partners such as the United States and Japan might bristle at Duterte\u2019s rhetoric and rights record, but they are willing to speak softly because they need his help countering Chinese claims to most of the South China Sea.\n\nDuterte supporters attend an overnight vigil to demonstrate backing for his drug crackdown in Manila. (Noel Celis/AFP/Getty Images)\n\nDuterte, meanwhile, seems happy to flirt with his various suitors, alternating between swearing and sweet talk, backtracking as required.\n\nAs a presidential candidate, the longtime mayor of Davao City promised Filipinos an \u201cindependent\u201d foreign policy, vowing to stand up to the Americans and make money from everyone else. With deals and dignitaries streaming in, Duterte can credibly say he delivered \u2014 at least for now.\n\nBut much of the Philippines prefers the United States to China; Duterte may want to align himself with Beijing\u2019s \u201cideological flow,\u201d as he put it, but swaths of the country\u2019s establishment do not.\n\nDuterte\u2019s defense secretary, Maj. Gen. Delfin Lorenzana, recently expressed concern about Chinese survey ships lingering in waters off the Philippine coast. Faced with questions from reporters, Duterte seemed confused; he eventually asserted that he would ask the military to tell Beijing to back off \u2014 but in a friendly way.\n\nThere is a growing sense that his foreign policy is a short-term fix, said Herman Kraft, a political scientist at the University of the Philippines. \u201cWe have a tiny window when we can still play both sides.\u201d\n\nDuterte has a flair for the dramatic, and his entrance to the foreign policy stage was nothing short of spectacular.\n\nCasting insults at President Barack Obama, he made a show about finding new \u201cbest friends\u201d in Moscow and Beijing \u2014 although his calls to curtail the decades-old U.S.-Philippine military partnership were quickly played down by members of his own cabinet.\n\nChina, seeing an opportunity to curry favor with a key U.S. ally, invited him to the Chinese capital, where he signed billions in deals. Duterte thanked his hosts by railing against the United States.\n\nNot a month after his speech in Beijing, Donald Trump\u2019s triumph had Duterte singing a different tune. The two countries could now stop feuding, he said \u2014 a turnaround that gave him room to quietly reach out to the United States.\n\nWhile Trump prepared for his inauguration, a U.S. ally stepped in. Japanese Prime Minister Shinzo Abe paid a visit. He toured Duterte\u2019s home in the southern Philippines, reportedly admiring his bed and mosquito net, and announced $8.7 billion in aid.\n\nDuterte\u2019s diplomatic maneuvering allowed him to press ahead with state-led killings of alleged drug dealers and \u00adusers while securing billions of dollars\u2019 worth of deals. \u201cDespite all his shenanigans, he hit a strategic sweet spot,\u201d said Richard Javad Heydarian, an assistant professor of political science at Manila\u2019s De La Salle University.\n\n\u201cBut,\u201d Heydarian added, \u201cthis may be a bit of strategic beginner\u2019s luck. If he keeps at this for a few years, he will be seen as a flip-flopper.\u201d\n\nFilipino and foreign experts are skeptical about whether big promises from China and Russia will actually materialize and, if they do, whether the money will keep coming.\n\nChina will eventually make a move in the South China Sea that Filipinos find unpalatable, said Jose L. Cuisia Jr., who, until June, was the Philippine ambassador to the United States. When that happens, it will be hard for Duterte to do as China pleases, and those Chinese pledges could dry up, Cuisia said.\n\n\u201cI am not sure that we will see a strong relationship with China and Russia in the long term,\u201d he said.\n\nFor now, Duterte seems likely to woo as many allies and investors as possible, said Aileen S.P. Baviera, a China expert at the University of the Philippines\u2019 Asian Center.\n\n\u201cBecause of Trump, most countries want to hedge their bets and remain as flexible as possible,\u201d she said.
