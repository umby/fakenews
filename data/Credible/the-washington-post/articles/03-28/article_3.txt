administration sought to block former acting attorney general Sally Yates from testifying in the House investigation of possible links between Russian officials and Donald Trump\u2019s campaign, according to letters provided to The Washington Post. The effort to keep Yates from testifying has further angered Democrats, who have accused Republicans of trying to damage the inquiry. </p> <p>According to the letters, the Justice Department notified Yates earlier this month that the administration considers her possible testimony \u2014 including on the ouster of former national security adviser Michael Flynn for his contacts with the Russian ambassador \u2014 to be off-limits in a congressional hearing because the topics are covered by attorney-client privilege or the presidential communication privilege.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/apps/g/page/national/read-the-letters-on-sally-yatess-potential-congressional-testimony-on-russia/2185/\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"apps.washingtonpost.com\">Read the letters on Sally Yates testifying before Congress</a>]</i> </p> <p>The issue of Yates\u2019s testimony adds to the political controversy surrounding the House Intelligence Committee\u2019s investigation of Russian meddling in last year\u2019s election and any possible coordination between Trump associates and Moscow.</p> <p>David O\u2019Neil, an attorney for Yates, met at the Justice Department to discuss the issue with government officials on Thursday. At the meeting, O\u2019Neil presented a letter in which he said the Justice Department had \u201cadvised\u201d him that Yates\u2019s official communications on issues of interest to the House panel are \u201cclient confidences\u201d that cannot be disclosed without written consent. O\u2019Neil challenged that interpretation as \u201coverbroad\u201d in the letter.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"wirereuters\" data-duration=\"117982\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Spicer says Post report on Yates is \u2018false\u2019\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58da9aaae4b0e929b8921527\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-28-2017/t_1490721626282_name_20170328_spicer.jpg\" data-uuid=\"6edd23d2-13da-11e7-bb16-269934184168\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"NGW0mpGkf4Q\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Reuters)</span> </div> </div> <p>The following day, in a letter to O\u2019Neil, the Justice Department responded with another objection: that Yates\u2019s communications with the White House are probably covered by \u201cpresidential communications privilege,\u201d and referred him to the White House.</p> <p>A Justice Department spokeswoman declined to comment.</p> <p>O\u2019Neil then wrote to White House Counsel Donald McGahn, saying that he believed any privilege had been waived as a result of past White House statements and that Yates planned to testify unless he heard back from McGahn.</p> <p>But that same day, the hearing, which also would have included former CIA director John Brennan and former director of national intelligence James R. Clapper Jr., was canceled by the House Intelligence Committee\u2019s chairman, Rep. Devin Nunes (R-Calif.), and any White House decision on Yates\u2019s testimony became moot.</p> <p>In his Tuesday briefing, press secretary Sean Spicer said that the White House did not weigh in on whether Yates could testify. \u201cTo suggest in any way, shape or form that we stood in the way of that is 100\u00a0percent false,\u201d he said.</p> <p>Nunes has said he canceled the hearing to first hear from FBI Director James B. Comey in a classified setting. That session was also canceled.</p> <p>Democrats charge that Nunes has aligned himself too closely with the White House to conduct an independent probe.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-left\" style=\"width:300px;\"> <a name=\"b64932bcb86d1211e71c122b44b15054f8293a59\"></a> <img alt=\"\" class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1024w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/01/09/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/GQ7A77511431703991.jpg?uuid=XS4l0tbCEea4sstRZL66aw\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/01/09/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/GQ7A77511431703991.jpg?uuid=XS4l0tbCEea4sstRZL66aw\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/01/09/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/GQ7A77511431703991.jpg?uuid=XS4l0tbCEea4sstRZL66aw\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/01/09/Production/Daily/A-Section/Images/GQ7A77511431703991.jpg?uuid=XS4l0tbCEea4sstRZL66aw\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Then-Deputy Attorney General Sally Yates at the Justice Department on May 15, 2015. (Evelyn Hockstein/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>\u201cYou see the unraveling of this committee happening overnight for no good reason,\u201d said Rep. Jackie Speier (D-Calif.), a committee member. \u201cWe have a responsibility to do this investigation.\u201d</p> <p>O\u2019Neil\u2019s meeting at the Justice Department and the exchange of letters came to light as the House GOP leadership continued to stand by Nunes. House Speaker Paul D. Ryan (R-Wis.) rejected demands that Nunes recuse himself. Nunes said Tuesday that he had no plans to step aside.</p> <p>Yates was the deputy attorney general in the final years of the Obama administration and served as the acting attorney general in the first days of the Trump administration.</p> <p> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/national-security/acting-attorney-general-an-obama-administration-holdover-wont-defend-trump-immigration-order/2017/01/30/a9846f02-e727-11e6-b82f-687d6e6a3e7c_story.html?tid=a_inl&amp;utm_term=.bdd426640f25\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Trump fired Yates in January</a> after she ordered Justice Department lawyers not to defend his first immigration order temporarily banning entry to the United States for citizens of seven Muslim-majority countries and refugees from around the world.</p> <p>As acting attorney general, Yates played a key part in the investigation surrounding Flynn, who was ousted after revelations that he had discussed sanctions with the Russian ambassador to the United States.</p> <p>Yates and Brennan had made clear to government officials by Thursday that their testimony to the committee would probably contradict some statements that White House officials had made, according to a person familiar with the matter who spoke on the condition of anonymity.</p> <p>O\u2019Neil and Ken Wainstein, a lawyer for Brennan, declined to comment.</p> <p>During his press briefing Tuesday, Spicer said of Yates: \u201cI hope she testifies. I look forward to it.\u2019\u2019</p> <p>Spicer said the White House did not seek to have the House hearing canceled.</p> <p>Rep. Adam B. Schiff (Calif.), the ranking Democrat on the Intelligence Committee, said the panel was aware that Yates \u201csought permission to testify from the White House. Whether the White House\u2019s desire to avoid a public claim of executive privilege to keep her from providing the full truth on what happened contributed to the decision to cancel today\u2019s hearing, we do not know. But we would urge that the open hearing be rescheduled without delay and that Ms. Yates be permitted to testify freely and openly.\u2019\u2019</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/national-security/chairman-and-partisan-the-dual-roles-of-devin-nunes-raise-questions-about-house-investigation/2017/03/26/2c95ade2-1096-11e7-9b0d-d27c98455440_story.html?tid=a_inl&amp;utm_term=.c28c277d9021\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Chairman and partisan: The dual roles of Devin Nunes raise questions about House investigation</a>]</i> </p> <p>In January, Yates warned McGahn that statements White House officials, including Vice President Pence, had made about Flynn\u2019s contact with the ambassador were incorrect and could therefore expose the national security adviser to future blackmail by the Russians.</p> <p>In his March 23 letter \u2014 addressed to acting assistant attorney general Samuel Ramer \u2014 O\u2019Neil noted that Yates was willing to testify and that she would avoid discussing classified information and details that could compromise investigations. The correspondence was later shared with the House Intelligence Committee.</p> <p>O\u2019Neil went on to memorialize the government\u2019s position:</p> <p>\u201cThe Department of Justice has advised that it believes there are further constraints on the testimony Ms. Yates may provide at the [House Intelligence Committee] hearing. Generally, we understand that the department takes the position that all information Ms. Yates received or actions she took in her capacity as Deputy Attorney General and acting Attorney General are client confidences that she may not disclose absent written consent of the department,\u2019\u2019 he wrote.</p> <p>O\u2019Neil continued: \u201cWe believe that the department\u2019s position in this regard is overbroad, incorrect, and inconsistent with the department\u2019s historical approach to the congressional testimony of current and former officials.</p> <p>\u201cIn particular, we believe that Ms. Yates should not be obligated to refuse to provide nonclassified facts about the department\u2019s notification to the White House of concerns about the conduct of a senior official. Requiring Ms. Yates to refuse to provide such information is particularly untenable given that multiple senior administration officials have publicly described the same events.\u2019\u2019</p> <p>The following day, Scott Schools, a senior Justice Department lawyer, replied to O\u2019Neil, writing that Yates\u2019s conversations with the White House \u201care likely covered by the presidential communications privilege and possibly the deliberative process privilege. The president owns those privileges. Therefore, to the extent Ms. Yates needs consent to disclose the details of those communications to [the intelligence panel], she needs to consult with the White House. She need not obtain separate consent from the department.\u2019\u2019</p> <p>That letter, in essence, marked Justice Department officials backing away from their earlier strictures, saying that although they thought executive privilege probably applied to Yates\u2019s discussions, that was a conversation she would have to have with lawyers at the White House.</p> <p>In response, O\u2019Neil sent a letter Friday to McGahn, the White House counsel, saying that any claim of privilege \u201chas been waived as a result of the multiple public comments of current senior White House officials describing the January 2017 communications. Nevertheless, I am advising the White House of Ms. Yates\u2019 intention to provide information.\u2019\u2019</p> <p>He closed the letter by saying that if he did not hear back from the White House by 10\u00a0a.m. Monday, he would assume that it \u201cdoes not exert executive privilege over these matters with respect to the hearings or other settings.\u2019\u2019</p> <p>The cancellation of the hearing made O\u2019Neil\u2019s deadline moot, although Spicer said the lack of a response to the lawyer\u2019s letter showed the administration had no problem with Yates testifying.</p> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>Karoun Demirjian contributed to this report.</p> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <b>Read more:</b> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"http://wapo.st/2mjYM5E\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"wapo.st\">The web of relationships between Team Trump and Russia</a> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"http://wapo.st/2lTmkvU\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"wapo.st\">Who is Sally Yates? "date": "03-28-2017", "source": "the-washington-post", "text": "The Trump administration sought to block former acting attorney general Sally Yates from testifying in the House investigation of possible links between Russian officials and Donald Trump\u2019s campaign, according to letters provided to The Washington Post. The effort to keep Yates from testifying has further angered Democrats, who have accused Republicans of trying to damage the inquiry.\n\nAccording to the letters, the Justice Department notified Yates earlier this month that the administration considers her possible testimony \u2014 including on the ouster of former national security adviser Michael Flynn for his contacts with the Russian ambassador \u2014 to be off-limits in a congressional hearing because the topics are covered by attorney-client privilege or the presidential communication privilege.\n\n[Read the letters on Sally Yates testifying before Congress]\n\nThe issue of Yates\u2019s testimony adds to the political controversy surrounding the House Intelligence Committee\u2019s investigation of Russian meddling in last year\u2019s election and any possible coordination between Trump associates and Moscow.\n\nDavid O\u2019Neil, an attorney for Yates, met at the Justice Department to discuss the issue with government officials on Thursday. At the meeting, O\u2019Neil presented a letter in which he said the Justice Department had \u201cadvised\u201d him that Yates\u2019s official communications on issues of interest to the House panel are \u201cclient confidences\u201d that cannot be disclosed without written consent. O\u2019Neil challenged that interpretation as \u201coverbroad\u201d in the letter.\n\n(Reuters)\n\nThe following day, in a letter to O\u2019Neil, the Justice Department responded with another objection: that Yates\u2019s communications with the White House are probably covered by \u201cpresidential communications privilege,\u201d and referred him to the White House.\n\nA Justice Department spokeswoman declined to comment.\n\nO\u2019Neil then wrote to White House Counsel Donald McGahn, saying that he believed any privilege had been waived as a result of past White House statements and that Yates planned to testify unless he heard back from McGahn.\n\nBut that same day, the hearing, which also would have included former CIA director John Brennan and former director of national intelligence James R. Clapper Jr., was canceled by the House Intelligence Committee\u2019s chairman, Rep. Devin Nunes (R-Calif.), and any White House decision on Yates\u2019s testimony became moot.\n\nIn his Tuesday briefing, press secretary Sean Spicer said that the White House did not weigh in on whether Yates could testify. \u201cTo suggest in any way, shape or form that we stood in the way of that is 100 percent false,\u201d he said.\n\nNunes has said he canceled the hearing to first hear from FBI Director James B. Comey in a classified setting. That session was also canceled.\n\nDemocrats charge that Nunes has aligned himself too closely with the White House to conduct an independent probe.\n\nThen-Deputy Attorney General Sally Yates at the Justice Department on May 15, 2015. (Evelyn Hockstein/For The Washington Post)\n\n\u201cYou see the unraveling of this committee happening overnight for no good reason,\u201d said Rep. Jackie Speier (D-Calif.), a committee member. \u201cWe have a responsibility to do this investigation.\u201d\n\nO\u2019Neil\u2019s meeting at the Justice Department and the exchange of letters came to light as the House GOP leadership continued to stand by Nunes. House Speaker Paul D. Ryan (R-Wis.) rejected demands that Nunes recuse himself. Nunes said Tuesday that he had no plans to step aside.\n\nYates was the deputy attorney general in the final years of the Obama administration and served as the acting attorney general in the first days of the Trump administration.\n\nTrump fired Yates in January after she ordered Justice Department lawyers not to defend his first immigration order temporarily banning entry to the United States for citizens of seven Muslim-majority countries and refugees from around the world.\n\nAs acting attorney general, Yates played a key part in the investigation surrounding Flynn, who was ousted after revelations that he had discussed sanctions with the Russian ambassador to the United States.\n\nYates and Brennan had made clear to government officials by Thursday that their testimony to the committee would probably contradict some statements that White House officials had made, according to a person familiar with the matter who spoke on the condition of anonymity.\n\nO\u2019Neil and Ken Wainstein, a lawyer for Brennan, declined to comment.\n\nDuring his press briefing Tuesday, Spicer said of Yates: \u201cI hope she testifies. I look forward to it.\u2019\u2019\n\nSpicer said the White House did not seek to have the House hearing canceled.\n\nRep. Adam B. Schiff (Calif.), the ranking Democrat on the Intelligence Committee, said the panel was aware that Yates \u201csought permission to testify from the White House. Whether the White House\u2019s desire to avoid a public claim of executive privilege to keep her from providing the full truth on what happened contributed to the decision to cancel today\u2019s hearing, we do not know. But we would urge that the open hearing be rescheduled without delay and that Ms. Yates be permitted to testify freely and openly.\u2019\u2019\n\n[Chairman and partisan: The dual roles of Devin Nunes raise questions about House investigation]\n\nIn January, Yates warned McGahn that statements White House officials, including Vice President Pence, had made about Flynn\u2019s contact with the ambassador were incorrect and could therefore expose the national security adviser to future blackmail by the Russians.\n\nIn his March 23 letter \u2014 addressed to acting assistant attorney general Samuel Ramer \u2014 O\u2019Neil noted that Yates was willing to testify and that she would avoid discussing classified information and details that could compromise investigations. The correspondence was later shared with the House Intelligence Committee.\n\nO\u2019Neil went on to memorialize the government\u2019s position:\n\n\u201cThe Department of Justice has advised that it believes there are further constraints on the testimony Ms. Yates may provide at the [House Intelligence Committee] hearing. Generally, we understand that the department takes the position that all information Ms. Yates received or actions she took in her capacity as Deputy Attorney General and acting Attorney General are client confidences that she may not disclose absent written consent of the department,\u2019\u2019 he wrote.\n\nO\u2019Neil continued: \u201cWe believe that the department\u2019s position in this regard is overbroad, incorrect, and inconsistent with the department\u2019s historical approach to the congressional testimony of current and former officials.\n\n\u201cIn particular, we believe that Ms. Yates should not be obligated to refuse to provide nonclassified facts about the department\u2019s notification to the White House of concerns about the conduct of a senior official. Requiring Ms. Yates to refuse to provide such information is particularly untenable given that multiple senior administration officials have publicly described the same events.\u2019\u2019\n\nThe following day, Scott Schools, a senior Justice Department lawyer, replied to O\u2019Neil, writing that Yates\u2019s conversations with the White House \u201care likely covered by the presidential communications privilege and possibly the deliberative process privilege. The president owns those privileges. Therefore, to the extent Ms. Yates needs consent to disclose the details of those communications to [the intelligence panel], she needs to consult with the White House. She need not obtain separate consent from the department.\u2019\u2019\n\nThat letter, in essence, marked Justice Department officials backing away from their earlier strictures, saying that although they thought executive privilege probably applied to Yates\u2019s discussions, that was a conversation she would have to have with lawyers at the White House.\n\nIn response, O\u2019Neil sent a letter Friday to McGahn, the White House counsel, saying that any claim of privilege \u201chas been waived as a result of the multiple public comments of current senior White House officials describing the January 2017 communications. Nevertheless, I am advising the White House of Ms. Yates\u2019 intention to provide information.\u2019\u2019\n\nHe closed the letter by saying that if he did not hear back from the White House by 10 a.m. Monday, he would assume that it \u201cdoes not exert executive privilege over these matters with respect to the hearings or other settings.\u2019\u2019\n\nThe cancellation of the hearing made O\u2019Neil\u2019s deadline moot, although Spicer said the lack of a response to the lawyer\u2019s letter showed the administration had no problem with Yates testifying.\n\nKaroun Demirjian contributed to this report.\n\nRead more:\n\nThe web of relationships between Team Trump and Russia\n\nWho is Sally Yates?
