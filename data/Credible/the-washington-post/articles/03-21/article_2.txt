(Alan Diaz/Associated Press)</span> </div> <div class=\"pb-sig-line hasnt-headshot has-0-headshots hasnt-bio is-not-column\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/marc-fisher/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Marc Fisher</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-byline-tool-mobile\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/marc-fisher/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Marc Fisher</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:fisherm@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Labor nominee Acosta cut deal with billionaire guilty in sex abuse case'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> </span> <span class=\"pb-timestamp\" itemprop=\"datePublished\" content=\"2017-03-21T08:28-500\">March 21 at 8:28 PM</span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:fisherm@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Labor nominee Acosta cut deal with billionaire guilty in sex abuse case'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> <span class=\"tweet-authors\"><span class=\"pb-twitter-follow unprocessed\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/@mffisher\" class=\"twitter-follow-button\" data-show-count=\"false\" data-lang=\"en\">Follow @mffisher</a></span> </span> </div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p>There was once a time \u2014 before the investigations, before the sexual abuse conviction \u2014 when rich and famous men loved to hang around with Jeffrey Epstein, a billionaire money manager who loved to party.</p> <p>They visited his mansion in Palm Beach, Fla. They flew on his jet to join him at his private estate on the Caribbean island of Little Saint James. They even joked about his taste in younger women.</p> <p>President Trump called Epstein a \u201cterrific guy\u201d back in 2002, saying that \u201che\u2019s a lot of fun to be with. It is even said that he likes beautiful women as much as I do, and many of them are <a href=\"http://nymag.com/nymetro/news/people/n_7912/\" shape=\"rect\">on the younger side</a>.\u201d</p> <p>Now, Trump is on the witness list in a Florida court battle over how federal prosecutors handled allegations that Epstein, 64, sexually abused more than 40 minor girls, most of them between the ages of 13 and 17. The lawsuit questions why Trump\u2019s nominee for labor secretary, former Miami U.S. attorney Alexander Acosta, whose confirmation hearing is scheduled to begin Wednesday, cut a non-prosecution deal with Epstein a decade ago rather than pursuing a federal indictment that Acosta\u2019s staff had advocated.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.scribd.com/document/342637849/Acosta-Epstein\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.scribd.com\">Read the non-prosecution deal Acosta made with Epstein</a>]</i> </p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"56213\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"What you need to know about Alexander Acosta\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58a5f248e4b0c999437693ca\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/02-16-2017/t_1487270456841_name_2017_02_16T172139Z_1740128260_RC1D232470F0_RTRMADP_3_USA_TRUMP_ACOSTA.jpg\" data-uuid=\"804ba27e-f477-11e6-9fb1-2d8f3fc9c0ed\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"3VDaxehkE3k\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Thomas Johnson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>Although Epstein\u2019s friends and visitors once included past and future presidents, rock stars, and some of the country\u2019s richest men, he is no longer a social magnet. Epstein pleaded guilty to a Florida state charge of felony solicitation of underage girls in 2008 and served a 13-month jail sentence. Politicians who had accepted his donations, including former New Mexico governor Bill Richardson and former New York governor Eliot Spitzer, have scurried to give them back. (Harvard University <a href=\"http://www.thecrimson.com/article/2006/9/13/harvard-to-keep-epstein-gift-after/\" shape=\"rect\">kept a $6.5\u00a0million gift</a>, saying it was \u201cfunding important research\u201d in mathematics.)</p> <p>But Epstein\u2019s unusually light punishment \u2014 he was facing up to a life sentence had he been convicted on federal charges \u2014 has raised questions about how Acosta handled the case.</p> <p>Former Palm Beach police chief Michael Reiter, whose department conducted the initial investigation into Epstein\u2019s behavior, said in a civil lawsuit deposition that Epstein got off easy.</p> <p>\u201cThat wasn\u2019t an appropriate resolution of this matter,\u201d Reiter said, arguing that the charges leveled against Epstein were \u201cvery minor,\u201d compared with what the facts called for. In a letter to parents of Epstein\u2019s victims, Reiter said justice had not been served.</p> <p>Prosecutors in Acosta\u2019s Miami office who had joined the FBI in the investigation concluded, according to documents produced by the U.S. attorney\u2019s office, that Epstein, working through several female assistants, \u201cwould recruit underage females to travel to his home in Palm Beach to engage in lewd conduct in exchange for money. .\u2009.\u2009. Some went there as much as 100 times or more. Some of the women\u2019s conduct was limited to performing a topless or nude massage while Mr. Epstein masturbated himself. For other women, the conduct escalated to full sexual intercourse.\u201d</p> <p>Epstein has a near-legendary reputation in New York financial circles as a money manager who made many millions for his clients. Although he never graduated from college, he taught advanced math at the Dalton School, one of the city\u2019s top private schools, and went on to be a successful trader at Bear Stearns before starting his own firm, J. Epstein &amp; Co., which managed the finances of clients who had a minimum of $1\u00a0billion in assets.</p> <p>Federal prosecutors detailed their findings in an 82-page prosecution memo and a 53-page indictment, but Epstein was never indicted. In 2007, Acosta signed a non-prosecution deal in which he agreed not to pursue federal charges against Epstein or four women who the government said procured girls for him. In exchange, Epstein agreed to plead guilty to a solicitation charge in state court, accept a 13-month sentence, register as a sex offender and pay restitution to the victims identified in the federal investigation.</p><div></div> <p>\u201cThis agreement will not be made part of any public record,\u201d the deal between Epstein and Acosta says. The document was unsealed by a federal judge in a civil lawsuit in 2015.</p> <p>Reiter said in the 2009 deposition that federal prosecutors in Miami told him \u201cthat typically these kinds of cases with one victim would end up in a ten-year sentence.\u201d Reiter said he was surprised not only by the decision to pull back from prosecuting the case, but also by the light sentence and liberal privileges granted to Epstein during his jail term.</p> <p>Acosta did not return a call seeking comment. He explained his decision in a \u201cTo whom it may concern\u201d letter that he released to news organizations three years after the decision: \u201cThe bottom line is this: Mr. Jeffrey Epstein, a billionaire, served time in jail and is now a registered sex offender. He has been required to pay his victims restitution, though restitution clearly cannot compensate for the crime.\u201d Acosta wrote that the case against Epstein grew stronger over the years because more victims spoke out after Epstein was convicted.</p> <p>Acosta is Trump\u2019s second nominee to be secretary of labor; the first, Andrew Puzder, withdrew last month after Senate Republicans questioned his past employment of an undocumented housekeeper. Support for Acosta seems strong, as some Democrats and union leaders have joined with Senate Republicans in praising the nominee, who has been confirmed for federal positions three times in the past.</p> <p>In the 2011 letter explaining his decision in the Epstein case, Acosta said he backed off from pressing charges after \u201ca year-long assault on the prosecution and the prosecutors\u201d by \u201can army of legal superstars\u201d who represented Epstein, including Harvard Law professor Alan Dershowitz; Kenneth Starr, who as independent counsel led the investigation that brought about President Bill Clinton\u2019s impeachment; and some of the nation\u2019s most prominent defense attorneys, such as Roy Black, Gerald Lefcourt and Jay Lefkowitz.</p> <p>\u201cThe defense strategy was not limited to legal issues,\u201d Acosta wrote. \u201cDefense counsel investigated individual prosecutors and their families, looking for personal peccadilloes that may provide a basis for disqualification.\u201d</p> <p>Dershowitz said in an interview that no such effort to rattle the prosecutors ever took place. \u201cThat\u2019s just dead wrong,\u201d he said. \u201cI would never participate in anything of that kind. Of course we investigated the witnesses but not Acosta\u2019s deputies. That\u2019s absurd.\u201d</p> <p>Acosta\u2019s \u201cintention was to indict, and he fought hard and tried to get the best deal he could,\u201d Dershowitz said. \u201cWe outlawyered him.\u201d Epstein did not return a call seeking comment.</p> <p>Conchita Sarnoff, the author of \u201cTrafficKing,\u201d a book on the Epstein case, said in an interview that Acosta told her a few years after his decision not to prosecute that \u201che felt incapable of going up against those eight powerful attorneys. He felt his career was at stake.\u201d</p> <p>In his letter about the decision, Acosta, who has been dean of the law school at Florida International University since 2009, acknowledged that \u201csome prosecutors felt that we should just go to trial, and at times I felt that frustration myself.\u201d He also complained that Epstein \u201creceived highly unusual treatment while in jail,\u201d including being allowed to serve much of his sentence in the county jail rather than a state prison, and being permitted to leave the jail six days a week to work at home before returning to jail to sleep.</p> <p>\u201cThe treatment that he received while in state custody undermined the purpose of a jail sentence,\u201d Acosta said.</p> <p>Dershowitz said Acosta \u201cwas very anxious to prosecute\u201d Epstein, but \u201cwe persuaded them that they didn\u2019t have enough evidence of interstate transportation\u201d of the underage girls to warrant federal charges.</p> <p>But Reiter, the former police chief, said the FBI had evidence \u201cfrom flight logs or something\u201d that an underage victim \u201cwas transported on an aircraft of Mr. Epstein.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cSome may feel that the prosecution should have been tougher,\u201d Acosta wrote. \u201cEvidence that has come to light since 2007 may encourage that view.\u201d But the prosecutor argued that his office\u2019s investigation allowed state prosecutors to strengthen their charges against Epstein. And Acosta said that those who disagree with his decision \u201care not the ones who at the time reviewed the evidence available for trial and assessed the likelihood of success.\u201d</p> <p>The deal Acosta made with Epstein precluded any new federal prosecution based on offenses he may have committed between 2001 and 2007, but in Florida, Trump is on the witness list in a civil case in which two attorneys accuse federal prosecutors of having deceived Epstein\u2019s victims by failing to inform them that they would not charge Epstein.</p> <p>Lawyers for the women argue that they had a right under the federal Crime Victims\u2019 Rights Act to know about Acosta\u2019s deal with Epstein. They say Acosta sought to keep the deal under wraps to avoid \u201cthe intense public criticism that would have resulted from allowing a politically-connected billionaire\u201d to escape from federal prosecution.</p> <p>Although Trump and Bill Clinton flew on Epstein\u2019s plane and visited his homes, neither president has been accused of taking part in the sexual misdeeds. But lawyers for Epstein\u2019s victims say Trump nonetheless may have useful information. Trump banned Epstein from his Mar-a-Lago Club in Palm Beach \u201cbecause Epstein sexually assaulted an underage girl at the club,\u201d Bradley Edwards, an attorney who represents three of the young women, said in court documents.</p> <p>Lawyers involved with the various Epstein cases said there is virtually no chance that the president will be required to testify in a matter in which both sides agree his involvement was tangential.</p> <p>Trump and Clinton are both among the dozens of names that appeared in a \u201cblack book\u201d of Epstein\u2019s phone contacts that his houseman, Alfredo Rodriguez, obtained. Rodriguez, who died in 2015, was convicted of obstruction of justice in 2010 after he tried to sell the book for $50,000 to lawyers representing Epstein\u2019s victims. In the book, Rodriguez circled the names of contacts he said were involved in sexual misbehavior at Epstein\u2019s properties. There were no circles around the names of Trump, Clinton or other boldfaced names such as former Israeli prime minister Ehud Barak, former British prime minister Tony Blair, and celebrities Mick Jagger, Michael Jackson, David Frost and Jimmy Buffett.</p> <p>Rodriguez spent 18\u00a0months in prison, five months longer than Epstein served in jail.</p> <p>Epstein has continued to move among his homes in New York City, where he owns one of the largest private residences in Manhattan, Palm Beach and the Caribbean.</p> </article> <div class=\"clear\"></div> </div> </div> <div The lawsuit questions why Trump\u2019s nominee for labor secretary, former Miami U.S. attorney Alexander Acosta, whose confirmation hearing is scheduled to begin Wednesday, cut a non-prosecution deal with Epstein a decade ago rather than pursuing a federal indictment that Acosta\u2019s staff had advocated.\n\n[Read the non-prosecution deal Acosta made with Epstein]\n\n(Thomas Johnson/The Washington Post)\n\nAlthough Epstein\u2019s friends and visitors once included past and future presidents, rock stars, and some of the country\u2019s richest men, he is no longer a social magnet.
