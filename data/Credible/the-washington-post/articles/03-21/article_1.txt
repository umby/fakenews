the Republican health-care overhaul to skeptical House members, warning his party that failure would endanger his legislative agenda and their own political careers.</p> <p>But more than two dozen GOP lawmakers remained firmly opposed to the legislation amid the high-stakes persuasion campaign led by Trump and House Speaker Paul D. Ryan (R-Wis.) \u2014 more than enough to block the bill ahead of a planned Thursday vote.</p> <p>House passage would represent a powerful, if symbolic, achievement for both men \u2014 and failure would send leaders back to the drawing board on a key issue that Trump and congressional Republicans promised voters they would address. Even if the House approves the package, the legislation faces an uphill battle in the Senate.</p> <p>The holdouts are mainly hard-line conservatives who believe that the bill, known as the American Health Care Act, does not do nearly enough to undo the Affordable Care Act passed by Democrats in 2010. But they also include moderates who fear that the bill will imperil their constituents and their party\u2019s prospects at the ballot box.</p> <p>In a morning address to a closed-door meeting of House Republicans, Trump used both charm and admonishment as he made his case, reassuring skittish members that they would gain seats in Congress if the bill passed.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"wirereuters\" data-duration=\"33070\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Spicer: Trump \u2018had some fun\u2019 with Meadows\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58d1788ee4b059a9e428017a\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-21-2017/t_1490125792774_name_20170321_spicer_AP_thumbnail.jpg\" data-uuid=\"cf37d384-0e68-11e7-aa57-2ca1b05c41b8\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"_bMFDr2Kifo\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Reuters)</span> </div> </div> <p>He singled out Rep. Mark Meadows (R-N.C.), the chairman of the House Freedom Caucus, which has led the right-wing opposition to the bill.</p> <p>\u201cI\u2019m gonna come after you, but I know I won\u2019t have to, because I know you\u2019ll vote \u2018yes,\u2019\u00a0\u201d Trump said, according to several lawmakers who attended the meeting. \u201cHonestly, a loss is not acceptable, folks.\u201d</p> <p>Trump\u2019s remarks \u2014 which Meadows said he took as good-natured ribbing \u2014 reflected his mounting urgency to secure a major legislative victory in the early months of his presidency and fulfill a central campaign promise by repealing the signature domestic achievement of President Barack Obama. Passing a health-care measure is key to unlocking momentum for the president\u2019s other legislative priorities, such as tax reform and infrastructure spending.</p> <p>\u201cHe wants to get this bill done,\u201d said Sen. David Perdue (R-Ga.), a Trump ally. \u201cI don\u2019t hear that as a threat. It\u2019s a statement of reality.\u201d </p> <p>In interviews, more than two dozen lawmakers said they were either firmly opposed to the bill or leaning toward voting against it. Ryan can lose only 21 members of his party for the bill to succeed, as no Democrats have pledged to support the package.</p> <p>Several Republicans privately said Tuesday that the Thursday vote could be postponed if leaders are unable to secure enough firm votes for passage beforehand.</p> <p>One top Republican not authorized to speak about the whipping process said the leadership remained confident that it would collect enough support but was weighing scheduling options.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-graphic-linked-296\"> <div class=\"photo-wrapper\"> <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/politics/obamacare-replacement-next-steps/\"><img src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/13/National-Politics/Graphics/obamacare-replacement-next-steps-promo.jpg?uuid=8JG0_AeLEee9Gf06-g9-Kg\"/></a> <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/politics/obamacare-replacement-next-steps/\"> <span class=\"overlay\"> <span class=\"view-graphic\">View Graphic <span class=\"fa fa-arrow-circle-right\"></span></span></span> </a> </div> <span class=\"pb-caption\">What\u2019s next for the Obamacare replacement bill</span> </div> <p>\u201cThe White House is engaged, the leadership is engaged, everyone is working together,\u201d the Republican said. \u201cBut this is the House GOP, and you can\u2019t assume that it\u2019s going to go perfect. You leave options,\u201d meaning a vote on Friday or even the weekend.</p> <p>A second Republican, also not authorized to discuss internal deliberations, said others in the leadership orbit were eager to bring the bill to the floor, even if the count is narrow, because they would like opponents to take ownership of their position and the consequences of what it would mean for the president.</p> <p id=\"U1200852840392mdH\">Addressing reporters Tuesday, Ryan played down the possibility that the bill could fail Thursday and argued that conservatives should be pleased that many of their demands will probably be in the legislation. Adding further changes, he said, could jeopardize the legislation\u2019s chances in the Senate.</p> <p id=\"U12008528403925mH\">\u201cIf you get 85 percent of what you want, that\u2019s pretty darn good,\u201d he said. \u201cWe don\u2019t want to put something in this bill that the Senate is telling us is fatal.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U1200852840392lMD\">Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell (R-Ky.) sounded a cautiously optimistic note Tuesday, promising that the Senate would forge ahead with plans for votes on the measure \u2014 if it passed the House first.</p> <p id=\"U12008528403923DB\">\u201cIf the House passes something, I will bring it up,\u201d McConnell said. \u201cWe\u2019ll try to move it across the floor next week.\u201d</p> <p>On Tuesday afternoon, Trump hosted more than a dozen members of the Tuesday Group, a moderate House faction, in the Oval Office for a lower-key lobbying session that involved the president asking each person to relay their concerns about the bill.</p> <p>On Friday, a similar meeting helped Trump win converts among members of the Republican Study Committee, a key conservative bloc. But on Tuesday, he found more resistance.</p> <p>Going into the White House meeting, Rep. Leonard Lance (R-N.J.) described himself as \u201ca strong lean no,\u201d citing a variety of concerns. \u201cMy views are based on fundamentals in the legislation,\u201d he said. \u201cI don\u2019t see the lower premiums in this bill.\u201d</p> <p>After the meeting, he said his views had hardened: \u201cI\u2019m a no,\u201d he told reporters.</p> <p>The meeting came less than 24 hours after GOP leaders released changes to the bill that they believe are sufficient to win a House majority. </p> <p>Many of the changes were made to placate conservatives, including giving states the option to take a fixed Medicaid block grant and to impose work requirements on childless, able-bodied adults covered under the program. Others responded to broader concerns about the sufficiency of the tax credits offered to help Americans purchase insurance.</p> <p>One revision was more narrowly targeted \u2014 added at the behest of a group of Upstate New York Republicans who wanted to end their state\u2019s practice of commandeering local tax revenue to fund state Medicaid benefits.</p> <p>That compounded the concerns of Rep. Daniel Donovan (R-N.Y.), a Tuesday Group member who represents parts of New York City that would be hurt by the change.</p> <p>\u201cI have four hospital systems in my district; they are my biggest employers,\u201d he said. \u201cAll of them have grave concerns about how they are going to survive if this gets passed.\u201d</p> <p>After the White House session, Donovan said he welcomed the meeting with Trump but had not reached a final decision: \u201cWe\u2019ll know on Thursday. Some of these things have to be addressed.\u201d</p> <p>One surprising holdout was Rep. Lou Barletta (R-Pa.), who was among the first House members to endorse Trump and has emerged as one of his most stalwart backers. But he is a hard-liner on illegal immigration and cited the issue Tuesday in opposing the bill.</p> <p>The tax credits offered under the GOP plan, he said, could be claimed by individuals who are not \u201clawfully in this country and eligible to receive them.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI would have a hard time explaining to families in the 11th District .\u2009.\u2009. why they should be helping to pay for the health expenses of someone who broke the law to get here and has no right to those federal dollars,\u201d he said.</p> <p>But it was Trump\u2019s warning to Meadows that sent the sharpest message Tuesday. \u201cHe was kidding around \u2014 I think,\u201d said Rep. Even if the House approves the package, the legislation faces an uphill battle in the Senate.\n\nThe holdouts are mainly hard-line conservatives who believe that the bill, known as the American Health Care Act, does not do nearly enough to undo the Affordable Care Act passed by Democrats in 2010. But they also include moderates who fear that the bill will imperil their constituents and their party\u2019s prospects at the ballot box.\n\nIn a morning address to a closed-door meeting of House Republicans, Trump used both charm and admonishment as he made his case, reassuring skittish members that they would gain seats in Congress if the bill passed.\n\n(Reuters)\n\nHe singled out Rep. Passing a health-care measure is key to unlocking momentum for the president\u2019s other legislative priorities, such as tax reform and infrastructure spending.\n\n\u201cHe wants to get this bill done,\u201d said Sen. David Perdue (R-Ga.), a Trump ally. \u201cI don\u2019t hear that as a threat. It\u2019s a statement of reality.\u201d\n\nIn interviews, more than two dozen lawmakers said they were either firmly opposed to the bill or leaning toward voting against it. Ryan can lose only 21 members of his party for the bill to succeed, as no Democrats have pledged to support the package.\n\nSeveral Republicans privately said Tuesday that the Thursday vote could be postponed if leaders are unable to secure enough firm votes for passage beforehand.\n\nOne top Republican not authorized to speak about the whipping process said the leadership remained confident that it would collect enough support but was weighing scheduling options.\n\nView Graphic What\u2019s next for the Obamacare replacement bill\n\n\u201cThe White House is engaged, the leadership is engaged, everyone is working together,\u201d the Republican said. \u201cBut this is the House GOP, and you can\u2019t assume that it\u2019s going to go perfect. Daniel Donovan (R-N.Y.), a Tuesday Group member who represents parts of New York City that would be hurt by the change.\n\n\u201cI have four hospital systems in my district; they are my biggest employers,\u201d he said. \u201cAll of them have grave concerns about how they are going to survive if this gets passed.\u201d\n\nAfter the White House session, Donovan said he welcomed the meeting with Trump but had not reached a final decision: \u201cWe\u2019ll know on Thursday. Some of these things have to be addressed.\u201d\n\nOne surprising holdout was Rep. Lou Barletta (R-Pa.), who was among the first House members to endorse Trump and has emerged as one of his most stalwart backers. But he is a hard-liner on illegal immigration and cited the issue Tuesday in opposing the bill.\n\nThe tax credits offered under the GOP plan, he said, could be claimed by individuals who are not \u201clawfully in this country and eligible to receive them.\u201d\n\n\u201cI would have a hard time explaining to families in the 11th District . . . why they should be helping to pay for the health expenses of someone who broke the law to get here and has no right to those federal dollars,\u201d he said.\n\nBut it was Trump\u2019s warning to Meadows that sent the sharpest message Tuesday. \u201cHe was kidding around \u2014 I think,\u201d said Rep. Harold Rogers (R-Ky.), a bill supporter.\n\nWhite House press secretary Sean Spicer said later in the day: \u201cMark Meadows is a longtime, early supporter of the president. He had some fun at his expense this morning during the conference meeting.\u201d\n\nAsked whether Trump believed that Republicans who opposed the bill would be damaged at the ballot box, Spicer answered: \u201cI think they\u2019ll probably pay a price at home.\u201d\n\nSpicer explained that statement was not a threat but \u201ca political reality.\u201d\n\nMeadows told reporters that he had a \u201csincere and deep friendship\u201d with Trump and appreciated the many hours of negotiation that were involved in the package.
