(Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p> <span class=\"dateline\">HAGERHILL, Ky.</span> \u2014 Chad Trador was, like many people here, a onetime coal miner struggling to find work. He, his three children and his wife had stayed afloat in a tough economy for years, but after he was laid off from his job managing a convenience store, the unemployment epidemic in this region appeared to have finally reached him, too.</p> <p>\u201cThe best opportunities I had were another convenience store, maybe as a clerk, making minimum wage,\u201d said Trador, 43, reflecting on last year. \u201cAnd then I heard that radio ad.\u201d</p> <p>The intensive 33-week job training program being advertised \u2014 TechHire Eastern Kentucky \u2014 promised to teach him computer coding from scratch. It would even pay him decent money while he learned. Trador signed up, and he is on track to complete the training in April, when<b> </b>he will emerge with a job as an Apple iOS developer.</p> <p>Like many such programs and infrastructure projects here in Eastern Kentucky and across Appalachia, the job training course has the federal government\u2019s fingerprints all over it. The Appalachian Regional Commission, an independent federal agency, helped jump-start it last year with a multiagency $2.75 million grant to a state organization that developed it.</p> <p>But after decades of work, the commission\u2019s future is in doubt, with the Trump administration\u2019s 2018 budget proposal threatening to eliminate funding for the commissions and other rural development endeavors. Voters in this part of the country, which overwhelmingly supports President Trump, could be disproportionately affected if that happens.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/wonk/wp/2017/03/16/if-youre-a-poor-person-in-america-trumps-budget-is-not-for-you/?utm_term=.b184775de30c\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">If you\u2019re a poor person in America, Trump\u2019s budget is not for you</a>]</i> </p> <p>Trador, who voted for Trump, said the cuts are a bad idea. The job training program\u2019s funding is secure for several more years, but the absence of further investment, he said, would<b> </b>exacerbate the region\u2019s deep poverty.<b></b> </p> <p>\u201cI think it\u2019s horrible. I think that maybe there are some things that don\u2019t need to be funded, but don\u2019t cut things that are working like the Appalachian Regional Commission and the work that they\u2019ve done,\u201d Trador said.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-left\" style=\"width:300px;\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img alt=\"\" class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1024w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky01.jpg?uuid=yW-Plg42EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky01.jpg?uuid=yW-Plg42EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky01.jpg?uuid=yW-Plg42EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky01.jpg?uuid=yW-Plg42EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Chad Trador, with his dog Tinker Bell, is a former coal miner from Hagerhill, Ky., who has benefited from a federally funded program teaching him how to write computer code. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>As a political statement, Trump\u2019s budget proposal delighted many small-government conservatives, whose long-held wish for a more focused, less-wasteful government appeared ready to be fulfilled. As a practical political matter, though, the proposed budget\u2019s cuts to pocketbook programs such as this one have few friends in Congress, where members\u2019 political stock often rises and falls based on what they can do for constituents. The result will likely be a final federal budget that bears little resemblance to the one Trump proposed.</p> <p>In a statement last week, Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell (R-Ky.) said he would not allow any cuts to the Appalachian Regional Commission, pitting the powerful Republican against a Trump budget priority and opening the door for fellow GOP lawmakers to do the same. <b></b>U.S. House Appropriations Committee Chairman Harold Rogers (R-Ky.) said he understands that Congress and the White House \u201chave a responsibility\u201d to reduce the deficit. But he worries that cuts to such programs could hurt his Eastern Kentucky district.</p> <p>\u201cI am disappointed that many of the reductions and eliminations proposed in the president\u2019s skinny budget are draconian, careless and counterproductive,\u201d said Rogers, who was first elected in 1981 and is Kentucky\u2019s longest-serving member of Congress.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2010/11/22/AR2010112207049.html\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">From the archives: Sen. Mitch McConnell\u2019s earmark power credited for revitalizing Louisville</a>]</i> </p> <p>Because so much of the Appalachian commission\u2019s budget \u2014 $146 million in 2016 \u2014 goes toward infrastructure projects, many of the people living in communities that have benefited from the funding are unaware of its impact. In recent years, using a community development block grant from the Department of Housing and Urban Development and a commission grant, Frenchburg has received a combined $1\u00a0million to develop a senior citizens center and a regional meal-assistance kitchen.</p> <p>HUD\u2019s community development grants also would disappear with Trump\u2019s proposed budget.</p> <p>\u201cReally? Wow. So now what?\u201d Collins said. \u201cThe government shouldn\u2019t take it away. This place is below the poverty level. There is nothing here, and people need something to stay out of drugs.\u201d</p> <p>Collins, 45, who draws disability benefits, became emotional as she spoke about her own involvement with drugs; she estimates she has known about 30 people who have died from opioid abuse. A Trump supporter, Collins said she could not vote for him because she has a felony drug conviction. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <div class=\"clear\"></div> </div> <p>But some of Trump\u2019s supporters in the region appear willing to accept development funding cuts because they believe Trump\u2019s broader agenda will help revitalize the region. The president has promised he will bring the coal economy back to life by cutting industry regulations, though experts have largely dismissed the effect such actions would have on jobs.</p> <p>\u201cAs we speak, we are preparing new executive actions to save our coal industry and to save our wonderful coal miners from continuing to be put out of work,\u201d Trump told a crowd in Louisville on Monday. \u201cThe miners are coming back.\u201d</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky09.jpg?uuid=6uGgAA5BEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky09.jpg?uuid=6uGgAA5BEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky09.jpg?uuid=6uGgAA5BEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky09.jpg?uuid=6uGgAA5BEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">The locker room of a shuttered coal operation sits abandoned in Hi Hat. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p channel=\"!Daily\">David Conn, 49, of West Liberty, Ky., said he is hopeful Trump can deliver. Conn, a coal miner, said that the region\u2019s problems hinge on the loss of coal jobs. He doesn\u2019t think that will be fixed with federal funding.</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">\u201cIt\u2019s not Trump\u2019s fault if the money\u2019s not there. Somebody\u2019s got to say enough is enough,\u201d Conn said. \u201cPeople on low income that may be abusing medication .\u2009.\u2009. sometimes a line\u2019s got to be drawn. I still stand behind him with that.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"!Daily\">West Liberty last year benefited from more than $250,000 in Appalachian Regional Commission funding, which went into improvements to the area\u2019s water infrastructure.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/trump-budget-asks-for-6-billion-in-hud-cuts-drops-development-grants/2017/03/15/1b157338-09a0-11e7-b77c-0047d15a24e0_story.html?utm_term=.8e5d65be96fa\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Trump budget asks for $6 billion in HUD cuts, drops development grants</a>]</i> </p> <p>Johnny Kouns, of rural Inez, Ky., voted for Trump and remains confident that the president can help redevelop the Appalachian economy. Kouns, 59, worked in coal fields until 2010. When the industry began to contract, he had to take an early retirement.</p> <p>\u201cI think he\u2019s doing pretty well considering what he had to take on,\u201d he said. \u201cBut it\u2019s not all going to bloom overnight. There\u2019s a lot of work to be done, and there\u2019s a lot of things coming at him.\u201d</p> <p>The federal government, through the Appalachian commission, provided funding for the Roy F. Collier Community Center in town to expand its programing with the Dream Discovery Center. The center will promote science and technology education.</p> <p>Many in the region share the view that coal can be revitalized, and while Trador agreed that perhaps some coal jobs could be brought back, he said the region needs to diversify its economy. And he takes issue with people using food assistance dollars on junk food.</p> <p>But what about spending on infrastructure?</p> <p>\u201cNot here,\u201d he said. \u201cIt\u2019s going to get worse around here. She restored it and turned it into a once-a-week bakery. (She\u2019s an internal medicine doctor by trade and has since hired employees to keep the bakery open most of the week. \u201cWe\u2019ve created jobs!\u201d) She has become an advocate for revitalizing the entire downtown as a member of the town council, to significant effect. Walking through Mount Sterling, King spoke excitedly as she pointed to several new shops that have joined hers on the street.</p> <p>\u201cThe only thing that was downtown was banks and lawyers,\u201d she said, pointing around. \u201cAnd this used to be all liquor and bars.\u201d</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky03.jpg?uuid=cgAnig46EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky03.jpg?uuid=cgAnig46EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky03.jpg?uuid=cgAnig46EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Others/Images/2017-03-21/Kentucky03.jpg?uuid=cgAnig46EeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Danielle King is the owner of the Spoonful of Sugar bakery and coffee shop in Mount Sterling, Ky. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>Mount Sterling has been successful in cleaning up its main street. And it has benefited greatly from federal dollars: There are apartments for the elderly and disabled funded with HUD housing grants, and a $1 million grant in 2010 helped clean up a blighted neighborhood to the north, which had become a center for crime and drug activity.</p> <p>About a mile away, Valerie Scott checked on the newly installed gym floor at the Dubois Community Center, which she and a community board have helped revitalize through commission and community development grant money to serve a largely African American community in the surrounding neighborhood. She said Dubois closed several years ago because of structural problems that rendered it unsafe, leaving the neighborhood without a place to gather.</p> <p>\u201cThat was pretty hard for our community because it was utilized a whole lot. Scott hopes another grant might allow the downstairs to become an educational space.</p> <p>King is eager to accelerate the success Mount Sterling has seen.
