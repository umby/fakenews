first budget Thursday, with defense hawks saying the proposed hike in Pentagon spending wasn\u2019t big enough, while rural conservatives and others attacked plans to cut a wide range of federal agencies and programs.</p> <p>The bad mood among Republican critics was tempered by a consensus that the president\u2019s budget wasn\u2019t going very far on Capitol Hill, where lawmakers reminded everybody that they ultimately control the nation\u2019s purse strings. </p> <p>\u201cWhile we have a responsibility to reduce our federal deficit, I am disappointed that many of the reductions and eliminations proposed in the president\u2019s skinny budget are draconian, careless and counterproductive,\u201d Rep. Hal Rogers (R-Ky.) the former chairman of the House Appropriations Committee, said in a <a href=\"https://halrogers.house.gov/press-releases?ID=24250FDA-5BF7-4A2D-BD7C-8D4579A5AA1A\" shape=\"rect\">statement</a>. \u201cWe will certainly review this budget proposal, but Congress ultimately has the power of the purse.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cWe\u2019ve not had our chance yet,\u201d he added in an interview.</p> <p>Rogers was one of several GOP lawmakers to dismiss Trump\u2019s budget as a pie-in-the sky wish list with little hope of surviving negotiations in Congress. Most Republicans gave passing support to Trump\u2019s general goal of increasing defense spending while reducing costs elsewhere in the budget. But none of the Republicans interviewed would embrace the specific White House blueprint.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"109184\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Five reactions to Trump\u2019s budget\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58cae49ae4b059a9e4267321\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-16-2017/t_1489692617939_name_20170315_trump_thumbnail.jpg\" data-uuid=\"1828df74-0a7d-11e7-bd19-fd3afa0f7e2a\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"T6xJDJAEMvU\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Bastien Inzaurralde,Jayne Orenstein,Alice Li/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>\u201cI\u2019ve never seen a president\u2019s budget proposal not revised substantially,\u201d said Sen. Charles E. Grassley (R-Iowa). \u201cAs a member of the Budget Committee, I\u2019ll carefully scrutinize and assess priorities as the president has with his proposal.\u201d</p> <p>The upcoming budget clash between Congress and the president has emerged as another obstacle in Trump\u2019s young presidency. Just this week, a federal judge in Hawaii <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/social-issues/lawyers-face-off-on-trump-travel-ban-in-md-court-wednesday-morning/2017/03/14/b2d24636-090c-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html?hpid=hp_hp-banner-low_travelban-1010am%3Ahomepage%2Fstory&amp;utm_term=.91dcaef81c08\" shape=\"rect\">issued</a> a sweeping freeze of Trump\u2019s latest travel order. </p> <p>The House GOP plan to revise the <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/powerpost/obamacare-revision-moves-to-next-step-thursday-house-budget-committee/2017/03/15/31fa1ec8-09f5-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html\" shape=\"rect\">Affordable Care Act</a> is embattled, as is Trump\u2019s push to build a <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/powerpost/republicans-skeptical-about-paying-for-wall-as-trump-prepares-to-release-budget/2017/03/15/dd2b5848-099b-11e7-b77c-0047d15a24e0_story.html\" shape=\"rect\">wall</a> on the U.S.-Mexico border. His tax reform and infrastructure plans have yet to get off the ground. </p> <p>As he passes the halfway mark of his first 100 days, Trump is under increasing pressure to show that he can make good on his ambitious promises.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/the-cost-of-failure-on-health-care-it-may-be-the-rest-of-trumps-agenda/2017/03/14/f502a846-08be-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html?utm_term=.8875dd68183c\" shape=\"rect\">The cost of failure on health care? It may be the rest of Trump\u2019s agenda</a>.]</i> </p> <p>Some of Trump\u2019s closest allies said his budget has virtually no chance in Congress, pointing to what they expect to be vociferous opposition from Democrats.</p> <p>\u201cThe left is not going to let him decrease nondefense discretionary to the extent that he wants to,\u201d Rep. Ra\u00fal R. Labrador (R-Idaho) told reporters on Thursday. \u201cWe\u2019re going to have to find a different way to balance the budget.\u201d</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"analysis\" data-duration=\"222912\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Trump's budget plan, by the numbers\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58c9d71be4b01a7d5042be4d\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/03-16-2017/t_1489639615511_name_Botsford170315Trump12785.JPG\" data-uuid=\"77ca2c36-09dc-11e7-bd19-fd3afa0f7e2a\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"bj9XSgeEc4Q\"> <script src=\"//js.washingtonpost.com/video/resources/env/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\"> (Jenny Starrs/The Washington Post)</span> </div> </div> <p>It is not uncommon for Congress to disagree with some priorities in a White House budget. But the blueprint risks putting GOP lawmakers on a collision course with Trump over demands for spending cuts they cannot deliver. Even those fiscal conservatives who do want to cut spending don\u2019t necessarily think slashing major domestic programs is the answer.</p> <p>In the past, the White House has worked directly with congressional leaders to agree on an overall spending number for the whole government, which is then passed to Appropriations Committee members to divvy up among different departments and agencies.</p> <p>White House budget director Mick Mulvaney defended the president\u2019s proposal on Thursday, acknowledging that the next challenge will be to sell it to lawmakers.</p> <p>\u201cThe message we\u2019re sending to the Hill is, we want more money for the things the president talked about, defense being the top one, national security,\u201d he said. \u201cAnd we don\u2019t want to add to the budget deficit. If Congress has another way to do that, we\u2019re happy to talk to them about it.\u201d</p> <p>One reason for the tepid response on the Hill is lawmakers are mired in high-level negotiations to craft an interim budget before the current one expires on April 28. Talks so far have centered on sticking to the two-year bipartisan spending agreement with an overall spending level of $1.07 trillion for 2017.</p> <p>Republicans expect the spending targets for 2018 to stay about the same, according to several aides familiar with the negotiations.</p> <p>Trump has proposed spending more next year \u2014 upward of $1.15 trillion \u2014 by tapping into a separate war fund account as well as other funds.</p> <p>Many lawmakers also want to increase spending, but doing so would require a bipartisan agreement. Republicans have a slim 52-to-48 majority in the Senate, and any spending deal will require support from Democrats who will not back increased defense spending without corresponding hikes in domestic spending.<br/> </p> <p>Democrats and some Republicans are worried that the $54\u00a0billion hike in defense spending will cripple the operations of 18 other federal agencies \u2014 most prominently the Environmental Protection Agency and the State Department. </p> <p>Several Republicans also said they were wary of the deep cuts Trump proposed for foreign aid.</p> <p>\u201cAs General [Jim] Mattis said prophetically, slashing the diplomatic efforts will cause them to have to buy more ammunition,\u201d Rogers said, referring to the defense secretary. \u201cThere is two sides to fighting the problem that we\u2019re in: There is military and then there\u2019s diplomatic. And we can\u2019t afford to dismantle the diplomatic half of that equation.\u201d</p> <p>Rogers predicted the foreign aid cuts \u201cwill not stand,\u201d adding: \u201cThis too shall pass.\u201d</p> <p>Conservatives are also skeptical that Trump\u2019s budget will significantly reduce the deficit. The only way to accomplish that, they argue, is to overhaul entitlement programs like Social Security and Medicare.<b> </b>Those programs, along with other mandatory spending, help make up nearly two-thirds of all federal spending while Trump\u2019s proposal only targets a third of it.</p> <p>But Trump promised during the campaign that those programs, including Medicaid, would not be touched.</p> <p>\u201cI can tell you that I brought up entitlement reform [with Trump] a week or so ago, [and] the pushback was a little stronger than I expected,\u201d said Rep. Mark Meadows (R-N.C.), chairman of the House Freedom Caucus. \u201cIt\u2019s going to take a lot more encouragement in terms of actually tackling entitlement reform.\u201d</p> <p>Republicans also worried that some of Trump\u2019s cuts would undermine critical environmental programs in their states. Sen. Rob Portman (R-Ohio) said he plans to oppose major cuts to the $300 million Great Lakes Restoration Initiative. </p> <p>\u201cI\u2019m committed to continuing to do everything I can to protect and preserve Lake Erie, including preserving this critical program and its funding,\u201d Portman said in a statement.</p> <p>The same could be said for Republicans from rural and agriculture-heavy states that stand to lose big under Trump\u2019s proposed cuts. House Agriculture Committee Chairman K. Michael Conaway (R-Tex.) raised concerns that farmers could be hit hard at a time when farm income is already down 50 percent compared with four years ago.</p> <p>Agriculture cuts are a particularly sensitive issue because periodically lawmakers spend months, if not years, hammering out the details of a comprehensive farm bill. </p> <p>\u201cAgriculture has done more than its fair share,\u201d Conaway said in a statement. \u201cThe bottom line is this is the start of a longer, larger process. It is a proposal, not THE budget.\u201d </p> <p>One of the greatest pockets of opposition to the Trump blueprint can be found among defense hawks. Defense and national security programs would see the biggest boost in funding under the president\u2019s budget.</p> <p>But these military-minded members are not satisfied, accusing the president of everything from accounting gimmicks to playing fast and loose with the lives of soldiers in war zones to follow through on his campaign promises.</p> <p>Republicans have long contended that defense cuts introduced during the last administration damaged the military and hampered its war readiness. Many supported Trump\u2019s call for a dramatic increase in military investment \u2014 but they don\u2019t believe this goes far enough.</p> <p>\u201cThe Administration\u2019s budget request is not enough to repair that damage and to rebuild the military as the president has discussed,\u201d House Armed Services Committee Chairman Mac Thornberry (R-Tex.) said in a statement, noting \u201cserious shortcomings\u201d that \u201cwill worsen without immediate action.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cIt is morally wrong to task someone with a mission for which they are not fully prepared and fully supported with the best weapons and equipment this nation can provide,\u201d he added.</p> <p>Trump\u2019s budget puts $603 billion toward defense \u2014 but Thornberry and Senate Armed Services Committee Chairman John McCain (R-Ariz.) are both asking for $640 billion. </p> <p>They also stress that the $54 billion proposed by Trump is misleading because it is only $19 billion more than what the country spent on
