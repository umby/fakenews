of the Washington Monument to turn Earth Day into an homage to science. After four hours of speeches and musical performances, they marched down Constitution Avenue to the foot of Capitol Hill, chanting \u201cBuild labs, not walls!\u201d and \u201cHey, Trump, have you heard, you can\u2019t silence every nerd!\u201d</p> <p> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/speaking-of-science/wp/2017/04/20/why-scientists-are-marching-on-washington-and-more-than-400-other-cities/?utm_term=.e8540be0b26c\" shape=\"rect\">The March for Science</a> began as a notion <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/speaking-of-science/wp/2017/04/21/the-march-for-science-began-with-this-persons-throwaway-line-on-reddit/?utm_term=.c07e09c386cc\" shape=\"rect\">batted around online on Reddit</a> after the Women\u2019s March on Washington, which was held Jan.\u00a021, the day after President Trump\u2019s inauguration. The idea snowballed after it was endorsed by numerous mainstream science organizations, which vowed that it would not be a partisan event. It eventually became a global phenomenon, held in more than 600 cities on six continents \u2014 and cheered on by scientists on a seventh, Antarctica.</p> <p>\u201cWe are at a critical juncture. Science is under attack,\u201d said Cara Santa Maria, a science communicator who is one of several emcees of the four-hour rally that kicked off at 10\u00a0a.m. \u201cThe very idea of evidence and logic and reason is being threatened by individuals and interests with the power to do real harm.\u201d</p> <p>She went on: \u201cWe\u2019re gathered here today to fight for science. [The crowd cheered.] We\u2019re gathered to fight for education. [Cheer.] To fight for knowledge. [Cheer.] And to fight for planet Earth.\u201d [Cheer.]</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/to-your-health/wp/2017/04/22/surgeon-general-vivek-h-murthy-removed-by-trump-administration-replaced-by-deputy-for-now/?utm_term=.435e113d32b6\" shape=\"rect\">Surgeon general is removed by Trump administration</a>]</i> </p> <p>She was followed by the musician Questlove, who said \u201cmany people\u201d are refusing to follow scientific facts, and he pointed toward the White House. \u201cThat guy over there,\u201d he said in a whisper. Still, she said, she has been trying not to get too political.</p> <p>\u201cEven people who voted Republican can still get behind clean air for their grandchildren,\u201d she said.</p> <p>Next to her, 44-year-old Jeffrey C. Science helps everyone.\u201d</p> <p>Three federal scientists, approached by a reporter, refused to give their names for fear of repercussions at work.</p> <p>Another person was dressed as the <a href=\"https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hA4TiL9L0hc\" shape=\"rect\">Muppet \u201cBeaker\u201d</a> and, when interviewed, would say only \u201cMeep.\u201d</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"other\" data-duration=\"103765\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"Questlove: 'We need to make sure science belongs to the people.'\" data-is-truth-teller=\"0\" data-max-height=\"-1\" data-max-width=\"-1\" data-object-id=\"58fb5b7ee4b07c67ff0906e0\" data-show-caption=\"1\" data-show-endscreen=\"1\" data-show-posttv-overlay=\"1\" data-show-promo=\"http://s3.amazonaws.com/posttv-thumbnails-prod/04-22-2017/t_1492872011093_name_20170422_questlove_marchforscience.jpg\" data-uuid=\"2c1d0712-2760-11e7-928e-3624539060e8\" data-variants=\"0\" data-video-360=\"0\" data-youtube-id=\"drqObMEHTZk\"> <script src=\"https://d1pz6dax0t5mop.cloudfront.net/sandbox/powaBoot.js\"></script> <script src=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-stat/posttv/WapoVideo/dist/WapoVideoEmbed.min.js\"></script> </div> <div class=\"inline-video-caption\"> <span class=\"pb-caption\">(Earth Day Network)</span> </div> </div> <p>Next to Beaker, however, was Erik Molvar, director of the Western Watersheds Project, who had traveled from Wyoming. Molvar is a sage grouse expert who studies the impact of livestock on grouse habitat. Politicians supporting the livestock industry ignore his research into cheatgrass, which is highly flammable and leads to damaging wildfires, he said. \u201cLivestock spread cheatgrass like mosquitoes spread the Zika virus,\u201d he said.</p> <p>Emily Fink, 28, and Kayla Denson, 29, are biomedical researchers who drove seven hours from Buffalo to attend the march, and they said they fear the Trump administration\u2019s proposed budget cuts will imperil their careers.</p> <p>\u201cIt feels like we\u2019re getting our foot in the door right as the door is closing on us,\u201d Fink said.</p> <p>Fink brought several copies of her r\u00e9sum\u00e9 to the march and held up a neon sign that read, \u201cAre you looking for a highly motivated post doc? Ask for CV.\u201d She thought the march might be a good networking opportunity, though so far no one had asked for a r\u00e9sum\u00e9.</p> <p>\u201cI\u2019m a meteoriticist,\u201d said Conel Alexander, 56, as he wrote down his occupation to make sure he spelled it correctly. He studies meteorites and was with a group from the Carnegie Institution for Science. \u201cMost people just think I\u2019m a meteorologist,\u201d he said.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp/2017/04/22/historians-say-the-march-for-science-is-pretty-unprecedented/\" shape=\"rect\">Historians say the March for Science is \u2018pretty unprecedented\u2019</a>]</i> </p> <p>Speaking of meteorology: Favorable weather forecasts took a turn by week\u2019s end, and this turned out to be a soggy Saturday in which <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/capital-weather-gang/wp/2017/04/22/d-c-area-forecast-damp-and-dreary-this-weekend-with-more-of-the-same-early-week/?utm_term=.bad9897c3ac0\" shape=\"rect\">periodic showers</a> gave way to heavy late-morning downpours.</p> <p>But many people present are veterans of protests. Grace Francis, 33, and Ron Frerker, 36, of Washington, said they\u2019ve been to a series of protests since Trump took office. Francis, a former public school teacher who conducts special-education research at George Mason University, said when she heard about the science march she asked her daughters Sage and Eleanor, who are 5 and 3 years old, respectively, if they wanted to go.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s important for them to see that not just our family feels this way,\u201d she said.</p> <p>The two girls were dressed in koala costumes, and holding signs that read \u201c DeVos is un-koala-fied\u201d and \u201ceveryone deserves a Koala-ity education.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cWhat do we feel about schools,\u201d Francis asked Sage.</p> <p>\u201cBad,\u201d Sage answered.</p> <p>\u201cBecause everybody deserves to have a .\u2009.\u2009.\u00a0\u201d</p> <p>\u201cEducation.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cWhat kind of education?\u201d</p> <p>\u201cA good one.\u201d</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-html\"> <blockquote class=\"twitter-tweet\" lang=\"en\"><p dir=\"ltr\" lang=\"en\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/AmerChemSociety/status/855800724751601665\"></a></p></blockquote> <script async=\"\" charset=\"utf-8\" src=\"//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js\"></script> </div> <p>Maryam Zaringhalam and Kelly Fleming, both 28, came with poster board signs they\u2019d made at an event Friday night with the group 500 Women Scientists. Zaringhalam, a molecular biologist, and Fleming, a chemical engineer, had been concerned about the way diversity issues were dealt with by the march organizing committee.</p> <p>\u201cBut I thought, people are going to be taking pictures at the march and this is what I want them to see,\u201d Zaringhalam said. \u201cI want them to see someone who looks like me.\u201d</p> <p>Zaringhalam is an Iranian American who was in Iran when Trump issued his executive order on immigration. Although she is a U.S. citizen, Zaringhalam worried she would not be allowed back into the country.</p> <p>At their sign-making event Friday night, a passerby had asked what the women were doing. When they told him, he responded, \u201cYou don\u2019t look like scientists.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI think he thought he was flirting,\u201d Fleming said, making a face. Zaringhalam came up with the motto for her sign: \u201cThis is what a scientist looks like.\u201d</p> <p>Craig Fryer, 47, marched down Constitution Avenue alongside several of his colleagues from the University of Maryland School of Public Health, all wearing T-shirts that read \u201cBlack Scientists Matter.\u201d Fryer, a behavioral scientist who studies substance abuse, carried a sign that proclaimed \u201cBlack Scientists Speaking Truth to Power.\u201d He said he and his colleagues are concerned about racial disparities in funding for research.</p> <p>\u201cWe need increased funding, not budget cuts, and we need an equal opportunity to be funded,\u201d Fryer said.</p> <p>Carol Trosset, 57, an anthropologist at Carleton College, traveled to Washington from Northfield, Minn., having never been to a political rally before. I\u2019ll wear mom\u2019s lab coat,\u201d Trosset said. Her mother was also a naturalist, collecting data at their home in Cincinnati, recording when birds would arrive and flowers would bloom. Trosset has begun analyzing her mother\u2019s data and sees clear signs of a warming climate.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/speaking-of-science/wp/2017/04/22/here-are-some-of-the-best-signs-from-the-march-for-science-in-d-c/\" shape=\"rect\">Here are some of the best signs from the March for Science</a>]</i> </p> <p>Brooke Hardesty, 16, waited nervously in front of the science tent. She was looking for the other \u201cScience Teens,\u201d fellow high school students who are slated to speak at the rally. From far-flung cities around the country \u2014 Hardesty is from Buckeye, Ariz. \u2014 they\u2019d previously communicated only through social media and Skype. On Saturday, Hardesty met her fellow nerds for the first time in person.</p> <p>\u201cI\u2019ve never been around so many other people who are excited about science,\u201d she said.</p> <p>The teens, chatting awkwardly, discussed who they\u2019re most excited to see \u2014 \u201cBill Nye!\u201d they said in unison \u2014 and talked about how surreal this experience has been.</p> <p>\u201cI\u2019m kind of surprised they let a bunch of teenagers do this,\u201d said Sam Rosenberg, 17, of Gaithersburg.</p> <p>\u201cWe\u2019re even verified on Twitter!\u201d Hardesty agreed.</p> <p>The lineup for the rally includes some prominent names in science and environmentalism, including Nye, chief executive of the Planetary Society and an honorary co-chair of the march. But the organizers wanted to erase the stereotype of science as a stodgy enterprise dominated by older white men, and the lineup includes speakers from a broad range of ages, backgrounds and expertise.</p> <p>They included Taylor Richardson, a 13-year-old aspiring astronaut who raised $17,000 this year to send other girls to see the film \u201cHidden Figures\u201d; chemist Mary Jo Ondrechen, a member of the Mohawk Nation and chair of the board of the American Indian Science and Engineering Society; and Gallaudet University biologist Caroline Solomon, who is deaf.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-html\"> <blockquote class=\"twitter-tweet\" lang=\"en\"><p dir=\"ltr\" lang=\"en\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/JaneGoodallInst/status/855597289208983552\"></a></p></blockquote> <script async=\"\" charset=\"utf-8\" src=\"//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js\"></script> </div> <p>\u201cWe need more girls in computer science. We need more diversity in computer science,\u201d said speaker Kavya Kopparapu, 16, a student at Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology and founder of the Girls Computing League.</p> <p>\u201cIn my future career, I don\u2019t want to be known as a girl that happens to be a computer scientist. I want to be known as a computer scientist that happens to be a girl,\u201d she told the crowd.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/speaking-of-science/wp/2017/04/21/the-march-for-science-began-with-this-persons-throwaway-line-on-reddit/\" shape=\"rect\">The Redditor who kicked the march off with a throwaway line</a>]</i> </p> <p>No politicians were given speaking roles, though some reportedly planned to show up for the march.</p> <p>Some scientists in recent weeks have said they worried the march would politicize the broader scientific enterprise and signal an alignment with left-leaning ideologies. There is no Planet B.\u201d</p> <p>Molly Jung, 29, a doctoral candidate at Johns Hopkins, echoed that view: \u201cIt\u2019s time for scientists to get out of the ivory towers and get the message out.\u201d</p> <p>The Washington march may have been the biggest gathering \u2014 organizers received a permit for up to 75,000 people \u2014 but there were similar events in more than 600 cities on six continents. Seven researchers in Antarctica went on Twitter to express their support for the march. Thousands of people gathered in Sydney, Australia, according to <a href=\"http://www.smh.com.au/technology/sci-tech/thousands-march-across-australia-for-science-and-reason-in-public-policy-20170422-gvqbbo.html\" shape=\"rect\">the Sydney Morning Herald</a>. Science advocates went on social media to post bulletins from marches in Austria, England and Malawi, among other places.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-html\"> <blockquote class=\"twitter-tweet\" lang=\"en\"><p dir=\"ltr\" lang=\"en\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/AlbertEinstein/status/855795188429717504\"></a></p></blockquote> <script async=\"\" charset=\"utf-8\" src=\"//platform.twitter.com/widgets.js\"></script> </div> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>Jia Naqvi, Kayla Epstein, Perry Stein, Martine Powers and Taylor Hartz contributed to this report.</p> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <b>Read more:</b> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/speaking-of-science/wp/2017/04/21/as-scientists-erupt-in-protest-a-volcanologist-runs-for-congress/?tid=a_inl&amp;utm_term=.b3d4cc61cb17\" shape=\"rect\">As scientists erupt in protest, a volcano researcher is running for Congress</a> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"http://wapo.st/2ovzfUg\" shape=\"rect\">U-Md. president: Why I march for science</a> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"http://wapo.st/2ovKpIF\" shape=\"rect\">Atom-smashing scientists are unnerved by harsh Trump budget</a> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/speaking-of-science/wp/2017/04/20/why-scientists-are-marching-on-washington-and-more-than-400-other-cities/\" shape=\"rect\">Why scientists are marching on Washington \u2014 and more than 600 other cities</a> </p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp/2017/04/21/here-come-back-to-back-science-and-climate-marches-organizers-insist-theres-no-competition/?utm_term=.409cdff34465\" shape=\"rect\">Here come back-to-back science and climate marches. People danced when Thomas Dolby took the stage to perform his 1982 blockbuster hit \u201cShe Blinded Me With Science.\u201d\n\nSome people wore lab coats.
