(Nick Otto/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <div class=\"pb-sig-line hasnt-headshot has-0-headshots hasnt-bio is-not-column\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/juliet-eilperin/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Juliet Eilperin</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-byline-tool-mobile\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/juliet-eilperin/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Juliet Eilperin</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:eilperinj@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Though shovels are ready, Trump officials delay grant for Caltrain upgrade'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> </span> <span class=\"pb-timestamp\" itemprop=\"datePublished\" content=\"2017-04-22T04:24-500\">April 22 at 4:24 PM</span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:eilperinj@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Though shovels are ready, Trump officials delay grant for Caltrain upgrade'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> <span class=\"tweet-authors\"><span class=\"pb-twitter-follow unprocessed\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/@eilperin\" class=\"twitter-follow-button\" data-show-count=\"false\" data-lang=\"en\">Follow @eilperin</a></span> </span> </div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p> <span class=\"dateline\">SAN JOSE \u2014</span> The railway shuttles 65,000 people a day between San Francisco and San Jose, its cars crammed with Silicon Valley workers tapping on sleek laptops and hoisting bikes into designated cars. But the signs of aging are unmistakable \u2014 1980s control panels devoid of digital technology, the dusting of sea-green foam that has escaped from the seat cushions and settled on the floor. </p> <p>All of that was supposed to change with the launch of a $2\u00a0billion upgrade, underwritten in part by a $647\u00a0million grant from the Federal Transit Administration approved days before President Barack Obama left office. But then the Trump administration arrived, and within a month the FTA informed Caltrain that it was \u201cdeferring a decision.\u201d</p> <p>The delay has infuriated California officials, who had hoped the long-awaited project would mesh nicely with President Trump\u2019s call for fresh spending on the nation\u2019s aging infrastructure. But in this era of distrust and polarization, an otherwise popular initiative has become a GOP target, seen as a pet project of the former president.</p> <p> <b></b>The move to shelve the grant is reverberating far beyond the Golden State, alarming officials in cities across the nation. The White House wants to slice nearly $1\u00a0billion from the transportation budget this year, with the cuts aimed primarily at urban transit projects such as the Purple Line in Maryland\u2019s Montgomery and Prince George\u2019s counties.</p> <p>More cuts may be in store: Trump\u2019s budget request for fiscal 2018 ignores two major New York City projects: an extension of the Second Avenue subway line and a new train tunnel under the Hudson River. In a note to Congress last month, the White House budget office wrote that when it comes to improvements to Caltrain and the D.C. Metro system, \u201clocalities should fund these localized projects.\u201d</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-graphic-linked\"> <div class=\"photo-wrapper\"> <a href=\"http://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/politics/trump-rolling-back-obama-rules/\"><img class=\"unprocessed\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/24/National-Politics/Advance/Graphics/trump-rolling-back-obama-promo-2300.jpg?uuid=gosyKhCmEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/trafficandcommuting/federal-money-to-build-purple-line-in-question-under-trump-budget-plan/2017/03/16/d9023ab4-09ac-11e7-b77c-0047d15a24e0_story.html?utm_term=.26c6edfc6421\" shape=\"rect\">Trump\u2019s budget cuts put the Purple Line\u2019s development at risk</a>]</i> </p> <p>Christopher Leinberger, chair of George Washington University\u2019s Center for Real Estate and Urban Analysis, said the cuts suggest Trump is \u201cplaying to the base,\u201d because he got much less support in urban areas than in \u201cdrivable suburban locations.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cThis is about pure politics,\u201d Leinberger said.</p> <p>Last month, the American Public Transportation Association<b> </b>sent a letter to Transportation Secretary Elaine Chao calling the Caltrain delay \u201cconcerning.\u201d In more than two decades, the association wrote, \u201cno project has failed to secure final signature after successfully meeting evaluation criteria.\u201d</p> <p>Transportation officials were noncommittal, saying the project would be considered along with other priorities for fiscal 2018.</p> <p>For Caltrain general manager and chief executive Jim Hartnett, whose company started planning for the upgrade in the late 1990s, the delay is disheartening. The project, which would finance a switch from diesel engines to high-performance electric commuter rail trains, has already received $73\u00a0million in federal appropriations but cannot tap the cash without the Transportation Department\u2019s approval.</p> <p>\u201cWe are more than shovel-ready,\u201d Hartnett said. \u201cOur shovel is in the ground and ready to turn.\u201d</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-graphic-embedded-296\"> <img src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/22/National-Enterprise/Graphics/2300-TRUMPTRAIN0423.jpg?uuid=qUN57ieQEeeSjjYkU5Bg6A\"/><br/> </div> <p>At Caltrain\u2019s San Jose Diridon Station last month, company officials pointed out the signs of wear and tear on a railway system that was inaugurated during Abraham Lincoln\u2019s presidency.</p> <p>More than two-thirds of its locomotives date to 1985; more than half of its passenger trains are that old. There is no diagnostic software. \u201cWhen something goes wrong, we put in a part and hope for the best,\u201d said Caltrain\u2019s director of rail operations, Joe Navarro.</p> <p>A few weeks ago, half of the red-and-silver Caltrain signs started peeling off the side of a passenger car at the South San Francisco stop, prompting a half-hour delay. Doing a \u201cmidlife\u201d overhaul, which extends a locomotive or passenger car\u2019s life by an additional decade, costs $2.2\u00a0million per locomotive and $1.5\u00a0million per car.</p> <p>\u201cWe\u2019re the second-oldest railroad west of the Mississippi, and we have advanced that far beyond the steam engine,\u201d Navarro said. \u201cWe\u2019re running diesel.\u201d</p> <p>Caltrain first contemplated an electric rail line two decades ago, but the idea has taken on new urgency as Silicon Valley has boomed and ridership has doubled since 2005.</p> <p>Officials approached the FTA about the project in 2001, while also tapping local funding sources, including money approved by Proposition 1A, a 2008 ballot measure intended to connect transit projects to the state\u2019s planned high-speed rail system.</p> <p>This annoyed Rep. Jeff Denham (R-Calif.), who chairs a key House Transportation and Infrastructure subcommittee. Denham has lobbied Chao to deny the grant because the new Caltrain cars would run slower than 220\u00a0mph, the rate that defines high-speed rail. He urged California Gov. Jerry Brown (D) to find a different source of state financing for Caltrain and then reapply for the federal money.</p> <p>\u201cI am supportive of Caltrain and the electrification project, but they have to be funded the right way,\u201d Denham said. \u201cI would expect any new administration to fund what their new transportation policy is going to be and what their priorities are.\u201d</p> <p>Brown, who met with Chao last month to discuss the grant, said of Denham in a phone interview: \u201cThat\u2019s called blackmail.\u201d</p> <p>Californians \u201cvoted for a bond issue\u201d for high-speed rail \u201cbut envisioned other projects\u201d using the cash, the governor said. \u201cTo go against it is the rawest, stupidest form of politics.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/powerpost/white-house-installs-political-aides-at-cabinet-agencies-to-be-trumps-eyes-and-ears/2017/03/19/68419f0e-08da-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html?utm_term=.b60aa9606a79\" shape=\"rect\">White House installs staff at Cabinet agencies to be Trump\u2019s eyes and ears</a>]</i> </p> <p>For the moment, Caltrain has obtained a four-month delay from its contractors in exchange for paying a penalty, meaning it could still proceed with the project if it gets an infusion of federal funds by June\u00a030.</p> <p>The electrification upgrade is expected to generate 4,700 jobs in more than a dozen states, including Utah, where the new trains would be manufactured. Caltrain officials have reached out to more than a dozen members of the House and Senate who represent areas that would benefit from the project.</p> <p>In an interview, Senate Finance Committee Chairman Orrin G. Hatch (R-Utah) said the project \u201cwould be very beneficial to Utah, no question.\u201d</p> <p>Although it may be challenging to free up the money, Hatch said, \u201cwe\u2019re going to do what we can to get that done.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\"></p> </article> <div class=\"clear\"></div> </div> </div> <div class=\"pb-container\"> <div class=\"moat-trackable pb-f-theme-normal pb-2
