class=\"article-body\"> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"cedd024bb665ea5d12a4fe0701f57e2a0fa9fe58\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13950.JPG?uuid=F-bHdh_PEee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13950.JPG?uuid=F-bHdh_PEee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13950.JPG?uuid=F-bHdh_PEee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13950.JPG?uuid=F-bHdh_PEee7WadMyvHQLw\"><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Gary Cohn, director of the National Economic Council, has found an edge within the Trump administration by hiring two dozen policy experts, most with government experience. (Jabin Botsford/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <div class=\"pb-sig-line hasnt-headshot has-0-headshots hasnt-bio is-not-column\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"/people/damian-paletta\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Damian Paletta</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-byline-tool-mobile\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"/people/damian-paletta\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Damian Paletta</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:Damian.Paletta@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Within Trump\u2019s inner circle, a moderate voice captures the president\u2019s ear'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> </span> <span class=\"pb-timestamp\" itemprop=\"datePublished\" content=\"2017-04-13T07:58-500\">April 13 at 7:58 PM</span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:Damian.Paletta@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Within Trump\u2019s inner circle, a moderate voice captures the president\u2019s ear'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> </div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p>As power struggles and ideological battles engulfed the White House, an unlikely player is exercising new influence on the direction of President Trump\u2019s administration.</p> <p>Gary Cohn, a former Goldman Sachs president, is capitalizing on his new position as director of Trump\u2019s National Economic Council to push a centrist vision and court bipartisan support on some of Trump\u2019s top agenda items such as tax reform and a $1\u00a0trillion infrastructure plan.<b></b> </p> <p>The growing strength of Cohn and like-minded moderates was on display this week as Trump reversed himself on several high-profile issues \u2014 including a less confrontational approach to China, an endorsement of government subsidies for exports and the current leadership of the Federal Reserve. The president\u2019s new positions move him much closer to the views of Cohn and others on Wall Street, not to mention mainstream Republicans and Democrats.</p> <p>It was the clearest sign yet that an alliance of moderates in the White House \u2014 including Cohn; senior adviser Jared Kushner, the president\u2019s son-in-law; and another influential Goldman Sachs alumnus, Dina Powell \u2014 is racking up successes in a battle over ideology and control with hardcore conservatives led by chief strategist Stephen K. Bannon, who held sway at the start of the administration.</p> <p>In a White House short on experienced personnel, Cohn has found an edge by hiring two dozen policy experts, most with government experience. His team produced detailed proposals on overhauling the tax code, rebuilding infrastructure, cutting back financial regulations and restructuring international trade deals. He is widely considered a future candidate to be chief of staff.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-left\" style=\"width:300px;\"> <a name=\"f32f06ac1e4e065d83c12b12a6260cd5ae9ad077\"></a> <img alt=\"\" class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1024w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13912.JPG?uuid=HCUsch_MEee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13912.JPG?uuid=HCUsch_MEee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13912.JPG?uuid=HCUsch_MEee7WadMyvHQLw\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/National-Politics/Images/Botsford170412Trump13912.JPG?uuid=HCUsch_MEee7WadMyvHQLw\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">From left, National Economic Council Director Gary Cohn, White House press secretary Sean Spicer and Dan Scavino, assistant to the president and director of White House social media, listen during a news conference this week at the White House. (Jabin Botsford/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>\u201cCohn might be a newbie to policy and Washington, but you have to give him credit for one thing,\u201d said Gene Sperling, who held Cohn\u2019s job during the Obama administration. \u201cWhile others seemed engaged in ideological and \u2018House of Cards\u2019-like staff warfare, he quietly and quickly focused on the first rule of governing: He hired some competent, professional staff at the NEC, and it has paid off for him.\u201d</p> <p>Cohn now finds himself in the awkward \u2014 and politically risky \u2014 position of being praised by Democrats but shunned by conservative allies of Trump who see the former Goldman Sachs executive as anathema to the values that got Trump elected.</p> <p>\u201cFrom a pure political perspective, I do not know if the White House appreciates how Gary Cohn is a liability with the Republican and conservative base, as well as the Republican Congress,\u201d said Sam Nunberg, a strategist on Trump\u2019s 2016 campaign. \u201cThe Trump White House will always be held in suspicion when you have someone who\u2019s consolidated full economic power in the White House who is also a liberal, New York Democrat.\u201d</p> <p>Cohn has been getting flak in the conservative media as he has risen in profile. Rush Limbaugh last week called him \u201ca very ideological liberal Democrat\u201d and a \u201ctrader at Goldman Sachs.\u201d He expressed concern that Cohn and his allies in the White House \u201care starting to have sway\u201d at Bannon\u2019s expense.</p> <p>Cohn, who declined to comment for this article, has given thousands of dollars to candidates from both parties, including President Barack Obama and former candidate Hillary Clinton.</p> <p>White House aides say Cohn has done well because Trump sees him, more than anything else, as a dealmaker. Cohn represents a bloc of White House officials who are working harder than before to court Democratic support for key parts of Trump\u2019s agenda, having seen the Republican Party splinter during the health-care debate.</p> <p>\u201cI\u2019m not a Democrat, and I\u2019m not a Republican,\u201d Cohn often says in meetings with business executives, according to two people familiar with his exchanges. \u201cI just want to get things done.\u201d</p><div></div> <p>People who have met with Cohn in his new role said they weren\u2019t aware of what his ideology was. He just seemed driven to forge agreements.</p> <p>That philosophy has led Cohn to show enthusiasm for ideas such as a new tax on carbon \u2014 a Democrat-friendly idea which would raise revenue to ease tax reform, a top presidential priority, while also helping to curb carbon emissions. The idea is ridiculed by many conservatives on Capitol Hill, and the White House rapidly distanced itself last week after word leaked that senior officials were studying the concept.</p> <p>\u201cI think the National Economic Council has done a terrible job,\u201d said Larry Kudlow, who was one of Trump\u2019s top economic advisers during the campaign. \u201cIt\u2019s the NEC\u2019s job to put a plan together and show the president options and make decisions. So far, I would say they are way behind the eight\u00a0ball.\u201d</p> <p>But even as the legislative agenda struggles to gain momentum, Cohn and his allies are having a clear impact on the president\u2019s thinking. In the past week, Trump reversed his earlier statements and said he supported the Export-Import Bank, would not declare China a \u201ccurrency manipulator\u201d and said flattering things about Federal Reserve Board Chair Janet L. Yellen.</p> <p>Conservatives took aim at the Ex-Im Bank and the Fed throughout much of Obama\u2019s term, while Trump, as part of his tough trade rhetoric, promised to go after China\u2019s currency practices on Day One of his administration.</p> <p>Cohn\u2019s stature among the top advisers is notable because he is one of the few who played no role in the campaign. Cohn, who grew up in a middle-class family and struggled in a number of schools because of dyslexia, graduated from American University and took a job with U.S. Steel in Ohio. During a trip to New York, he coaxed a well-dressed senior Wall Street executive into sharing a cab with him to the airport, acting as if he knew financial markets (he knew virtually nothing), according to an interview he gave author Malcolm Gladwell. Cohn schmoozed his way into his first Wall Street job and then climbed the ranks, eventually becoming Goldman\u2019s president and chief operating officer.</p> <p>While friends say he loves his new job, they say Cohn also holds the traditions of Washington in low regard.</p> <p>At a recent dinner with friends in New York, he called Washington a \u201cs---show,\u201d according to a person familiar with the exchange.</p> <p>Cohn has not tried to shirk his past at Goldman Sachs or hide his lavish lifestyle. He recently had drinks at the Four Seasons with Goldman Sachs chief executive Lloyd Blankfein, and shortly after the failure of the House GOP health-care legislation, he went on vacation in the Bahamas.</p> <p>If he is able to deflect the growing criticism from hardcore conservatives, White House officials say Cohn will have a strong future as a Trump adviser given his experience and the deep bench of experts he has established.</p> <p>This includes DJ Gribbin, an infrastructure expert, and Shahira Knight, a former congressional aide on tax policy who joined the White House from Fidelity Investments.</p> <p>Other top members of the team include Kenneth Juster, who is slated to play a top White House role in international negotiations; Jeremy Katz, a former White House official in the George W. Bush administration; and Ray Starling, who works on agriculture issues and was formerly the general counsel for the North Carolina Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services.</p> <p>While Cohn has met with lawmakers from both parties and executives from numerous companies in his role, he rarely telegraphs what the White House plans to do.</p> <p>One exception came last week, when \u2014 during a gathering of chief executives \u2014 he went into great detail about how the U.S. air-traffic-control system needed to be reworked.</p> <p>He quickly moved through a technical discussion on why the United States should scrap its land-based radar system and adopt a global-positioning system, suggesting he had already devoted time to the topic. He said their approach would save 25\u00a0percent of the jet fuel consumed each year.</p> <p>\u201cWe are going to cut flight times down fairly dramatically,\u201d he told the executives. \u201cWe are going to cut the experience down. We are going to cut tarmac time down.\u201d</p> <p>His penchant for dealmaking has even attracted the admiration of Office of Management and Budget Director Mick Mulvaney, a tough fiscal conservative and longtime critic of government spending. Cohn, working to fulfill Trump\u2019s pledge to spend billions to rebuild infrastructure, has toyed with an idea that would pair $200\u00a0billion in taxpayer money with $800\u00a0billion in additional funds, mostly from private investors.</p> <p>\u201cYou\u2019ve got to give these Goldman Sachs guys credit,\u201d Mulvaney said this week on CNBC about Cohn\u2019s plan. \u201cThey know how to lever up.\u201d</p> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>Ashley Parker contributed to this report.</p> </p> <div class=\"extra\"> <p> </p> </div></article> <div class=\"clear\"></div> </div> </div> <div Rush Limbaugh last week called him \u201ca very ideological liberal Democrat\u201d and a \u201ctrader at Goldman Sachs.\u201d He expressed concern that Cohn and his allies in the White House \u201care starting to have sway\u201d at Bannon\u2019s expense.\n\nCohn, who declined to comment for this article, has given thousands of dollars to candidates from both parties, including President Barack Obama and former candidate Hillary Clinton.\n\nWhite House aides say Cohn has done well because Trump sees him, more than anything else, as a dealmaker. Cohn represents a bloc of White House officials who are working harder than before to court Democratic support for key parts of Trump\u2019s agenda, having seen the Republican Party splinter during the health-care debate.\n\n\u201cI\u2019m not a Democrat, and I\u2019m not a Republican,\u201d Cohn often says in meetings with business executives, according to two people familiar with his exchanges. \u201cI just want to get things done.\u201d\n\nPeople who have met with Cohn in his new role said they weren\u2019t aware of what his ideology was. He just seemed driven to forge agreements.\n\nThat philosophy has led Cohn to show enthusiasm for ideas such as a new tax on carbon \u2014 a Democrat-friendly idea which would raise revenue to ease tax reform, a top presidential priority, while also helping to curb carbon emissions. The idea is ridiculed by many conservatives on Capitol Hill, and the White House rapidly distanced itself last week after word leaked that senior officials were studying the concept.\n\n\u201cI think the National Economic Council has done a terrible job,\u201d said Larry Kudlow, who was one of Trump\u2019s top economic advisers during the campaign. \u201cIt\u2019s the NEC\u2019s job to put a plan together and show the president options and make decisions. So far, I would say they are way behind the eight ball.\u201d\n\nBut even as the legislative agenda struggles to gain momentum, Cohn and his allies are having a clear impact on the president\u2019s thinking. In the past week, Trump reversed his earlier statements and said he supported the Export-Import Bank, would not declare China a \u201ccurrency manipulator\u201d and said flattering things about Federal Reserve Board Chair Janet L. Yellen.\n\nConservatives took aim at the Ex-Im Bank and the Fed throughout much of Obama\u2019s term, while Trump, as part of his tough trade rhetoric, promised to go after China\u2019s currency practices on Day One of his administration.\n\nCohn\u2019s stature among the top advisers is notable because he is one of the few who played no role in the campaign. Cohn, who grew up in a middle-class family and struggled in a number of schools because of dyslexia, graduated from American University and took a job with U.S. Steel in Ohio. During a trip to New York, he coaxed a well-dressed senior Wall Street executive into sharing a cab with him to the airport, acting as if he knew financial markets (he knew virtually nothing), according to an interview he gave author Malcolm Gladwell. Cohn schmoozed his way into his first Wall Street job and then climbed the ranks, eventually becoming Goldman\u2019s president and chief operating officer.\n\nWhile friends say he loves his new job, they say Cohn also holds the traditions of Washington in low regard.\n\nAt a recent dinner with friends in New York, he called Washington a \u201cs---show,\u201d according to a person familiar with the exchange.\n\nCohn has not tried to shirk his past at Goldman Sachs or hide his lavish lifestyle. He recently had drinks at the Four Seasons with Goldman Sachs chief executive Lloyd Blankfein, and shortly after the failure of the House GOP health-care legislation, he went on vacation in the Bahamas.\n\nIf he is able to deflect the growing criticism from hardcore conservatives, White House officials say Cohn will have a strong future as a Trump adviser given his experience and the deep bench of experts he has established.\n\nThis includes DJ Gribbin, an infrastructure expert, and Shahira Knight, a former congressional aide on tax policy who joined the White House from Fidelity Investments.\n\nOther top members of the team include Kenneth Juster, who is slated to play a top White House role in international negotiations; Jeremy Katz, a former White House official in the George W. Bush administration; and Ray Starling, who works on agriculture issues and was formerly the general counsel for the North Carolina Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services.\n\nWhile Cohn has met with lawmakers from both parties and executives from numerous companies in his role, he rarely telegraphs what the White House plans to do.\n\nOne exception came last week, when \u2014 during a gathering of chief executives \u2014 he went into great detail about how the U.S. air-traffic-control system needed to be reworked.\n\nHe quickly moved through a technical discussion on why the United States should scrap its land-based radar system and adopt a global-positioning system, suggesting he had already devoted time to the topic. He said their approach would save 25 percent of the jet fuel consumed each year.\n\n\u201cWe are going to cut flight times down fairly dramatically,\u201d he told the executives. \u201cWe are going to cut the experience down. We are going to cut tarmac time down.\u201d\n\nHis penchant for dealmaking has even attracted the admiration of Office of Management and Budget Director Mick Mulvaney, a tough fiscal conservative and longtime critic of government spending. Cohn, working to fulfill Trump\u2019s pledge to spend billions to rebuild infrastructure, has toyed with an idea that would pair $200 billion in taxpayer money with $800 billion in additional funds, mostly from private investors.\n\n\u201cYou\u2019ve got to give these Goldman Sachs guys credit,\u201d Mulvaney said this week on CNBC about Cohn\u2019s plan. \u201cThey know how to lever
