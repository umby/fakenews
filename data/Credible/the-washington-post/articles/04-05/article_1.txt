class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:greg.jaffe@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Trump and his \u2018America First\u2019 philosophy face first moral quandary in Syria'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> <span class=\"tweet-authors\"><span class=\"pb-twitter-follow unprocessed\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/@GregJaffe\" class=\"twitter-follow-button\" data-show-count=\"false\" data-lang=\"en\">Follow @GregJaffe</a></span> </span> </div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p id=\"U12001413912911pMC\">President Trump has vowed to follow a radically new approach to foreign policy that jettisons the costly mantle of moral leadership in favor of America\u2019s most immediate economic and security interests.</p> <p id=\"U120014139129110Q\">This week, crises in Syria and North Korea have put Trump\u2019s \u201cAmerica First\u201d foreign policy to perhaps its biggest test.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911XtG\">On Wednesday, the president stood next to Jordan\u2019s King Abdullah II in the Rose Garden and delivered a statement on the brutal chemical weapons attack in Syria that sounded as though it could have been given by any one of his recent predecessors in office.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911ZYH\">Trump condemned the attack as a \u201chorrific\u201d strike by Syrian President Bashar al-Assad\u2019s regime against \u201cinnocent people, including women, small children and even beautiful little babies.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911hUH\">Asked if it crossed a red line for him, Trump replied: \u201cIt crossed a lot of lines for me. .\u2009.\u2009. Beyond a red line. Many, many lines.\u201d</p><div></div> <p id=\"U12001413912911BdB\">Despite the tough talk, the Syrian chemical weapons attack poses a particular problem for Trump\u2019s foreign policy philosophy. The attack by Assad\u2019s forces offends America\u2019s values and it violates long-standing international norms of behavior, but it does not present an immediate threat to America\u2019s security or its economic interests. In an \u201cAmerica First\u201d world, it is an atrocity, but hardly a call to action for the United States and its allies.</p> <p id=\"U120014139129112u\">The president\u2019s statement in the Rose Garden suggested that the horrors he had seen on television were causing him to rethink some of the core beliefs he had held about the U.S. role in the world when he was running for office.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911QOG\">The big question was how long Trump\u2019s sense of outrage would last and whether it would lead to substantive action.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911anG\">\u201cThe president just made a statement on Assad that looks 180\u00a0degrees different from his actual policy,\u201d said Kori Schake, a research fellow at Stanford University and former official in the George W. Bush administration. \u201cThis may be a scattershot administration with a president that responds to near-term stimulus rather than long-term planning or strategy.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911VUC\">Less than a week before the chemical weapons strike, Secretary of State Rex Tillerson and U.N. Ambassador Nikki Haley suggested that the Trump administration could live with an outcome that wiped out the Islamic State in Syria but did not remove Assad.</p> <p id=\"U1200141391291177B\">In the immediate aftermath of the attack, Trump seemed to be sticking to that instinct. His first impulse was to focus blame for the attack on President Barack Obama for threatening, but not executing, military strikes when Assad killed hundreds in a 2013 chemical attack.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911BfH\">Assad\u2019s \u201cheinous actions,\u201d Trump said in a statement Tuesday after the attack, were a direct consequence of Obama\u2019s \u201cweakness and irresolution.\u201d</p><div></div> <p id=\"U12001413912911NZF\">Sean Spicer, the White House press secretary, played down the prospects of a policy change given the \u201cpolitical realities that exist in Syria.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911uWE\">By Wednesday afternoon, Trump seemed to be hinting \u2014 without directly saying it \u2014 that Assad\u2019s actions must be punished and that the Syrian strongman might have to go.</p> <p id=\"U120014139129112RH\">\u201cI don\u2019t have one specific way,\u201d Trump said. \u201c.\u2009.\u2009. I do change, and I\u2019m flexible. .\u2009.\u2009. And I will tell you that the attack on children yesterday had a big impact on me \u2014 big impact. That was a horrible, horrible thing.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911mmD\">The Trump administration is in the middle of a major review of its policies in Iraq and Syria that is being led by the Pentagon and Defense Secretary Jim Mattis.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911xbG\">A similar review is underway regarding its policy on North Korea, which on Wednesday <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/north-korea-fires-a-ballistic-missile-as-trump-prepares-to-host-chinas-xi/2017/04/04/8983fa36-1988-11e7-8003-f55b4c1cfae2_story.html?utm_term=.6560bafd0c6a\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">launched</a> another medium-range ballistic missile. The isolated and dangerous regime seems likely to develop a long-range missile capable of delivering a nuclear weapon to the United States before Trump leaves office.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911zTF\">Trump, during his Rose Garden appearance, was clearly feeling the weight of both of those foreign policy crises. When asked whether he felt responsibility to respond to the Syrian attack, he answered affirmatively and then naturally shifted to North Korea and his meeting later this week with Chinese President Xi Jinping.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911BDB\">\u201cWe have a big problem,\u201d he said of North Korea. \u201cWe have somebody that is not doing the right thing, and that\u2019s going to be my responsibility.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U1200141391291143F\">Whether that burden will push Trump away from his \u201cAmerica First\u201d outlook and toward a more traditional foreign policy remains to be seen. In just a few months in office, Trump has consistently upended foreign policy norms and shown that he has little interest in leading or enforcing the rules-based international order as other presidents did over the past 70\u00a0years.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911LvE\">For the moment, the biggest changes from the Obama years are in style and rhetoric. Trump often has seemed more ambivalent than outraged over Russia\u2019s invasion of Ukraine and its annexation of Crimea. His interest in NATO, the cornerstone of security in Europe, is often largely confined to whether the allies are paying their fair share and the United States is getting a good deal.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911ZpD\">He has shown a clear preference for stable dictators over the spread of democracy as indicated by the warm Oval Office welcome given to Egyptian President Abdel Fatah al-Sissi, who was shunned by the Obama White House for his brutal crackdown on dissidents.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911UP\">The Syrian chemical weapons attack seemed to awaken Trump\u2019s sense of moral responsibility as leader of the world\u2019s sole remaining superpower. A president who has often seemed indifferent to suffering in faraway countries \u2014 including the plight of Syrian refugees \u2014 reacted with a natural revulsion.</p> <p id=\"U120014139129116eE\">Less clear was whether that revulsion would produce a significant shift in policy.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911YOB\">\u201cI\u2019m still trying to sort out what the reactions of the White House are and whether they have changed their position on Assad or whether it\u2019s just a rhetorical shift,\u201d said Danielle Pletka, a vice president at the American Enterprise Institute, a conservative Washington think tank.</p> <p id=\"U1200141391291197D\">Other analysts echoed that confusion. \u201cThey have not yet figured out what they are trying to do,\u201d said Peter Feaver, a professor at Duke University and an adviser in the second Bush administration. \u201cWhat looks like recalibration might be multiple voices.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911tfG\">In a White House marked by rival factions, it has become difficult to figure out who exactly is in charge of foreign policy. On Wednesday, Trump removed White House chief strategist Stephen K. Bannon from the National Security Council. The change suggested that national security adviser H.R. McMaster, who has a traditional view of U.S. power and global leadership obligations, was gaining influence over policymaking.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911sNC\">For his part, Trump left many guessing about his ultimate intentions in Syria and North Korea. \u201cI don\u2019t like to say where I\u2019m going and what I\u2019m doing,\u201d he said.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911hjB\">His broader view of foreign policy, though, was clearer and unchanging.</p> <p id=\"U12001413912911rm\">\u201cI just have to say that the world is a mess,\u201d he said. \u201cI inherited a mess.\u201d</p> </article>
