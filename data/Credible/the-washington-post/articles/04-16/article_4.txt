Paulson shows up for coffee at the Quik Mart in Kiron, Iowa. Regulars gather one by one each morning and afternoon, except Sundays. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p id=\"U12001708473890c4\"> <span class=\"dateline\">KIRON, Iowa \u2014 </span>Russell Paulson had already heard by the time he arrived at the Quik Mart for his afternoon coffee. Walt Miller had died.</p> <p>\u201cDied last night, huh?\u201d someone was saying as Russell pulled up a chair.</p> <p>\u201cYeah, last night,\u201d another man said.</p> <p id=\"U12001708473890ATH\">Russell listened; he had known Walt. At the age of 80, he knew almost everyone in Kiron, a town of 229 people, one of whom is U.S. Rep. Steve King, who has a house on the edge of town. Russell knew King, too, knew that he was the sort of person always stirring controversy, often by raging against what he called \u201ccultural suicide by demographic transformation.\u201d More recently, King had said that \u201cwe can\u2019t restore our civilization with somebody else\u2019s babies,\u201d a comment embraced by prominent white supremacists and widely condemned around the country as demonizing Latino and other non-European immigrants. </p> <p id=\"U12001708473890OvC\">There was little controversy across King\u2019s district, though, a swath of rural America made up of tiny towns with tiny, aging white populations that routinely elected King with more than 70\u00a0percent of the vote. In Kiron, people brushed it off as King being King, a man they all knew, expressing a plain truth they all understood: The white population was shrinking, and towns like theirs were vanishing, with the few exceptions being places such as Denison, a pork-processing town 20 minutes down the highway where population growth was being driven by immigrants from Mexico and Central America.</p> <p>Kiron, meanwhile, was losing steam. According to the most recent census figures, the population included nine Mexicans; the other 220 were all white, and their numbers were decreasing by 10 or so each year, and now, on a Wednesday, by one.</p> <p>\u201cOh, Walt Miller? He did pass?\u201d Dwain Swensen, 67, said, sipping his coffee.</p> <p>\u201cWhat\u2019d he have, pancreatic cancer or something?\u201d said Ron Streck, 70.</p> <p>\u201cLiver,\u201d said Herman Kohnekamp, also 70. \u201cI think that\u2019s what it was, wasn\u2019t it, Russell?\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI knew he passed but didn\u2019t know any details,\u201d Russell said.</p> <p>It was a quiet afternoon, the ritual 3 p.m. coffee in a place where, as one regular put it, \u201cyou can figure out Steve King by understanding all of us.\u201d Every day but Sunday, the bell on the front door rang as they arrived. The wood-paneled backroom was waiting. The Bunn-o-Matic and the Styrofoam cups. The space heater humming. The clock with the squinting Merit cigarette man on one wall, the calendar on the other, the cracked blinds dangling over the window where the view through the slats was a sea of farm fields, and on a hill in the distance, a stand of evergreens where the cemetery was. Now the bell on the front door rang again, and Russell looked up.</p> <p>\u201cOh,\u201d Ron said under his breath, seeing who it was. \u201cHere comes trouble.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>***</i> </p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron011.JPG?uuid=jcW_VB82Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron011.JPG?uuid=jcW_VB82Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron011.JPG?uuid=jcW_VB82Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron011.JPG?uuid=jcW_VB82Eee7WadMyvHQLw\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Coffee time for the regulars in the wood-paneled backroom of the Quik Mart. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p></p> <p id=\"U12001708473890OgH\">It was Kevin Lloyd, 52, who came in occasionally, and had been in the day before, all riled up about the latest Steve King situation, waving his hands and going on about how people had misunderstood what he\u2019d meant about \u201cother peoples\u2019 babies.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001708473890kqC\">\u201cIf you\u2019re American, you got to take care of America!\u201d he had said then. \u201cI love that people want to come here from Mexico, from Ukraine, from the Middle East, but they need to come here legally.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001708473890e3H\">Dwain, Ron, a woman named Jane Gronau and Russell had been there, sipping their coffees, as Kevin had continued that he had no idea why people would call King a \u201cwhite supremacist,\u201d or, for that matter, why people would call President Trump racist. \u201cNow, is Barack Hussein Obama a Muslim? In my opinion, yes,\u201d he had said, and that had brought him to the other thing he figured King meant about babies. He had meant Muslim babies of the Muslims that Obama had allowed into the country.</p> <p>\u201cAnd here, I\u2019m going to quote a great president, Abe Lincoln,\u201d he had said. \u201cHe said the fall of America will come from the inside. Well, if you\u2019re allowing all these children in, and if they hate America, how long is it going to be before we\u2019re not the United States of America anymore?\u201d</p> <p>Jane had nodded: \u201cIf you study the number of Muslims, there are going to be so many here, and they\u2019re going to have so many kids, they\u2019re going to be able to take over that way.\u201d</p> <p>Dwain had nodded: \u201cThey say \u2018freedom of religion\u2019 but if you\u2019re Muslim, and you become Christian, you\u2019re ousted. Sometimes, they kill \u2019em.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cThey behead \u2019em,\u201d Kevin had said into a quiet Iowa afternoon.</p> <p>\u201cI think what King was trying to get across is, look: We can only grow so many hogs, so much beans and so much corn,\u201d Kevin had said. \u201cIf we let everybody in, we\u2019re going to be without a food source. And what happens when that\u2019s gone? Then we\u2019re all in trouble.\u201d</p> <p>Chaos, beheadings, starvation, the death of one America and the rise of another \u2014 that was the trouble Kevin had raised the day before, and now he was back, interrupting the conversation about Walt Miller.</p> <p>\u201cWhat are you up to, Mr. Paulson?\u201d he said to Russell.</p> <p>\u201cJust listening and learning,\u201d Russell said, looking at the floor, holding his coffee. \u201cEvery once in a while, I learn something here. Every once in a while, I learn something about myself.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cSo how old was Walt?\u201d Ron continued.</p> <p>\u201cMid-60s, I\u2019d say,\u201d said Herman.</p> <p>\u201cDied last night,\u201d Ron said again.</p> <p>\u201cLast night,\u201d Herman said again.</p> <p>After a while, Russell asked, \u201cI wasn\u2019t sure if he was home?\u201d</p> <p>\u201cYeah, he was at home,\u201d Herman said, and Russell was quiet the rest of the afternoon.</p> <p></p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>***</i> </p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-11/Kiron02.JPG?uuid=DWhiwB8tEee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-11/Kiron02.JPG?uuid=DWhiwB8tEee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-11/Kiron02.JPG?uuid=DWhiwB8tEee7WadMyvHQLw\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-11/Kiron02.JPG?uuid=DWhiwB8tEee7WadMyvHQLw\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Paulson holds a portrait of his wife, Glenda, who had died 18 months earlier. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p id=\"U12001708473890amG\">The next day, Russell had his morning coffee and got into his car.</p> <p>He stopped by the bank where he\u2019d been going since the 1940s.</p> <p>\u201cHi, Russell,\u201d the one teller said to her one customer.</p> <p>He got back into his car and drove one block to the edge of town, turned onto the two-lane highway, then one long gravel road after another, straight lines stretching out into still-fallow fields.</p> <p>\u201cSome of the roads have been abandoned,\u201d he said. \u201cBecause there\u2019s not as many people living out here, the roads just disappeared.\u201d</p> <p>He knew the roads better than anyone. His own family\u2019s roots in the area stretched back to the 19th century, when the U.S. government was aggressively removing Native American tribes to make way for one of the largest immigration waves in American history. The Swedes came, the Germans came, the farms, the towns and generations of babies, one of whom was Russell Elmer Paulson, born in 1937. He was raised on his mother\u2019s family farm in rural Kiron and never left other than a stint in the Army, and one in Dubuque.</p> <p>\u201cIt wasn\u2019t for me,\u201d he said, driving along.</p> <p>He and his wife, Glenda, inherited land when Russell\u2019s parents died and lived on it until they retired and moved into town. Russell\u2019s work had been farming and insurance adjusting. His culture was being a Methodist and a Mason and listening to polka, though most of that had fallen away. The church he and Glenda had gone to \u201cdied for lack of people and money,\u201d he said. There were hardly any Masons left. Polka was not enjoying a revival. His kids had left for jobs in other areas. Glenda had died last year.</p> <p>\u201cSee that ridge? That\u2019s the old railroad bed,\u201d he said now, driving along, squinting through his gold-rimmed glasses.</p> <p>\u201cMy aunt bought this,\u201d he said, passing a stand of trees where farmhouses had been.</p> <p>\u201cWalt would go there,\u201d he said, pointing out a repair shop where Walt Miller had coffee, and soon he turned onto a narrow dirt road leading to the farm where he and Glenda had lived, a collection of storage buildings where Russell now kept his old tractors, and one he used as an office, where he went these days to work crossword puzzles or just sit and think.</p> <p>\u201cCommune with God and the birds,\u201d he said. \u201cWell, not too many birds now.\u201d</p> <p>He glanced around at the old buildings, now shuttered and locked, though someone had broken into one of them recently.</p> <p>\u201cThey stole a bunch of tools and such,\u201d Russell said, pulling back onto the gravel road. \u201cNo need to get all worked up about it.\u201d</p> <p>He had a huge bag of peppermint Life Savers on the console, and he unwrapped one and put it in his mouth. He passed a rotting barn and a bird on a stretch of barbed wire, and after a while, a gray house with a huge American flag.</p> <p>\u201cThis is Steve King\u2019s house here,\u201d he said, looking at it.</p> <p>He had known King a long time and saw no reason to be bothered by something or other he said. He supported King \u2014 \u201cI have no reason in the world to dislike the man\u201d \u2014 but wasn\u2019t one to rant about politics. He had no computer, no smartphone. His television had no cable. He watched a half-hour of national news, a half-hour of local, followed by \u201cWheel of Fortune\u201d and Lawrence Welk. He ate chicken tenders and food he described as \u201cAmerican.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cHe\u2019s just kind of one of us,\u201d Russell said of King, driving on past a field where a church had burned down, and the home of a man who\u2019d died last year. It began to rain.</p> <p>\u201cWhen it comes down like it\u2019s doing now, it\u2019s just wonderful,\u201d he said.</p> <p>He drove past fields and more fields until he came to another stand of trees on a hill.</p> <p>\u201cThis is the cemetery,\u201d he said, pulling in.</p> <p>He drove slowly past the headstones. \u201cA lot of these people I knew,\u201d he said and began reading names.</p> <p>\u201cLarson.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cLind.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cGustafson.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cPaulson \u2014 this would be my folks right here,\u201d he said, and then he noticed the time, almost 3 p.m.</p> <p>He headed back to town, pulling onto Main Street where a wooden sign said, \u201cKiron, Blessed with the Best.\u201d</p> <p>After King had made his comment about babies, some out-of-town protest group had put up another sign below that one that said, \u201cWhite Supremacist.\u201d</p> <p>The sign didn\u2019t make any sense to Russell, and, after it was removed, his main worry was that the protesters might have damaged the town sign, which had started to rot a few years ago.</p> <p>Russell had taken on the job of maintaining it. He had trimmed the tree branches that had grown through the wood. He had taken down \u201cBlessed with the Best\u201d and repainted each of the letters. He went to a lumberyard and had a new K, I, R, O, and N made, painting each letter several times and spraying them with wood preservative. One year, he and Glenda had planted a bed of petunias and geraniums.</p> <p>\u201cI don\u2019t think we will ever have a better display of flowers,\u201d he said now, and soon he was pulling up to the Quik Mart for the afternoon coffee. As he walked inside, he saw a funeral notice on the front door with a photo of a smiling man in gold-rimmed glasses.</p> <p>\u201cOh,\u201d Russell said, pausing for a moment. \u201cThere\u2019s Walt.\u201d</p> <p>He glanced at the funeral information for Walt Miller, poured his fourth coffee of the day, and sat down in the backroom. Dwain and Charlie Harm were already there, but they weren\u2019t talking. A car swooshed by. An eighteen-<br align=\"block\"/>wheeler swooshed by. Charlie tapped his nails on the table.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>***</i> </p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron020.JPG?uuid=EhSY-h9_Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron020.JPG?uuid=EhSY-h9_Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron020.JPG?uuid=EhSY-h9_Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron020.JPG?uuid=EhSY-h9_Eee7WadMyvHQLw\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">The Kiron Cemetery sits on a hill overlooking the tiny town. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p></p> <p id=\"U12001708473890cqE\">The next day, the bell rang as the door with the funeral notice swung open, and it was Dwain, then Bob James, then Herman, then Russell. The Merit cigarette clock showed a few minutes after 3 p.m. Russell got the coffee pot and poured. The bell rang again, and it was a man named Glen Ballantine.</p> <p>\u201cTime for plowing?\u201d Herman asked the 84-year-old farmer.</p> <p>\u201cTwo weeks,\u201d Glen said, sitting down.</p> <p>Bob was reading the paper. Russell was sipping his coffee, looking out the window.</p> <p>\u201cGot the visitation tonight,\u201d Herman said.</p> <p>He didn\u2019t have to mention Walt Miller\u2019s name because they all knew what he meant.</p> <p>They went back to talking about plowing, and Glen was saying how different farming was now than when he was a young man, which for some reason reminded him of one of his first jobs, digging graves.</p> <p>\u201cFor 18 bucks,\u201d he said.</p> <p>\u201cYou dug a regular grave for 18\u2009bucks?\u201d said Dwain.</p> <p>\u201cOh yeah, and we had to fill \u2019em back up again,\u201d said Glen.</p> <p>\u201cI helped dig one once,\u201d said Russell. \u201cYou know, manually. Only one. I don\u2019t know what I got paid. But. That\u2019s a long way down to the bottom of that.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cIf there was frost in the first foot, you got $1 more,\u201d said Glen.</p> <p>\u201cWhat\u2019d you use to get through the frost?\u201d asked Bob.</p> <p>\u201cPickax and sledgehammer,\u201d said Glen. \u201cAnd when we\u2019d fill \u2019em, we\u2019d fill \u2019em in 14 scoops. We were just little kids, more or less.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cWe had more dirt than we needed,\u201d Russell said. \u201cAnd had to \u2014\u201d</p> <p>\u201cHad to haul that away,\u201d said Glen, finishing his sentence.</p> <p>\u201cHad to put that on the pickup,\u201d said Russell, and they went on talking like that until Herman got up to leave. It was after 3:30\u2009p.m.</p> <p>\u201cFuneral home starts, what, at 4?\u201d Herman said.</p> <p>\u201cFour till 7, it says on there,\u201d Russell said.</p> <p>The funeral home was in Denison, and the sun was going down as Russell turned onto the two-lane highway toward one of the only towns in Steve King\u2019s district that was growing, and which appeared in the distance as a cluster of lights and rising steam from the pork-processing plant.</p> <p>Russell turned by the Walmart, bustling on a Friday payday, and turned again into a neighborhood where Latino kids were playing in a yard. Up a hill, he parked in front of the funeral home, where people were still streaming in near 7 p.m.</p> <p>Russell made his way through the receiving line, his hat off, comb lines visible in his gray hair. He shook hands with Walt\u2019s family, who thanked him for coming, and inched forward until he reached the open casket.</p> <p>He stood there a moment. He looked at Walt. He looked at the light-blue satin lining and the farm scene etched into it. A man stood next to Russell.</p> <p>\u201cWent fast,\u201d he said of Walt, who had passed away soon after his diagnosis. \u201cThat\u2019s what you hope for.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI do,\u201d said Russell, still looking at Walt, and soon, he headed back to Kiron.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>***</i> </p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron019.JPG?uuid=ppotsB9-Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron019.JPG?uuid=ppotsB9-Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron019.JPG?uuid=ppotsB9-Eee7WadMyvHQLw\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/12/Others/Images/2017-04-12/Kiron019.JPG?uuid=ppotsB9-Eee7WadMyvHQLw\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">The fresh grave of Walt Miller at Kiron Cemetery. (Michael S. Williamson/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p></p> <p id=\"U12001708473890MuB\">The funeral was the next day at Zion Lutheran Church in Denison, and more people came from Kiron and other vanishing towns like Odebolt and Ida Grove. They sat in jeans and dresses and suits on the wooden pews of a church founded in 1872, and read about Walt in the program, where it was said that \u201cfarming and fixing equipment and household items were his favorite things to do,\u201d and soon the church bells began ringing.</p> <p>The pews creaked as everyone stood and watched the pallbearers roll in the coffin draped in a white cloth with a red cross, and a procession of dozens of family members that included exactly one baby, a girl with a black ribbon around her head.</p> <p>\u201cYour world has changed,\u201d the pastor began.</p> <p>When it was over, people got back into their cars and drove 20 minutes up the highway to the cemetery in Kiron, a long procession of headlights passing through fields and more fields, then turning right, then heading up the hill to the stand of evergreens, and afterward, at 3 p.m., the bell on the Quik Mart door began ringing.</p> <p>It rang for Herman, who arrived with a loaf of homemade bread. It rang for Dwain, for Bob, and for Charlie, who shuffled into the backroom and said, \u201cBuried a nice guy this morning.\u201d</p> <p>It rang for Russell, who poured his coffee, walked back into the wood-paneled room, and pulled up a chair.</p> <p>\u201cStrawberries come to life this time of year, Russell?\u201d Dwain asked.</p> <p>\u201cI don\u2019t know,\u201d Russell said.</p> <p>They talked about the frost, and when spring might arrive.</p> <p>\u201cWell, I better get moving,\u201d Charlie said and headed out.</p> <p>\u201cI got things to do, too,\u201d Russell said, but then he didn\u2019t leave, not yet.</p> <p>He got up and sat where Charlie had been, closer to the window.</p> <p>\u201cWell, I gotta go,\u201d Herman said.</p> <p>\u201cSee you, Herman,\u201d Russell said.</p> <p>\u201cBye, Herman,\u201d Dwain said, and now there were just the three of them left.</p> <p>Dwain cleared his throat. A car passed by. The space heater hummed. Bob finished his coffee. Russell swallowed the last of his.</p> <p>\u201cYou want more coffee, Mr. Bob?\u201d Russell asked.</p> <p>\u201cDo you?\u201d said Bob.</p> <p>\u201cYeah,\u201d Russell decided, and walked over to get the coffee pot.</p> <p>He poured some into Bob\u2019s cup. He poured some into Dwain\u2019s cup. He filled his own and sat down again. He tapped his thumb on the table. Eventually he stood up and walked toward the door, where Walt\u2019s funeral notice no longer was.</p> <p>\u201cSee ya, Russell,\u201d said Dwain.</p> <p>\u201cSee ya, Russell,\u201d said Bob.</p> In Kiron, people brushed it off as King being King, a man they all knew, expressing a plain truth they all understood: The white population was shrinking, and towns like theirs were vanishing, with the few exceptions being places such as Denison, a pork-processing town 20 minutes down the highway where population growth was being driven by immigrants from Mexico and Central America.\n\nKiron, meanwhile, was losing steam.
