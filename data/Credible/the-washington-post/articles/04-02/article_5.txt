data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/02/Others/Images/2017-04-02/RTR4SU0O1491165541.jpg?uuid=aXAtghfkEeeFmJqZ2lWfng\" (Lucas Jackson/Reuters)</span> </div> <div class=\"pb-sig-line hasnt-headshot has-0-headshots hasnt-bio is-not-column\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/louisa-loveluck/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Louisa Loveluck</span></a> and <span itemprop=\"name\">Zakaria Zakaria</span></span> <span class=\"pb-byline-tool-mobile\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/louisa-loveluck/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Louisa Loveluck</span></a> and <span itemprop=\"name\">Zakaria Zakaria</span></span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:louisa.loveluck@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for '\u2018The hospitals were slaughterhouses\u2019: A journey into\u00a0Syria\u2019s secret torture wards'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> </span> <span class=\"pb-timestamp\" itemprop=\"datePublished\" content=\"2017-04-02T07:33-500\">April 2 at 7:33 PM</span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:louisa.loveluck@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for '\u2018The hospitals were slaughterhouses\u2019: A journey into\u00a0Syria\u2019s secret torture wards'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> <span class=\"tweet-authors\"><span class=\"pb-twitter-follow unprocessed\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/leloveluck\" class=\"twitter-follow-button\" data-show-count=\"false\" data-lang=\"en\">Follow leloveluck</a></span> </span> </div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p> <span class=\"dateline\">BEIRUT \u2014</span> One evening in the early days of Syria\u2019s uprising,\u00a0Mohsen al-Masri\u2019s band of activists slipped through the Damascus streets and waited for the coast to clear. Then they crouched, opened their bags and let out\u00a0a stream of color.</p> <p>Thousands of ping-pong balls, painted green, pink, blue and yellow, bounced past policemen, who scrambled to stop them. Residents would find balls tucked in nooks and crannies for months. Each was marked with a single word: \u201cFreedom.\u201d</p> <p>The punishment for Masri\u2019s acts of peaceful protest would begin a journey into hell, unusual not because of what he saw, but because he survived.</p> <p>In a series of interviews, he described how he was tortured and interrogated over a two-year period in four detention facilities before arriving in a hospital at the heart of a nationwide system of brutality.</p> <p>The hospital, known as 601, is not the only site of torture in Syria. But after it was seen in a cache of photographs showing thousands of skeletal corpses, it became one of the most notorious.</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-photo-left\" style=\"width:300px;\"> <a name=\"9607d71ee1b6593ac42d52938f5c392f80c33e6b\"></a> <img alt=\"\" class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1024w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/23/Foreign/Images/Mohamed_Mounir.JPG?uuid=gO1J_A_zEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/23/Foreign/Images/Mohamed_Mounir.JPG?uuid=gO1J_A_zEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/23/Foreign/Images/Mohamed_Mounir.JPG?uuid=gO1J_A_zEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/23/Foreign/Images/Mohamed_Mounir.JPG?uuid=gO1J_A_zEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Mohsen al-Masri before and after his imprisonment. (Mohsen al-Masri /Mohsen al-Masri )</span> </div> <p>Inside the facility, about a half-mile from Syrian President Bashar al-Assad\u2019s palace, sick prisoners are tortured as they lie shackled to beds\u00a0crammed with dying\u00a0men, according to Masri and former detainees and military personnel who worked there. Corpses\u00a0are piled in bathrooms, outhouses and anywhere else they will fit, then meticulously documented\u00a0and trucked\u00a0away for mass burial.</p> <p>In interviews across Lebanon, Turkey and Europe, more than a dozen survivors and army defectors described horrors in Syrian military hospitals across the country for which war crimes lawyers\u00a0say they have\u00a0struggled to find a modern parallel.</p> <p>The former detainees come from all walks of life. Elite, \u00adworking-class, leftist and Islamist, their only connection to one another was involvement in <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2017/03/15/bbbcebc0-097a-11e7-a15f-a58d4a988474_story.html?utm_term=.b3b7a2047b87\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Syria\u2019s 2011 uprising</a>. Some were its instigators. Others said they had simply commented on the Facebook statuses of friends who supported protests.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/middle_east/how-the-syrian-revolt-went-so-horribly-tragically-wrong/2016/03/12/4aba6c86-d979-11e5-8210-f0bd8de915f6_story.html?utm_term=.7a21d7eeeb80\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">How the Syrian revolt went so horribly, tragically wrong</a>]</i> </p> <p>Investigators say that testimony and\u00a0documentation from Syria\u2019s military hospitals offer some of the most concrete evidence to date of crimes against humanity that could one day see senior government figures tried in court.</p> <p>\u201cWe were swept into a system that was ready for us. Even the hospitals were slaughterhouses,\u201d Masri said in an interview last month.</p> <p>Medicine has been used as a weapon of war since the earliest days of the uprising, when pro-government doctors performed amputations on protesters for minor injuries.\u00a0</p><div class=\"inline-content inline-graphic-embedded-296\"> <img src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/03/Foreign/Graphics/2300_syrianhospitals_0325_2.jpg?uuid=lMy-8BgHEeeFmJqZ2lWfng\"/><br/> </div> <p>Military hospitals across Syria have long set aside wards for prisoners. But since 2011, these have been packed with men left starving and broken by the conditions they have already endured.</p> <p>More than 100,000 people have been arrested or forcibly disappeared in Syria since the country\u2019s revolt began, according to a list compiled by the Syrian Network for Human Rights, a monitoring group.\u00a0During that time, international aid groups have gotten access to only a handful of prisons with the government\u2019s permission, none of which the detainees interviewed by The Washington Post spent time in.</p> <p>Masri\u2019s ordeal began in the spring of\u00a02012 when he was arrested on his way to a conference in Turkey. Repeatedly tortured as he was transferred from jail to jail, he arrived at Sednaya,\u00a0<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/syria-has-secretly-executed-thousands-of-political-prisoners-rights-group/2017/02/06/e4a7f56a-ecc5-11e6-a100-fdaaf400369a_story.html?utm_term=.e44bafc46a25\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">one of the most feared</a>.</p> <p>In a report published in February, Amnesty International said torture and forced starvation are systematic at the prison. But Masri said that prisoners learned to stay silent when guards asked who needed to go to the hospital.</p> <p>\u201cIt didn\u2019t matter what they did to us; we had to pretend we were fine. People rarely came back from those trips,\u201d he said.</p> <p>After months of starvation, Masri\u2019s name was added to the weekly transfer list. As darkness fell on an evening in May 2012, he was chained to another man and taken to trucks outside. Attaching a number to Masri\u2019s body, a guard told him to forget his name. Then he was blindfolded.</p> <p>Everyone gets the \u201cwelcome\u201d party, Masri said \u2014 a savage beating involving guards and medical staffers wearing white coats over military uniforms. In\u00a0Hospital\u00a0601, the weakest man was pushed to the floor and brutalized first. In the nearby Tishreen Military Hospital, a former technician at the facility, Mohammed al-Hammoud, said he had seen prisoners dragged down steps by the hair.</p> <p>\u201cEverything was about control,\u201d said Somar Mustafa, a physics student from Damascus who was sent to Hospital 601 at the end of 2012. Inside, he saw detainees chained to their beds and packed so tightly that they sat\u00a0with their knees jutting into their rib cages.\u00a0</p> <p>Bathroom breaks were so rare that prisoners would defecate where they sat, remaining in the same spot for days.\u00a0\u201cWe were blindfolded with that smell all around us. You can\u2019t shake the memory of it, even when you leave,\u201d Mustafa said.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/syria-has-secretly-executed-thousands-of-political-prisoners-rights-group/2017/02/06/e4a7f56a-ecc5-11e6-a100-fdaaf400369a_story.html?utm_term=.d3b27269b20e\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Syria has secretly executed thousands of political prisoners, rights group says</a>]</i> </p> <p>At least five branches of the Syrian security forces have operated wards inside Hospital 601\u00a0since 2011, according to the U.N. Commission of Inquiry, a body set up to monitor the conflict.\u00a0\u201cDetainees, including children, have been beaten, burned with cigarettes, and subjected to torture that exploits preexisting injuries,\u201d it said in a 2013 report. The commission concluded that many patients had been tortured to death inside the facility.</p> <p>The Harasta Military Hospital, also in Damascus, moved its ward from the first floor to the seventh to prevent detainees from escaping, a defector said. \u201cIt was the only floor without an elevator, and we knew they couldn\u2019t jump out the window.\u201d</p> <p>Investigators say the abuses could become central pillars in\u00a0any eventual case for prosecution of the hospitals\u2019 doctors, as well as senior figures in the Syrian government. </p> <p>In 601, Masri and Mustafa\u00a0said, they\u00a0saw high-ranking officers from the security branches accompanying doctors on their rounds. Sometimes the teams would pause by a prisoner to discuss his treatment. Other times\u00a0the men would beat him.</p> <p>The doctors were helped by service staffers in blue uniforms, many of them former\u00a0supporters of the revolt who had been co-<br align=\"block\"/>opted by their jailers. \u201cOur best men had been broken by torture. If they didn\u2019t beat us, they risked a worse fate themselves,\u201d Masri said.\u00a0</p> <p>The guards went by nicknames to avoid identification. Four survivors said the most famous was known as Azrael, or the Angel of Death. They described him as a thickset man from Assad\u2019s coastal stronghold of Latakia who carried a stick laced with razor blades. They said he selected prisoners, most of them deathly ill, for a fate he called \u201cjustice.\u201d The detainees called it execution.\u00a0</p> <p>Masri recalled Azrael taking a lighter to a plastic bag and melting it drop by drop onto a prisoner\u2019s face until he died, apparently of a heart attack. Other prisoners said he used an iron rod to smash their bedmates\u2019 skulls.</p> <p>Many died where they lay, slumped against their bedmates until morning came.\u00a0For Mustafa\u00a0in the winter of 2012, that meant sharing a bed until sunrise the next day with three corpses.</p> <p>As the uprising outside morphed into a war, former prisoners say, their interrogators became obsessed with the notion of accomplices,\u00a0torturing prisoners\u00a0to extract the names of new suspects to arrest.</p> <p>Documents signed by senior government and security\u00a0officials\u00a0acknowledged the upsurge in deaths, at times complaining that the bodies were building up.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s impossible to interrogate, torture and kill tens of thousands of detainees without a system in place,\u201d said Scott Gilmore, a staff attorney at the Center for Justice and Accountability. \u201cBefore the revolution, the regime was not generating thousands of dead bodies. Then all of a sudden it was. So what did you do with them?\u201d</p> <p>A December 2012 order signed by the head of Syria\u2019s military intelligence department instructed every security branch\u00a0to send their dead to a military hospital\u2019s morgue. The document, obtained by the\u00a0Commission for International Justice and Accountability, a Europe-based investigative unit, said that each body should be examined and logged.</p> <p> <a href=\"http://www.spiegel.de/international/world/spiegel-reporting-supports-accounts-of-torture-and-execution-in-syria-a-945760.html\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.spiegel.de\">A trove of these photographs</a> was published around the world in 2014, after being smuggled out of Syria by a military police defector known only by his code-name, Caesar. Most were taken inside Hospital 601. Skeletal bodies of children as young as 11 bore signs of torture, with eyes gouged out and limbs drilled through and burned. Following Syrian government protocol, Caesar had methodically documented the deaths of some 11,000 people.</p> <p>\u201cYou have to realize that these were just the photographs taken by a single man during a single period, and even then, they were only a fraction of what he\u2019d actually recorded,\u201d said Nadim Houry, who examined the photographs for Human Rights Watch.</p> <p>Assad recently described the images as\u00a0\u201cfake news,\u201d suggesting they had been doctored to suit the aims of human rights groups.\u00a0</p> <p>But defectors describe hauling numbered bodies into transparent bags in Hospital 601 and nearby <a href=\"https://www.hrw.org/report/2015/12/16/if-dead-could-speak/mass-deaths-and-torture-syrias-detention-facilities\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.hrw.org\">military hospitals</a> in Tishreen and Harasta. Investigators from the United Nations and private law firms have collected similar testimony from the cities of Homs, Aleppo and Daraa.</p> <p>By late 2012, the system had buckled, and the December order berated individual military departments for failing to register their dead on time.\u00a0 </p> <p>Those who survive are funneled back to nearby jails, Masri said. Others, like Mustafa, are released to a Damascus court packed with prisoners and dismissed from custody on the spot, after a judge acknowledges that they had\u00a0been\u00a0forced to make false confessions under torture. The young man\u00a0said he\u00a0remembers falling\u00a0into the arms of his sobbing parents. </p> <p>Masri\u2019s discharge from 601 sent him back to <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/syria-has-secretly-executed-thousands-of-political-prisoners-rights-group/2017/02/06/e4a7f56a-ecc5-11e6-a100-fdaaf400369a_story.html?utm_term=.5fe2cf56536e\" shape=\"rect\" title=\"www.washingtonpost.com\">Sednaya</a>. Another year of torture followed, with nights spent packed next to\u00a0other men in the darkness. He felt forgotten.</p> <p>In the winter of 2014, he dreamed he was taking a hot shower, its stream stripping back two years of dirt and leaving him clean. He woke to find a guard in his cell. \u201cHe told me it was time to go,\u201d Masri said.\u00a0\u201cI cannot describe that feeling. It was too much, too big. Indescribable.\u201d</p> <p>Back home in Damascus, he said, he remembers closing the bathroom door to stand alone for a moment, shutting his eyes to finally feel at peace. When he opened them, he saw a sheet-white, rawboned man staring back from the mirror.</p> <p>\u201cI started screaming,\u201d Masri said. He did not recognize himself.</p> <p class=\"trailer\"> <p>Zakaria reported from Istanbul. Then they crouched, opened their bags and let out a stream of color.\n\nThousands of ping-pong balls, painted green, pink, blue and yellow, bounced past policemen, who scrambled to stop them. Residents would find balls tucked in nooks and crannies for months. Each was marked with a single word: \u201cFreedom.\u201d\n\nThe punishment for Masri\u2019s acts of peaceful protest would begin a journey into hell, unusual not because of what he saw, but because he survived.\n\nIn a series of interviews, he described how he was tortured and interrogated over a two-year period in four detention facilities before arriving in a hospital at the heart of a nationwide system of brutality.\n\nThe hospital, known as 601, is not the only site of torture in Syria. But after it was seen in a cache of photographs showing thousands of skeletal corpses, it became one of the most notorious.\n\nMohsen al-Masri before and after his imprisonment. (Mohsen al-Masri /Mohsen al-Masri )\n\nInside the facility, about a half-mile from Syrian President Bashar al-Assad\u2019s palace, sick prisoners are tortured as they lie shackled to beds crammed with dying men, according to Masri and former detainees and military personnel who worked there. Corpses are piled in bathrooms, outhouses and anywhere else they will fit, then meticulously documented and trucked away for mass burial.\n\nIn interviews across Lebanon, Turkey and Europe, more than a dozen survivors and army defectors described horrors in Syrian military hospitals across the country for which war crimes lawyers say they have struggled to find a modern parallel.\n\nThe former detainees come from all walks of life. Elite, \u00adworking-class, leftist and Islamist, their only connection to one another was involvement in Syria\u2019s 2011 uprising. Some were its instigators. Others said they had simply commented on the Facebook statuses of friends who supported protests.\n\n[How the Syrian revolt went so horribly, tragically wrong]\n\nInvestigators say that testimony and documentation from Syria\u2019s military hospitals offer some of the most concrete evidence to date of crimes against humanity that could one day see senior government figures tried in court.\n\n\u201cWe were swept into a system that was ready for us. Even the hospitals were slaughterhouses,\u201d Masri said in an interview last month.\n\nMedicine has been used as a weapon of war since the earliest days of the uprising, when pro-government doctors performed amputations on protesters for minor injuries.\n\n\n\n\n\nMilitary hospitals across Syria have long set aside wards for prisoners. But since 2011, these have been packed with men left starving and broken by the conditions they have already endured.\n\nMore than 100,000 people have been arrested or forcibly disappeared in Syria since the country\u2019s revolt began, according to a list compiled by the Syrian Network for Human Rights, a monitoring group. During that time, international aid groups have gotten access to only a handful of prisons with the government\u2019s permission, none of which the detainees interviewed by The Washington Post spent time in.\n\nMasri\u2019s ordeal began in the spring of 2012 when he was arrested on his way to a conference in Turkey. Repeatedly tortured as he was transferred from jail to jail, he arrived at Sednaya, one of the most feared.\n\nIn a report published in February, Amnesty International said torture and forced starvation are systematic at the prison. But Masri said that prisoners learned to stay silent when guards asked who needed to go to the hospital.\n\n\u201cIt didn\u2019t matter what they did to us; we had to pretend we were fine. People rarely came back from those trips,\u201d he said.\n\nAfter months of starvation, Masri\u2019s name was added to the weekly transfer list. As darkness fell on an evening in May 2012, he was chained to another man and taken to trucks outside. Attaching a number to Masri\u2019s body, a guard told him to forget his name. Then he was blindfolded.\n\nEveryone gets the \u201cwelcome\u201d party, Masri said \u2014 a savage beating involving guards and medical staffers wearing white coats over military uniforms. In Hospital 601, the weakest man was pushed to the floor and brutalized first. In the nearby Tishreen Military Hospital, a former technician at the facility, Mohammed al-Hammoud, said he had seen prisoners dragged down steps by the hair.\n\n\u201cEverything was about control,\u201d said Somar Mustafa, a physics student from Damascus who was sent to Hospital 601 at the end of 2012. Inside, he saw detainees chained to their beds and packed so tightly that they sat with their knees jutting into their rib cages.\n\nBathroom breaks were so rare that prisoners would defecate where they sat, remaining in the same spot for days. \u201cWe were blindfolded with that smell all around us. You can\u2019t shake the memory of it, even when you leave,\u201d Mustafa said.\n\n[Syria has secretly executed thousands of political prisoners, rights group says]\n\nAt least five branches of the Syrian security forces have operated wards inside Hospital 601 since 2011, according to the U.N. Commission of Inquiry, a body set up to monitor the conflict. \u201cDetainees, including children, have been beaten, burned with cigarettes, and subjected to torture that exploits preexisting injuries,\u201d it said in a 2013 report. The commission concluded that many patients had been tortured to death inside the facility.\n\nThe Harasta Military Hospital, also in Damascus, moved its ward from the first floor to the seventh to prevent detainees from escaping, a defector said. \u201cIt was the only floor without an elevator, and we knew they couldn\u2019t jump out the window.\u201d\n\nInvestigators say the abuses could become central pillars in any eventual case for prosecution of the hospitals\u2019 doctors, as well as senior figures in the Syrian government.\n\nIn 601, Masri and Mustafa said, they saw high-ranking officers from the security branches accompanying doctors on their rounds. Sometimes the teams would pause by a prisoner to discuss his treatment. Other times the men would beat him.\n\nThe doctors were helped by service staffers in blue uniforms, many of them former supporters of the revolt who had been co-\n\nopted by their jailers. \u201cOur best men had been broken by torture. Four survivors said the most famous was known as Azrael, or the Angel of Death. They said he selected prisoners, most of them deathly ill, for a fate he called \u201cjustice.\u201d The detainees called it execution.\n\nMasri recalled Azrael taking a lighter to a plastic bag and melting it drop by drop onto a prisoner\u2019s face until he died, apparently of a heart attack. Then all of a sudden it was. So what did you do with them?\u201d\n\nA December 2012 order signed by the head of Syria\u2019s military intelligence department instructed every security branch to send their dead to a military hospital\u2019s morgue. The document, obtained by the Commission for International Justice and Accountability, a Europe-based investigative unit, said that each body should be examined and logged.\n\nA trove of these photographs was published around the world in 2014, after being smuggled out of Syria by a military police defector known only by his code-name, Caesar. He felt forgotten.\n\nIn the winter of 2014, he dreamed he was taking a hot shower, its stream stripping back two years of dirt and leaving him clean.
