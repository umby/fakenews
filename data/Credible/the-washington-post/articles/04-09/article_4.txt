(Jabin Botsford/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>Stephen K. Bannon could barely finish his sentences as he implored the listeners of his Breitbart News radio show to see the new movie \u201cClinton Cash.\u201d<i></i> </p> <p>It was July 20, the homestretch of the 2016 presidential campaign, and Bannon was describing Bill and Hillary Clinton as \u201cscumbags\u201d and \u201cbandits\u201d who had made millions of dollars through political connections.</p> <p id=\"U12001381707739DMD\">\u201cHillary and Bill Clinton are the two single-biggest grifters ever to run for president of the United States,\u201d Bannon told his guest, Peter Schweizer, the author of the book behind the movie.</p> <p>Bannon, now President Trump\u2019s chief strategist, framed his radio show that day as an urgent effort to reveal important information for voters \u2014 but there was more to it.</p> <p id=\"U12001381707739poC\">The show and \u201cClinton Cash\u201d were components of an intricate multimedia machine comprising nonprofit organizations and private companies that Bannon had leveraged to advance his conservative, populist agenda and bring in millions of dollars. That effort ultimately helped propel Trump into the White House and Bannon into national prominence.</p> <p>A close look behind Bannon\u2019s radio broadcast that day offers insights about how that machine worked.</p> <p>As it happened, the research behind \u201cClinton Cash\u201d had been funded by the Government Accountability Institute, or the GAI, a tax-exempt public charity that Bannon had created a few years earlier and that had paid him hundreds of thousands of dollars as executive chairman, documents show.</p> <p>\u201cClinton Cash\u201d had been produced by Glittering Steel and Bannon Film Industries, two companies owned by Bannon, who was one of the screenwriters.</p> <p>Bannon also was an owner of ARC Entertainment, the firm listed as distributor of the film.</p> <p>And he was receiving a six\u00adfigure salary as executive chairman of Breitbart News, which heavily promoted the film through Bannon\u2019s radio program and its controversial website.</p> <p>There\u2019s no telling how many in his audience understood the connections. Many of the links are scattered among corporate, court and tax records, as well as in a financial disclosure report for 2016 that the White House released last month. </p> <p>During the July broadcast, Bannon only hinted at the connections, saying, \u201cWe\u2019re now going to put this film up for the whole world to see starting this weekend.\u201d</p> <p>A Washington Post examination found that Bannon was able to produce more than a dozen conservative documentaries over the past decade by drawing on a network of two dozen nonprofit organizations and private companies. Bannon helped arrange donations from wealthy Republicans to the nonprofits that paid him for films and other work, documents show. At the same time, Bannon and his firms took in at least $2 million from the nonprofits and an additional $5\u2009million from the private companies, records show.</p> <p>Bannon, who had already made millions on Wall Street, often was paid in multiple ways for each project \u2014 a common practice in Hollywood, where he had worked as an entertainment financier. Because he was paid through the nonprofit and private companies, which have limited obligations to disclose details about their activities, the total pay to Bannon remains unknown.</p> <p>In a personal financial disclosure released by the White House last month, Bannon reported his net worth as between $11.8 million and $53.8 million.</p> <p>Bannon, the White House and Schweizer did not respond to repeated requests for comment.</p> <p>Tax specialists told The Post that some of Bannon\u2019s activities raise questions about compliance with Internal Revenue Service restrictions against using tax\u00adexempt charities to attack a political candidate or for excessive personal financial benefit.</p> <p>\u201cThe appearance is you have a tightknit group of friends and political allies working together to provide benefits, to Mr. Bannon in particular,\u201d said David Nelson, a specialist on nonprofit organizations and former tax partner at the Ernst &amp; Young accounting firm, who reviewed records detailing the complex ties between Bannon and the nonprofit groups.</p> <p>Misuse of the tax-exempt nonprofit system for financial and political benefit has become widespread, but such transgressions rarely result in meaningful penalties, according to the experts interviewed by The Post.</p> <p>\u201cIt is being manipulated so badly,\u201d said Paul Streckfus, a former\u00a0tax lawyer at the IRS and editor of the EO Tax Journal. \u201cIt\u2019s a joke.\u201d</p> <p>David Bossie, who heads two nonprofit groups that collaborated with Bannon on some of his films, praised him in an interview with The Post.</p> <p>\u201cWe stand shoulder-to-shoulder with Steve Bannon and our products. We\u2019re very proud of all our films,\u201d said Bossie, president of the nonprofit Citizens United and its affiliate foundation. \u201cThe First Amendment is an amazing thing.\u201d</p> <div class=\"subhead\">Wall Street and Hollywood</div> <p>Bannon, 63, accumulated millions from the 1980s through the early 2000s as a financier on Wall Street and in Hollywood, where he immersed himself in the nuances of film production and distribution.</p> <p>He had a yen for making movies and could sequester himself for days and weeks to write scripts and film treatments, former colleagues said. Drawing on lessons from both New York and Hollywood, Bannon was able to command substantial consulting fees from companies while serving as a part-time adviser for both financing and creative work.</p> <p>In 2004, Bannon made a \u00adpolitical-oriented documentary, \u201cIn the Face of Evil: Reagan\u2019s War in Word and Deed,\u201d based on a book by Schweizer about the 40th president. During the movie\u2019s promotion, Bannon met a rising star of conservative media named Andrew Breitbart.</p> <p>But more important to Bannon\u2019s career as the auteur of \u00adagenda-driven documentaries was Bossie, who got his start as a political activist and investigator working in opposition to Bill Clinton and his Democratic allies. Bossie had served a controversial stint as the chief investigator for the House Committee on Government Reform and Oversight, under Rep. Dan Burton (R-Ind.).</p> <p>By the mid-2000s, Bossie was the head of Citizens United, a conservative tax-exempt group in the District whose mission was to educate the public about the importance of \u201ca strong national defense, Constitutionally limited government, free market economics, belief in God and Judeo-Christian values, and the recognition of the family as the basic social unit of our society.\u201d</p> <p>At the time, Bannon was working as chairman of the board for a media-distribution company in Los Angeles called Genius Products, which focused on independent films. </p> <p>\u00a0\u201cSteve really liked the idea of helping the smaller guy, the smaller independent filmmakers\u201d who might not get attention from larger distributors, Trevor Drinkwater, chief executive of Genius, told The Post in an interview.</p> <p>Bossie and Bannon bonded over their shared interest in using film to spread a conservative message. Both men wanted to replicate what liberal activist Michael Moore had done two years earlier with his cinematic scorching of President George W. Bush in \u201cFahrenheit 9/11.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cWe wanted to deliver a message,\u201d Bossie told The Post.\u00a0</p> <p>In 2006, Bossie envisioned a movie, to be called \u201cBorder War: The Battle Over Illegal Immigration,\u201d that would take viewers into drug- and human-trafficking tunnels under the U.S. border.\u00a0Bannon agreed to serve as executive producer of the film. He agreed to use Genius, his fledgling company, to distribute it on DVD.</p> <p>It was the start of a lucrative, increasingly influential relationship that in the coming decade would help him rise in the conservative ranks \u2014 and increase the wealth Bannon had amassed on Wall Street and in Hollywood. According to a divorce filing submitted by his third wife in 2008, Bannon had assets worth $41 million.</p> <p>Corporate documents show Genius, a public company, started out paying his consulting firm, Bannon Strategic Advisors, $250,000 per year. Drinkwater said Bannon offered business advice and had ties to investment bankers and hedge-fund managers. \u201cHe had incredible connections everywhere. I don\u2019t think there was one meeting he couldn\u2019t get,\u201d Drinkwater said.</p> <p>Even as Genius struggled financially, Bannon continued earning his fees, about $1.5 million over five years, court records show. In May 2008, in a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Genius said, \u201cWe have a history of significant losses, and we may never achieve or sustain profitability.\u201d The company later went bankrupt.</p> <div class=\"subhead\">A side career</div> <p>In 2010, Bannon and Drinkwater started a film-distribution company based in Santa Monica, Calif., called ARC Entertainment. At the same time, Bannon jumped headlong into his side career as a political-documentary filmmaker.</p> <p>It was a fertile time for a conservative filmmaking. The midterm elections were approaching, the tea party was coalescing in opposition to President Barack Obama and radical conservatives saw themselves at war with what they perceived as a federal government bent on imposing godless socialism.</p> <p>In January 2010, the Supreme Court voted 5 to 4 in favor of Citizens United in a two-year-old lawsuit that the charity filed over election rules prohibiting it from spending money to promote an anti-Clinton documentary during the 2008 primary season. That decision allowed new forms of unlimited political spending by organizations such as Citizens United \u2014 an opening that was immediately seized upon by mega\u00addonors on the right opposed to the Obama administration.</p> <p>When Citizens United decided to move forward with additional films, Bossie turned to Bannon to write, direct and produce for Citizens United Productions, a for-profit company started by the nonprofit organization and an anonymous investor. Bossie declined to reveal the investor\u2019s identity.</p> <p>Tax filings examined by The Post show that Citizens United invested hundreds of thousands of dollars in the first movie, \u201cGeneration Zero.\u201d Bossie was co-producer.</p> <p>The film focused on the global financial crisis that began in 2008. It had the hallmarks of a Bannon production. Fast-cut editing. Ominous music. The use of images of tornadoes and nuclear explosions to symbolize the ravages of Wall Street\u2019s meltdown, along with footage of free-loving hippies to suggest that the blame lay with the political left.</p> <p>\u201cHe\u2019s an incredible visionary filmmaker and well read, and he gets immersed in his subject matter,\u201d Bossie said of Bannon. \u201cHe\u2019s brilliant, a brilliant writer and director.\u201d</p> <p>Costs were kept down on the low-budget films by the use of stock footage and commentary from Republican luminaries. It is not clear from the public documents how much of the fees paid to Bannon or his companies went to production costs or overhead.</p> <p>In 2010, Bannon made the next two films, \u201cBattle for America,\u201d a harsh critique of the Obama administration and big government, and \u201cFire from the Heartland,\u201d which spotlights the rise of conservative women. Citizens United Foundation Productions spent $1.7 million that year on the production and distribution of what it called \u201ceducational documentary films,\u201d while Citizens United spent $1.9 million on film production, according to financial filings.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon1.jpg?uuid=ieyluBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon1.jpg?uuid=ieyluBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon1.jpg?uuid=ieyluBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon1.jpg?uuid=ieyluBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\"/><br/> </div> <p>That same year, Bannon\u2019s filmmaking career got a boost from another conservative tax-exempt charity.</p> <p>Young America\u2019s Foundation in Northern Virginia hired Bannon Film Industries to begin work on at least three films, paying nearly $578,000 between 2010 and 2012, according to tax filings. That included two documentaries for the Reagan Ranch in Santa Barbara, Calif., which the foundation manages, and \u201cThe Conservatives,\u201d a 29-minute short film meant to inspire conservative student leaders on college campuses.</p> <p>On its website, the foundation calls itself \u201cthe principal outreach organization of the Conservative Movement.\u201d It was led by a conservative activist, Ronald Robinson, who also sat on the boards of Citizens United and the Citizens United Foundation.</p> <p>Driven by a fount of ideas and restless energy, Bannon was becoming a tribune of the insurgent conservative vanguard. \u201cI think he worked on a lot of things simultaneously,\u201d Drinkwater said. \u201cThat\u2019s his personality. He probably sleeps three hours a night and can do a lot of things at the same time well.\u201d</p> <p>In addition to his fees for writing, directing and producing, Bannon was receiving $22,500 a month, or $270,000 annually, in consulting fees from ARC Entertainment, his film-distribution company, according to a home lease application Bannon signed in 2013 that was obtained by The Post this spring.</p> <p>Kirk Wickstrom, chief financial officer of ARC Entertainment from 2010 to 2013, said that Bannon was the company\u2019s sole board member and played an advisory role. \u201cHe wasn\u2019t tremendously hands-on,\u201d Wickstrom said. \u201cWe didn\u2019t use him that much; mostly when deals came along, he\u2019d take a look at them.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001381707739gcB\">In 2011, Bannon decided to make a film about Sarah Palin, a darling of the tea party and presumed presidential contender, to be known as \u201cThe Undefeated.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001381707739TkE\">According to one associate who spoke on the condition of anonymity because he did not want national exposure, Bannon had developed a set of principles for his filmmaking. They would be built around \u201cmale-, testosterone-, spiritual-driven content.\u201d And Bannon would \u201cnever put [his] own money into any project.\u201d</p> <p id=\"U12001381707739j7D\">The producer of the Palin film was a Tallahassee-based company that Bannon had formed with a few investors in 2010. Bannon told some of his investors that he was treating \u201cThe Undefeated\u201d as a moneymaking venture and expected solid returns, the associate said. Bannon\u2019s company, ARC Entertainment, would distribute it.</p> <p>Despite Bannon\u2019s optimism, the movie tanked after Palin backed away from a White House run.</p> <div class=\"subhead\">Forging an alliance</div> <p>In 2011, Bannon forged an alliance with the wealthy Mercer family in New York.</p> <p>Robert Mercer was a hedge-fund billionaire who was on his way to becoming one of the most generous conservative political donors in the country.</p> <p>Mercer and his daughter Rebekah Mercer operated the Mercer Family Foundation, a fast-growing source of funding for conservative causes, with assets of nearly $38 million as of 2012. They began relying on Bannon for political advice.</p> <p>Bannon drafted a business plan for the family that called on them to invest $10 million in the fledgling Breitbart News operated by Bannon\u2019s friend Andrew Breitbart, according to the New Yorker magazine. Bannon joined the board of the news site. Documents show that money from the Mercer Family Foundation began flowing to some of the same charities that funded Bannon\u2019s films and other work.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon2.jpg?uuid=iwak5By3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon2.jpg?uuid=iwak5By3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon2.jpg?uuid=iwak5By3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon2.jpg?uuid=iwak5By3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\"/><br/> </div> <p>In 2012, the Mercer foundation donated $2 million to the nonprofit Citizens United Foundation, which then gave one of Bannon\u2019s consulting firms $300,000 as a \u201cfund raising fee,\u201d according to IRS documents.</p> <p>New York and other states require that individuals or firms that raise money for nonprofit organizations register with the state. Bannon\u2019s firm was not registered in New York at the time, state officials told The Post.</p> <p id=\"U12001381707739z0G\">When asked about the fee, Bossie, president of both Citizens United and its foundation, described the fundraising label as a clerical error. He said the payment was actually related to film consulting that Bannon provided. In a statement, Bossie said the fee \u201chas no material bearing on the organization\u2019s financial affairs and given the time that has transpired does not merit a correction\u201d to the group\u2019s tax filing.</p> <p>An audit report for the Citizens United Foundation that year appears to contradict Bossie. It lists fundraising expenses of $301,200, according to a copy of the audit examined by The Post.</p> <p>After The Post asked about the discrepancy, officials in the New York attorney general\u2019s office issued letters last month to both Citizens United and Bannon\u2019s consulting firm seeking more information.</p> <p>Bannon would receive an additional $150,000 in fees after the Mercer Family Foundation donated $1\u2009million to the Citizens United Foundation in 2013. In its annual filing to the IRS for that year, Citizens United described the $150,000 as a payment for both film consulting and fundraising, and the group\u2019s auditors listed $150,000 as the group\u2019s spending on fundraising.</p> <p>Other money flowed to Bannon for new political documentaries. Working with Citizens United, he was the writer, director and producer of \u201cThe Hope &amp; The Change,\u201d an examination of Democrats disaffected with Obama, and \u201cOccupy Unmasked,\u201d a salvo aimed at the political left and Wall Street protesters.</p> <p>That same year, Bannon collaborated with a conservative charity, Judicial Watch, on a film called \u201cDistrict of Corruption,\u201d a critique of the Obama administration and the money flooding into Washington. Judicial Watch, which describes itself as an accountability-oversight group, paid Bannon\u2019s Victory Film Project $382,143, documents show.</p> <div class=\"subhead\">An \u2018educational charity\u2019</div> <p>In March 2012, Bannon formed his own nonprofit group, the GAI. In filings with the IRS seeking tax-exempt status, he said it would operate as an \u201ceducational charity\u201d to promote \u201ceconomic freedom\u201d and seek to expose corruption.</p> <p>Such groups are known as 501(c)(3) charities, a title related to a section of the tax code they fall under.</p> <p>The GAI was based in Tallahassee. Bannon served as chairman and executive director, earning an estimated $100,000 annually, documents show. His colleague, Schweizer, served as president and received an estimated $200,000. Robinson, named as a GAI director, was unpaid, the document shows.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon3.jpg?uuid=i7UxbBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon3.jpg?uuid=i7UxbBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon3.jpg?uuid=i7UxbBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon3.jpg?uuid=i7UxbBy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\"/><br/> </div> <p>Bannon, Schweizer and Robinson were linked through nonprofit organizations and filmmaking work. Bannon and Schweizer had made the film about President Ronald Reagan together in 2004; more recently, they were colleagues at Breitbart News.\u00a0Schweizer sat on the board of Robinson\u2019s Young America\u2019s Foundation, which had paid Bannon $404,904 to produce films in 2010 and 2011, documents show, and would pay him almost $173,000 in 2012.</p> <p>In the document seeking tax-exempt status for the GAI, Bannon signed a document under penalty of perjury repeatedly attesting that each of them \u201chas no family or business relationships with any other persons involved with GAI.\u201d</p> <p>A GAI spokeswoman told The Post that the relationships were later disclosed in the group\u2019s annual filing with the IRS \u2014 submitted in November 2013, nine months after the GAI\u2019s tax-exempt status was approved.</p> <p>Charities are required to disclose business relationships among their directors on their initial applications for tax exemption so that the IRS can examine whether the organization is being formed for a proper public purpose.</p> <p>Robinson told The Post that he had not seen the application.</p> <p>\u201cI do not know the definition of \u2018business relationship,\u2019 \u201d he said. \u201cThat is an issue to take up with those who prepared it.\u201d</p> <p>The GAI would only have a relative handful of donors in the coming years. Its first $2 million infusion of cash came from another conservative charity.</p> <p>The Mercer Family Foundation and Donor\u2019s Trust, a conservative nonprofit group tied to the wealthy Koch brothers, would donate more than $7 million to the GAI over the following three years, documents show.</p> <p>A spokeswoman for the GAI previously told The Post that the organization \u201cis and always has been in total compliance with all 501(c)(3) rules.\u201d</p> <p>She also said that the group has a \u201cbipartisan track record of working with top investigative units in media on award-winning reports.\u201d</p> <div class=\"subhead\">The Breitbart era</div> <p>Early on March 19, 2012, the same day that the IRS accepted the GAI application, Bannon took on yet another leadership role: He was named the executive chairman of Breitbart News, filling the void left by the unexpected death of Andrew Breitbart earlier that month.</p> <p>Wickstrom, the CFO of ARC Entertainment, said he was with Bannon on a business trip in New York when Bannon got the call that Breitbart had died. Bannon spent the day on the phone, he said.</p> <p>\u201cHe felt very much responsible for [Breitbart News],\u201d Wickstrom said.</p> <p>Bannon immediately began to expand Breitbart\u2019s reach while also working with Schweizer and others to put the GAI on the political and journalistic map.</p> <p>In February 2013, Bannon said on a rental application that the Mercer-backed Breitbart News was paying him $62,500 per month, the equivalent of $750,000 a year. He also reported receiving $377,000 in pay between 2012 and 2015 from the GAI for working an average of 30 hours per week.</p> <p>In 2014 and 2015, Bannon took on additional paid positions in two Mercer-backed companies that were positioning themselves to play a role in the 2016 presidential election.</p> <p>Bannon became a vice president of Cambridge Analytica, a data-science firm, in June 2014. With Rebekah Mercer, he also co-founded another film-production firm called Glittering Steel LLC and became its chairman, documents show.</p> <p>Through it all, Bannon was managing Breitbart\u2019s 24/7 news operation and also hosted a radio program. Bannon often highlighted the GAI\u2019s work and invited its employees as guests on his show. As chairman of the GAI, Bannon approved spending more than $200,000 on advertising on Breitbart News\u2019s website, according to disclosures that the charity submitted to the IRS.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon4.jpg?uuid=iTMkihy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon4.jpg?uuid=iTMkihy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon4.jpg?uuid=iTMkihy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/04/09/Investigative/Images/bannon4.jpg?uuid=iTMkihy3EeeYhxpTFLVqCA\"/><br/> </div> <p>Bannon also was maintaining ties to Bossie from Citizens United and often invited Bossie onto his Breitbart News radio program. In 2015, the GAI also gave $125,000 to Citizens United Foundation, calling the donation \u201cgeneral public assistance.\u201d</p> <p>Citizens United and Bannon\u2019s Glittering Steel produced his 2016 \u201cTorchbearer,\u201d featuring \u201cDuck Dynasty\u201d star Phil Robertson, a film that Breitbart News said was a \u201cclarion call for Christians and people of faith in America to engage their culture before it\u2019s too late.\u201d</p> <p>The efforts of Bannon\u2019s network culminated with release of \u201cClinton Cash,\u201d the book Schweizer wrote using researchers from the GAI. Bannon, Schweizer and others turned it into a movie with the help of Glittering Steel, which paid Bannon $167,500 in 2016, documents show.</p> <p>Bannon promoted the movie in the run-up to the presidential election. He invited Schweizer onto his radio program on April 29, 2016, to discuss the Clintons.</p> <p>\u201cWhat is it about the permanent political class and how they\u2019re stealing from the audience that listens to this show and goes to Breitbart every day?\u201d Bannon asked.</p> <p>Schweizer responded, \u201cI mean, this film and \u2018Clinton Cash\u2019 .\u2009.\u2009. it\u2019s not a book about stuff that\u2019s happening in the abstract. It\u2019s about how a political class of leaders, personified by the Clintons .\u2009.\u2009. are systematically selling out the interests of the country for personal profit.\u201d</p> <p>On July 20, 2016, as the campaign was going into overdrive, Bannon again had Schweizer on Breitbart News\u2019s SiriusXM radio program, broadcast from the Republican National Convention in Cleveland.</p> <p>\u201cThese people are corrupt,\u201d Bannon said. \u201cThey are corrupt from stem to stern.\u201d</p> <p>Less than a month later, Bannon became the chief executive of Trump\u2019s campaign. Bossie, of Citizens United, joined as his deputy.</p> </article> <div class=\"clear\"></div> </div> </div> <div class=\"pb-container\"> <div class=\"moat-trackable pb-f-theme-normal pb-3 pb-feature The use of images of tornadoes and nuclear explosions to symbolize the ravages of Wall Street\u2019s meltdown, along with footage of free-loving hippies to suggest that the blame lay with the political left.\n\n\u201cHe\u2019s an incredible visionary filmmaker and well read, and he gets immersed in his subject matter,\u201d Bossie said of Bannon. \u201cHe\u2019s brilliant, a brilliant writer and director.\u201d\n\nCosts were kept down on the low-budget films by the use of stock footage and commentary from Republican luminaries. It is not clear from the public documents how much of the fees paid to Bannon or his companies went to production costs or overhead.\n\nIn 2010, Bannon made the next two films, \u201cBattle for America,\u201d a harsh critique of the Obama administration and big government, and \u201cFire from the Heartland,\u201d which spotlights the rise of conservative women. Citizens United Foundation Productions spent $1.7 million that year on the production and distribution of what it called \u201ceducational documentary films,\u201d while Citizens United spent $1.9 million on film production, according to financial filings.\n\nThat same year, Bannon\u2019s filmmaking career got a boost from another conservative tax-exempt charity.\n\nYoung America\u2019s Foundation in Northern Virginia hired Bannon Film Industries to begin work on at least three films, paying nearly $578,000 between 2010 and 2012, according to tax filings. That included two documentaries for the Reagan Ranch in Santa Barbara, Calif., which the foundation manages, and \u201cThe Conservatives,\u201d a 29-minute short film meant to inspire conservative student leaders on college campuses.\n\nOn its website, the foundation calls itself \u201cthe principal outreach organization of the Conservative Movement.\u201d It was led by a conservative activist, Ronald Robinson, who also sat on the boards of Citizens United and the Citizens United Foundation.\n\nDriven by a fount of ideas and restless energy, Bannon was becoming a tribune of the insurgent conservative vanguard. \u201cI think he worked on a lot of things simultaneously,\u201d Drinkwater said. \u201cThat\u2019s his personality. He probably sleeps three hours a night and can do a lot of things at the same time well.\u201d\n\nIn addition to his fees for writing, directing and producing, Bannon was receiving $22,500 a month, or $270,000 annually, in consulting fees from ARC Entertainment, his film-distribution company, according to a home lease application Bannon signed in 2013 that was obtained by The Post this spring.\n\nKirk Wickstrom, chief financial officer of ARC Entertainment from 2010 to 2013, said that Bannon was the company\u2019s sole board member and played an advisory role. \u201cHe wasn\u2019t tremendously hands-on,\u201d Wickstrom said. \u201cWe didn\u2019t use him that much; mostly when deals came along, he\u2019d take a look at them.\u201d\n\nIn 2011, Bannon decided to make a film about Sarah Palin, a darling of the tea party and presumed presidential contender, to be known as \u201cThe Undefeated.\u201d\n\nAccording to one associate who spoke on the condition of anonymity because he did not want national exposure, Bannon had developed a set of principles for his filmmaking. They would be built around \u201cmale-, testosterone-, spiritual-driven content.\u201d And Bannon would \u201cnever put [his] own money into any project.\u201d\n\nThe producer of the Palin film was a Tallahassee-based company that Bannon had formed with a few investors in 2010. Bannon told some of his investors that he was treating \u201cThe Undefeated\u201d as a moneymaking venture and expected solid returns, the associate said. Bannon\u2019s company, ARC Entertainment, would distribute it.\n\nDespite Bannon\u2019s optimism, the movie tanked after Palin backed away from a White House run.\n\nForging an alliance\n\nIn 2011, Bannon forged an alliance with the wealthy Mercer family in New York.\n\nRobert Mercer was a hedge-fund billionaire who was on his way to becoming one of the most generous conservative political donors in the country.\n\nMercer and his daughter Rebekah Mercer operated the Mercer Family Foundation, a fast-growing source of funding for conservative causes, with assets of nearly $38 million as of 2012. They began relying on Bannon for political advice.\n\nBannon drafted a business plan for the family that called on them to invest $10 million in the fledgling Breitbart News operated by Bannon\u2019s friend Andrew Breitbart, according to the New Yorker magazine. Bannon joined the board of the news site. Documents show that money from the Mercer Family Foundation began flowing to some of the same charities that funded Bannon\u2019s films and other work.\n\nIn 2012, the Mercer foundation donated $2 million to the nonprofit Citizens United Foundation, which then gave one of Bannon\u2019s consulting firms $300,000 as a \u201cfund raising fee,\u201d according to IRS documents.\n\nNew York and other states require that individuals or firms that raise money for nonprofit organizations register with the state. Bannon\u2019s firm was not registered in New York at the time, state officials told The Post.\n\nWhen asked about the fee, Bossie, president of both Citizens United and its foundation, described the fundraising label as a clerical error. He said the payment was actually related to film consulting that Bannon provided. In a statement, Bossie said the fee \u201chas no material bearing on the organization\u2019s financial affairs and given the time that has transpired does not merit a correction\u201d to the group\u2019s tax filing.\n\nAn audit report for the Citizens United Foundation that year appears to contradict Bossie. It lists fundraising expenses of $301,200, according to a copy of the audit examined by The Post.\n\nAfter The Post asked about the discrepancy, officials in the New York attorney general\u2019s office issued letters last month to both Citizens United and Bannon\u2019s consulting firm seeking more information.\n\nBannon would receive an additional $150,000 in fees after the Mercer Family Foundation donated $1 million to the Citizens United Foundation in 2013. In its annual filing to the IRS for that year, Citizens United described the $150,000 as a payment for both film consulting and fundraising, and the group\u2019s auditors listed $150,000 as the group\u2019s spending on fundraising.\n\nOther money flowed to Bannon for new political documentaries. Working with Citizens United, he was the writer, director and producer of \u201cThe Hope & The Change,\u201d an examination of Democrats disaffected with Obama, and \u201cOccupy Unmasked,\u201d a salvo aimed at the political left and Wall Street protesters.\n\nThat same year, Bannon collaborated with a conservative charity, Judicial Watch, on a film called \u201cDistrict of Corruption,\u201d a critique of the Obama administration and the money flooding into Washington. Judicial Watch, which describes itself as an accountability-oversight group, paid Bannon\u2019s Victory Film Project $382,143, documents show.\n\nAn \u2018educational charity\u2019\n\nIn March 2012, Bannon formed his own nonprofit group, the GAI. In filings with the IRS seeking tax-exempt status, he said it would operate as an \u201ceducational charity\u201d to promote \u201ceconomic freedom\u201d and seek to expose corruption.\n\nSuch groups are known as 501(c)(3) charities, a title related to a section of the tax code they fall under.\n\nThe GAI was based in Tallahassee. Bannon served as chairman and executive director, earning an estimated $100,000 annually, documents show. His colleague, Schweizer, served as president and received an estimated $200,000. Robinson, named as a GAI director, was unpaid, the document shows.\n\nBannon, Schweizer and Robinson were linked through nonprofit organizations and filmmaking work. Bannon and Schweizer had made the film about President Ronald Reagan together in 2004; more recently, they were colleagues at Breitbart News. Schweizer sat on the board of Robinson\u2019s Young America\u2019s Foundation, which had paid Bannon $404,904 to produce films in 2010 and 2011, documents show, and would pay him almost $173,000 in 2012.\n\nIn the document seeking tax-exempt status for the GAI, Bannon signed a document under penalty of perjury repeatedly attesting that each of them \u201chas no family or business relationships with any other persons involved with GAI.\u201d\n\nA GAI spokeswoman told The Post that the relationships were later disclosed in the group\u2019s annual filing with the IRS \u2014 submitted in November 2013, nine months after the GAI\u2019s tax-exempt status was approved.\n\nCharities are required to disclose business relationships among their directors on their initial applications for tax exemption so that the IRS can examine whether the organization is being formed for a proper public purpose.\n\nRobinson told The Post that he had not seen the application.\n\n\u201cI do not know the definition of \u2018business relationship,\u2019 \u201d he said. \u201cThat is an issue to take up with those who prepared it.\u201d\n\nThe GAI would only have a relative handful of donors in the coming years. Its first $2 million infusion of cash came from another conservative charity.\n\nThe Mercer Family Foundation and Donor\u2019s Trust, a conservative nonprofit group tied to the wealthy Koch brothers, would donate more than $7 million to the GAI over the following three years, documents show.\n\nA spokeswoman for the GAI previously told The Post that the organization \u201cis and always has been in total compliance with all 501(c)(3) rules.\u201d\n\nShe also said that the group has a \u201cbipartisan track record of working with top investigative units in media on award-winning reports.\u201d\n\nThe Breitbart era\n\nEarly on March 19, 2012, the same day that the IRS accepted the GAI application, Bannon took on yet another leadership role: He was named the executive chairman of Breitbart News, filling the void left by the unexpected death of Andrew Breitbart earlier that month.\n\nWickstrom, the CFO of ARC Entertainment, said he was with Bannon on a business trip in New York when Bannon got the call that Breitbart had died. Bannon spent the day on the phone, he said.\n\n\u201cHe felt very much responsible for [Breitbart News],\u201d Wickstrom said.\n\nBannon immediately began to expand Breitbart\u2019s reach while also working with Schweizer and others to put the GAI on the political and journalistic map.\n\nIn February 2013, Bannon said on a rental application that the Mercer-backed Breitbart News was paying him $62,500 per month, the equivalent of $750,000 a year. He also reported receiving $377,000 in pay between 2012 and 2015 from the GAI for working an average of 30 hours per week.\n\nIn 2014 and 2015, Bannon took on additional paid positions in two Mercer-backed companies that were positioning themselves to play a role in the 2016 presidential election.\n\nBannon became a vice president of Cambridge Analytica, a data-science firm, in June 2014. With Rebekah Mercer, he also co-founded another film-production firm called Glittering Steel LLC and became its chairman, documents show.\n\nThrough it all, Bannon was managing Breitbart\u2019s 24/7 news operation and also hosted a radio program. Bannon often highlighted the GAI\u2019s work and invited its employees as guests on his show. As chairman of the GAI, Bannon approved spending more than $200,000 on advertising on Breitbart News\u2019s website, according to disclosures that the charity submitted to the IRS.\n\nBannon also was maintaining ties to Bossie from Citizens United and often invited Bossie onto his Breitbart News radio program. In 2015, the GAI also gave $125,000 to Citizens United Foundation, calling the donation \u201cgeneral public assistance.\u201d\n\nCitizens United and Bannon\u2019s Glittering Steel produced his 2016 \u201cTorchbearer,\u201d featuring \u201cDuck Dynasty\u201d star Phil Robertson, a film that Breitbart News said was a \u201cclarion call for Christians and people of faith in America to engage their culture before it\u2019s too late.\u201d\n\nThe efforts of Bannon\u2019s network culminated with release of \u201cClinton Cash,\u201d the book Schweizer wrote using researchers from the GAI. Bannon, Schweizer and others turned it into a movie with the help of Glittering Steel, which paid Bannon $167,500 in 2016, documents show.\n\nBannon promoted the movie in the run-up to the presidential election. He invited Schweizer onto his radio program on April 29, 2016, to discuss the Clintons.\n\n\u201cWhat is it about the permanent political class and how they\u2019re stealing from the audience that listens to this show and goes to Breitbart every day?\u201d Bannon asked.\n\nSchweizer responded, \u201cI mean, this film and \u2018Clinton Cash\u2019 . . . it\u2019s not a book about stuff that\u2019s happening in the abstract. It\u2019s about how a political class of leaders, personified by the Clintons . . . are systematically selling out the interests of the country for personal profit.\u201d\n\nOn July 20, 2016, as the campaign was going into overdrive, Bannon again had Schweizer on Breitbart News\u2019s SiriusXM radio program, broadcast from the Republican National Convention in Cleveland.\n\n\u201cThese people are corrupt,\u201d Bannon said. \u201cThey are corrupt from stem to stern.\u201d\n\nLess than a month later, Bannon became the chief executive of Trump\u2019s campaign.
