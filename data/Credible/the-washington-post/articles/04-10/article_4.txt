(Spencer Platt/Getty Images)</span> </div> <div class=\"pb-sig-line hasnt-headshot has-0-headshots hasnt-bio is-not-column\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/thomas-heath/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Thomas Heath</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-byline-tool-mobile\"> <span class=\"pb-byline\" itemprop=\"author\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/Person\">By <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/people/thomas-heath/\"><span itemprop=\"name\">Thomas Heath</span></a></span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:thomas.heath@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Tesla\u2019s \u2018crazy\u2019 climb to America\u2019s most valuable car company'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> </span> <span class=\"pb-timestamp\" itemprop=\"datePublished\" content=\"2017-04-10T07:24-500\">April 10 at 7:24 PM</span> <span class=\"pb-tool email\"><a href=\"mailto:thomas.heath@washpost.com?subject=Reader feedback for 'Tesla\u2019s \u2018crazy\u2019 climb to America\u2019s most valuable car company'\"><span class=\"fa fa-envelope\"></span></a></span> <span class=\"tweet-authors\"><span class=\"pb-twitter-follow unprocessed\"><a href=\"https://twitter.com/@addedvalueth\" class=\"twitter-follow-button\" data-show-count=\"false\" data-lang=\"en\">Follow @addedvalueth</a></span> </span> </div> <article itemprop=\"articleBody\"> <p>The luxury electric-car company Tesla has yet to turn a profit, losing hundreds of millions of dollars last year alone. But on Monday, the darling of Silicon Valley became the most valuable American car company, surpassing General Motors, the Detroit granddaddy with $10 billion in sales on nearly 10 million vehicles.</p> <p>Shares of Tesla, run by high-profile chief executive Elon Musk, put the company\u2019s value at $51.5\u00a0billion, above GM\u2019s $50.2\u00a0billion. Tesla blew by Ford ($44.6 billion) last week. </p> <p>Musk\u2019s company produced just 84,000 cars last year, with starting prices of $68,000. </p> <p> </p> <p>The story of Tesla\u2019s rise speaks to the divided American economy in 2017. Eco-friendly government tax credits, a boom in financial backing and the promise of futuristic innovation have created in Tesla a badge for the drivers who can afford its lofty prices.</p><div></div> <p>At the same time, Tesla, with its long-running saga of production problems, hasn\u2019t come close to fulfilling its mass-market ambitions. Beyond selling far fewer cars than its Detroit rivals, its automated factories employ a fraction of GM\u2019s factory workforce. </p> <p>Tesla\u2019s stock-market rise has made Musk one of the country\u2019s richest people and given him widespread influence, including another meeting with President Trump on Tuesday. But even critics who say Tesla could represent a technology bubble in the stock market acknowledge that the company\u2019s success points to a new reality in the automotive industry that will reshape the experience of driving for most Americans.</p> <p>\u201cThis is the ultimate bubble, which is doomed to burst,\u201d former GM vice chairman Bob Lutz said. \u201cTesla cars are fine, but the business model is not,\u201d he said, pointing to the high cost of production, which is not recovered in the sale price. But, he added, \u201call legacy car companies will soon have a variety\u201d of similar electric vehicles.</p> <p>Tesla has two models for sale and a third due this year. The Model S starts at $68,000 and goes up to $134,000, depending on power and speed. The Tesla Model X, its sport-utility vehicle, begins at $85,000.</p> <p>Its midmarket entry, the long-awaited Tesla Model 3, is expected to hit showrooms in the second half of the year and is priced around $35,000. At that price, the Model 3 will compete with GM\u2019s Chevrolet all-electric Bolt and Ford\u2019s all-electric Focus.</p> <p>Ivan Feinseth, chief investment officer at Tigress Financial Partners, said Tesla competes in a rarefied market: \u201cThe car is a high-performance, luxury car that happens to have an electric engine,\u201d Feinseth said. \u201cIt competes with BMW, Mercedes and Lexus.\u201d</p> <p>The stratospheric climb of Tesla\u2019s stock price from $40 in 2013 to more than $312 in trading on Monday, propelled the company to its highest value yet.</p><div></div> <p>A stock analyst\u2019s recommendation to buy Tesla shares is \u201cone of the more absurd I\u2019ve seen in a while,\u201d said Michael Farr, president of Farr, Miller &amp; Washington, a D.C. investment firm. He noted that the company is forecast to lose money in 2018 but that the stock is expected to increase to $368 a share. </p> <p>\u201cInvestors were asked to employ a \u2018creative\u2019 valuation methodology. I think that means that when the numbers don\u2019t make any sense, one should ignore them and focus on other things,\u201d Farr said. \u201cIt\u2019s like being told to ignore the flames coming out of that airplane, I\u2019m sure your trip will be fine.\u201d </p> <p>Feinseth said when you look at Tesla\u2019s potential for growth, its dealer network, supply chain and the quality of the Tesla car itself, the value makes more sense.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s not unusual to value growth companies even to the point of being a little crazy,\u201d Feinseth said.</p> <p> Tesla\u2019s reputation as beyond-a-car company \u2014 it recently absorbed Musk\u2019s Solar City company for $5 billion \u2014 has captured the imagination of California\u2019s technology pack and, apparently, investors. The company has been developing batteries that could store power from rooftop solar panels, expanding its mission into a renewable-energy enterprise. Tesla also is exploring technology for self-driving cars.</p> <p>Musk\u2019s outreach to Trump and the new administration\u2019s emphasis on U.S. manufacturing is working to Tesla\u2019s advantage, helping propel the stock even more since the November election and by more than 40 percent since January.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/economy/teslas-feat-of-financial-engineering/2013/09/20/c4d4667e-e26a-11e2-a11e-c2ea876a8f30_story.html?utm_term=.9575f85dd376\" shape=\"rect\">Tesla\u2019s feat of financial engineering</a>]</i> </p> <p>\u201cThe market is willing to say I\u2019m going to value you based on what I think your future potential is, and it could be off of what you think they could make selling cars, what they could make selling batteries, what they could make selling solar or what they could make as a mobility company,\u201d said Matthew Stover, an analyst with Susquehanna Financial Group. \u201cEverything is on the table, and it\u2019s all speculative.\u201d</p> <p>Musk, 45, is a South African-born Canadian American business mogul with an estimated net worth of $14.8 billion, according to the latest estimates by Forbes magazine. He made his fortune selling money-transfer- service PayPal to eBay in 2002 for $1.5\u00a0billion. </p> <p> Like Amazon.com founder (and Washington Post owner) Jeffrey P. Bezos, Musk is known for pursuits that take him beyond his primary business. Bezos, through Blue Origin, and Musk, through SpaceX, have launched private companies aimed at human spaceflight.</p> <p>Despite\u2019s GM\u2019s dominance in car sales (its U.S. market share is 17.3 percent compared with 0.2\u00a0percent for Tesla), analysts admire what Musk has built.</p> <p>\u201cMaking cars is a hard business,\u201d Feinseth said, \u201cand putting together in such a short time a state-of-the-art manufacturing facility along with a dealer and showrooms and a support network of service centers and charging facilities, that is very hard.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/economy/the-battle-between-tesla-and-your-neighborhood-car-dealership/2016/09/09/55fb1878-6864-11e6-99bf-f0cf3a6449a6_story.html?utm_term=.4dde651492b2\" shape=\"rect\">The battle between Tesla and your neighborhood car dealership</a>]</i> </p> <p>Despite the engaging story, the Tesla ending is still up for grabs. </p> <p>One challenge is drivers\u2019 fear that they will run out of power and be left stranded, known as range anxiety. Tesla\u2019s models have a range generally less than 250 miles.</p> <p> According to Tesla\u2019s website, Tesla buyers, like all buyers of electric vehicles, may claim a $7,500 federal income tax credit. Several states offer additional incentives, often taking the form of a rebate. </p> <p>There is some chance that a Republican Congress and administration could remove such incentives, although investor fears have apparently receded based on the soaring stock price.</p> <p>Stover said that Tesla still has to prove it can make money.
