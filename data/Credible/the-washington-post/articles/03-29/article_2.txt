His undocumented wife, Veronica Castro, sits behind him with their disabled son, Juan. (Griselda San Martin/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>Ricardo Pineda was hesitant to wear his uniform.</p> <p>Two years had passed since he had served in the Army. Then again, so much was at stake, and the disabled veteran knew the uniform would leave no confusion about who he was: a man who had been willing to die for this country and now needed help to keep his family living in it.</p> <p>Pineda straightened the nameplate on his dress blues one day last month and entered a room in the Rayburn House Office Building, where he took a seat at a wooden table with members of the Congressional Hispanic Caucus. His wife, Veronica Castro, stood behind him in a red blouse, and next to her, with his hair buzzed military short like his father\u2019s, sat their son Juan, a 17-year-old who suffered brain damage during heart surgery as a toddler.</p> <p>When Pineda\u2019s turn came to speak, he told the lawmakers about his family\u2019s precarious situation.</p> <p>On April 4, Castro will walk into an Immigration and Customs Enforcement office in Baltimore, and she doesn\u2019t know if she will be allowed to return home to her husband and their four children, who are all U.S. citizens.<b></b> </p> <p>Castro, who twice entered the country illegally<b> </b>from Mexico, has faced these check-ins since 2011. But this one is different, she said. This is the 38-year-old\u2019s first appointment with ICE since the inauguration of President Trump, whose aggressive stance on illegal immigration has widened the pool of those <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/post-nation/wp/2017/03/25/she-thought-trump-would-deport-bad-hombres-instead-hes-deporting-her-law-abiding-husband/?utm_term=.7ff2d9851137\" shape=\"rect\">vulnerable to deportation</a>, making the routine check-ins that thousands of immigrants face each year feel more fraught \u2014 even for a military veteran\u2019s wife.</p> <p>\u201cI totally depend on my wife, 100 percent,\u201d Pineda, 47, told the lawmakers. \u201cMy son totally depends on her.\u201d</p> <p>After the meeting, some of the caucus\u2019s members posted support for military families on social media. Several, including Reps. Ra\u00fal M. Grijalva and <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/im-a-marine-veteran-a-recently-retired-marine-shouldnt-be-defense-secretary/2016/12/22/f8354d7e-c62f-11e6-85b5-76616a33048d_story.html?utm_term=.a11a9a3c035d\" shape=\"rect\">Ruben Gallego</a>, both Arizona Democrats, haveintroduced legislation in the House that would help prevent the deportation of service members convicted of certain crimes and permit some to return to the United States. But none of the proposals would help their relatives.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_25.JPG?uuid=DrXVuA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_25.JPG?uuid=DrXVuA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_25.JPG?uuid=DrXVuA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_25.JPG?uuid=DrXVuA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Although her husband and four children are U.S. citizens, Veronica Castro fears being deported when she checks in with immigration authorities on April 4. She twice entered the United States from Mexico illegally. (Griselda San Martin/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>Pineda left the meeting with little hope, he said. Although he had been a soldier for six years and had dressed the part that day, he didn\u2019t know whether this was a fight he could win.</p> <div class=\"subhead\">\u2018Really bad dudes\u2019</div> <p>It is unclear how many veterans or their relatives have been deported or are in deportation proceedings. ICE officials said they don\u2019t keep track.</p> <p>But one deported veteran, Hector Barajas-Varela, runs a small shelter called \u201cThe Bunker\u201d in Tijuana, Mexico, for others who have lost the right to live in the United States because of drug convictions or other crimes. Although he has housed 20 deported veterans since 2013, Barajas said he has made contact with a total of 311 who have been returned to 36 countries.</p> <p>Emma Lozano, a Chicago church pastor, began helping service members and their families with deportation cases three years ago after she noticed men in U.S. military uniforms during a trip to Mexico. She attended the Congressional Hispanic Caucus meeting on Feb. 7 and plans to return to Washington on April 4 for Castro\u2019s ICE check-in.</p> <p>\u201cIt\u2019s just so blatantly wrong,\u201d Lozano said. \u201cEverywhere you go, they\u2019re talking about we have to honor our veterans. Then they are doing this to veterans and military families.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/in-trumps-capital-undocumented-immigrants-live-and-work-in-the-shadow-of-the-white-house/2017/02/07/ed837844-e8d3-11e6-b82f-687d6e6a3e7c_story.html?utm_term=.3f88c7245904\" shape=\"rect\">In Trump\u2019s capital, undocumented immigrants live and work in the shadow of the White House</a>]</i> </p> <p>Lozano has fought to keep Miguel Perez Jr., who served two tours of duty in Afghanistan and suffered a brain injury in an explosion there, from being deported. In 2010, Perez was convicted of selling more than two pounds of cocaine. This month, an immigration judge in Chicago ordered the father of two U.S.-born children to be removed to Mexico, the country he left when he was 8. Perez is appealing the deportation order.</p> <p>Sarah Rodriguez, a spokeswoman for ICE, said the agency \u201crespects the service and sacrifice of those in military service, and is very deliberate in its review of cases involving veterans.\u201d At the same time, she said, the law requires ICE to detain and deport anyone convicted of aggravated felonies.</p> <p>Trump has vowed to ramp up deportations, especially of people convicted of crimes: \u201cWe\u2019re getting really bad dudes out of this country, and at a rate that nobody\u2019s ever seen before.\u201d</p> <p>Castro\u2019s attorney, Joshua Doherty, who works for the nonprofit group Ayuda, said the enforcement changes under Trump have made it much more ambiguous whether an immigration officer will take into consideration Castro\u2019s role as a military wife and the mother of two children with disabilities. In addition to Juan, the couple\u2019s 14-year-old son Kevin has cerebral palsy.</p> <p>Doherty said on the day of her appointment, Castro could be sent on her way and told to call if she moves; she could be given her next check-in date; she could be ordered to come back later with her bags packed for Mexico; or she could be detained on the spot.</p> <p>Asked about Castro\u2019s situation, ICE officials said deportation decisions are made on \u201ca case-by-case basis.\u201d</p> <p></p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-video\"> <div class=\"posttv-video-embed wpv-player\" data-aspect-ratio=\"0.5625\" data-category-id=\"segments\" data-duration=\"195648\" data-emoji=\"0\" data-headline=\"He was medically discharged from the U.S. Army. He knows Juan will need more heart surgeries as he grows and wouldn\u2019t receive the same medical services there. The couple\u2019s other children, who don\u2019t speak fluent Spanish and know little about Mexico, are showing signs of depression. Ivan, 19, and Emily, 11, barely speak. Kevin recently asked his parents to renew his expired passport in case someone \u201ctries to kick him out.\u201d</p> <p>\u201cI think there should be some humanity,\u201d Pineda said. \u201cI swore to protect this nation and asking for a little bit of protection for my family, I don\u2019t think that is too much to ask for.\u201d</p> <div class=\"subhead\">\u2018Ineligible for any relief\u2019</div> <p>Pineda apologizes before guests walk through the front door of the family\u2019s mobile home in Lothian, Md.</p> <p>When he received an honorable discharge for medical reasons in 2014, the family had to leave its five-bedroom house on the Fort Belvoir base in Northern Virginia. The trailer and lot is all they can afford on Pineda\u2019s $2,250-a-month veteran\u2019s benefits, he said. Their three sons share a bedroom, the fire detector goes off every time the dryer is on, and each room is filled with reminders of repairs Pineda can no longer easily make: a curtain standing in for a bathroom door, a broken shower, bowed floorboards outside the front door.</p> <p>Pineda was a carpenter before he was a soldier. Now, he can\u2019t wield a hammer without pain. He cracked the bone at the base of his thumb during a combat training exercise in Richmond and a surgery left him with limited ability to move that finger.</p> <p>\u201cThis is the most important finger of all,\u201d Pineda said, pointing his thumb upward. \u201cThis is what makes us different from monkeys.\u201d</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_05.JPG?uuid=AUIxnA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_05.JPG?uuid=AUIxnA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_05.JPG?uuid=AUIxnA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_05.JPG?uuid=AUIxnA5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">The Pineda family eat dinner together in the kitchen of their mobile home\u2019s kitchen in Lothian, Md. The family moved to the area when Ricardo Pineda was discharged from the Army in 2014. (Griselda San Martin/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>Pineda was 39 when he joined the army, older than most recruits but healthy enough to pass all the medical tests and compete with men half his age in boot camp. Now, he takes a half-dozen medications a day and has appointments at VA medical centers twice a week. Sometimes for his hand. Other times for diabetes and depression.</p> <p>He was stationed in South Korea for more than a year and said the stress wore on his health. He not only had to worry about the threat in front of him but also what could go wrong back home. Juan landed in the hospital four times during his father\u2019s deployment. Castro, unable to get a Virginia driver\u2019s license because of her immigration status, pushed their son\u2019s wheelchair about 30 minutes each way from their home to the grocery store.</p> <p>\u201cI kept thinking if my husband is carrying a backpack with a rifle, I can do this,\u201d she said in Spanish.</p> <p>Ret. Sgt. Major Gabriel Berhane, who was Pineda\u2019s commanding officer at Fort Belvoir, said he was disappointed to see Pineda leave the Army.</p> <p>\u201cI can\u2019t say enough good things about him,\u201d he said. \u201cAlways, you could count on him, regardless what the task, what the mission was, he\u2019d give it 100 percent plus.\u201d</p> <p>Berhane, who works at the Pentagon, said he knows other soldiers who were not U.S. citizens. He was one of them. Born in Ethiopia, he had a green card when he enlisted and was a staff sergeant when he gained his citizenship.</p> <p>Immigrants with permanent residency are eligible to join the military, and about 18,700 on average were serving on active duty between 2010 and 2016, according to the Defense Department.</p> <p>Pineda gained his green card in 1986 and became a U.S. citizen two months after enlisting.</p> <p>While serving, he applied for his wife and their two oldest children, who were born in Mexico, to receive parole in place, which allows relatives of military members to apply for legal status while remaining in the country. He thought their approval would be automatic, but he was wrong.</p> <p>His sons\u2019 requests were granted, and they eventually gained citizenship.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_47.JPG?uuid=IxCXqg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_47.JPG?uuid=IxCXqg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_47.JPG?uuid=IxCXqg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_47.JPG?uuid=IxCXqg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Pineda and Castro stand outside a VA hospital in Washington just before an appointment in March. Pineda left the Army because of medical problems after serving for six years. (Griselda San Martin/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>His wife\u2019s was denied. A 2011 letter from U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services stated the reason: \u201ca fraudulently obtained Border Crossing Card.\u201d It also informed her that the order of removal originally entered against her was being reinstated and that she was \u201cineligible for \u2018any relief\u2019 under the immigration laws.\u201d</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/this-company-is-making-millions-from-americas-broken-immigration-system/2017/03/08/43abce9e-f881-11e6-be05-1a3817ac21a5_story.html?utm_term=.7bf604c23125\" shape=\"rect\">This company is making millions from America\u2019s broken immigration system</a>]</i> </p> <p>Castro said she was given the border crossing card, which allows Mexican citizens limited travel in the United States, by a woman she paid to help her enter the country in 1998. Pineda was living in California at the time, and Castro had hoped to join him with their 1-year-old son, Ivan. After she was caught by Border Patrol agents and sent back to Guadalajara, Castro said she planned to stay there permanently and see her husband whenever he came to visit.</p> <p>Then two years later, Juan was born.</p> <div class=\"subhead\">\u2018My family would be destroyed\u2019</div> <p>Juan was 3 months old when Castro looked at him one night and noticed his eyes crossing and his mouth turning purple. She took him to doctors and eventually learned that he had a transposition of the great arteries in his heart and needed surgery.</p> <p>\u201cThe doctor said if you have the ability to take him to the United States, take him,\u201d Pineda said. \u201cIt was life or death.\u201d</p> <p>Pineda, then a member of the carpenters union, had insurance to pay for the surgery. So the family handed Juan to strangers who took him across the border using another child\u2019s birth certificate, and Castro walked with Ivan, then 3, nearly 20 hours, drinking at one point from an animal trough when they ran out of water.</p> <p>At 3, Juan received his first surgery at a Northern Virginia hospital. Pineda doesn\u2019t like to talk about those months his son spent in a hospital bed. He said the toddler could talk and walk before the surgery, but after an allergic reaction to the anesthesia he was left with brain damage, unable to speak or move much of his body.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-double\"> <div class=\"left\" style=\"width:49%\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_742w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_29.JPG?uuid=GRYH7g5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_240w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_29.JPG?uuid=GRYH7g5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_29.JPG?uuid=GRYH7g5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_240w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_29.JPG?uuid=GRYH7g5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Castro caresses her disabled son, Juan, in the bedroom he shares with his two brothers. (Griselda San Martin/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <div class=\"right\" style=\"width:49%\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_742w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_42.JPG?uuid=JkTYvg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_240w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_42.JPG?uuid=JkTYvg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_42.JPG?uuid=JkTYvg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_240w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2017/03/21/Local/Images/militarydeport_42.JPG?uuid=JkTYvg5NEeeqVyyhsFxBuA\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Castro helps Juan, 17, brush his teeth. A heart surgery when he was 3 left him brain-damaged. (Griselda San Martin/For The Washington Post)</span> </div> <div class=\"clear\"></div> </div> <p>He was 8 when he began taking steps on his own again and nowuses a walker at times to get around. On a recent afternoon, he pushed it down the hallway of his private school, the Linwood Center, which took him in 2015 after his public high school said it no longer could meet his needs. At the time, he would hurt others and himself, take off his clothes at inappropriate times and could communicate only by spelling out letters with his fingers, Linwood behavior analyst Erika Greszler said. Now, his challenging behavior has decreased and he\u2019s \u201ca talker,\u201d she said. He uses an electronic tablet to communicate, calling it his \u201cvoice\u201d by forming a V with his fingers and pointing to his throat.</p> <p>Greszler said she thinks that if his mother is deported it \u201cwould be devastating to his progress.\u201d Castro is the parent who calls the school when he is sick or to check on his development. She\u2019s also the one who helps him shower, brushes his teeth and meets him each afternoon at the bus stop.</p> <p>Juan sat next to her on a March afternoon outside a Washington church, pressing his shoulder into hers. They\u2019d come to an immigration rights rally being staged by people from more than 60 different congregations. Christian, Jewish and Muslim leaders spoke about the need for solidarity, and when Castro\u2019s turn at the microphone came she explained why she needed their support.</p> <p>\u201cMy family would be destroyed if I\u2019m not here to take care of them,\u201d she said. </p> <p>Afterward, she and Pineda, along with two of their children, marched with the crowd toward the White House, comforted in part by a promise made to them. Members of those congregations plan to go to Baltimore on April 4 and wait alongside the family to learn Castro\u2019s fate.</p> <div class=\"extra\"> <p> <b>Read more:</b> </p> <p></p> <p> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/in-trumps-capital-undocumented-immigrants-live-and-work-in-the-shadow-of-the-white-house/2017/02/07/ed837844-e8d3-11e6-b82f-687d6e6a3e7c_story.html?utm_term=.849470a4d672\" shape=\"rect\">In Trump\u2019s capital, undocumented immigrants live and work in the shadow of the White House</a> </p> <p></p> <p> <a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/she-helped-deport-hundreds-of-undocumented-immigrants-now-shes-fighting-for-them/2017/03/27/9dc59cc6-04e7-11e7-b9fa-ed727b644a0b_story.html?utm_term=.881396874360\" shape=\"rect\">She helped deport hundreds of immigrants.
