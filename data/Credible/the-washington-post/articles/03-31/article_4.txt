class=\"pb-caption\">The Environmental Protection Agency headquarters. (Matt McClain/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>The Environmental Protection Agency has issued a new, more detailed plan for laying off 25 percent of its employees and scrapping 56\u00a0programs including pesticide safety, water runoff control, and environmental cooperation with Mexico and Canada under the North American Free Trade Agreement.</p> <p>At a time when the agency is considering a controversial rollback in fuel efficiency standards adopted under President Obama, the plan would cut by more than half the number of people in EPA\u2019s division for testing the accuracy of fuel efficiency claims by automakers.</p> <p>It would transfer funding for the program to fees paid by the automakers themselves.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp/2017/03/31/its-our-central-hub-for-clean-energy-science-trump-wants-to-cut-it-massively/\">Trump aims deep cuts at the clean energy agency that made solar power affordable</a>] </i> </p> <p>The spending plan, obtained by The Washington Post, offers the most detailed vision to date of how the 31 percent budget cut to the EPA ordered up by President Trump\u2019s Office of Management and Budget would diminish the agency.</p> <p>The March 21 plan calls for even deeper reductions in staffing than earlier drafts. It maintains funding given to states to administer waste treatment and drinking water. But as a result, the budget for the rest of EPA is slashed 43 percent.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-graphic-embedded\"> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2017/03/2300-EPACUTS0401-2.jpg\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2017/03/2300-EPACUTS0401-2.jpg\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2017/03/2300-EPACUTS0401-2.jpg\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/news/energy-environment/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2017/03/2300-EPACUTS0401-2.jpg\"/><br/> </div><p></p> <p>The Trump administration says the EPA cuts reflect a philosophy of limiting federal government and devolving authority to the states, localities and, in some cases, corporations. But environmental groups say the Trump administration is answering the call of companies seeking lax regulation and endangering Americans\u2019 air and water.</p> <p>In a memorandum at the front of the March 21 document, the EPA\u2019s acting chief financial officer David A. Bloom said the agency would now\u00a0 \u201ccenter on our core legal requirements,\u201d eliminating voluntary activities on scientific research, climate change and education, and leaving other activities to state and local governments.</p> <p>John Konkus, the agency spokesman said: \u201cEPA will work with the President and Congress to redesign the way we do business to focus on achieving our core responsibilities \u2013 working with the states to ensure clean and breathable air, protecting water quality and investing in infrastructure, restoring our communities, ensuring timely review of chemicals and products to ensure safety for American families, all of which will have a positive impact on the environment and the economy.\u201d</p> <p>The $5.7 billion EPA budget will likely undergo massive rewriting by congressional lawmakers, but the document is a declaration of intent by the Trump administration \u2014 one that sets the agency fundamentally at odds with the environmental policies of the past eight years and in some cases nearly three decades.</p> <p><em>[<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/national/trump-epa-rollbacks/\">How eliminating two EPA programs could affect large parts of America</a>]\u00a0 </em></p> <p>Because of the sweeping cuts to scientific programs, the administrator\u2019s own Science Advisory Board budget would be cut 84 percent. As the document explains, it would not need much money due to \u201can anticipated lower number of peer reviews.\u201d</p> <p>Reductions in research funds will curtail programs on climate change, water quality, and chemical safety, and \u201csafe and sustainable water resources,\u201d the document said.</p> <p>Ken Kopocis, who headed EPA\u2019s Office of Water in 2014 and 2015, said in an interview that the $165 million proposed cut to the agency\u2019s nonpoint source pollution program would deprive farmers of critical funds to help curb agricultural runoff.</p> <p>Several congressional Republicans have expressed support for reorienting the EPA\u2019s mission, though lawmakers are likely to restore some of the funding.</p> <p>Senate Environment and Public Works Committee Chairman John Barrasso (R-Wyo.) said in a statement Friday, \u201cThere is room to cut wasteful programs in EPA\u2019s budget and at the same time, realign how taxpayer money is best allocated\u201d by \u201cgiving states greater say in how they protect and manage their resources.\u201d</p> <p>In a recent interview, Sen. James M. Inhofe said he would like the department to focus on more traditional environmental concerns rather than addressing climate change.</p> <p>\u201cWhat I want them to do is to do what they are supposed to be doing \u2013 be concerned about the environment, the water, the air,\u201d he said.\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 \u201cI\u2019d like to see an EPA there to actually serve people and make life better for them.\u201d</p> <p>Inhofe said some of the members of the scientific advisory boards scheduled for cuts had political biases. \u201cThey\u2019re going to have to start dealing with science, not rigged science.\u201d</p> <p>But S. William Becker, executive director of the National Association of Clean Air Agencies, said eliminating the money for such advisory boards would inevitably compromise the agency\u2019s science capabilities.</p> <p>\u201cThis is becoming a self-fulfilling prophecy,\u201d he said. \u201cIf they have less research and less peer review, they\u2019re going to have less of a foundation on which to base human health-based air quality standards.\u201d</p> <p>The list of programs completely eliminated grew in the latest plan.</p> <p channel=\"wp.com\" class=\"interstitial-link\"> <i> [<a href=\"https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/health-science/how-james-inhofe-is-upending-the-nations-energy-and-environmental-policies/2017/03/14/2bebdbfa-081c-11e7-a15f-a58d4a988474_story.html?utm_term=.cc50dc8bb3bd%20%20\">How Sen. Jim Inhofe\u2019s (R-Okla.) former aides are shaping Trump\u2019s environmental policies</a>] </i> </p> <p><em>\u00a0</em>A program to teach and monitor the proper handling of pesticides would be nearly eliminated, and instead rely fees paid by the industry.</p> <p>Many people in industry might enlist lawmakers\u2019 help in opposing those plans. CropLife America executive vice president Beau Greenwood, whose group represents U.S. pesticide manufacturers and distributors, said in an interview Friday that while the industry is \u201cwilling to put skin in the game to ensure the agency has sufficient funding\u201d for reviewing its products, it already provides more than $46 million a year under the Pesticide Registration Improvement Extension Act.</p> <p>\u201cExtra fees on top of extra fees is something that we would oppose,\u201d Greenwood said.</p> <p>The latest EPA budget plan would abolish programs that study known environmental hazards including lead, poor indoor air quality, and radiation.</p> <p>Others programs that help protect Americans from cancer would also face the axe \u2014 including\u00a0 the $ 1.34 million indoor air radon program which works to protect the public against the invisible gas that is the leading cause of lung cancer among non-smokers. Radon kills 21,000 people annually, according to the EPA.</p> <p>The EPA\u2019s radiation program, currently funded at $2.34 million, which sets standards for safe levels of ionizing radiation in the environment caused by radioactive elements such as uranium, is also slated for elimination \u2014 but it is unclear how fully eliminating its activities is possible.</p> <p>The document also recommends a $28.9 million cut in the enforcement of clean up projects for Superfund sites, places where hazardous materials require long-term response plans.</p> <p>In each case, the budget document says that these programs can be eliminated because they do not represent core agency priorities or can be deferred to states.</p> <p>The budget document also proposes the elimination of regional programs focused on restoring watersheds and coastal and marine habitats. These include programs for the Chesapeake Bay, the Gulf of Mexico, Lake Champlain, Long Island Sound, Puget Sound, San Francisco Bay, the Great Lakes, and South Florida.</p> <p>The document notes that these are areas where EPA is working with localities to restore damaged ecosystems and watersheds. It says that EPA\u2019s regulatory heft isn\u2019t needed and that localities must take responsibility.</p> <p>\u201cIn some ways, the common thread \u2026.\u00a0is, unless there\u2019s an explicit legal mandate that EPA has to do something, that EPA shouldn\u2019t be doing it,\u201d said Stan Meiburg, a longtime EPA career employee who served as acting deputy administrator of the agency late in the Obama years.</p> <p><strong>More at Energy and Environment:</strong></p> <p><a
