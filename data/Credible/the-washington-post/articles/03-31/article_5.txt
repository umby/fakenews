Bannon, chief strategist to President Trump, at the Mar-a-Lago Club in Palm Beach, Fla., in late 2016. (Jabin Botsford/The Washington Post)</span> </div> <p>Stephen K. Bannon was running an investment banking company in Beverly Hills when his partner called with urgent news: a potential $10 billion deal was about to unfold in New York City<b> </b>involving a company they hoped to continue representing \u2014 and they didn\u2019t want to be left out of the action. </p> <p>Bannon, then in his mid-40s, told his business partner to meet him at the Los Angeles airport in an hour. Soon, they appeared at the Manhattan offices of PolyGram, a worldwide music company that they had previously represented in a film deal and now was for sale.</p> <p>Before long, Bannon came up with an angle. He had represented Saudi Prince Alwaleed bin Talal, in a prior deal, and now he proffered the royal-family member, one of the world\u2019s wealthiest Arabs, as a bidder. PolyGram was impressed and eventually paid Bannon a sizable fee for work on the overall deal.</p> <p>\u201cThose out of the room are out of the deal,\u201d Bannon, now 63, said in an interview, recalling the 1998 meeting. \u201cOnce you make your way into the room, you stay.\u201d</p> <p>Years before Bannon became the architect of an anti-globalist revolution \u2014 working as chief strategist under President Trump to weaken free-trade deals, restrict immigration from a number of majority-Muslim nations and slam corporations that move jobs overseas \u2014 he made his fortune as the quintessential global capitalist.</p> <p>An examination of Bannon\u2019s career as an investment banker found that the Bannon of the 1980s and 1990s lived what looks like an alternate reality from the fiery populist of today who recently declared that \u201cglobalists gutted the American working class and created a middle class in Asia.\u201d</p> <p> <b> </b>With stints at Goldman Sachs and his own firm, Bannon was a creature of corporatism, wealth-building and international finance. His company received crucial financial backing from banks in Japan and France, and one of his key clients was the Saudi prince. Co.</p> <p>But he said the world in which he once operated, and made millions, had been changed by banks that now look out for themselves rather than their clients \u2014 and by multinational corporations that undermined the working class.</p> <p>\u201cWe always were the financier of entrepreneurs, of which I\u2019m a huge believer, and I\u2019m not a believer in the corporatist mentality,\u201d he said.</p> <p> <b></b>By his telling, this is the sentiment that led to the Bannon of today, the one who helped transform the far-right Breitbart News site into a platform for nationalism and then joined forces with Trump to upend much of Republican ideology.</p> <p>Today, Bannon functions as a counterweight in the White House to an array of fellow Trump advisers who inhabit his former world, top officials from Goldman Sachs as well as other Wall Street veterans who embrace a more traditional brand of GOP policies such as free trade.</p> <p>Yet in many respects, it is Bannon\u2019s experience in their world that has positioned him for the struggle.</p> <p> <b></b>\u201cI have lived all over the world, worked all over the world, financed with great partners all over the world,\u201d Bannon said. \u201cI\u2019m not some angry guy sitting there. But much of his time was spent abroad in places such as Hong Kong and Singapore.</p> <p>One of the company\u2019s early successes involved a sale of television properties that wound up giving Bannon and his partners a huge payoff from future airings of what was then a little-known situation comedy: \u201cSeinfeld.\u201d</p> <p>By 1996, with the Japanese deal having run its course,<b> </b>Bannon &amp; Co. entered a partnership with Soci\u00e9t\u00e9 G\u00e9n\u00e9rale, a giant French investment company. He had great ambitions. He said he had far exceeded whatever he was likely to have earned by staying with Goldman Sachs. During his 14 years in investment banking, Bannon was invariably described by friends as a country-club type of Republican, trim, athletic and movie-star handsome. A number of former colleagues said that they don\u2019t recognize the Bannon of today, either in his rhetoric or disheveled appearance.</p> <p>Bannon has said he was fundamentally changed by the impact of the 9/11 terrorist attacks and the 2008 financial crisis.</p> <p>The crisis prompted Bannon to say that Wall Street no longer operated in the way he was taught, by working to build companies and watch out for a client\u2019s interest. The financial crisis, he said, was caused by those whose main motivation was to make as much money as possible for oneself, a \u201cfinancialization\u201d of Wall Street that put bankers\u2019 profits over those of everyday investors. \u201cIt was before all these quants and mathematicians really came to Wall Street,\u201d Bannon <a href=\"https://vimeo.com/49904381\" shape=\"rect\">said in a French documentary </a>about Goldman Sachs.</p> <p>He said he was further infuriated by the taxpayer bailouts of major banks, which were engineered by the former chief executive of Goldman Sachs, Henry M. Paulson Jr., who was President George W. Bush\u2019s treasury secretary.</p> <p>Bannon has also said that he took a hard turn to the right because his father, who worked for AT&amp;T, lost money selling the company\u2019s stock in the wake of the financial crisis.</p> <p>Bannon began using some of his fortune to produce movies that reflected a darker worldview. His films, such as \u201cGeneration Zero,\u201d portrayed the United States as sliding into a cultural and financial decline. Bannon channeled his anger into support for the tea party movement and, later, into pressing an anti-globalist message as chairman of Breitbart News.</p> <p>Bannon has said Breitbart News is \u201cthe platform for the alt-right,\u201d referring to a small movement that seeks a whites-only state. Bannon, however, has said he is not a white nationalist but a \u201cstrong American nationalist.\u201d Bannon\u2019s views eventually were embraced by Trump, who was an avid reader of Breitbart News and had been interviewed by Bannon on a radio program. <b></b> </p> <p>Trump hired Bannon in August 2016 as his campaign\u2019s chief executive, and the former investment banker helped steer the self-described multibillionaire to the presidency with a populist message.</p> <p>While Bannon shaped Trump\u2019s agenda, his former client, Prince Alwaleed bin Talal, was aghast at Trump\u2019s proposal to have a \u201ctotal and complete and shutdown of Muslims entering the United States.\u201d The prince said on Twitter \u2014 in which he <a href=\"https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2016-11-16/saudi-prince-alwaleed-says-twitter-investment-at-breakeven-level\" shape=\"rect\">owned </a>a $1\u2009billion stake \u2014 that Trump\u2019s proposal was a \u201cdisgrace.\u201d Trump tweeted back that he was a \u201cdopey prince.\u201d</p> <p>Bannon said he has not talked with the prince in years, and he said that his views on Islam are misunderstood. \u201cI don\u2019t have any issue with Islam,\u201d he said. \u201cI have a problem with radical Islam.\u201d</p> <p>Trump\u2019s victory changed the prince\u2019s outlook, and the prince again took to Twitter, this time to congratulate the new president. That was just the beginning of an effort by the Saudis to court the new president, and they were rewarded with a March 14 meeting in the Oval Office with Trump and Saudi Deputy Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, a son of Saudi King Salman bin Abdul Aziz.</p> <p>Bannon, whose principle for decades has been to be in the room when a deal is being made, was by Trump\u2019s side during the meeting.</p> <div class=\"inline-content inline-photo inline-photo-normal\"> <a name=\"\"></a> <img class=\"unprocessed\" data-hi-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_1484w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2016/12/01/National-Politics/Images/Botsford161201TrumpIN9144.JPG?uuid=Z2M7FLgEEeaTnJF0lEPF5Q\" data-low-res-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2016/12/01/National-Politics/Images/Botsford161201TrumpIN9144.JPG?uuid=Z2M7FLgEEeaTnJF0lEPF5Q\" data-raw-src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rw/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2016/12/01/National-Politics/Images/Botsford161201TrumpIN9144.JPG?uuid=Z2M7FLgEEeaTnJF0lEPF5Q\" src=\"https://img.washingtonpost.com/rf/image_480w/2010-2019/WashingtonPost/2016/12/01/National-Politics/Images/Botsford161201TrumpIN9144.JPG?uuid=Z2M7FLgEEeaTnJF0lEPF5Q\"/><br/> <span class=\"pb-caption\">Bannon walks from then-President-elect Donald Trump\u2019s plane on Dec. 1, 2016, in Indianapolis, on the way to their meeting with Carrier Corporation. He landed a summer job at the company\u2019s New York City office and then a full-time position upon graduation. He arrived in 1985 to work on mergers and acquisitions.\n\nBannon \u201cwas unusual insofar as he was a bit older and he came from a military background, and that fact was core to his persona. . . . [He was] independent minded, with strong points of view,\u201d said John L. (Courtesy of the Bannon family)\n\nBannon\u2019s main task during his first three years at Goldman Sachs was to respond to a boom in hostile takeovers. Goldman Sachs took the side of companies under attack from corporate raiders and leveraged buyout firms. Bannon had to come up with strategies to protect companies from unwanted suitors.\n\n\u201cA takeover is a very intense time. It is like you are going to battle,\u201d said Scot Vorse, who attended Harvard with Bannon and worked with him at Goldman Sachs and Bannon & Co. \u201cIt was crazy. I worked many, many weeks in excess of 100 hours, and I know Steve did that often. At 10 p.m., you have 150 people walking around a floor with a piece of pizza and cup of coffee.\u201d\n\nAfter three years and a promotion to vice president \u2014 a title that was given to many Goldman Sachs employees \u2014 Bannon moved to the company\u2019s Los Angeles office and was given the mission of drumming up business. He cold-called companies, looking to represent them as they considered splitting up or acquiring new businesses.\n\nA turning point came in 1989, when Bannon said he persuaded Goldman Sachs to invest heavily in a fledgling company called Qualcomm, which was working on cellular telephone technology. Bannon said he did not receive extra compensation from the deal, which he said made a huge profit for Goldman Sachs. While he said he made about $450,000 annually at the time, that was a \u201cnight and day\u201d difference, compared with what he might have made at a firm that paid him a huge fee for the deal. The philosophy of Goldman Sachs was to pay generously but not to provide the highest compensation until executives were made into partners, which could take years.\n\n\u201cIt was a huge effort to get it done,\u201d Bannon said. \u201cI thought about it: \u2018I will get all the grief if this deal doesn\u2019t work out and none of the upside if it does well.\u2019 . . . That investment was worth hundreds of millions of dollars.\u201d\n\nSo in 1990, Bannon started his own firm.\n\nHe said the move was not about money. \u201cIt has always been about doing something I want to do and proving to myself I could do it,\u201d he said. \u201cStarting Bannon & Co. was a huge risk. People thought I was insane.\u201d\n\nBannon stayed in California, he said, because he was focused on valuing intellectual property such as the movie industry \u2014 as opposed to the hard assets in the oil and gas business \u2014 and he believed there were big profits to be made as \u201cthe media and entertainment industry was just about to globalize.\u201d\n\nBannon wanted to do big deals, but he didn\u2019t have the financing. So he began to explore options overseas, including Japan, where investors were eager to get involved in Hollywood projects.\n\nHe struck a five-year deal with a major Japanese bank, Nissho Iwai, which he said supplied millions of dollars.\n\nRelying on foreign money became a model for Bannon\u2019s work. \u201cOur forte was working with American companies to work with foreign investors,\u201d Bannon said.\n\nBannon set up shop in Beverly Hills with Vorse, his former classmate and Goldman Sachs colleague. But much of his time was spent abroad in places such as Hong Kong and Singapore.\n\nOne of the company\u2019s early successes involved a sale of television properties that wound up giving Bannon and his partners a huge payoff from future airings of what was then a little-known situation comedy: \u201cSeinfeld.\u201d\n\nBy 1996, with the Japanese deal having run its course, Bannon & Co. entered a partnership with Soci\u00e9t\u00e9 G\u00e9n\u00e9rale, a giant French investment company. The joint venture, which allowed Bannon to retain control over his company, was called Soci\u00e9t\u00e9 G\u00e9n\u00e9rale Bannon.\n\nAt the same time, Bannon\u2019s global aspirations were solidified through a deal in which he got to know Prince Alwaleed bin Talal, who today is the world\u2019s 45th wealthiest person, with a net worth of $18 billion, according to Forbes magazine. The prince declined to comment.\n\nThe prince, a nephew of the Saudi king, headed the United Saudi Commercial Bank and had major investments in companies including Euro Disney, Citibank and Saks Fifth Avenue. He made much of his fortune trading tens of millions of dollars on U.S. stock markets. He also made two purchases of properties that he has said \u201cbailed out\u201d Trump: the 1991 purchase of Trump\u2019s yacht; and a $325 million purchase \u2014 in 1995, with a Singapore investor \u2014 of the Plaza Hotel.\n\n\n\nBillionaire Saudi Prince Alwaleed bin Talal, center, relaxes with his daughter Reem and son Kalid on his yacht, which he bought from President Trump. In 1996, Bannon advised the prince on Kingdom\u2019s purchase of a 50 percent stake in Landmark Entertainment, a Hollywood-based company that produced music and television programs and developed theme parks. Bannon remained in touch with the prince, meeting him at the Four Seasons Hotel in Beverly Hills, in which the prince owned a stake, Vorse said. In addition, Vorse traveled to Riyadh to meet with the Saudi prince.\n\n\u201cAlwaleed was very savvy,\u201d Bannon said. \u201cHe was a client for five or six years. . . . He had great ambitions. He wanted to be the Walt Disney of the Middle East.\u201d\n\nIn 1998, the prince became an important part of one of Bannon\u2019s biggest deals, when Vorse heard a rumor that a suitor wanted to buy PolyGram in what was expected to be a $10 billion deal.\n\n\u201cWe show up in New York,\u201d Vorse said. \u201cWe march into their executive suites. We say, \u2018We are here to help; can we hang out in your conference room til you need us?\u2019 It was almost like we were white-collar squatters. We moved into the conference room.\u201d\n\nA couple of possible bidders, including the Canadian conglomerate Seagram\u2019s, were lined up when Bannon proposed the prince as an alternative.\n\nWhen a PolyGram executive questioned Bannon\u2019s relationship with the prince, Bannon responded that he should \u201ccall the prince\u2019s main guy\u201d to check it out, Vorse said. A short while later, the PolyGram president signaled to Bannon and Vorse with \u201cthe big thumbs-up,\u201d Vorse said.\n\nIn the end, the prince didn\u2019t buy PolyGram, but his work led him to be retained by the company, and Bannon said the prospect of an alternative bidder helped boost the price by 20 percent.\n\nBannon said he received \u201ca big fee,\u201d though he declined to disclose the amount.\n\nAs a result of this and other deals, Bannon\u2019s company was ranked as the world\u2019s fourth most valuable media investment bank, ahead of firms with thousands more employees, according to Vorse.\n\nSoon, Bannon agreed to sell his company for an undisclosed amount to a subsidiary of his French partner, Soci\u00e9t\u00e9 G\u00e9n\u00e9rale, a move that eventually put an end to his role at the company he created.\n\nWhile it is not possible to independently verify Bannon\u2019s profits during this time, he said the deals and the sale of the company made him very wealthy. He said he had far exceeded whatever he was likely to have earned by staying with Goldman Sachs. During his 14 years in investment banking, Bannon was invariably described by friends as a country-club type of Republican, trim, athletic and movie-star handsome. A number of former colleagues said that they don\u2019t recognize the Bannon of today, either in his rhetoric or disheveled appearance.\n\nBannon has said he was fundamentally changed by the impact of the 9/11 terrorist attacks and the 2008 financial crisis.\n\nThe crisis prompted Bannon to say that Wall Street no longer operated in the way he was taught, by working to build companies and watch out for a client\u2019s interest. The financial crisis, he said, was caused by those whose main motivation was to make as much money as possible for oneself, a \u201cfinancialization\u201d of Wall Street that put bankers\u2019 profits over those of everyday investors. \u201cIt was before all these quants and mathematicians really came to Wall Street,\u201d Bannon said in a French documentary about Goldman Sachs.\n\nHe said he was further infuriated by the taxpayer bailouts of major banks, which were engineered by the former chief executive of Goldman Sachs, Henry M. Paulson Jr., who was President George W. Bush\u2019s treasury secretary.\n\nBannon has also said that he took a hard turn to the right because his father, who worked for AT&T, lost money selling the company\u2019s stock in the wake of the financial crisis.\n\nBannon began using some of his fortune to produce movies that reflected a darker worldview. His films, such as \u201cGeneration Zero,\u201d portrayed the United States as sliding into a cultural and financial decline. Bannon channeled his anger into support for the tea party movement and, later, into pressing an anti-globalist message as chairman of Breitbart News.\n\nBannon has said Breitbart News is \u201cthe platform for the alt-right,\u201d referring to a small movement that seeks a whites-only state. Bannon, however, has said he is not a white nationalist but a \u201cstrong American nationalist.\u201d Bannon\u2019s views eventually were embraced by Trump, who was an avid reader of Breitbart News and had been interviewed by Bannon on a radio program.\n\nTrump hired Bannon in August 2016 as his campaign\u2019s chief executive, and the former investment banker helped steer the self-described multibillionaire to the presidency with a populist message.\n\nWhile Bannon shaped Trump\u2019s agenda, his former client, Prince Alwaleed bin Talal, was aghast at Trump\u2019s proposal to have a \u201ctotal and complete and shutdown of Muslims entering the United States.\u201d The prince said on Twitter \u2014 in which he owned a $1 billion stake \u2014 that Trump\u2019s proposal was a \u201cdisgrace.\u201d Trump tweeted back that he was a \u201cdopey prince.\u201d\n\nBannon said he has not talked with the prince in years, and he said that his views on Islam are misunderstood. \u201cI don\u2019t have any issue with Islam,\u201d he said. \u201cI have a problem with radical Islam.\u201d\n\nTrump\u2019s victory changed the prince\u2019s outlook, and the prince again took to Twitter, this time to congratulate the new president. That was just the beginning of an effort by the Saudis to court the new president, and they were rewarded with a March 14 meeting in the Oval Office with Trump and Saudi Deputy Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, a son of Saudi King Salman bin Abdul Aziz.\n\nBannon, whose principle for decades has been to be in the room when a deal is being made, was by Trump\u2019s side during the meeting.\n\n\n\nBannon walks from then-President-elect Donald Trump\u2019s plane on Dec. 1, 2016, in Indianapolis, on the way to their meeting with Carrier Corporation.
