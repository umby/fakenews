data-para-count=\"523\" data-total-count=\"523\">Millennials, generally defined as people born between 1982 and 2000, were supposed to be the <a href=\"https://books.google.com/books/about/Millennial_Momentum.html?Id=eddswngfme4c\">generation</a> that forged what has been called \u201c<a href=\"Https://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2013/10/race-no-millennials-care-most-about-gender-equality/430305/\">a new national consensus</a>\u201d in favor of gender equality. Indeed, in February the prominent Columbia professor Jeffrey Sachs labeled the 2016 election, where an extremely qualified female candidate lost to a man with a history of disrespecting women, \u201c<a href=\"https://www.project-syndicate.org/commentary/america-generational-divide-over-trump-by-jeffrey-d-sachs-2017-02\">a blip</a>\u201d on the road to the egalitarian society that will be achieved once millennial voters outnumber their conservative elders.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"426\" data-total-count=\"949\">But the millennial category <a href=\"https://contemporaryfamilies.org/8-furstenberg-millennials-analytic-category\">lumps together everybody</a> from age 17 to 34, a group varied by race, ethnicity, religion, income, education and life experience. Don\u2019t think for a second they are united. As a set of reports released Friday by the Council on Contemporary Families reveals, fewer of the youngest millennials, those aged 18 to 25, support egalitarian family arrangements than did the same age group 20 years earlier.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"720\" data-total-count=\"1669\">Using a survey that has monitored the attitudes of high school seniors for nearly 40 years, the sociologists <a href=\"https://contemporaryfamilies.org/2-pepin-cotter-traditionalism\">Joanna Pepin and David Cotter find</a> that the proportion of young people holding egalitarian views about gender relationships rose steadily from 1977 to the mid-1990s but has fallen since. In 1994, only 42 percent of high school seniors agreed that the best family was one where the man was the main income earner and the woman took care of the home. But in 2014, 58 percent of seniors said they preferred that arrangement. In 1994, fewer than 30 percent of high school seniors thought \u201cthe husband should make all the important decisions in the family.\u201d By 2014, nearly 40 percent subscribed to that premise.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"537\" data-total-count=\"2206\">A <a href=\"https://contemporaryfamilies.org/7-fate-dixon-millennials-rethinking-gender-revolution\">different survey</a> found a similar trend, in this case concentrated mainly among men. In 1994, 83 percent of young men rejected the superiority of the male-breadwinner family. By 2014 that had fallen to 55 percent. Women\u2019s disagreement fell far less, from 85 percent in 1994 to 72 percent in 2014. Since 1994, young women\u2019s confidence that employed women are just as good mothers as stay-at-home moms has continued to inch up, but young men\u2019s has fallen. Among 18- to 29-year-olds, that rises to 91 percent favoring paid leave for mothers and 82 percent favoring it for fathers.</p><button class=\"button comments-button  theme-speech-bubble-large\" data-skip-to-para-id=\"\">\n</button>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"264\" data-total-count=\"6469\">If, but only if, we can win such reforms, we may find that rather than growing out of youthful egalitarian idealism, as the popular view of aging might lead us to expect, more young Americans may <em>grow into</em> it, creating the most egalitarian family arrangements yet.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-8\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-8 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-6\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"508\" data-total-count=\"6977\" id=\"story-continues-6\"><strong>Update</strong>: After this article was posted, 2016 data from the General Social Survey became available, adding some nuance to this analysis. The latest numbers show a rebound in young men\u2019s disagreement with the claim that male-breadwinner families are superior. The trend still confirms a rise in traditionalism among high school seniors and 18-to-25-year-olds, but the new data shows that this rise is no longer driven mainly by young men, as it was in the General Social Survey results from 1994 through 2014.</p><footer class=\"story-footer story-content\">\n    <div class=\"story-meta\">\n                <div class=\"story-notes\"><p>Stephanie Coontz,
