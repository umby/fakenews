It took me until recently to be able to say \u2014 or write \u2014 this. I used to think of and refer to them as \u201cordinary Germans,\u201d as if that was a distinct and morally neutral category. Understanding why and how this woman I knew and loved was swept up in a movement that became synonymous with evil has been, for me, a lifelong question.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"533\" data-total-count=\"1168\">She and my grandfather grew up in a working-class suburb of industrial Dortmund, where unemployment was rife; it had been occupied by the French after World War I. They joined the Nazi Party to be youth leaders in an agricultural education program called the Landjahr, or \u201cyear on the land,\u201d in which teenagers got agricultural training. My grandmother always maintained that she had joined the Nazis as an \u201cidealist\u201d drawn to the vision of rebuilding Germany, returning to a simpler time and, perversely, promoting equality.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"543\" data-total-count=\"1711\">In the Landjahr, sons and daughters of factory workers would live and work side by side with sons and daughters of aristocrats and wealthy industrialists. (In 1939, Nazi operatives donned Polish Army uniforms and staged a takeover of a German <a href=\"http://www.telegraph.co.uk/history/world-war-two/6106566/World-War-IIs-first-victim.html\">radio station at Gleiwitz</a> that Hitler then held up as an act of provocation by the Poles.)</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-4\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-4\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"170\" data-total-count=\"4005\" id=\"story-continues-4\">\u201cBut what did you think when you started hearing the rumors about concentration camps?\u201d I would press her. \u201cDidn\u2019t you ever listen to the foreign news reports?\u201d</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"192\" data-total-count=\"4197\">\u201cAllied propaganda\u201d was my grandmother\u2019s answer. That\u2019s what Hitler said it was. And she, like many Germans, trusted him. Her trust, apparently, relieved her of the need to understand.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-6\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-6 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-5\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"327\" data-total-count=\"4524\" id=\"story-continues-5\">How do I square the loving grandmother I knew until her death, in 2011, with this person? I have often worried that my attempt to understand the choices she made \u2014 and didn\u2019t make \u2014 might be confused with an attempt to justify or forgive. But for me it is the only way I know to confront the past and take responsibility.</p><button class=\"button comments-button  theme-speech-bubble-large\" data-skip-to-para-id=\"\">\n</button>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"265\" data-total-count=\"4789\">My grandmother heard what she wanted from a leader who promised simple answers to complicated questions. She chose not to hear and see the monstrous sum those answers added up to. And she lived the rest of her life with the knowledge of her indefensible complicity.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"137\" data-total-count=\"4926\">But in her willingness to talk about a subject few members of her generation would, she taught me the vital importance of knowing better.</p><footer class=\"story-footer story-content\">\n    <div
