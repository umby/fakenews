Nick and I met in college and dated for a few months before dropping out and driving cross-country. Over the next few years, we worked a series of low-wage jobs. On the rare occasions when we discussed our future, he said he wasn\u2019t ready to settle down because one day, he claimed, he would probably need to \u201csow\u201d his \u201cwild oats\u201d \u2014 a saying I found tacky and a concept I found ridiculous.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"118\" data-total-count=\"4608\">When I told Neal about this years later, he said, \u201cMaybe you found it ridiculous because you\u2019d already done it.\u201d</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"318\" data-total-count=\"4926\">It\u2019s true that from ages 16 to 19 I had a lot of boyfriends. But with Nick, I became happily domestic. We adopted cats. I had changed in such a way that I had no problem being with just one person. I was done changing and thought he should be, too. Certainly, I thought he should not change into a man who sows oats.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"223\" data-total-count=\"5149\">When we got married at the courthouse so he could get his green card (he was Canadian), I didn\u2019t feel different the next day. We still fell asleep to \u201cPolitically Incorrect\u201d with our cats at our feet as we always had.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"232\" data-total-count=\"5381\">We told anyone who asked that the marriage was no big deal, just a formality so the government wouldn\u2019t break us up. But when pressed, it was hard to say what differentiated us from the truly married beyond the absence of a party.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"321\" data-total-count=\"5702\">When I grew depressed a few months later, I decided that he and our pseudo-marriage were part of the problem. After three years of feeling like the more committed person, I was done and asked him to move out. When he left, I felt sad but also thrilled by the prospect of dating again. A couple of years later, I met Neal.</p><div id=\"story-ad-3\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-5\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"286\" data-total-count=\"5988\" id=\"story-continues-5\">Recently, I asked Nick if we could talk. We hadn\u2019t spoken in a decade. He lives in London now, so we Skyped. I saw that he looked almost exactly as he had at 22, though he\u2019d grown a long beard. We had a pleasant conversation. Finally, I asked him if he thought our marriage counted.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"45\" data-total-count=\"6033\">\u201cYeah,\u201d he said. \u201cI think it counts.\u201d</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"341\" data-total-count=\"6374\">We were married, just not very well. The marriage didn\u2019t mean much to us, and so when things got rough, we broke up. I had been too immature to know what I was getting into. I thought passion was the most important thing. When my romantic feelings left, I followed them out the door. It was just like any breakup, but with extra paperwork.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"221\" data-total-count=\"6595\">Nick now works at a European arts venue. He\u2019s unmarried. I wouldn\u2019t have predicted his life or his facial hair. I don\u2019t regret our split, but if we had stayed married, I think I would have liked this version of him.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"188\" data-total-count=\"6783\">My hair is long and blond now. When Neal and I met, it was dyed black and cut to my chin. When I took to bleaching it myself, it was often orange, because I didn\u2019t know what I was doing.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"387\" data-total-count=\"7170\">Now I weigh about 160 pounds. When I left the hospital after being treated for a burst appendix, I weighed 140. When I was nine months pregnant and starving every second, I weighed 210. I have been everything from size 4 to 14. I have been the life of the party and a drag. I have been broke and loaded, clinically depressed and radiantly happy. Spread out over the years, I\u2019m a harem.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"287\" data-total-count=\"7457\">How can we accept that when it comes to our bodies (and everything else, for that matter), the only inevitability is change? And what is the key to caring less about change as a marriage evolves \u2014 things like how much sex we\u2019re having and whether or not it\u2019s the best sex possible?</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"284\" data-total-count=\"7741\">One day in the country, Neal and I heard a chipmunk in distress. It had gotten inside the house and was hiding under the couch. Every few minutes, the creature let out a high-pitched squeak. I tried to sweep it out the door to safety with a broom, but it kept running back at my feet.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"39\" data-total-count=\"7780\">\u201cWow, you\u2019re dumb,\u201d I said to it.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"109\" data-total-count=\"7889\">\u201cI got this,\u201d Neal said, mysteriously carrying a plastic cereal bowl. \u201cShoo it out from under there.\u201d</p><button class=\"button comments-button  theme-speech-bubble-large\" data-skip-to-para-id=\"\">\n</button>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"296\" data-total-count=\"8185\">I did, and the chipmunk raced through the living room. Neal, like an ancient discus thrower, tossed the bowl in a beautiful arc, landing it perfectly atop the scampering creature. He then slid a piece of cardboard under the bowl and carried the chipmunk out into the bushes, where he set it free.</p><div id=\"story-ad-4\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-6\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"41\" data-total-count=\"8226\" id=\"story-continues-6\">\u201cThat was really impressive,\u201d I said.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"22\" data-total-count=\"8248\">\u201cI know,\u201d he said.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"192\" data-total-count=\"8440\">To feel awed by a man I thought I knew completely: It\u2019s a shock when that happens after so many years. And a boon. And yet being forever content with a spouse, or a street, requires finding ways to be happy with different versions of that person or neighborhood.\n\nBecause I like to fix broken things quickly and shoddily (my husband, Neal, calls my renovation aesthetic \u201cLittle Rascals Clubhouse\u201d), I frequently receive the advice: \u201cDon\u2019t just do something, stand there.\u201d\n\nSuch underreacting may also be the best stance when confronted by too much or too little change. Whether or not we want people to stay the same, time will bring change in abundance.\n\nA year and a half ago, Neal and I bought a place in the country. We hadn\u2019t been in the market for a house, but our city apartment is only 500 square feet, and we kept admiring this lovely blue house we drove by every time we visited my parents. It turned out to be shockingly affordable.\n\nSo now we own a house. We bought furniture, framed pictures and put up a badminton net. We marveled at the change that had come over us. Who were these backyard-grilling, property-tax-paying, shuttlecock-batting people we had become?\n\nWhen we met in our 20s, Neal wasn\u2019t a man who would delight in lawn care, and I wasn\u2019t a woman who would find such a man appealing. Certainly, I thought he should not change into a man who sows oats.\n\nWhen we got married at the courthouse so he could get his green card (he was Canadian), I didn\u2019t feel different the next day. After three years of feeling like the more committed person, I was done and asked him to move out. When he left, I felt sad but also thrilled by the prospect of dating again. Finally, I asked him if he thought our marriage counted.\n\n\u201cYeah,\u201d he said. \u201cI think it counts.\u201d\n\nWe were married, just not very well. The marriage didn\u2019t mean much to us, and so when things got rough, we broke up. I had been too immature to know what I was getting into. I thought passion was the most important thing. When my romantic feelings left, I followed them out the door. It was just like any breakup, but with extra paperwork.\n\nNick now works at a European arts venue. He\u2019s unmarried. I wouldn\u2019t have predicted his life or his facial hair. I don\u2019t regret our split, but if we had stayed married, I think I would have liked this version of him.\n\nMy hair is long and blond now.
