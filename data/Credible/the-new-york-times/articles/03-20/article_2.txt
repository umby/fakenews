data-total-count=\"315\"><a href=\"http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/people/r/david_rockefeller/index.html?inline=nyt-per\" title=\"More articles about David Rockefeller\" class=\"meta-per\">David Rockefeller</a>, the banker and philanthropist with the fabled family name who controlled Chase Manhattan bank for more than a decade and wielded vast influence around the world for even longer as he spread the gospel of American capitalism, died on Monday morning at his home in Pocantico Hills, N.Y. He was 101.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"58\" data-total-count=\"373\">A family spokesman, Fraser P. Seitel, confirmed the death.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"320\" data-total-count=\"693\">Chase Manhattan had long been known as the Rockefeller bank, although the family never owned more than 5 percent of its shares. But Mr. Rockefeller was more than a steward. As chairman and chief executive throughout the 1970s, he made it \u201cDavid\u2019s bank,\u201d as many called it, expanding its operations internationally.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"237\" data-total-count=\"930\">His stature was greater than any corporate title might convey, however. His influence was felt in Washington and foreign capitals, in the corridors of New York City government, in art museums, in great universities and in public schools.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"380\" data-total-count=\"1310\">Mr. Rockefeller could well be the last of a less and less visible family to have cut so imposing a figure on the world stage. As a peripatetic advocate of the economic interests of the United States and of his own bank, he was a force in global financial affairs and in his country\u2019s foreign policy. Rockefeller, the tycoon who founded the Standard Oil Company in the 19th century and built a fortune that made him America\u2019s first billionaire and his family one of the richest and most powerful in the nation\u2019s history.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"285\" data-total-count=\"1864\">As an heir to that legacy, David Rockefeller lived all his life in baronial splendor and privilege, whether in Manhattan (when he was a boy, he and his brothers would roller skate along Fifth Avenue trailed by a limousine in case they grew tired) or at his magnificent country estates.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"441\" data-total-count=\"2305\">Imbued with the understated manners of the East Coast elite, he loomed large in the upper reaches of a New York social world of glittering black-tie galas. His philanthropy was monumental, and so was his art collection, a museumlike repository of some 15,000 pieces, many of them masterpieces, some lining the walls of his offices 56 floors above the streets at Rockefeller Center, to which he repaired, robust and active, well into his 90s.</p><figure id=\"media-100000004998935\" class=\"media photo embedded layout-large-vertical media-100000004998935\" data-media-action=\"modal\" itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemid=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/20/obituaries/21ROCKEFELLER1/21ROCKEFELLER1-blog427.jpg\" itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" aria-label=\"media\" role=\"group\">\n    <span class=\"visually-hidden\">Photo</span>\n    <div class=\"image\">\n            <img src=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/20/obituaries/21ROCKEFELLER1/21ROCKEFELLER1-blog427.jpg\" alt=\"\" class=\"media-viewer-candidate\" data-mediaviewer-src=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/20/obituaries/21ROCKEFELLER1/21ROCKEFELLER1-superJumbo.jpg\" data-mediaviewer-caption=\"The philanthropist David Rockefeller in 2002.\" data-mediaviewer-credit=\"Fred R. There were years when Chase had the most troubled loan portfolio among major American banks.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-8\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-8 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-6\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"279\" data-total-count=\"4642\" id=\"story-continues-6\">\u201cIn my judgment, he will not go down in history as a great banker,\u201d John J. Rockefeller, the former vice president and four-term governor of New York, David Rockefeller stood almost alone as a member of the family with an outsize national profile. Only Jay Rockefeller, a great-grandson of John D. Rockefeller, had earned prominence, as a governor and United States senator from West Virginia. No one from the family\u2019s younger generations has attained or perhaps aspired to David Rockefeller\u2019s stature.</p><div id=\"story-ad-2\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-9\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"206\" data-total-count=\"6464\" id=\"story-continues-9\">\u201cNo one can step into his shoes,\u201d Warren T. His father, John D. Rockefeller Jr., the only son of the oil titan, devoted his life to philanthropy. He said his brand of personal diplomacy, meeting with heads of state, was crucial in furthering Chase\u2019s interests.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"357\" data-total-count=\"11793\">\u201cThere were many who claimed these activities were inappropriate and interfered with my bank responsibilities,\u201d Mr. Rockefeller wrote in his autobiography. \u201cI couldn\u2019t disagree more.\u201d His \u201cso-called outside activities,\u201d he insisted, \u201cwere of considerable benefit to the bank both financially and in terms of its prestige around the world.\u201d</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-28\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-15\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"441\" data-total-count=\"12234\" id=\"story-continues-15\">By 1976, Chase Manhattan\u2019s international arm was contributing 80 percent of the bank\u2019s $105 million in operating profit. But instead of vindicating Mr. Rockefeller\u2019s avidity for banking abroad, those figures underlined Chase\u2019s lagging performance at home. From 1974 to 1976, its earnings fell 36 percent while those of its biggest rivals \u2014 Bank of America, Citibank, Manufacturers Hanover and J.P. Morgan \u2014 rose 12 to 31 percent.</p><div id=\"story-ad-4\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-16\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"329\" data-total-count=\"12563\" id=\"story-continues-16\">The 1974 recession hammered Chase, which had an unusually large portfolio of loans in the depressed real estate industry. It also owned more New York-related securities than any other bank in the mid-1970s, when the city was edging toward bankruptcy. And among major banks, Chase had the largest portfolio of nonperforming loans.</p><figure id=\"media-100000004998953\" class=\"media photo embedded layout-large-horizontal media-100000004998953 ratio-tall\" data-media-action=\"modal\" itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemid=\"https://static01.nyt.com/images/2017/03/20/obituaries/21ROCKEFELLER4/21ROCKEFELLER4-master675.jpg\" ad-aggro-10 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-17\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"348\" data-total-count=\"12911\" id=\"story-continues-17\">Chase also got caught up in a scandal in 1974. An internal audit discovered that its bond trading account was overvalued by $34 million and that losses had been understated. A resulting $15 million drain in net income tarnished the bank\u2019s image. In 1975, the Federal Reserve and the comptroller of the currency branded Chase a \u201cproblem\u201d bank.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"325\" data-total-count=\"13236\">Even as he struggled to reverse Chase Manhattan\u2019s decline, Mr. Rockefeller found time to address New York City\u2019s financial problems. His involvement in municipal affairs dated to the early 1960s, when, as founder and chairman of the Downtown-Lower Manhattan Association, he recommended that a World Trade Center be built.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-32\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-8 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-18\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"222\" data-total-count=\"13458\" id=\"story-continues-18\">In 1961, largely at his instigation, Chase opened its 64-story headquarters in the Wall Street area, a huge investment that helped revitalize the financial district and encouraged the World Trade Center project to proceed.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"322\" data-total-count=\"13780\">In the mid-1970s, with New York City facing a default on its debts because of sluggish economic growth and uncontrolled municipal spending, Mr. Rockefeller helped bring together federal, state and city officials with New York business leaders to work out an economic plan that eventually pulled the city out of its crisis.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"203\" data-total-count=\"13983\">At the same time, he put his bank\u2019s affairs in order. By 1981, he and his prot\u00e9g\u00e9 <a title=\"Mr. Butcher\u2019s obituary.\" href=\"http://dealbook.nytimes.com/2012/08/27/willard-c-butcher-former-chief-of-chase-manhattan-dies-at-85/\">Willard C. Butcher</a> had restored Chase Manhattan to full health. He yielded his chairmanship to Mr. Butcher that year.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"458\" data-total-count=\"14441\">From 1976 to 1980, the bank\u2019s earnings more than doubled, and it outperformed its archrival, Citibank, in returns on assets, a critical indicator of a bank\u2019s profitability. Even after retiring from active management in 1981, Mr. Rockefeller continued to serve Chase as chairman of its international advisory council and to act as the bank\u2019s foreign diplomat. He did not hesitate to criticize United States officials for policies he considered mistaken.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-36\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-6 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-19\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"256\" data-total-count=\"14697\" id=\"story-continues-19\">He was notably harsh about President Carter. In 1980, he told The Washington Post that Mr. Carter had not done \u201cwhat most other countries do themselves, and expect us to do \u2014 namely, to make U.S. national interests our prime international objective.\u201d</p><div id=\"story-ad-5\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent \">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-20\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"370\" data-total-count=\"15067\" id=\"story-continues-20\">But Mr. Rockefeller also played the gadfly to Mr. Carter\u2019s far more conservative successor, President Ronald Reagan. While the Reagan administration was supporting anti-Marxist guerrillas in Africa, Mr. Rockefeller took a 10-nation tour of the continent in 1982 and declared that African Marxism was not a threat to the United States or to American business interests.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"471\" data-total-count=\"15538\">Late in life, Mr. Rockefeller was involved in controversies over Rockefeller Center, the Art Deco office building complex his father built in the 1930s. In 1985, the Rockefeller family mortgaged the property for $1.3 billion, pocketing an estimated $300 million. In 1989, the family sold 51 percent of the Rockefeller Group, which owned Rockefeller Center and other buildings, to the Mitsubishi Estate Company of Japan. Mitsubishi later increased its share to 80 percent.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"439\" data-total-count=\"15977\">The purchase represented the high tide of a buying spree of American properties by Japanese corporations, and it opened the family to criticism that it had surrendered an important national symbol to them. When Japan\u2019s economic bubble burst in the early 1990s and Mitsubishi was forced to declare Rockefeller Center in bankruptcy in 1995, Mr. Rockefeller was criticized again, this time for allowing the site to slip into financial ruin.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-40\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 ad-aggro-8 ad-aggro-10 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-21\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"224\" data-total-count=\"16201\" id=\"story-continues-21\">Before the year ended, Mr. Rockefeller put together a syndicate that bought control of Rockefeller Center. Then, in 2000, it was sold in a $1.85 billion deal that severed the center\u2019s last ties with the Rockefeller family.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"302\" data-total-count=\"16503\">As an octogenarian, Mr. Rockefeller, whose fortune was <a title=\"Web article by Forbes.\" href=\"http://www.forbes.com/profile/david-rockefeller/\">estimated in 2012 at $2.7 billion,</a> increasingly devoted himself to philanthropy, donating tens of millions of dollars in particular to Harvard, the Museum of Modern Art and <a title=\"Its Web site.\" href=\"http://www.rockefeller.edu/about/history\">the Rockefeller University</a>, which John D. Rockefeller Sr. founded in 1901.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-42\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-6 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-22\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"398\" data-total-count=\"16901\" id=\"story-continues-22\">Even in his 90s, David Rockefeller continued to work at a pace that would tire a much younger person. He spent more than half the year traveling on behalf of Chase or groups like the Council on Foreign Relations and the Trilateral Commission. In 2005, when he was interviewed in his offices at Rockefeller Center, he remained physically active, working with a trainer at the center\u2019s sports club.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"139\" data-total-count=\"17040\">He continued to collect art, including hundreds of paintings as well as furniture and works in colored glass, porcelain and petrified wood.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"501\" data-total-count=\"17541\">That same year, he pledged a $100 million bequest to the Museum of Modern Art. Such giving became grist for the society pages. One celebrity-filled fund-raising gala at the museum in 2005 drew 850 people paying as much as $90,000 for a table. The occasion was Mr. Rockefeller\u2019s 90th birthday, and at the end of the evening, he was presented with a birthday cake modeled after his house in Maine. Then it was off to a week in southern France to continue the celebration with 21 members of his family.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-44\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-23\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"282\" data-total-count=\"17823\" id=\"story-continues-23\">With the book \u201cMemoirs\u201d in 2002, he became, at age 87, the first in three generations of Rockefellers to publish an autobiography. Asked why he wrote it, he replied in his characteristic reserved tone, \u201cWell, it just occurred to me that I had led a rather interesting life.\u201d</p><div id=\"addenda\" class=\"addenda\">\n    <div class=\"story-addendum story-content theme-correction\">\n        <strong> Correction: March 20, 2017 </strong> <br/>\n        Because of an editing error, an earlier version of a picture caption with this obituary misstated the year a photograph of David Rockefeller was taken.
