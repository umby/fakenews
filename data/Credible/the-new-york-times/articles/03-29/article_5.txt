Perhaps, but our polling suggests the opposite. In our <a href=\"https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2016/10/30/upshot/florida-poll.html?_r=0\">pre-election</a> <a href=\"https://www.nytimes.com/2016/09/19/upshot/introducing-the-new-york-times-upshot-siena-college-poll.html\">Upshot/Siena</a> <a href=\"https://www.nytimes.com/2016/11/08/upshot/trump-and-clinton-tied-in-final-upshot-poll-of-north-carolina.html\">polls</a>, voters were likelier than nonvoters to support their party\u2019s nominee.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"107\" data-total-count=\"8325\">Survey data, along with countless journalistic accounts, also suggest that voters switched in huge numbers.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"190\" data-total-count=\"8515\">Throughout the campaign, polls of registered voters \u2014 which are not subject to changes in turnout \u2014 showed Mrs. Clinton faring much worse than Mr. Obama among white working-class voters.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-28\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-4 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-13\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"236\" data-total-count=\"8751\" id=\"story-continues-13\">The postelection survey data tells a similar story: Mrs. Clinton won Mr. Obama\u2019s white-working class supporters by a margin of only 78 percent to 18 percent against Mr. Trump, according to the Cooperative Congressional Election Study.</p><p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"153\" data-total-count=\"8904\">In the Midwestern battleground states and Pennsylvania, Mrs. Clinton had an advantage of 76 percent to 20 percent among white working-class Obama voters.</p><button class=\"button comments-button  theme-speech-bubble-large\" data-skip-to-para-id=\"\">\n</button>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"281\" data-total-count=\"9185\">The survey data isn\u2019t perfect. It relies on voters\u2019 accurate recall of their 2012 vote, and that type of recall is often biased toward the winner. Indeed, the C.C.E.S. found that Mr. Obama had 54 percent of support among 2012 voters, compared with his actual 51 percent finish.</p><div id=\"story-ad--aggro-30\" class=\"story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent  ad-aggro-6 ad-aggro-10 aggro-only\">\n    <div class=\"accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden\">\n    <p>Advertisement</p>\n</div>\n<a class=\"visually-hidden skip-to-text-link\" href=\"#story-continues-14\">Continue reading the main story</a>\n</div>\n<p class=\"story-body-text story-content\" data-para-count=\"152\" data-total-count=\"9337\" id=\"story-continues-14\">But the data all points in the same direction: Shifts in turnout were not the dominant factor in Mr. Trump\u2019s success among white working-class voters.</p><footer class=\"story-footer story-content\">\n    <div class=\"story-meta\">\n                        <div class=\"story-info\"><p><a href=\"http://www.nytimes.com/upshot/\">The Upshot</a> provides news, analysis and graphics about politics, policy and everyday life.
