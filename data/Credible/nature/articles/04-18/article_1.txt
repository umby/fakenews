entry-title article-heading'><a href='/aviewfromthebridge/2017/04/18/change-agent/'><em>Change Agent</em>: CRISPR-flavoured fiction</a></h1><p class=\"wpn-byline\"><time datetime=\"2017-04-18T09:08:57+01:00\" pubdate><span class=\"published\"><abbr class=\"value\" title=\"2017-04-18\">April 18, 2017 </abbr>|<abbr class=\"value\" title=\"2017-04-18\"> 9:08 am</abbr></span></time><span class=\"divider\"> | </span>Posted by <span class=\"author vcard\"><a class=\"url fn n\" href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/author/barbara-kiser/\" title=\"Barbara Kiser\">Barbara Kiser</a></span><span class=\"divider\"> | </span><span class=\"category\"><span class=\"before\">Category: </span><a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/arts/\" rel=\"tag\">Arts</a>, <a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/genomics/\" rel=\"tag\">Genomics</a>, <a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/science-fiction/\" rel=\"tag\">Science fiction</a>, <a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/technology/\" rel=\"tag\">Technology</a></span> </p><!--  before_entry  -->\r\n\t\t\t\t</header>\r\n\t\t\t\t\r\n\t\t\t\t<section>\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"wpn-entry-content content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<h3><em>Posted on behalf of Sara Reardon</em></h3>\n<p><a class=\"wpn-image-link\" href=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/9781101984666.jpg\"><img class=\"size-full wp-image-6111 wpn-image alignright\" title=\"9781101984666\" src=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/9781101984666.jpg\" alt=\"9781101984666\" width=\"298\" height=\"450\" /></a>It\u2019s 2045, and the genetic editing system <a href=\"http://www.nature.com/news/crispr-1.17547\">CRISPR</a> has become a mainstay of society, producing\u00a0everything from housecat-sized tigers to geopolitical intrigues. The United Nations has approved a sensible list of gene edits that can be legally used to eliminate specific genetic diseases from human embryos. This international concord works as well as one could expect from a sluggish bureaucracy trying to rein in a lucrative new enterprise. Before the treaty\u2019s ink is dry, underground labs in Asia are offering \u201cvanity edits\u201d to parents willing to pay for smarter, healthier children. A single CRISPR snip to a gene that reduces the risk of heart disease might be routine and relatively cheap; altering the many genes that contribute to a complex feature like intelligence will cost much more. And that\u2019s before you factor in the legal consequences if you get caught designing your perfect baby. As one illicit geneticist says, \u201call genetics is warfare\u201d.</p>\n<p>So begins <em><a href=\"http://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/536914/change-agent-by-daniel-suarez/9781101984666/\">Change Agent</a></em>: a sci-fi thriller set in Southeast Asia with colourful and scientifically believable elements embellishing a fairly tired plot. Former software developer <a href=\"http://thedaemon.com/\">Daniel Suarez</a> drew on still-cutting-edge research for his novel, one of the first to namecheck CRISPR as the catalyst for dystopia.</p>\n<p>In Suarez\u2019s imagined future, crime involving genetically modified humans has become so pervasive that international police organisation INTERPOL has devoted massive resources to dealing with it. But when detective Kenneth Durand finds himself hot on the heels of an organized crime ring in Singapore, he gets jabbed with a \u201cchange agent\u201d. He awakens weeks later, shocked to find his body inexplicably transformed into that of the cartel\u2019s ringleader, Marcus Demang Wyckes<strong>.</strong></p>\n<p>No one believes Durand\u2019s explanation, least of all fellow INTERPOL agents who see him as the man whose face is on every wanted poster in Asia. After all, even the best scientists in 2045 believe it is impossible to genetically edit a living person<strong>.</strong> So Durand-as-Wyckes sets off alone to track down the real Wyckes and find a way to reverse-engineer his own body. That journey takes him through a landscape of sci-fi clich\u00e9 \u2013 an underground nightclub of bio-enhancement enthusiasts, a shadowy Chinese trafficking ring with an invisible leader, intrusive augmented-reality ads.</p>\n<h3>Biotechnological flights of fancy</h3>\n<p>Yet Suarez has sprinkled the narrative with clever ideas inspired by current technologies. Singapore\u2019s streets crawl with drug addicts, who tattoo molecular compounds onto their bodies so that dealers with 3-D printers can synthesize the drugs to deliver personalized highs. The Burmese government, which is waging genocide on its hill tribes, destroys their crops with <a href=\"http://www.nature.com/news/gene-drives-thwarted-by-emergence-of-resistant-organisms-1.21397\">gene drives</a> \u2014 a controversial technology that can destroy populations by introducing genes that kill offspring. Nearly every other page is a glimpse into some biotechnological flight of fancy.</p>\n<p>Suarez\u2019s descriptions of the capacities and limitations of CRISPR, among other real-life technologies, are clear and mostly accurate, with minimal artistic licence. It\u2019s the novel\u2019s plot that \u2014 although fast-moving \u2014 fails to impress. As Durand flees his pursuers, he fights an unconvincing war with himself, as Wyckes\u2019 grafted-on persona tries to drive him to violence. The enemies and allies that he picks up along the way are hackneyed and forgettable. This is especially true of the moustache-twirling Wyckes, whose denouement would be described as disappointing if we had cared about him in the first place. I won\u2019t spoil everything, but suffice to say that Suarez wastes his most original idea in Wyckes\u2019s bizarre engineered hitman, whose clever biochemical makeup repulses normal humans<strong>.</strong></p>\n<p>When we finally meet the CRISPRers, it\u2019s in coastal Thailand (where else?). Potential parents sit through a parade of perfect children as if it\u2019s a presentation for timeshare vacation condos. Predictably, the youngsters are a cover for the criminals\u2019 more profitable product: children engineered with defective brains and enhanced muscles that make them disciplined workers and soldiers. Certainly people in 2045 must have read <a href=\"https://rap.macmillan.com/owa/,DanaInfo=cas-eu.macmillan.com,SSL+redir.aspx?C=zF62vUBM7XvUtLoemhbso1tCVUUULifc8eEp0hjht0z31-XXXIPUCA..&amp;URL=http%3a%2f%2fwww.nature.com%2fnature%2fjournal%2fv503%2fn7476%2ffull%2f503338a.html\"><em>Brave New World</em></a>. And meanwhile, readers will experience less shock than scepticism over how INTERPOL ever let crime get this bad right under their noses.</p>\n<p>Perhaps that dulled reaction is what makes <em>Change Agent</em> most memorable. We have become so used to fictional explorations and academic treatises on engineering humans \u2014 from <a href=\"http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v535/n7613/full/535490a.html\">Mary Shelley\u2019s <em>Frankenstein</em></a> to recent editorials in <em>Nature</em> \u2014 that the deranged possibilities presented by the technologies fail to thrill us any more. In an era stranger than fiction, sci-fi writers are increasingly hard-pressed to generate the requisite surprise, even as the scientific advances motor on<strong>.</strong></p>\n<p><strong>Sara Reardon is a reporter for <em>Nature</em> working on biomedical research and policy, based in Washington DC. She tweets at <a href=\"https://twitter.com/Sara_Reardon\">@Sara_Reardon</a>. </strong></p>\n<p>&nbsp;</p>\n<p><strong>For\u00a0<em>Nature</em>\u2019s full coverage of science in culture, visit www.nature.com/news/booksandarts.</strong></p>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t</div>\r\n\t\t\t\t</section><!--  .entry-content  -->\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t<div class=\"wpn-entry-meta\">\n
