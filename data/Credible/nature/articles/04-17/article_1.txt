datetime=\"2017-04-17T13:00:18+01:00\" pubdate><span class=\"published\"><abbr class=\"value\" title=\"2017-04-17\">17 Apr 2017 </abbr>|<abbr class=\"value\" title=\"13:00:18+0100\"> 13:00 BST</abbr></span></time><span class=\"divider\"> | </span>Posted by <span class=\"author vcard\"><a class=\"url fn n\" href=\"/naturejobs/author/Jack-Leeming/\" title=\"Jack Leeming\">Jack Leeming</a></span><span class=\"divider\"> | </span><span class=\"category\"><span class=\"before\">Category: </span><a href=\"/naturejobs/category/scientistonthemove-2/\" rel=\"tag\">#ScientistOnTheMove</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/academia-2/\" rel=\"tag\">Academia</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/careers-articles/\" rel=\"tag\">Careers articles</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/diversity-2/\" rel=\"tag\">Diversity</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/events/\" rel=\"tag\">Events</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/in-the-news/\" rel=\"tag\">In the news</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/mobility-2/\" rel=\"tag\">Mobility</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/perspectives/\" rel=\"tag\">Perspectives</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/scientists-on-the-move/\" rel=\"tag\">Scientists on the move</a>, <a href=\"/naturejobs/category/us/\" rel=\"tag\">US</a></span> </p><!--  before_entry  -->\r\n\t\t\t\t</header>\r\n\t\t\t\t\r\n\t\t\t\t<section>\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"wpn-entry-content content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<h2>How is the Trump administration affecting foreign scientists working in the US?</h2>\n<p><em>A Spanish bioscience postdoc on a non-immigrant J1 visa, based in California, who wishes to remain anonymous, shares her perspective as a foreigner in the US in the current political climate with science writer Louisa Cockbill.</em></p>\n<h2>We\u2019re now three months into Trump\u2019s presidency.\u00a0 What effect has it had on you?</h2>\n<p>On election night I was very disappointed and upset. It was frustrating not to have the right to vote as the political decisions made in this country affect my life. The uncertainty of what happens now is kind of scary. In general I still feel welcomed and still love living here. However, as an immigrant, a woman and a scientist it\u2019s not surprising that I\u2019m a little worried &#8211; these aren\u2019t positions that seem to be supported by the administration. I check nearly every day to see if they have decided to change something with my visa or green card applications that may affect me personally.</p>\n<div style=\"width:871px\" class=\"wpn-caption aligncenter\"><img src=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Inauguration_of_Donald_Trump_C24oVNRWEAMnIWo.jpg\" alt=\"\" width=\"871\" height=\"504\" /><div class=\"wpn-caption-text\"><p class=\"caption\">The inauguration of President Trump</p></div>\n            </div>\n<p><span id=\"more-13395\"></span></p>\n<h2>How do you think the political situation in the UK and US will affect your career prospects?</h2>\n<p>My plan is to stay in the US. At the moment, as far as I am aware, there have been no restrictions on bioscience visas, but the administration has spoken about targeting other science groups, such as engineers in Silicon Valley, by changing <a href=\"https://www.forbes.com/sites/mattdrange/2017/01/30/trumps-proposed-shakeup-of-work-visa-program-could-leave-silicon-valley-scrambling/#4abc1894a899\">H1B visa allocation</a>. Maybe tomorrow bioscience immigrants will be targeted.</p>\n<p>If they reduce the number of green cards, competition for the cards will increase and I don\u2019t know how they will decide who deserves one. The US attracts a lot of talent and there are a lot of people in my situation, but I\u2019ve never heard of anyone in science applying and being denied. I don\u2019t know whether that will change now.</p>\n<p>If I can\u2019t get permanent residency then I will have to reconsider my plan B, which had been England, but Brexit has made my prospects there just as unlikely.</p>\n<h2>How did you feel about Trump\u2019s executive order banning travel into the US from six Muslim-majority countries?</h2>\n<p>It\u2019s scary because even people who had legal visas and permanent residency documentation were treated like they didn\u2019t have a right to be here. It feels like no one is safe.</p>\n<h2>How has your institute responded to the changes introduced since the election?</h2>\n<p>A rather shy email was sent out after the election in November that was concerned with how funding might be affected, and then after the travel ban another email was sent that advised against foreign travel.</p>\n<p>I was surprised that they didn\u2019t state in a slightly stronger way that they disagreed with the way the administration was dealing with, especially immigration issues, as half of the institute\u2019s post-docs are not Americans. I was expecting more support.</p>\n<h2>Have any of the executive orders affected you directly?</h2>\n<p>Not so far. I\u2019m guessing that the funding cuts will affect the institute in the long run, but for now the funding for our lab is secure.</p>\n<h2>How do you think funding cuts will affect science in the US in the long run?</h2>\n<p>The US is the best place for funding science and if it gets cut here, where do we go? A cut down in science, even if it is only for four years, will have a slow recovery. That is what happened in Spain. It takes a long time to get back up to speed with the rest of the world.</p>\n<h2>Have you or do you plan to be involved in any protests?</h2>\n<p>I went to the Women\u2019s March and I thought it was a great experience. I\u2019m planning on going to the March for Science and I\u2019m just generally trying to get involved in politics as much as I can.</p>\n<h2>What are you hoping to achieve there?</h2>\n<p>Protests are good because they bring awareness to an issue. At the Women\u2019s March it was great to see so many different people gather together and publicly declare that they are upset. But protests are only the first step, other steps have to be taken to really make your voice heard \u2014 communicating with representatives to ensure that they know who you are and what your opinion is. However, without the right to elect I don\u2019t think that my opinion will be considered valuable to politicians and congress. I\u2019m very grateful for the Americans who are getting involved to change things, but it\u2019s very frustrating that I cannot do much.</p>\n<h2>What are you hoping for in the next few years?</h2>\n<p>I hope that the house and senate will go back to the democrats in the next couple of years. As for me, I am going to continue with my plan and apply for permanent residency.</p>\n<p>&nbsp;</p>\n<p><em>Louisa Cockbill is a freelance science writer with a popular science blog at </em><a What effect has it had on you?\n\nOn election night I was very disappointed and upset. It was frustrating not to have the right to vote as the political decisions made in this country affect my life. At the moment, as far as I am aware, there have been no restrictions on bioscience visas, but the administration has spoken about targeting other science groups, such as engineers in Silicon Valley, by changing H1B visa allocation. The US attracts a lot of talent and there are a lot of people in my situation, but I\u2019ve never heard of anyone in science applying and being denied. I don\u2019t know whether that will change now.\n\nIf I can\u2019t get permanent residency then I will have to reconsider my plan B, which had been England, but Brexit has made my prospects there just as unlikely.\n\nHow did you feel about Trump\u2019s executive order banning travel into the US from six Muslim-majority countries?\n\nIt\u2019s scary because even people who had legal visas and permanent residency documentation were treated like they didn\u2019t have a right to be here. It feels like no one is safe.\n\nHow has your institute responded to the changes introduced since the election?\n\nA rather shy email was sent out after the election in November that was concerned with how funding might be affected, and then after the travel ban another email was sent that advised against foreign travel.\n\nI was surprised that they didn\u2019t state in a slightly stronger way that they disagreed with the way the administration was dealing with, especially immigration issues, as half of the institute\u2019s post-docs are not Americans. I was expecting more support.\n\nHave any of the executive orders affected you directly?\n\nNot so far. I\u2019m guessing that the funding cuts will affect the institute in the long run, but for now the funding for our lab is secure.\n\nHow do you think funding cuts will affect science in the US in the long run?\n\nThe US is the best place for funding science and if it gets cut here, where do we go? A cut down in science, even if it is only for four years, will have a slow recovery. That is what happened in Spain. It takes a long time to get back up to speed with the rest of the world.\n\nHave you or do you plan to be involved in any protests?\n\nI went to the Women\u2019s March and I thought it was a great experience. But protests are only the first step, other steps have to be taken to really make your voice heard \u2014 communicating with representatives to ensure that they know who you are and what your opinion is. However, without the right to elect I don\u2019t think that my opinion will be considered valuable to politicians and congress.
