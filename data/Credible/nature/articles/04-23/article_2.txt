article-heading'><a href='/aviewfromthebridge/2017/04/23/henrietta-lacks-on-film/'>An immortal life: Henrietta Lacks on film</a></h1><p class=\"wpn-byline\"><time datetime=\"2017-04-23T08:54:59+01:00\" pubdate><span class=\"published\"><abbr class=\"value\" title=\"2017-04-23\">April 23, 2017 </abbr>|<abbr class=\"value\" title=\"2017-04-23\"> 8:54 am</abbr></span></time><span class=\"divider\"> | </span>Posted by <span class=\"author vcard\"><a class=\"url fn n\" href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/author/barbara-kiser/\" title=\"Barbara Kiser\">Barbara Kiser</a></span><span class=\"divider\"> | </span><span class=\"category\"><span class=\"before\">Category: </span><a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/arts/\" rel=\"tag\">Arts</a>, <a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/government/\" rel=\"tag\">Government</a>, <a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/lab-life/\" rel=\"tag\">Lab life</a>, <a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/medical-research/\" rel=\"tag\">Medical research</a>, <a href=\"/aviewfromthebridge/category/society/\" rel=\"tag\">Society</a></span> </p><!--  before_entry  -->\r\n\t\t\t\t</header>\r\n\t\t\t\t\r\n\t\t\t\t<section>\r\n\t\t\t\t\t<div class=\"wpn-entry-content content\">\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<h3><strong><em>Posted on behalf of Ewen Callaway</em></strong></h3>\n<div id=\"attachment_6157\" style=\"width:385px\" class=\"wpn-caption alignright\"><a class=\"wpn-image-link\" href=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/p12871796_p_v8_aa.jpg\"><img class=\"wpn-image wp-image-6157\" title=\"p12871796_p_v8_aa\" src=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/p12871796_p_v8_aa-683x1024.jpg\" alt=\"p12871796_p_v8_aa\" width=\"385\" height=\"578\" /></a><div class=\"wpn-caption-text\"><p class=\"caption\">In the HBO film based on Rebecca Skloot&#8217;s book of the same name, Oprah Winfrey plays Henrietta Lacks&#8217; daughter Deborah Lacks.</p></div>\n            </div>\n<p>The idea that people should have a say over how their cells are used in research isn\u2019t revolutionary, but it flies in the face of research practices over the past century. That it nearly became law is <a href=\"https://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v500/n7461/full/500141a.html\">due</a> in no small part to Rebecca Skloot\u2019s 2010 bestseller <a href=\"http://rebeccaskloot.com/the-immortal-life/\"><em>The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks</em></a>, the story of the African-American woman living in Baltimore, Maryland, whose fatal tumour \u2013 taken by scientists at Johns Hopkins Hospital in 1951 without the knowledge or permission of Lacks or her family \u2014 gave rise to the first immortal human cell line, HeLa.</p>\n<p>The book fuelled a much-needed conversation about scientists\u2019 moral obligations to research participants and their families. Now a powerful film adaptation of the same name, starring Oprah Winfrey as Lacks\u2019 youngest daughter Deborah, looks set to amplify that.</p>\n<p>Skloot\u2019s book covered a lot of ground, and the film\u2019s director George C. Wolfe (best known for directing and producing Broadway hits such as Tony Kushner\u2019s <em>Angels in America</em>) does an admirable job cramming in details about how HeLa cells were established and their ongoing impact on research. But the movie, broadcast on 22 April on premium US television network HBO, largely covers the decade it took for Skloot to report and publish her book. It focuses in particular on her efforts to gain the trust of Lacks\u2019 family and build an emotional bond with Deborah Lacks.</p>\n<p>Their relationship can feel overly dramatized, although Wolfe should not be faulted for taking some dramatic licence with Skloot\u2019s book in what is, after all, a dramatisation (she also served as an executive producer). But much of the film rings true. A scene in which Deborah Lacks questions Skloot\u2019s financial motives and grabs her arm is exactly as described in the book.</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_6173\" style=\"width:640px\" class=\"wpn-caption alignright\"><a class=\"wpn-image-link\" href=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/Deborah-Lacks.jpg\"><img class=\"size-full wp-image-6173 wpn-image\" title=\"Deborah Lacks\" src=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/Deborah-Lacks.jpg\" alt=\"Deborah Lacks.\" width=\"640\" height=\"427\" /></a><div class=\"wpn-caption-text\"><p class=\"caption\">Deborah Lacks.</p><p class=\"credit\">Rebecca Skloot</p></div>\n            </div>\n<p>By omitting some key aspects of the book \u2014 the science and history of cell culture and large swathes of Lacks\u2019 biography \u2014 the film can feel meta. It is, after all, a film based on a book about a journalist trying to write a book. But it should encourage more people to read the story and absorb its powerful message of social injustice institutionalized by science.</p>\n<p>US National Institutes of Health (NIH) director Francis Collins and then-deputy Kathy Hudson have noted that Lacks\u2019 story <a href=\"https://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v500/n7461/full/500141a.html\">inspired policy changes</a> in the rules that govern research on human subjects (officially known as the Federal Policy for the Protection of Human Subjects, but widely known as the Common Rule). &#8220;The people who are participating in research and providing pieces of themselves should be providing permission as well,&#8221; Hudson told <em>Nature</em> in 2015, when the government floated a <a href=\"https://www.nature.com/news/us-agencies-plan-research-ethics-overhaul-1.18309\">proposal </a>that would have required them to get approval to reuse discarded samples of blood, urine and other specimens for studies beyond those the subject initially agreed to. But the proposal caused consternation among many scientists. They breathed a sigh of relief this year, when the\u00a0 <a href=\"http://www.nature.com/news/controversial-patient-consent-proposal-left-out-of-research-ethics-reforms-1.21330\">final version</a>. of the Common Rule largely maintained the status quo. As long as a participant\u2019s name is removed from the sample, scientists needn\u2019t obtain new consent.</p>\n<div id=\"attachment_6169\" style=\"width:330px\" class=\"wpn-caption alignleft\"><a class=\"wpn-image-link\" href=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/Lacks-pic.jpg\"><img class=\"wpn-image wp-image-6169\" title=\"Lacks pic\" src=\"http://blogs.nature.com/aviewfromthebridge/files/2017/04/Lacks-pic.jpg\" alt=\"Henrietta Lacks.\" width=\"330\" height=\"411\" /></a><div class=\"wpn-caption-text\"><p class=\"caption\">Henrietta Lacks.</p><p class=\"credit\">Courtesy of the Lacks family</p></div>\n            </div>\n<p>That may seem like a setback in a quest for justice the Lacks family is all too familiar with. But other developments suggest that the Lacks\u2019 story has changed how research participants are treated by scientists.</p>\n<p>Currently, a movement for \u201cdynamic consent\u201d \u2014 focused on the establishment of a lasting relationship between researchers and study participants \u2014 is growing. It was pioneered by <a href=\"http://www.nature.com/ejhg/journal/v23/n2/full/ejhg201471a.html\">professor of health, law and policy Jane Kaye</a>, while elements of it are being used <a href=\"http://www.nature.com/news/geneticists-attempt-to-heal-rifts-with-aboriginal-communities-1.20629\">in Australia</a>. Participants or their relatives (in cases where they are no longer alive) are kept up to date on how their samples are used in research, and they can opt out of particular studies or remove their sample entirely.</p>\n<p>The Lacks are finally gaining some control over HeLa cells, if not the remuneration many members have in the past and some still seek. In 2013, after researchers funded by the NIH sequenced the HeLa cell genome without the knowledge or consent of the Lacks family, Collins helped <a href=\"http://www.nature.com/news/deal-done-over-hela-cell-line-1.13511\">broker a deal</a> with the family to limit access to the data. Now, all NIH-funded scientists and others who want the best quality HeLa genome must explain their research to a <a href=\"http://blogs.nature.com/news/2013/09/nih-approves-first-uses-of-hela-genome.html\">committee</a> that includes a Lacks family member. It\u2019s enough for a sequel.</p>\n<p><strong>Ewen Callaway is a senior reporter for</strong><strong>\u00a0</strong><em><strong>Nature</strong></em><strong>\u00a0</strong><strong>based in London. He tweets at @ewencallaway.\u00a0</strong></p>\n<p>&nbsp;</p>\n<p><strong>For\u00a0</strong><em><strong>Nature</strong></em><strong>\u2019s full coverage of science in culture, visit That it nearly became law is due in no small part to Rebecca Skloot\u2019s 2010 bestseller The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks, the story of the African-American woman living in Baltimore, Maryland, whose fatal tumour \u2013 taken by scientists at Johns Hopkins Hospital in 1951 without the knowledge or permission of Lacks or her family \u2014 gave rise to the first immortal human cell line, HeLa.\n\nThe book fuelled a much-needed conversation about scientists\u2019 moral obligations to research participants and their families. Now a powerful film adaptation of the same name, starring Oprah Winfrey as Lacks\u2019 youngest daughter Deborah, looks set to amplify that.\n\nSkloot\u2019s book covered a lot of ground, and the film\u2019s director George C. Wolfe (best known for directing and producing Broadway hits such as Tony Kushner\u2019s Angels in America) does an admirable job cramming in details about how HeLa cells were established and their ongoing impact on research. But the movie, broadcast on 22 April on premium US television network HBO, largely covers the decade it took for Skloot to report and publish her book. It focuses in particular on her efforts to gain the trust of Lacks\u2019 family and build an emotional bond with Deborah Lacks.\n\nTheir relationship can feel overly dramatized, although Wolfe should not be faulted for taking some dramatic licence with Skloot\u2019s book in what is, after all, a dramatisation (she also served as an executive producer). But much of the film rings true. A scene in which Deborah Lacks questions Skloot\u2019s financial motives and grabs her arm is exactly as described in the book.\n\nRebecca Skloot\n\nBy omitting some key aspects of the book \u2014 the science and history of cell culture and large swathes of Lacks\u2019 biography \u2014 the film can feel meta. It is, after all, a film based on a book about a journalist trying to write a book. But it should encourage more people to read the story and absorb its powerful message of social injustice institutionalized by science.\n\nUS National Institutes of Health (NIH) director Francis Collins and then-deputy Kathy Hudson have noted that Lacks\u2019 story inspired policy changes in the rules that govern research on human subjects (officially known as the Federal Policy for the Protection of Human Subjects, but widely known as the Common Rule). \u201cThe people who are participating in research and providing pieces of themselves should be providing permission as well,\u201d Hudson told Nature in 2015, when the government floated a proposal that would have required them to get approval to reuse discarded samples of blood, urine and other specimens for studies beyond those the subject initially agreed to. But the proposal caused consternation among many scientists. They breathed a sigh of relief this year, when the final version. of the Common Rule largely maintained the status quo. As long as a participant\u2019s name is removed from the sample, scientists needn\u2019t obtain new consent.\n\nCourtesy of the Lacks family\n\nThat may seem like a setback in a quest for justice the Lacks family is all too familiar with. But other developments suggest that the Lacks\u2019 story has changed how research participants are treated by scientists.\n\nCurrently, a movement for \u201cdynamic consent\u201d \u2014 focused on the establishment of a lasting relationship between researchers and study participants \u2014 is growing. It was pioneered by professor of health, law and policy Jane Kaye, while elements of it are being used in Australia. Participants or their relatives (in cases where they are no longer alive) are kept up to date on how their samples are used in research, and they can opt out of particular studies or remove their sample entirely.\n\nThe Lacks are finally gaining some control over HeLa cells, if not the remuneration many members have in the past and some still seek. In 2013, after researchers funded by the NIH sequenced the HeLa cell genome without the knowledge or consent of the Lacks family, Collins helped broker a deal with the family to limit access to the data. Now, all NIH-funded scientists and others who want the best quality HeLa genome must explain their research to a committee that includes a Lacks family member.
