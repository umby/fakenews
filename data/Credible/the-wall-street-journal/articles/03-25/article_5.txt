(According to the U.S. Census Bureau, boomers are people born between 1946 and 1964). In about 20 years, all the boomers will have reached 70. So, the number of celebrity deaths last year wasn\u2019t exceptional, according to a study by researchers at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, though the number of \u201cmega famous\u201d celebrity deaths was. Because of their penchant for hard living, rocker deaths are likely to stay consistently high. \u201cThe musicians are ahead of their audience, if you will, on the death curve,\u201d says demographer Kenneth Johnson at the University of New Hampshire.</p> <p>The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in Cleveland is already scrambling to keep up. \u201cWe play their music all day long, lower our flag, and hang their image in a place of honor for a year. And we have a special exhibition,\u201d President Greg Davis says. \u201cBut the exhibitions are getting kind of crowded.\u201d Almost 60% of the Rock Hall\u2019s more than 800 inductees are still living, meaning the biggest wave of the boomer musician deaths has yet to come.</p> <h6>HOW IS THIS DIFFERENT?</h6> <p>Rock has an outsize influence on music sales. Rock\u2019s heyday is hard to fathom: Unlike jazz and classical music, which became niche businesses after dominating mainstream pop culture, rock was the first musical genre to become a huge, multibillion-dollar global industry with staying power.</p> <p>A confluence of technological, cultural and musical forces in the middle of the 20th century led to the modern pop/rock industry. Record players, a fixture in most American homes by the late 1960s, let America\u2019s restive youth listen to music away from their parents.</p> <p>Rock concerts went global and took over sports stadiums. The rise of mass media\u2014especially television\u2014enabled global celebrity. \u201cWe produced famous people at a rate we never did before,\u201d MIT professor C\u00e9sar Hidalgo and his colleagues said in a study.</p> <p></p> <h6>WHY IS ROCK SO BIG?</h6> <p>Much of rock\u2019s commercial success was possible because of the way the industry was structured. By the 1980s, cash-rich major labels were helping finance tours, throwing money at fledgling acts and investing huge sums in veteran stars even when their careers floundered.</p> <p>Such investments\u2014equivalent in spirit to the R&amp;D expenditures of pharmaceutical firms\u2014helped artists build enduring brands and transformed superstars into major corporations that overshadow young pop/rock acts even today.</p> <p>Guns N\u2019 Roses, whose 1987 album \u201cAppetite for Destruction\u201d is the biggest-selling debut in U.S. history, headlined last year\u2019s Coachella Valley Music and Arts Festival.
