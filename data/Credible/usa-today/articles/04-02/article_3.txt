As sure as clockwork, they come.</p><p>It's the uneasiness that first wakes her just before midnight. Her tossing and turning stirs her husband who reaches out for her in the dark. She manages to fall back to sleep a few more hours.</p><p>Then comes 3 a.m. \u00a0That was the time the ringing of her home phone woke Rowsey. Her father told her that her only child, 19-year-old Colton Cavazos, was hurt and she needed to get to the downtown hospital.</p><p>The 14th of every month since November she relives the drive from the Flour Bluff home she shares with her husband. She remembers where her husband\u00a0pulled his truck over on Yorktown Boulevard and told her no matter how fast he drove, it wouldn\u2019t matter.</p><p>Her son was dead.</p><p>Rowsey envisions her son lying wrapped in white cloth on the hospital table. His face was so beautiful. He\u2019d just gotten a haircut. She went to hold him but hospital staff held her back.</p><p>\u201cYou can\u2019t touch him. He\u2019s part of a murder investigation,\u201d they said.</p><p>Some of her memory from the hospital is blurred. She learned he\u2019d been shot in the parking lot outside his apartment. His friend put pressure on the gunshot wound to try in vain to stop the bleeding. So many people were there until the gunshots were fired. The shooter drove away. A neighbor\u2019s security camera caught the whole incident.</p><p>Rowsey expected her son\u2019s killer to be behind bars that same Monday morning. No one was arrested that day or the next. By the end of the week, Corpus Christi police still had no suspect.</p><p>Rowsey\u2019s grief was compounded with a newfound purpose: To bring her son\u2019s killer to justice.</p><p>\u201cI will live however long I can to see that someone is held accountable,\u201d Rowsey said.</p><div id=\"module-position-P2MJaPwQZfw\" class=\"story-asset image-asset\"><aside itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" class=\"wide single-photo\"><img itemprop=\"url\" src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/66323efae19f1fb4624381139594e9fdbb75201b/c=136-0-2264-1600&amp;r=x408&amp;c=540x405/local/-/media/2017/03/30/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636264685045376383-Unsolved-homicides-5.jpg\" alt=\"Sally Rowsey, talks about her son as she stand next\" width=\"540\" height=\"405\" data-mycapture-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/media/2017/03/30/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636264685045376383-Unsolved-homicides-5.jpg\"\n                    data-mycapture-sm-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/4044b1e7645488621f157de466cf1a990870caa2/r=500x333/local/-/media/2017/03/30/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636264685045376383-Unsolved-homicides-5.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"name\" content=\"636264685045376383-Unsolved-homicides-5.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"height\" content=\"405\" /><meta itemprop=\"width\" content=\"540\" /><p>Sally Rowsey, talks about her son as she stand next to the urn and photos of Colton Cavazos, 19, who was shot in the parking lot outside his apartment Nov. 14, 2016.<meta itemprop=\"copyrightHolder\" content=\"Courtney Sacco/Caller-Times\" />&nbsp;<span class=\"credit\">(Photo: Courtney Sacco/Caller-Times)</span></p></aside><div class=\"clearfix\"></div></div><p>Nine homicides in Corpus Christi  spanning\u00a0 the past five years remain unsolved. That\u2019s potentially nine killers on the loose. Most of those unsolved cases are from 2016\u00a0including the shooting of Cavazos. The police department's clearance rate in homicide case has historically been around 90 percent.</p><p>The Caller-Times interviewed\u00a0a few of the\u00a0homicide victims' families and police on the eve of National Crime Victims' Rights Week, which begins Sunday.</p><p>Families of those killed last year are just beginning a pursuit for justice. \u00a0There's no guarantee they\u2019ll ever find the answers they\u2019re looking for.</p><p>Donna Watkins, founder of the local Homicide Survivors Support Group, has gone to funerals of parents and spouses who died without knowing who killed their loved ones.</p><h2 class=\"presto-h2\"><span class=\"print_subhead\">Holding out hope</span></h2><p>Detectives warn these families they may wait a month, a year, maybe longer. Police tell the families someone, somewhere knows something. And when that person who knows something is arrested for an unrelated crime, they\u00a0may talk in hopes of wagering a deal.</p><p>It happened in the case of Breanna Wood, a 21-year-old woman\u00a0who was missing four months before a Nueces County Jail inmate led authorities to her body in January. Her remains were\u00a0stuffed in a cardboard box and left in a grassy area near an abandoned home outside city limits. The inmate said Joseph Tejeda ordered him to dispose of her. Another inmate told authorities Tejeda admitted to the crime, according to police reports. Tejeda is awaiting trial.</p><p>The grandparents of 13-year-old Alex Torres waited nearly two years for someone to admit to police they knew who the killer was last year. \u00a0He was shot on his grandparents\u2019 doorstep when he answered the door. Police said the shooter\u00a0was looking for someone else. David Davila is charged with the killing, along with his former girlfriend, Christina Trevino, who is accused of driving the getaway car.</p><p>Families hang their hopes on stories like those.</p><div id=\"module-position-P2MJaPxqdxk\" class=\"story-asset image-asset\"><aside itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" class=\"wide single-photo\"><img itemprop=\"url\" src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/66323efae19f1fb4624381139594e9fdbb75201b/c=136-0-2264-1600&amp;r=x408&amp;c=540x405/local/-/media/2017/03/31/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636265605512925168-99119142-Unsolved-Homicides.jpg\" alt=\"Christopher Cavazos looks down at the urn for his son\" width=\"540\" height=\"405\" data-mycapture-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/media/2017/03/31/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636265605512925168-99119142-Unsolved-Homicides.jpg\"\n                    data-mycapture-sm-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/4044b1e7645488621f157de466cf1a990870caa2/r=500x333/local/-/media/2017/03/31/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636265605512925168-99119142-Unsolved-Homicides.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"name\" content=\"-99119142-Unsolved-HomicidesMAIN.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"height\" content=\"405\" /><meta itemprop=\"width\" content=\"540\" /><p>Christopher Cavazos looks down at the urn for his son Colton Cavazos, 19, who was shot in the parking lot outside his apartment Nov. 14, 2016.<meta itemprop=\"copyrightHolder\" content=\"Courtney Sacco/Caller-Times\" />&nbsp;<span class=\"credit\">(Photo: Courtney Sacco/Caller-Times)</span></p></aside><div class=\"clearfix\"></div></div><p>Colton Cavazos\u2019 mom and dad, Christopher Cavazos, remain faithful.</p><p>For others, like the family of Nathaniel Gutierrez,\u00a0trust in the system has started to unravel.</p><p>More than two years have passed since the 29-year-old\u00a0was shot on Dec. 19, 2014, in a car in the middle of the afternoon near Carroll High School.</p><p>Police found bullet casings about a block away. Nearby, police pulled over the driver of a silver Cadillac riddled with bullet holes. Several witnesses were questioned, but no arrests were made.</p><p>Jeremy Flores and his mother still don't understand why Nathaniel\u2019s killer hasn't been found. Police told the family his killing was drug related.\u00a0That came as no surprise to Flores.</p><div id=\"module-position-P2MJaPwhMTA\" class=\"story-asset image-asset\"><aside itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" class=\"single-photo float\"><img itemprop=\"url\" src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/37e74cadcfd03f2abe13a7b42e93436fc97aa8d8/c=0-0-718-957&amp;r=183&amp;c=0-0-180-240/local/-/media/2017/03/31/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636265619831632954-Nathan-and-Jeremy.jpg\" alt=\"Nathaniel Gutierrez, 9, (left) hugs his 5-year-old\" width=\"180\" height=\"240\" data-mycapture-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/media/2017/03/31/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636265619831632954-Nathan-and-Jeremy.jpg\"\n                    data-mycapture-sm-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/093de18d1eb3f381cb2ab9cc3a22e7517e14ba11/r=232x400/local/-/media/2017/03/31/TXGroup/CorpusChristi/636265619831632954-Nathan-and-Jeremy.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"name\" content=\"Nathan-and-Jeremy.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"height\" content=\"240\" /><meta itemprop=\"width\" content=\"180\" /><p>Nathaniel Gutierrez, 9, (left) hugs his 5-year-old brother Jeremy Flores. Not long before\u00a0Gutierrez\u2019s death, Flores had stopped speaking to his brother in an effort to distance himself from his old lifestyle.</p><p>Flores, too, knows his brother's killer is out there.</p><p>\"In my head and in my heart I know I could figure out who did this,\" Flores said. \"It kills me to not know and also to know that other people out there know what happened.\"</p><p>There was not enough evidence for police to present Gutierrez's case to the Nueces County District Attorney's Office, said Police Lt. Mark Tuley of the robbery and homicide unit.</p><p>Surveillance video footage was obtained from a convenience store in the area but Tuley said footage only showed images of before and after the shooting. She manages to fall back to sleep a few more hours.\n\nThen comes 3 a.m. That was the time the ringing of her home phone woke Rowsey. Her father told her that her only child, 19-year-old Colton Cavazos, was hurt and she needed to get to the downtown hospital.\n\nThe 14th of every month since November she relives the drive from the Flour Bluff home she shares with her husband. She remembers where her husband pulled his truck over on Yorktown Boulevard and told her no matter how fast he drove, it wouldn\u2019t matter.\n\nHer son was dead.\n\nRowsey envisions her son lying wrapped in white cloth on the hospital table. His face was so beautiful. He\u2019d just gotten a haircut. She went to hold him but hospital staff held her back.\n\n\u201cYou can\u2019t touch him. He\u2019s part of a murder investigation,\u201d they said.\n\nSome of her memory from the hospital is blurred. She learned he\u2019d been shot in the parking lot outside his apartment. His friend put pressure on the gunshot wound to try in vain to stop the bleeding. So many people were there until the gunshots were fired. The shooter drove away. A neighbor\u2019s security camera caught the whole incident.\n\nRowsey expected her son\u2019s killer to be behind bars that same Monday morning. No one was arrested that day or the next. By the end of the week, Corpus Christi police still had no suspect.\n\nRowsey\u2019s grief was compounded with a newfound purpose: To bring her son\u2019s killer to justice.\n\n\u201cI will live however long I can to see that someone is held accountable,\u201d Rowsey said.\n\nSally Rowsey, talks about her son as she stand next to the urn and photos of Colton Cavazos, 19, who was shot in the parking lot outside his apartment Nov. 14, 2016. (Photo: Courtney Sacco/Caller-Times)\n\nNine homicides in Corpus Christi spanning the past five years remain unsolved. That\u2019s potentially nine killers on the loose. Most of those unsolved cases are from 2016 including the shooting of Cavazos. The police department's clearance rate in homicide case has historically been around 90 percent.\n\nThe Caller-Times interviewed a few of the homicide victims' families and police on the eve of National Crime Victims' Rights Week, which begins Sunday.\n\nFamilies of those killed last year are just beginning a pursuit for justice. There's no guarantee they\u2019ll ever find the answers they\u2019re looking for.\n\nDonna Watkins, founder of the local Homicide Survivors Support Group, has gone to funerals of parents and spouses who died without knowing who killed their loved ones.\n\nHolding out hope\n\nDetectives warn these families they may wait a month, a year, maybe longer. Police tell the families someone, somewhere knows something. And when that person who knows something is arrested for an unrelated crime, they may talk in hopes of wagering a deal.\n\nIt happened in the case of Breanna Wood, a 21-year-old woman who was missing four months before a Nueces County Jail inmate led authorities to her body in January. Her remains were stuffed in a cardboard box and left in a grassy area near an abandoned home outside city limits. The inmate said Joseph Tejeda ordered him to dispose of her. Another inmate told authorities Tejeda admitted to the crime, according to police reports. Tejeda is awaiting trial.\n\nThe grandparents of 13-year-old Alex Torres waited nearly two years for someone to admit to police they knew who the killer was last year. He was shot on his grandparents\u2019 doorstep when he answered the door. Police said the shooter was looking for someone else. David Davila is charged with the killing, along with his former girlfriend, Christina Trevino, who is accused of driving the getaway car.\n\nFamilies hang their hopes on stories like those.\n\nChristopher Cavazos looks down at the urn for his son Colton Cavazos, 19, who was shot in the parking lot outside his apartment Nov. 14, 2016. (Photo: Courtney Sacco/Caller-Times)\n\nColton Cavazos\u2019 mom and dad, Christopher Cavazos, remain faithful.\n\nFor others, like the family of Nathaniel Gutierrez, trust in the system has started to unravel.\n\nMore than two years have passed since the 29-year-old was shot on Dec. 19, 2014, in a car in the middle of the afternoon near Carroll High School.\n\nPolice found bullet casings about a block away. Nearby, police pulled over the driver of a silver Cadillac riddled with bullet holes. Several witnesses were questioned, but no arrests were made.\n\nJeremy Flores and his mother still don't understand why Nathaniel\u2019s killer hasn't been found. Police told the family his killing was drug related. That came as no surprise to Flores.\n\nNathaniel Gutierrez, 9, (left) hugs his 5-year-old brother Jeremy Flores. Gutierrez's 2014 killing remains one of the city's unsolved homicide cases. (Photo: Contributed Photo)\n\nGutierrez was on probation and had been dealing drugs.\n\nFlores had gotten a job and wanted to better his life and urged his older brother to do the same.\n\nBut Flores felt Gutierrez was addicted to the streets. Not long before Gutierrez\u2019s death, Flores had stopped speaking to his brother in an effort to distance himself from his old lifestyle.\n\nFlores, too, knows his brother's killer is out there.\n\n\"In my head and in my heart I know I could figure out who did this,\" Flores said. \"It kills me to not know and also to know that other people out there know what happened.\"\n\nThere was not enough evidence for police to present Gutierrez's case to the Nueces County District Attorney's Office, said Police Lt. Mark Tuley of the robbery and homicide unit.\n\nSurveillance video footage was obtained from a convenience store in the area but Tuley said footage only showed images of before and after the shooting. Gutierrez\u2019s case went cold in 2016 due to lack of leads or suspects, Tuley said.\n\nNathaniel Gutierrez looks at his phone with his two sons, Lincoln (far left) and Nathan. (Photo: Contributed Photo)\n\nFlores struggles to believe every effort has been made.\n\n\"I definitely feel the system has failed my family,\" he said. \"The streets know what happened.\"\n\nTuley said there's a strong desire from homicide detectives to help the victims' families.\n\n\"The detectives think about those cases daily,\" Tuley said. \"(The detectives) discuss (the cases) with each other, they go over them and go over every single lead they might have, making sure they\u2019re doing everything they could possibly do to solve that case.\"\n\nStruggling to cope\n\nFlores has channeled his frustration and grief into art and caring for his brother's three children, Nathan, 8; Leah, 3; and Lincoln, 6.\n\nJeremy Flores has tried to look after his nephews and niece after his brother was killed in 2014. Pictured are Nathan (from left), Jeremy Flores and Lincoln. (Photo: Contributed Photo)\n\nFlores, an entrepreneur and internationally recognized spray paint muralist, honors his brother with his work. Gutierrez\u2019s name can sometimes be spotted in Flores\u2019 pieces. It\u2019s a nod to a dream the brothers once shared of owning their own businesses.\n\nSome who've lost loved ones are like Flores and able to pour their emotions into a cause.\n\nWatkins, motivated by her brother\u2019s killing, started the Homicide Survivors Support Group in 1993 to help families with whom she can relate. Her brother\u2019s 1972 death in Tennessee went unsolved about a year. Police made arrests but the suspects were acquitted at trial.\n\nWatkins has worked with about two dozen families of unsolved killings. For some, the loss and the questions have immobilized them. Watkins has known people who\u2019ve quit their jobs, lost their homes and filed for bankruptcy.\n\n\u201cThe longer that there are no clues coming in, it just compounds the grief,\u201d Watkins said. \u201cIt literally is a hole in the heart that gets bigger and bigger and bigger.
