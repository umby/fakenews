visitation area,\u00a0Yolanda Compton\u00a0noticed how much weight her nephew had lost.\u00a0Once healthy, he looked\u00a0gaunt, and his cheeks were covered with rosacea, a condition she'd never known him to have.</p><p>\"It's the stress,\" said Compton. \"He feels like he's going crazy.\"</p><p>Her nephew, 35-year-old Washington Klever Calle Delgado, picked up the prison phone so that they could talk. Separated by the glass, Compton\u00a0and Calle Delgado recounted holidays\u00a0and vacations\u00a0\u2014 good times,\u00a0before his March 3 arrest for overstaying the work visa that brought him to the U.S. in 2013.</p><p>She was desperate to brighten his mood.\u00a0\u201cIt\u2019s the worst place,\" Compton said.\u00a0\"He\u2019s treated like a criminal there.\u201d</p><div class=\"partner-placement partner-spike\"></div><p>Some people might\u00a0say that\u00a0Calle Delgado is\u00a0indeed a criminal, but a visa overstay is a civil charge, not a criminal offense, said Stephen Converse, a York\u00a0attorney who <a href=\"/story/news/2017/03/01/after-immigrant-arrests-restaurant-reopens/98591516/\">often represents immigrants</a>. Converse doesn't represent Calle Delgado.</p><div id=\"module-position-P2gxbcHXcTk\" class=\"story-asset image-asset\"><aside itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" class=\"single-photo float\"><img itemprop=\"url\" src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/b9074a5de1a7c319c194f1a02b3f13bcd0f390a9/c=0-25-1762-2374&amp;r=183&amp;c=0-0-180-240/local/-/media/2017/04/03/PAGroup/YorkDailyRecord/636268066978685593-YDR-JP-033117-ICE-2.jpg\" alt=\"Washington Klever Calle Delgado, 35, of Ecuador, poses\" width=\"180\" height=\"240\" data-mycapture-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/media/2017/04/03/PAGroup/YorkDailyRecord/636268066978685593-YDR-JP-033117-ICE-2.jpg\"\n                    data-mycapture-sm-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/7f134d1d239959ada60263fdead1c5c5fab33ef2/r=293x400/local/-/media/2017/04/03/PAGroup/YorkDailyRecord/636268066978685593-YDR-JP-033117-ICE-2.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"name\" content=\"YDR-JP-033117-ICE\" /><meta itemprop=\"height\" content=\"240\" /><meta itemprop=\"width\" content=\"180\" /><p>Washington Klever Calle Delgado, 35, of Ecuador, poses for a photo with his nephew. Calle Delgado was detained by ICE in Philadelphia and is incarcerated in York County Prison.<meta itemprop=\"copyrightHolder\" content=\"Submitted\" />&nbsp;<span class=\"credit\">(Photo: Submitted)</span></p></aside></div><p>\u201cThey are considered to be civil charges, which doesn\u2019t make a whole lot of sense since the consequences of these civil charges are so serious \u2014\u00a0at least as serious as a lot of criminal charges,\" Converse said.</p><div class=\"partner-outstream\"></div><p>The\u00a0term \"civil\" may bring to mind\u00a0lawsuits and divorce proceedings, rather than a prison sentence, but one result of the civil categorization is that\u00a0those held\u00a0on immigration charges don't have a right to\u00a0a public defender, Converse said.</p><p>It's just one part of a system that <a href=\"/story/news/2017/04/04/yorks-immigration-court-can-black-hole-information/99421356/\">shrouds immigrants'\u00a0arrests, detention and trials from public scrutiny</a>.</p><p>Calle Delgado's name is not listed on York County Prison's inmate roster, which is published daily online.</p><p>Searches\u00a0in\u00a0the unified judicial system of Pennsylvania\u00a0public web portal yielded no information about him.</p><p>When he is tried, it will be a quiet affair at the prison, Converse said. His family will be allowed to attend, if they know when the trial will happen.</p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><strong style=\"margin-right:3px\">More:</strong><a href=\"/story/news/2017/04/04/yorks-immigration-court-can-black-hole-information/99421356/\">York's immigration court can be black hole of information</a></span></p><p>A search tool on the Immigration and Customs Enforcement website does show that he\u00a0is being held at York County Prison.\u00a0That tool has become a lifeline for the families\u00a0who struggle to stay in touch with their detained relatives, Converse said.</p><p>Immigration and Customs Enforcement regional spokesman Adrian Smith\u00a0confirmed that Calle Delgado\u00a0is being detained at the prison \"pending\u00a0immigration proceedings.\" He's one of about 750 inmates there for immigration violations, according to February 2017 numbers provided by York County spokesman Mark Walters.</p><p>Immigrants do have a right to pay bond and get released from prison prior to a hearing, said Converse.\u00a0However, he's seeing a lot of bonds set around $10,000 to $15,000, all of which would be required prior to an inmate being released before trial.\u00a0\"The amount of bond set by some of the immigration judges has been pretty extraordinary.\u201d</p><p>Calle Delgado was not immediately available for comment. Prisoners have limited access to phones, and Calle Delgado doesn't speak English.</p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><strong style=\"margin-right:3px\">More:</strong><a href=\"/story/news/2017/03/15/ice-hails-nearly-200-immigration-arrests-pa-region/99172120/\">ICE hails nearly 200 immigration arrests in Pa. region</a></span></p><p>Compton said that, although her nephew is guilty of overstaying his visa, a month in prison is a harsh punishment for someone with no criminal past.</p><p>She\u00a0said he\u00a0came to the U.S. in 2013 to work with his brother, a U.S. citizen who had started a carpet installation and removal business in Philadelphia.</p><p>He'd never had any problems with the law until the morning of March 3 around 6 a.m.,\u00a0when he stopped to get food on his way to work, she said. ICE agents were searching for someone else, but when Calle Delgado failed to provide proper identification, they arrested him.</p><p>Converse said he's seen a drastic uptick in arrests similar to this one. Under the Obama administration, ICE focused on\u00a0people with criminal records. Now\u00a0the agency detains anyone who lacks residency or citizenship documents.</p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><strong>More:\u00a0<a href=\"/story/news/2017/03/01/after-immigrant-arrests-restaurant-reopens/98591516/\">After ICE arrests immigrants, Springettsbury restaurant reopens</a></strong></span></p><p>That's led to an overflow in prisons and a backlog in cases, Converse said. He's seen detained immigrants sent across the state or out of state as they wait to be tried. Those who seek deportation can spend weeks or months in prison, while those who fight their charges might\u00a0spend longer there.</p><p>Recently one of Converse's clients was transferred from York to\u00a0Cambria County Prison. The Western Pa. prison <a href=\"http://www.tribdem.com/news/cambria-county-prison-sees-increase-in-illegal-immigrant-inmates/article_a3884ed2-fed0-11e6-8b7c-2f4ff33ed161.html\">has become a holding station for immigrants</a> from as far away as West Virginia, who eventually come to York for deportation hearings.</p><p>\"It really threw a wrench in the works,\" Converse said.\u00a0\"Fortunately (ICE)\u00a0agreed to bring him back here, but it just shows how those transfers can make it hard for a client to meet with his lawyer.\"</p><p>The flood of arrests\u00a0has created a situation where\u00a0people end up in prison, without\u00a0legal representation\u00a0and separated\u00a0from their families.</p><p>After a month in prison, Calle Delgado is begging to be deported, Compton said. He has\u00a0citizenship in Spain and Ecuador and is eager to go to either country.\u00a0\u201cHe sent a letter asking to voluntarily leave the country.\u00a0He can\u2019t spend any more time in jail.\"</p><p><em>Reporter\u00a0Joel Shannon contributed to this report.\u00a0</em></p><div id=\"module-position-P2gxbcG-9t0\" class=\"story-asset inline-share-tools-asset\"><div Once healthy, he looked gaunt, and his cheeks were covered with rosacea, a condition she'd never known him to have.\n\n\"It's the stress,\" said Compton. \"He feels like he's going crazy.\"\n\nHer nephew, 35-year-old Washington Klever Calle Delgado, picked up the prison phone so that they could talk. Separated by the glass, Compton and Calle Delgado recounted holidays and vacations \u2014 good times, before his March 3 arrest for overstaying the work visa that brought him to the U.S. in 2013.\n\nShe was desperate to brighten his mood. \u201cIt\u2019s the worst place,\" Compton said. \"He\u2019s treated like a criminal there.\u201d\n\nSome people might say that Calle Delgado is indeed a criminal, but a visa overstay is a civil charge, not a criminal offense, said Stephen Converse, a York attorney who often represents immigrants. Converse doesn't represent Calle Delgado.\n\nWashington Klever Calle Delgado, 35, of Ecuador, poses for a photo with his nephew. Calle Delgado was detained by ICE in Philadelphia and is incarcerated in York County Prison. (Photo: Submitted)\n\n\u201cThey are considered to be civil charges, which doesn\u2019t make a whole lot of sense since the consequences of these civil charges are so serious \u2014 at least as serious as a lot of criminal charges,\" Converse said.\n\nThe term \"civil\" may bring to mind lawsuits and divorce proceedings, rather than a prison sentence, but one result of the civil categorization is that those held on immigration charges don't have a right to a public defender, Converse said.\n\nIt's just one part of a system that shrouds immigrants' arrests, detention and trials from public scrutiny.\n\nCalle Delgado's name is not listed on York County Prison's inmate roster, which is published daily online.\n\nSearches in the unified judicial system of Pennsylvania public web portal yielded no information about him.\n\nWhen he is tried, it will be a quiet affair at the prison, Converse said. His family will be allowed to attend, if they know when the trial will happen.\n\nMore:York's immigration court can be black hole of information\n\nA search tool on the Immigration and Customs Enforcement website does show that he is being held at York County Prison. That tool has become a lifeline for the families who struggle to stay in touch with their detained relatives, Converse said.\n\nImmigration and Customs Enforcement regional spokesman Adrian Smith confirmed that Calle Delgado is being detained at the prison \"pending immigration proceedings.\" He's one of about 750 inmates there for immigration violations, according to February 2017 numbers provided by York County spokesman Mark Walters.\n\nImmigrants do have a right to pay bond and get released from prison prior to a hearing, said Converse. However, he's seeing a lot of bonds set around $10,000 to $15,000, all of which would be required prior to an inmate being released before trial. \"The amount of bond set by some of the immigration judges has been pretty extraordinary.\u201d\n\nCalle Delgado was not immediately available for comment. Prisoners have limited access to phones, and Calle Delgado doesn't speak English.\n\nMore:ICE hails nearly 200 immigration arrests in Pa. region\n\nCompton said that, although her nephew is guilty of overstaying his visa, a month in prison is a harsh punishment for someone with no criminal past.\n\nShe said he came to the U.S. in 2013 to work with his brother, a U.S. citizen who had started a carpet installation and removal business in Philadelphia.\n\nHe'd never had any problems with the law until the morning of March 3 around 6 a.m., when he stopped to get food on his way to work, she said. ICE agents were searching for someone else, but when Calle Delgado failed to provide proper identification, they arrested him.\n\nConverse said he's seen a drastic uptick in arrests similar to this one. Under the Obama administration, ICE focused on people with criminal records. Now the agency detains anyone who lacks residency or citizenship documents.\n\nMore: After ICE arrests immigrants, Springettsbury restaurant reopens\n\nThat's led to an overflow in prisons and a backlog in cases, Converse said. He's seen detained immigrants sent across the state or out of state as they wait to be tried. Those who seek deportation can spend weeks or months in prison, while those who fight their charges might spend longer there.\n\nRecently one of Converse's clients was transferred from York to Cambria County Prison. The Western Pa. prison has become a holding station for immigrants from as far away as West Virginia, who eventually come to York for deportation hearings.\n\n\"It really threw a wrench in the works,\" Converse said. \"Fortunately (ICE) agreed to bring him back here, but it just shows how those transfers can make it hard for a client to meet with his lawyer.\"\n\nThe flood of arrests has created a situation where people end up in prison, without legal representation and separated from their families.\n\nAfter a month in prison, Calle Delgado is begging to be deported, Compton said.
