Jerry Brown declared the end of California's\u00a0drought emergency on Friday, stressing that water conservation must be\u00a0a permanent part of life as the state adapts to climate change and prepares for the next drought.</p><p>Brown lifted the state of emergency in most of California after one of the state's wettest winters on record, which brought heavy snowfall to the Sierra Nevada and refilled reservoirs.</p><p>\u201cThis drought emergency is over, but the next drought could be around the corner,\u201d Brown said in a statement. \u201cConservation must remain a way of life.\u201d \u00a0\u00a0</p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><a href=\"/story/news/environment/2017/01/19/california-grapples-pivot-drought-rules-long-term-water-strategy/96805682/\" target=\"_blank\"><strong>RELATED</strong>: As drought eases, what's next for California water?</a></span></p><p>Brown\u2019s executive order lifts\u00a0the drought emergency in all California counties except Tulare, Tuolumne, Fresno and Kings, where state officials have emergency programs to bring drinking water to communities\u00a0where wells have gone dry.</p><div class=\"partner-placement partner-spike\"></div><p>The State Water Resources Control Board will continue requiring\u00a0cities and water agencies to report monthly on water use, and will keep in place prohibitions on water waste established during the drought, including bans on watering immediately after rain, hosing down sidewalks or watering grass on street medians.</p><p>\u201cWhile we\u2019re waiting to have that \u2018drought\u2019s over\u2019 party until after the flood risk season has passed, it\u2019s worth taking a moment just to be grateful for all the rain and snow out there,\" said Felicia Marcus, chair of the State Water Board. \"This drought has been one for the record books, but it won\u2019t be our last or our longest in the years to come.\u00a0It\u2019s a wake-up call that we can\u2019t hit the snooze button on.\u201d</p><div class=\"partner-outstream\"></div><p>The drought lasted five years and was the most severe in California\u2019s modern history. Some researchers concluded the <a href=\"http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/2014GL062433/full\">three-year period from 2012 to 2014</a> was the most severe drought in the last 1,200 years.\u00a0Brown declared the drought an emergency in January 2014 while standing on bare ground at a snow survey site in\u00a0the Sierra Nevada.</p><p>Groundwater levels declined\u00a0to record lows in many parts of the state. Wells went dry in places from Central Valley farming towns to mountain communities, and officials estimated that more than 100 million trees died during the drought due to stress and infestations of bark beetles.\u00a0</p><p>State regulators imposed\u00a0mandatory conservation targets for cities and water agencies in May\u00a02015, and tracked their progress monthly. A year later, state officials amended the drought rules to drop the mandatory water-saving targets, giving water suppliers leeway in calculating their own goals.</p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><a href=\"/featured-newsletter/the_current/\" target=\"_blank\"><strong>THE CURRENT:</strong>\u00a0Sign up for The Desert Sun's energy and water newsletter</a></span></p><p>This winter brought torrents of water that swelled rivers, unleashed floods and turned\u00a0parched hills and valleys bright green.</p><p>As of Friday, snow sensors across the Sierra Nevada measured California\u2019s snowpack at 161 percent of average. \u00a0</p><p>Since June\u00a02015, Californians\u00a0have responded to the\u00a0drought measures by reducing water use in cities and towns by 22.5 percent as compared to 2013, which has been used as a baseline.</p><p>Marcus said Californians have \"hit it out of the park\" with their conservation efforts, and she noted that water savings have continued even during the wet winter.</p><p>\"I don\u2019t think people are going to unlearn what they learned about their lawns,\" Marcus told reporters. \"Let\u2019s see how it goes, but I think folks have shown so far this year that they\u2019ll keep up a reasonable level.\"</p><p>Seeking to pivot to larger water challenges that transcend a single drought, state officials on Friday simultaneously released a plan focusing on long-term water conservation. The plan, titled \u201c<a href=\"http://www.water.ca.gov/wateruseefficiency/conservation/\" target=\"_blank\">Making Water Conservation a California Way of Life</a>,\u201d was prepared in response to an executive order signed by Brown last year.</p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><a href=\"/story/news/environment/2016/12/02/california-use-aerial-images-sharpen-lens-water-conservation/94824244/\" target=\"_blank\"><strong>READ MORE:</strong> California will use aerial images to sharpen lens on water conservation</a></span></p><p>The plan calls for setting new community-specific conservation targets. It also includes a variety of goals such as improving water data and efficiency standards, tracking water use by businesses, encouraging agencies to fix water leaks and requiring agricultural districts to submit more information about water use.</p><p>Water and climate scientists have voiced support for the plan. Environmental groups have also called for a continued focus on\u00a0using water more efficiently.</p><p>Sara Aminzadeh, executive director of the California Coastkeeper Alliance, said she\u2019s happy to see the governor and state agencies follow through on commitments to make continued conservation a priority.\u00a0</p><p>\u201cThe drastic change we saw from deep drought to flooding underscores the importance of drought preparedness as a climate change resiliency strategy,\u201d Aminzadeh said. \u201cMoving forward, it will be important to ensure that conservation and efficiency measures are applied to all sources of water, including recycled water.\u201d</p><p>California\u2019s drought coincided with years of record-breaking warmth, and climate change is projected to lead to more severe droughts. Some researchers concluded the three-year period from 2012 to 2014 was the most severe drought in the last 1,200 years. Brown declared the drought an emergency in January 2014 while standing on bare ground at a snow survey site in the Sierra Nevada.\n\nGroundwater levels declined to record lows in many parts of the state. Wells went dry in places from Central Valley farming towns to mountain communities, and officials estimated that more than 100 million trees died during the drought due to stress and infestations of bark beetles.\n\nState regulators imposed mandatory conservation targets for cities and water agencies in May 2015, and tracked their progress monthly. A year later, state officials amended the drought rules to drop the mandatory water-saving targets, giving water suppliers leeway in calculating their own goals.\n\nTHE CURRENT: Sign up for The Desert Sun's energy and water newsletter\n\nThis winter brought torrents of water that swelled rivers, unleashed floods and turned parched hills and valleys bright green.\n\nAs of Friday, snow sensors across the Sierra Nevada measured California\u2019s snowpack at 161 percent of average.\n\nSince June 2015, Californians have responded to the drought measures by reducing water use in cities and towns by 22.5 percent as compared to 2013, which has been used as a baseline.\n\nMarcus said Californians have \"hit it out of the park\" with their conservation efforts, and she noted that water savings have continued even during the wet winter.\n\n\"I don\u2019t think people are going to unlearn what they learned about their lawns,\" Marcus told reporters. \"Let\u2019s see how it goes, but I think folks have shown so far this year that they\u2019ll keep up a reasonable level.\"\n\nSeeking to pivot to larger water challenges that transcend a single drought, state officials on Friday simultaneously released a plan focusing on long-term water conservation. The plan, titled \u201cMaking Water Conservation a California Way of Life,\u201d was prepared in response to an executive order signed by Brown last year.\n\nREAD MORE: California will use aerial images to sharpen lens on water conservation\n\nThe plan calls for setting new community-specific conservation targets. It also includes a variety of goals such as improving water data and efficiency standards, tracking water use by businesses, encouraging agencies to fix water leaks and requiring agricultural districts to submit more information about water use.\n\nWater and climate scientists have voiced support for the plan. Environmental groups have also called for a continued focus on using water more efficiently.\n\nSara Aminzadeh, executive director of the California Coastkeeper Alliance, said she\u2019s happy to see the governor and state agencies follow through on commitments to make continued conservation a priority.\n\n\u201cThe drastic change we saw from deep drought to flooding underscores the importance of drought preparedness as a climate change resiliency strategy,\u201d Aminzadeh said. \u201cMoving forward, it will be important to ensure that conservation and efficiency measures are applied to all sources of water, including recycled water.\u201d\n\nCalifornia\u2019s drought coincided with years of record-breaking warmth, and climate change is projected to lead to more severe droughts. In the coming decades, global warming is also projected to diminish the snowpack that has long served as a critical reservoir for the state.\n\nBuy Photo Snow blankets the Sierra National Forest in March 2016. (Photo: Jay Calderon/The Desert Sun)\n\nAcross the mountains of the West, measurements since the 1950s show that the average snowpack has been decreasing in most areas as temperatures have risen. Precipitation that used to fall as snow is increasingly falling as rain. And stream gauges have shown earlier spring runoff from snowmelt than in the past.\n\nWater researchers have said California and other western states will need to take a variety of steps to anticipate the emerging changes in the region's hydrology.\n\nMarcus turned to the sports world for an analogy, recalling that hockey legend Wayne Gretzky once said he wasn\u2019t skating to the puck but rather to where the puck was going to be.\n\n\"With the extra challenges that climate change will bring, we\u2019ve got to start skating right away to where we think that puck\u2019s going to be, and this report should help us get the whole team skating in that direction,\" Marcus said. \"The extremes of severe drought followed by floods that we\u2019ve experienced these last few years is what will happen more often as climate change accelerates.\"\n\n\"To become more resilient to future droughts, we simply have to become more efficient in how we use water, because efficiency savings are the cheapest water out there,\u201d Marcus said. She saluted Californians for their efforts during the drought and said, \"Let\u2019s just keep all of that up and transition to the long-term and skate together to where that puck is going to be.\"\n\nREAD MORE: California water regulators expand focus on climate change\n\nThe state's new long-term conservation standards will let urban water agencies decide how to meet a customized water budget based on the regional climate and population.\n\n\u201cThe Inland Empire is typically hot and dry, and with climate change we must prepare longer periods through the year that are even hotter and drier,\" said Susan Lien Longville, president of the San Bernardino Valley Municipal Water District. She said conservation targets that account for different climate zones across California make sense and \"will help cities throughout the state save more water.\"\n\nThe Coachella Valley Water District was among a group of water agencies that had been lobbying for the state to lift the emergency in light of the wet winter.\n\n\u201cOur customers worked hard, many at great cost, to continue to reduce their water use even after the state conservation mandates were eliminated,\u201d CVWD General Manager Jim Barrett said in a statement. \u201cThey should be commended for that effort and for continuing to show a commitment to making conservation a way of life.\u201d\n\nBuy Photo The green landscaping of subdivisions, golf courses and the Annenberg Retreat at Sunnylands border the desert in Rancho Mirage. (Photo: Ian James/The Desert Sun)\n\nCVWD\u2019s customers have reduced water use by an average of 23 percent from 2013 levels since the state began tracking conservation on a monthly basis in June 2015. Customers of the Desert Water Agency have cut back by a cumulative 24 percent.\n\nSome of those reductions are likely to be permanent, as people have ripped out their lawns and replaced them with desert landscaping.\n\n\u201cShifting our focus back to long-term sustainability only makes sense,\u201d said Mark Krause, general manager of the Desert Water Agency. \u201cWe will definitely continue to work with our customers to enhance efficiency.\u201d\n\nAt a meeting on April 18, DWA\u2019s board members plan to consider changing restrictions that limit the days and hours when outdoor watering is permitted.\n\nLaura Feinstein, a senior research associate at the Pacific Institute in Oakland, said state officials are doing the right thing by \"pairing a message that the drought is easing for most of the state with a call to look to the future and to keep all the conservation efforts that were put into place over the last five years active, to not lose sight of the bigger goal, which is to do more with the water that we have.\"\n\nFeinstein said it's important to recognize that the problems are far from over for a number of people, especially the vulnerable communities in the Central Valley.\n\nState officials have been tracking reports of households with dry wells and other water supply \"outages,\" approximately 2,400 families are listed as having unresolved problems, many of them in the Central Valley.\n\nDifficult long-term challenges remain in farming regions where heavy pumping from wells has been drawing down aquifers for decades.\n\nIn many parts of the San Joaquin Valley, groundwater has been severely depleted and overpumping remains chronic \u2013 a central focus of the state\u2019s 2014 Sustainable Groundwater Management Act, which will require local agencies to develop plans for combating overdraft.\n\nIan James writes about water and environmental issues issues for The Desert Sun.
