(Photo: Kelsey Kremer/The Register)Buy Photo\n\nA dancer twirled, pirouette after pirouette, in the corner of Ballet Des Moines\u2019 studio on a recent spring afternoon. Gliding across a small stage, the dancer\u2019s arm flourished with the ornamentation of the music and punctuated each pass of the platform with a staccato embellishment.\n\nBut this particular dancer was distinctly different from the men and women relev\u00e9ing and jet\u00e9-ing nearby.\n\nThis ballerina isn\u2019t human \u2014 it\u2019s a robot.\n\nManibus, as this flapjack-sized dancing robot is called, is the creation of local engineer-turned-artist Amenda Tate Corso, Ballet Des Moines\u2019 newest artist-in-residence. The Manibus project, which captures a dancer\u2019s movements via a motion-sensor app and translates them into a painting, marks Ballet Des Moines' first foray into the emerging national trend of marrying computational technology with dance. Given the enthusiastic response of the company\u2019s eight dancers and the public, it won\u2019t be their last inroad into the digital-dance space, said Ballet Des Moines Executive Director Laurel Knox.\n\nCreated from an amalgamation of a childhood spent at science and math camps and a decades-long career in jewelry and metalsmithing, Corso said she wants her twirling creation to be as educational as it is artistic. To Corso, Manibus\u2019 main goal is to create a space for creative symbiosis, for an exchange of ideas. As Corso learns about the sensor\u2019s capabilities from the dancers, she hopes performers can glean new information on how their bodies move through time and space from the lines left on the page.\n\nEventually, Corso wants to take Manibus to underserved Iowa schools and community centers where children will be able to wear the app and see their movements painted on canvas, giving them access to cultural experiences \u2014 dance, painting, even robotics \u2014 they may not have otherwise. Graduating in a class of 26 from Melcher-Dallas High, Corso, 41, said she knows what missing out on the culture that others take for granted feels like. Advanced art classes weren't an easily accessible option for her until college, and she didn\u2019t see her first ballet until she was about 40.\n\n\u201cI think of how much my life changed after I was able to look at things from that different perspective, that creative perspective,\u201d she said. \u201cWhat if I was able to have that awareness earlier on? How would my life be different?\u201d\n\nCorso\u2019s creation, which is built from pieces of magnetic building kits her kids play with and held together by rubber bands and binder clips, has an accessibility about it, a \u201clow-fi\u201d nature. It doesn\u2019t look made in a laboratory, she said, and that\u2019s exactly what she wants. She hopes those who interact with Manibus will realize that anyone can be both scientific and artistic.\n\nJust like a ballet duet, logic and creativity can keep perfect rhythm when paired together.\n\nBuy Photo Devin Tokarski, a professional dancer for Ballet Des Moines rehearses for the upcoming production of Snow White on Monday, March 27, 2017, at the Ballet Des Moines studio in West Des Moines. While rehearsing Tokarski wears a cell phone transmitting a Bluetooth signal to a movement-controlled robot creating art based on his motion. (Photo: Kelsey Kremer/The Register)\n\n***\n\nDance, by its very nature, is fleeting.\n\n\u201cYou get this swelling, like this emotional high, and then all of a sudden it's over\u2026,\u201d Corso said, recalling seeing her first ballet, Ballet Des Moines\u2019 annual production of \u201cThe Nutcracker.\u201d \u201cSo I wanted a way to take that and make it into something that had a life beyond just that moment. I wanted to translate that moment into something that I could look at and be like, 'I remember the feeling I felt when that happened.' \u201d\n\nThe question of how to make the intangible tangible bounced around Corso\u2019s head for years after she saw that ballet. She\u2019s a problem solver by nature, so this seemingly unanswerable question bothered her.\n\nEven though she\u2019s an artist, she\u2019s always leaned toward the rational and the structural in life. Corso always wanted to be a mechanical engineer, but halfway through college she realized she \u201cdidn\u2019t want to design refrigerators for the rest of her life,\u201d so she switched from mechanical engineering to creative metal work, which was \"something more expressive,\" she said.\n\nLast fall, as she was taking apart her one of her daughter\u2019s latest magnetic creations \u2014 a Harry Potter-themed snake that was able to move via a Bluetooth app \u2014 the answer to the dance quandary she'd had since seeing \"The Nutcracker\" came to her.\n\nIf she could pin the Bluetooth app to a dancer, she could rig it to send a signal based on movement to a paintbrush that could traverse around a page. Later, she would perfect the idea so that the final picture wasn\u2019t a simple mapping of where the dancer had been \u2014 \u201cThe choreographer could probably just draw that on a page,\u201d she said \u2014 but was a representation of the dynamics, tilt, motion and, most importantly, emotions of the dance.\n\n\u201cAs soon as I had that moment where I was like, \u2018Wait, this responds to movement,\u2019 that was, for me, a very direct or literal way to represent ballet in art,\u201d she said.\n\nBuy Photo Manibus, a robot created by Ballet Des Moines' artist-in residence, Amenda Tate Corso, is controlled by the movement of a dancer through the Bluetooth signal on a cell phone. (Photo: Kelsey Kremer/The Register)\n\n***\n\nAs Corso was testing out prototypes, Ballet Des Moines was looking to raise its profile. Knox had recently joined Ballet Des Moines as its new executive director and discovered that only one out of her four artistic friends even knew there was a ballet company in Des Moines.\n\n\u201cWe needed to expand our audience, so we thought we will provide a platform for new or established artists to get in front of our patrons and, in exchange, people that love their work will be introduced to our dancers,\u201d Corso said.\n\nEnter: Manibus.\n\nBuy Photo Ballet Des Moines artist-in-residence, Amenda Tate Corso, readies a page for her movement-controlled robot, Manibus. The robot depicts with paint the motion of dancers while they rehearse on Monday, March 27, 2017, at the Ballet De Moines studio in West Des Moines. (Photo: Kelsey Kremer/The Register)\n\nWhile the dance world interacting with digital and computational technology isn\u2019t new \u2014 conferences were held on the topic in the late 1990s \u2014 it is still an idea that lives on the fringes. Many traditional dance companies, like Ballet Des Moines, aren\u2019t dedicating time or resources to working on ventures combining tap and tech.\n\nBut slowly, more groups are coming to the understanding that technology can be used to enhance a performance as well as bring new audiences to one of the world\u2019s oldest art forms, said Dan Fine, a newly hired assistant professor at the University of Iowa who studies the intersection of digital media and performance.\n\nKnox agreed: \u201cTechnology is how young people communicate. If we want to get young people to the ballet, we had to start thinking about pairing the digital with the dance.\u201d\n\nBuy Photo Paintings created by Ballet Des Moines artist-in-residence, Amenda Tate Corso and her motion-controlled robot, Manibus, hang on the wall above Tate Corso's studio space at Ballet Des Moines on Monday, March 27, 2017. (Photo: Kelsey Kremer/The Register)\n\n***\n\nMost days, Corso can be found on the fringes of Ballet Des Moines\u2019 studio. As the company prepares for its performance of \u201cSnow White,\" Corso flits from dancer to dancer, removing the phone that contains the sensor from one character and strapping it to another, before returning to hover over Manibus.\n\nThink of it like a Fitbit, she said.\n\n\"But instead of giving us statistical data,\" she added, \"it is giving us emotional data.\"\n\nBuy Photo Amenda Tate Corso, Ballet Des Moines artist-in-residence shows a completed piece of art created based on the movement of dancers at Ballet Des Moines, on Monday, March 27, 2017, in West Des Moines. (Photo: Kelsey Kremer/The Register)\n\nCorso stirred with excitement as the Prince and Snow White began to dance a duet. It was reflective of two different types of dancers.\u201d\n\nFocusing back on Manibus, she watches the gadget, gently turning it when it hits the canvas\u2019 edge or lifting it up and down when the dancers exit and enter the stage.
