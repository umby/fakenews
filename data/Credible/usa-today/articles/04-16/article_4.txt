Religious leaders said Easter\u2019s message of hope can help individuals push through dark times in their lives as well as political turmoil.\n\n\u201cThe whole story of Holy Week reveals the worst of what humans can do to one another,\u201d said Bishop Mark Beckwith, head of the Episcopal Diocese of Newark. \u201cIt\u2019s what people do when they get scared. But the power of love is stronger than death.\u201d\n\nBishop Mark Beckwith, head of the Episcopal Diocese of Newark. (Photo: Episcopal Diocese of newark)\n\nThe Rev. Mark Suriano, pastor of First Congregational Church of Park Ridge, put it this way: \u201cIf life can come from an empty tomb and seed can sprout from rocky soil, then even in this politically polarized world we can hold out the belief that we\u2019re not destined to be this way forever. It requires us to reach into our best selves rather than our most selfish selves.\u201d\n\nThe Rev. Marilyn Monroe Harris, pastor of the First Baptist Church of Teaneck, agreed. \u201cThrough Christ we can overcome all obstacles,\u201d she said. \u201cHe overcame the political tension of that period, and the hatred and the pain of the cross. Jesus said that wherever two or three are gathered in his name, he will be there \u2013 he didn\u2019t say, \u2018Only if it\u2019s in a really nice building.\u2019\u201d\n\nJesus embraced everyone \u2013 especially outcasts\n\nJesus mingled with the wealthy and poor, with tax collectors and prostitutes, and tried to reach beyond the Jewish community to gentiles.\n\n\u201cJesus insisted on love for everyone,\u201d Beckwith said. \u201cWe see today behavior that denies the full humanity of other people by denying access to our country, by blaming, by insulting. James Martin, a Jesuit and author of \"Jesus: A Pilgrimage,\" noted that Jesus was crucified between revolutionaries. \u201cHe dies with outcasts and his whole ministry is to people on the margin \u2013 and who are those people today? Refugees, the homeless, those in the LBGT community, prisoners.\u201d\n\nAs the U.S. today is polarized, the society Jesus spoke to was also divided \u2013 not only between Roman occupiers and the Jewish people, but also among conservative and liberal Jews said The Rev. Thomas Keinath, lead pastor of Calvary Temple International Assembly of God in Wayne.\n\n\u201cJesus spoke to every element of that society,\u201d Keinath said.\n\n\u201cThere will always be something to divide us,\u201d he added. \u201cBut there is far more we have in common. Jesus talked about loving our enemies and welcoming the stranger \u2013 things that people with power and wealth find threatening. Today it\u2019s the same, and we\u2019re afraid.\"\n\nThe story of the resurrection should provide perspective on how to make decisions in this world, Suriano said. \"To think about the impact of policy choices on folks with the least control over the outcome -- the poor, marginalized, those without control over the political system.\u201d\n\nToday, fear about deportations of undocumented immigrants is real in the Latino community served by the of Passaic County Christian Center in Paterson, said The Rev. The government, the president is not our enemy \u2013 it\u2019s important for us to work together. Ultimately though, faced with an angry crowd, Pilate handed Jesus over to be crucified.\n\nPilate caved to political expediency -- something Harris said happens too often today. \u201cEven though as leader he sensed the right thing to do, he went along with the popular sentiment,\u201d she said. \u201cPeople act with self-preservation rather than doing the right thing. What we see in Pilate\u2019s behavior we also see today.\u201d\n\nEric Barreto, a Baptist minister at Princeton Theological Seminary, said that \u201cPilate washes his hands, abdicating his responsibility, failing his basic duty to carry out justice.\u201d\n\nHong applied that to today\u2019s climate. \u201cOne theme in politics today is caving in to fear, caving in to the base instincts of people and this is not a good thing,\u201d he said. \u201cWhether it\u2019s populist nationalism or xenophobia, when leaders fail to exercise moral leadership and instead make appeals to populist leanings and don\u2019t follow their own moral findings, that\u2019s dangerous.\u201d\n\nJesus preached non-violence\n\nWhen Judas leads a group armed with swords to arrest Jesus, the apostle Peter draws his sword to defend Jesus, but he tells Peter that \u201call who draw the sword will die by the sword.\u201d\n\nTo Hong, it is a metaphor for our times. \"The story of Easter becomes love over force,\u201d he said. \"Why do we go toward the projection of power more easily than the projection of love? Why do we send bombs before schools?\n\n\u201cWe understand power, and love is riskier because it makes you vulnerable,\u201d he said. \u201cEaster is about the improbable victory of love.\u201d\n\nJesus echoed an Old Testament tradition of non-violent teaching, including Isaiah\u2019s plea to turn swords into plowshares. Barreto hopes that message can resonate today: \u201cDon\u2019t look to the most powerful - look for the actions of ordinary people doing extraordinary things.
