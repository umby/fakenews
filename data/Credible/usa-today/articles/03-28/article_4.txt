It's\u00a0not to be confused with the smaller (also brick) American Revolution Museum at Yorktown near the iconic battlefield, which kicked\u00a0off a 13-day grand opening\u00a0celebration Thursday, March 23 with patriotic salutes to individual colonies.\u00a0Delaware was first in line.</p><div id=\"module-position-P1YoZUL5Lwo\" class=\"story-asset image-asset\"><aside itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" class=\"wide single-photo\"><img itemprop=\"url\" src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/646e59ca5499a110c57ac0f87a58b3c0dcc132d0/c=46-0-755-533&amp;r=x408&amp;c=540x405/local/-/media/2017/03/16/Wilmington/Wilmington/636252761629288626-revolution-exterior.jpg\" alt=\"Exterior of the Museum of the American Revolution in\" width=\"540\" height=\"405\" data-mycapture-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/media/2017/03/16/Wilmington/Wilmington/636252761629288626-revolution-exterior.jpg\"\n                    data-mycapture-sm-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/4044b1e7645488621f157de466cf1a990870caa2/r=500x333/local/-/media/2017/03/16/Wilmington/Wilmington/636252761629288626-revolution-exterior.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"name\" content=\"Exterior of Philly museum\" /><meta itemprop=\"height\" content=\"405\" /><meta itemprop=\"width\" content=\"540\" /><p>Exterior of the Museum of the American Revolution in Old City, Philadelphia<meta itemprop=\"copyrightHolder\" content=\"Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution\" />&nbsp;<span class=\"credit\">(Photo: Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution)</span></p></aside><div class=\"clearfix\"></div></div><p>Leaders of both museums insist that the timing of their unveilings is completely coincidental.\u00a0Both facilities were in development for more than a decade, both marked by fits and starts.</p><p>The American Revolution Museum at Yorktown, formerly the Yorktown Victory Center, had a soft opening in October before all exhibits\u00a0were installed. The Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation, a state agency, operates the new $51\u00a0million museum, along with the Jamestown Settlement nearby.</p><p>Philadelphia's\u00a0$120 million museum, originally slated to occupy land within\u00a0Valley Forge National Park, is run by a nonprofit supported by private and state funding. The project's\u00a0biggest donor is local media entrepreneur and philanthropist H.F. \"Gerry\" Lenfest.</p><p>\"I don't see them as competition at all,\" Homer Lanker, the Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation's interpretive programs manager, said of the museum to the north. \"I see both of us telling an amazing story.\"</p><p>Surprisingly, top officials at both institutions said they haven't had time to check out what the other has\u00a0to offer. Nor did they have time to consult with each other's\u00a0curatorial teams to avoid duplication.</p><p>There are some striking similarities in retelling a uniquely American story: Both museums feature\u00a0lanterns hanging from a\u00a0\"Liberty Tree\" (Yorktown's is wired for instant messages). Both include images\u00a0of members of the Revolutionary War generation who survived into the age of photography. Both rely on diverse, personal narratives to keep modern viewers engaged, and chronicle simmering\u00a0rifts between the American colonists and the British Crown through\u00a0the afterglow of independence and the messy work\u00a0of a burgeoning democracy. Neither asks the viewer to\u00a0memorize\u00a0dates, battles or a teacup's provenance.</p><p>Along the way, both institutions\u00a0ponder existential questions like \"Are liberty and freedom synonymous?\" without wading into the current political climate.</p><div id=\"module-position-P1YoZULrYmA\" class=\"story-asset image-asset\"><aside itemprop=\"associatedMedia\" itemscope itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" class=\"wide single-photo\"><img itemprop=\"url\" src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/646e59ca5499a110c57ac0f87a58b3c0dcc132d0/c=46-0-755-533&amp;r=x408&amp;c=540x405/local/-/media/2017/03/16/Wilmington/Wilmington/636252725102590482-liberty-tree-philly.jpg\" alt=\"The Liberty Tree features real wood from a nearly 600-year-old\" width=\"540\" height=\"405\" data-mycapture-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/media/2017/03/16/Wilmington/Wilmington/636252725102590482-liberty-tree-philly.jpg\"\n                    data-mycapture-sm-src=\"https://www.gannett-cdn.com/-mm-/4044b1e7645488621f157de466cf1a990870caa2/r=500x333/local/-/media/2017/03/16/Wilmington/Wilmington/636252725102590482-liberty-tree-philly.jpg\" /><meta itemprop=\"name\" content=\"Liberty Tree\" /><meta itemprop=\"height\" content=\"405\" /><meta itemprop=\"width\" content=\"540\" /><p>The Liberty Tree features real wood from a nearly 600-year-old tulip poplar that was damaged by Hurricane Floyd in Annapolis, Maryland.<meta itemprop=\"copyrightHolder\" content=\"Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia\" />&nbsp;<span class=\"credit\">(Photo: Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia)</span></p></aside><div class=\"clearfix\"></div></div><p>This isn't the first time that\u00a0Pennsylvania and Virginia have traded\u00a0friendly fire over preserving wartime artifacts. National Civil War museums have sprouted\u00a0in both Harrisburg and Richmond.</p><p>Taking a comprehensive approach to the Revolutionary period, the new museums complement an array of historic sites preserved\u00a0by the Daughters of the American Revolution, National Park Service and regional organizations promoting their piece of the story, according to Cinnamon\u00a0Catlin-Legutko, who serves on the board of\u00a0the American Alliance of Museums.</p><p>Helped by the \"Hamilton\" craze on Broadway, the \"Revolutionary War is as current today as it was then,\" added\u00a0Catlin-Legutko, president and CEO of the Abbe Museum in Bar Harbor, Maine.\u00a0\u00a0\"Philadelphia is part of the story. Yorktown is part of the story.\"</p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><strong>STORY:</strong><a href=\"/story/news/2017/03/22/dupont-gives-hotel-art-3-area-museums-united-way/99486618/\">Hotel DuPont donates art</a></span></p><p><span class=\"exclude-from-newsgate\"><strong>STORY:</strong><a href=\"/story/life/food/2017/03/21/british-show-leads-random-act-cheesecake-baking/99413208/\">British baking show inspires</a></span></p><p>Visitors to both museums will leave\u00a0with an\u00a0understanding that the revolution wasn't a one-off rebellion, waged\u00a0by an appendage of a remote political entity on the other side of the Atlantic. Rather, the struggle continues\u00a0today (cue former presidential hopeful\u00a0Bernie Sanders), because liberty remains\u00a0fragile and an informed citizenry is, by no means,\u00a0guaranteed.</p><p>\"Tyranny, like hell, is not easily conquered,\" English-American writer and revolutionary Thomas Paine wrote at the close of\u00a01776.\u00a0\"Yet we have this consolation with us...\"</p><p>\"The harder the conflict, the more glorious the triumph.\"</p><h2 class=\"presto-h2\"><span class=\"print_subhead\">Yorktown: Where American independence was won</span></h2><p>What sets the Yorktown museum apart is its\u00a0backdrop, the Siege of Yorktown, often seen\u00a0as the culminating battle of the war. It was here that Washington's army, with a helping hand from the\u00a0French,\u00a0trapped Cornwallis' troops camped against the York River. An immersive film inside the museum deposits visitors\u00a0on the Chesapeake for a windy, explosive Battle of the Capes, which resulted\u00a0in a significant French blockade that choked Cornwallis from reinforcements.</p><p>Next door to the museum is an expanded\u00a0\"living history\" farm and recreated Continental Army encampment, using an authentic Von Steuben layout.</p><p>Interested in enlisting? You'll have to be a man between the ages of 16 and 60, with no obvious signs of scurvy or smallpox, and as tall as a halberd (a combined spear-and-battle-ax).</p><p>Join for three years and receive a bounty of $20; stay for the\u00a0entire\u00a0war and reap\u00a0100 acres of land. In exchange, you'll have to endure brutal winters vomiting and sweating the sickness away, joined by\u00a0five other guys in a\u00a0teepee.</p><p>Children will delight in using sandbags to shore up the camp's defenses, taking home musket cartridges as souvenirs. Artillery demonstrations and military drills promise continuous booms.</p><p>Nearby is a middle-class farmhouse, patterned after the home\u00a0belonging to\u00a0the real Edward Moss family of York County. In the kitchen, a historical re-enactor bakes\u00a0chess pie in a Dutch oven\u00a0(like pecan pie, minus the pecans). In 2007, the Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation decided to replace the outdated museum and beef up attractions on its 22-acre site.</p><p>The decision had nothing to do with the development of another revolution museum more than 300 miles away, according to Yorktown museum leaders. History buffs will travel for their fix, while casual visitors can incorporate either museum into their regional travel plans, said Susan Bak, the foundation's senior director of marketing and retail operations.</p><p>With 22,000 square feet of exhibition galleries, the new Yorktown museum is a testament to the ingenuity of Washington's scrappy army against mounting hardships. It also documents\u00a0how to live through a war, through the eyes of 20 historical characters.</p><p>They include Sarah Osborn Benjamin, a former servant who married a Continental soldier and traveled with him to the battlefield to distribute food and coffee while skirting cannonballs; Peter Harris of the Catawba Nation, who joined the 3rd South Carolina Regiment; Billy Flora, a free African American solidier who displayed courage at the Battle of Great Bridge in Virginia;\u00a0and\u00a0Trip, a Wheaten Terrier and a patriot.</p><p>\"We're not telling the story of war,\" the foundation's senior curator, Thomas Davidson, said.\u00a0\"We're telling the story of the creation of the nation.\"</p><p>Of the estimated 500 artifacts on display, about 100 are from the original museum.They include decorative objects, cookware, compasses, toys, tools and\u00a0furniture. Examples include\u00a0a Monty Python-esque film on the Bill of Rights, hologram-like soldiers from the Battle of Cowpens in South Carolina and\u00a0mobile app tours from the perspectives of different characters. One fun game allows participants to pick a side, strategy and battle and watch the fighting unfold on a monitor. Shortly after,\u00a0the historically correct sequence of events appears.</p><p>Museum leaders hope visitors will take advantage of the nearby Jamestown Settlement, which is also benefiting from\u00a0a $10\u00a0million upgrade. Pocahontas, whose image has been manipulated over history, married Englishman and tobacco planter John Rolfe and helped smooth tensions between the two cultures.</p><h2 class=\"presto-h2\"><span class=\"print_subhead\">Philadelphia: Headquarters of the Revolution</span></h2><p>The centerpiece of the\u00a0Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia is a fragile, 10-foot-tall elliptical tent that was missing its\u00a0left side before conservators intervened. Surveys show that younger museum visitors want to unplug, he adds.</p><p>Among the museum's highlights: A terracotta bust of Washington by Revolutionary War veteran William Rush, who founded the "source": "usa-today", "text": "CLOSE Skip in Skip x Embed x Share Two museums, opening less than a month apart, both lay claim to the story of the birth of our nation. Daniel Sato/The News Journal\n\nA statue of George Washington on display at the American Revolution Museum at Yorktown. (Photo: Courtesy of the American Revolution Museum at Yorktown)\n\nOpening less than a month apart, the debut of two nationally recognized, multi-million-dollar American Revolution museums begs the question: Who owns the story of the birth of our nation?\n\nDid America spring from a Yorktown, Virginia, battlefield, ravaged by pickaxes, cannonballs and horse carcasses before British Gen. Cornwallis surrendered his troops on Oct.19, 1781? Or was our republic sealed in the first capital, Philadelphia, where the Declaration of Independence and Constitution were debated and adopted?\n\nThe answer is both.\n\n\"It's like Jewelers' Row. We're all in the business of getting people hooked on diamonds,\" explains R. Scott Stephenson, vice president of collections, exhibitions and programming for the Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia.\n\nOpening April 19 at Third and Chestnut Streets, the three-story brick building is located steps from Independence Hall, the Liberty Bell and the National Constitution Center. It's not to be confused with the smaller (also brick) American Revolution Museum at Yorktown near the iconic battlefield, which kicked off a 13-day grand opening celebration Thursday, March 23 with patriotic salutes to individual colonies. Delaware was first in line.\n\nExterior of the Museum of the American Revolution in Old City, Philadelphia (Photo: Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution)\n\nLeaders of both museums insist that the timing of their unveilings is completely coincidental. Both facilities were in development for more than a decade, both marked by fits and starts.\n\nThe American Revolution Museum at Yorktown, formerly the Yorktown Victory Center, had a soft opening in October before all exhibits were installed. The Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation, a state agency, operates the new $51 million museum, along with the Jamestown Settlement nearby.\n\nPhiladelphia's $120 million museum, originally slated to occupy land within Valley Forge National Park, is run by a nonprofit supported by private and state funding. The project's biggest donor is local media entrepreneur and philanthropist H.F. \"Gerry\" Lenfest.\n\n\"I don't see them as competition at all,\" Homer Lanker, the Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation's interpretive programs manager, said of the museum to the north. \"I see both of us telling an amazing story.\"\n\nSurprisingly, top officials at both institutions said they haven't had time to check out what the other has to offer. Nor did they have time to consult with each other's curatorial teams to avoid duplication.\n\nThere are some striking similarities in retelling a uniquely American story: Both museums feature lanterns hanging from a \"Liberty Tree\" (Yorktown's is wired for instant messages). Both include images of members of the Revolutionary War generation who survived into the age of photography. Both rely on diverse, personal narratives to keep modern viewers engaged, and chronicle simmering rifts between the American colonists and the British Crown through the afterglow of independence and the messy work of a burgeoning democracy. Neither asks the viewer to memorize dates, battles or a teacup's provenance.\n\nAlong the way, both institutions ponder existential questions like \"Are liberty and freedom synonymous?\" without wading into the current political climate.\n\nThe Liberty Tree features real wood from a nearly 600-year-old tulip poplar that was damaged by Hurricane Floyd in Annapolis, Maryland. (Photo: Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia)\n\nThis isn't the first time that Pennsylvania and Virginia have traded friendly fire over preserving wartime artifacts. National Civil War museums have sprouted in both Harrisburg and Richmond.\n\nTaking a comprehensive approach to the Revolutionary period, the new museums complement an array of historic sites preserved by the Daughters of the American Revolution, National Park Service and regional organizations promoting their piece of the story, according to Cinnamon Catlin-Legutko, who serves on the board of the American Alliance of Museums.\n\nHelped by the \"Hamilton\" craze on Broadway, the \"Revolutionary War is as current today as it was then,\" added Catlin-Legutko, president and CEO of the Abbe Museum in Bar Harbor, Maine. \"Philadelphia is part of the story. Yorktown is part of the story.\"\n\nSTORY:Hotel DuPont donates art\n\nSTORY:British baking show inspires\n\nVisitors to both museums will leave with an understanding that the revolution wasn't a one-off rebellion, waged by an appendage of a remote political entity on the other side of the Atlantic. Rather, the struggle continues today (cue former presidential hopeful Bernie Sanders), because liberty remains fragile and an informed citizenry is, by no means, guaranteed.\n\n\"Tyranny, like hell, is not easily conquered,\" English-American writer and revolutionary Thomas Paine wrote at the close of 1776. \"Yet we have this consolation with us...\"\n\n\"The harder the conflict, the more glorious the triumph.\"\n\nYorktown: Where American independence was won\n\nWhat sets the Yorktown museum apart is its backdrop, the Siege of Yorktown, often seen as the culminating battle of the war. It was here that Washington's army, with a helping hand from the French, trapped Cornwallis' troops camped against the York River. An immersive film inside the museum deposits visitors on the Chesapeake for a windy, explosive Battle of the Capes, which resulted in a significant French blockade that choked Cornwallis from reinforcements.\n\nNext door to the museum is an expanded \"living history\" farm and recreated Continental Army encampment, using an authentic Von Steuben layout.\n\nInterested in enlisting? You'll have to be a man between the ages of 16 and 60, with no obvious signs of scurvy or smallpox, and as tall as a halberd (a combined spear-and-battle-ax).\n\nJoin for three years and receive a bounty of $20; stay for the entire war and reap 100 acres of land. In exchange, you'll have to endure brutal winters vomiting and sweating the sickness away, joined by five other guys in a teepee.\n\nChildren will delight in using sandbags to shore up the camp's defenses, taking home musket cartridges as souvenirs. Artillery demonstrations and military drills promise continuous booms.\n\nNearby is a middle-class farmhouse, patterned after the home belonging to the real Edward Moss family of York County. In the kitchen, a historical re-enactor bakes chess pie in a Dutch oven (like pecan pie, minus the pecans). Don't touch because they didn't have food inspectors back then.\n\nA Revolution-era farm offers visitors a chance to witness a typical rural lifestyle during the Revolutionary War. (Photo: Courtesy of the American Revolution Museum at Yorktown)\n\nThe outdoor area has tripled in size since the Yorktown Victory Center opened in 1976 as part of Virginia's bicentennial celebration. In 2007, the Jamestown-Yorktown Foundation decided to replace the outdated museum and beef up attractions on its 22-acre site.\n\nThe decision had nothing to do with the development of another revolution museum more than 300 miles away, according to Yorktown museum leaders. History buffs will travel for their fix, while casual visitors can incorporate either museum into their regional travel plans, said Susan Bak, the foundation's senior director of marketing and retail operations.\n\nWith 22,000 square feet of exhibition galleries, the new Yorktown museum is a testament to the ingenuity of Washington's scrappy army against mounting hardships. It also documents how to live through a war, through the eyes of 20 historical characters.\n\nThey include Sarah Osborn Benjamin, a former servant who married a Continental soldier and traveled with him to the battlefield to distribute food and coffee while skirting cannonballs; Peter Harris of the Catawba Nation, who joined the 3rd South Carolina Regiment; Billy Flora, a free African American solidier who displayed courage at the Battle of Great Bridge in Virginia; and Trip, a Wheaten Terrier and a patriot.\n\n\"We're not telling the story of war,\" the foundation's senior curator, Thomas Davidson, said. \"We're telling the story of the creation of the nation.\"\n\nOf the estimated 500 artifacts on display, about 100 are from the original museum.They include decorative objects, cookware, compasses, toys, tools and furniture. Life-cast figures depict gory battle scenes. In assembling the hand-sewn costume for an English captain, museum conservators took his red coat to a firing range to mark authentic bullet holes.\n\nBattle of Great Bridge diorama at American Revolution Museum at Yorktown. (Photo: Courtesy of the American Revolution Museum at Yorktown)\n\nAmong the museum's prized artifacts are a rare oil portrait from around 1733 of Ayuba Suleiman Diallo, a freeborn educated African who was kidnapped and sold as a slave. It is believed to be one of the earliest known portraits of an African enslaved in the colonies.\n\nA silver-dollar-size treaty badge from 1710 is one of only four in existence. The British gave such tokens to Native Americans to encourage trade in furs, pottery and other goods. Also on display is a rare first model \u201cBrown Bess\u201d British infantry musket from 1741, along with an early 19th-century sandstone marker from a Pennsylvania ferry house, carved with an eagle, stars and the word \u201cLiberty.\" A July 1776 broadside of the Declaration of Independence is tucked inside a shrine-like enclosure surrounded by weaponry.\n\nPortrait of Ayuba Suleiman Diallo, circa 1733. Diallo was a freeborn, educated African who was kidnapped and sold as a slave in Maryland. (Photo: Courtesy of the American Revolution Museum at Yorktown)\n\nThe museum devoted 10 percent of its budget to interactive technology and it shows. Examples include a Monty Python-esque film on the Bill of Rights, hologram-like soldiers from the Battle of Cowpens in South Carolina and mobile app tours from the perspectives of different characters. One fun game allows participants to pick a side, strategy and battle and watch the fighting unfold on a monitor. Shortly after, the historically correct sequence of events appears.\n\nMuseum leaders hope visitors will take advantage of the nearby Jamestown Settlement, which is also benefiting from a $10 million upgrade. Paid visitors to both attractions totaled more than 560,000 last year.\n\nInteractive exhibits at Jamestown explore the Powhatan Indian, English and West Central African cultures that converged in America's first permanent English colony, founded in 1607. Visitors can walk aboard three recreated ships that brought the English colonists to Virginia.\n\nIn July, the settlement will open a \"Pocahontas Reimagined\" exhibition, in tribute to the favorite daughter of supreme Indian Chief Powatan. Pocahontas, whose image has been manipulated over history, married Englishman and tobacco planter John Rolfe and helped smooth tensions between the two cultures.\n\nPhiladelphia: Headquarters of the Revolution\n\nThe centerpiece of the Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia is a fragile, 10-foot-tall elliptical tent that was missing its left side before conservators intervened. Dubbed the \"first Oval Office,\" the linen working and sleeping quarters for Washington and his enslaved assistant, Billy Lee, has been preserved behind shatter-resistant glass in its own auditorium at the museum.\n\nThe 23-foot-long, 14-foot-wide exterior tent was made in Reading, Pennsylvania, while Washington was encamped in Valley Forge in 1778. It traveled to the Battle of Yorktown; key decisions were reached within its folds. The museum also holds William Trego's iconic painting, \"The March to Valley Forge,\" along with a faded silk banner with 13 white, six-pointed stars that traveled with Washington.\n\nWashington's field tent, the \"first Oval Office\" on display at the Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia (Photo: Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution)\n\nCompared to Yorktown, the Philadelphia museum boasts a more expansive 3,000-piece collection, the core of which was assembled by the now-defunct Valley Forge Historical Society. Museum planners originally proposed building on 78 acres inside Valley Forge Park, before being inundated with traffic, noise and pollution concerns.\n\nIn 2009, they brokered a deal with the National Park Service to swap the Valley Forge land for the Chestnut Street site, the former home of the Independence National Historical Park Visitor Center.\n\nStored for a time in a secret location in the suburbs, collection pieces will rotate to lessen damage from the elements. Galleries are arranged chronologically from the rise of armed resistance to British taxation in the 1760s through the creation of the American republic.\n\nThere is a brief overview of the ratification of the Constitution so as not to encroach on messaging at the National Constitution Center.\n\nTechnology plays a less prominent role at the Philadelphia museum compared to Yorktown. In an immersive theater, visitors are fired on by the British light infantry while trying to defend a wall at the Battle of Brandywine. More common are basic flipboards with text throughout the galleries and films that reference the illuminated artifacts below.\n\n\"Dependence on technology will make you outmoded,\" Philadelphia museum Curator Philip Mead explained. Surveys show that younger museum visitors want to unplug, he adds.\n\nAmong the museum's highlights: A terracotta bust of Washington by Revolutionary War veteran William Rush, who founded the Pennsylvania Academy of the Fine Arts; a signed 1773 volume by Phillis Wheatley, American's first published black female poet; and a copy of the first newspaper printing of the Declaration of Independence in the Pennsylvania Evening Post.\n\nVisitors can also hop aboard a large-scale replica of an 18th-century privateer ship, and marvel at a piece of the gilded lead statue of King George III, which was toppled in Bowling Green, New York, and melted down into musketballs.\n\nThe message of ordinary people living through extraordinary times is reinforced throughout the museum, from a pair of baby booties made from the pilfered coat of a British footsoldier, to hand-forged shackles small enough to fit a child.\n\nNearby, life-cast figures representing members of the Oneida Indian Nation, a major museum donor, debate which side to join after the pivotal Battle of Princeton in 1777.\n\n\"They are in our story not just for modern ideas of diversity,\" said Mead, \"but they are truly significant actors in the American revolution.\"\n\nOn display at the Museum of the American Revolution in Philadelphia is a copy of the June 6, 1776 issue of the Pennsylvania Evening Post with the first newspaper printing of the Declaration of Independence. (Photo: Courtesy of the Museum of the American Revolution)\n\nMuseum leaders expect more than 500,000 annual visitors following the April 19 kickoff event, which will feature Former Vice President Joe Biden, Pulitzer Prize-winning author David McCullough, and journalist-political commentator Cokie Roberts. The celebration coincides with the anniversary of the \"shot heard round the world\" at the Battle of Lexington and Concord in 1775, which catapulted the colonies on their eight-year revolutoonary road.\n\nAs of mid-March, the museum had presold 25,000 tickets; weddings and special events are booked through 2020, a spokeswoman said.\n\nThere were no outright heroes or villains during the Revolutionary War, despite what appears in books and on film, according to Mead.\n\nFor instance, Charles Wilson Peale, an esteemed painter and war hero, also headed a Philadelphia committee to confiscate the land of Loyalists.\n\nThe colonists' pursuit of freedom while condoning slavery is a paradox, effortlessly articulated in one of the museum's glass cases. It contains a reproduction of a silver slave branding tool, flanked on either side by hat badges proclaiming \"liberty.\"\n\nIn the end, the trajectory of the American Revolution resembled that of a symphony, said Mead.\n\n\"You're uplifted, you crash, and you rise up again.\"\n\nContact Margie Fishman at (302) 324-2882, on Twitter @MargieTrende or mfishman@delawareonline.com.\n\nA tale of two museums:\n\nAmerican Revolution Museum at Yorktown\n\nSize: 22,000-square-feet of gallery space in an 80,000-square-foot building\n\nCost: $51 million\n\nLocation: 200 Water St., Yorktown, Virginia\n\nTickets: Adults, $12; Children ages 6-12, $7; free for children under age 6.
