Hooks would find more surreal today.</p><p>The 1980 photo of him shaking hands in the Oval Office with President Reagan, a man whose policies and politics he roundly condemned back then.</p><p>Or the 1987 photo of him standing beside future president Donald Trump, a man whose policies and politics he would have roundly condemned today.</p><p>Both are part of the new <a href=\"http://www.memphis.edu/hookspapers/\">Benjamin Lawson Hooks Papers</a>, a massive collection that has been digitized and made available to the public.</p><div class=\"partner-placement partner-spike\"></div><p>Both photographs were taken when Hooks, born and raised in Memphis, was national executive director of the NAACP.</p><p>Both are eery reminders of efforts -- then and now -- to shift the movement from civil rights to economic justice.</p><p>\"Today, we're free but not equal,\" said Rev. Jesse Jackson, a friend and colleague of Hooks.</p><p>Jackson was in Memphis Monday to commemorate the 49th anniversary of the death of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. \u00a0The struggle for economic justice that began with King in Memphis continued with Hooks in the 1980s.</p><div class=\"partner-outstream\"></div><p>Hooks, who died in 2010, led the NAACP's battles against Reagan's economic Hooks grew up in a middle-class family in South Memphis and worked in his father's Hooks Brothers photography studio. After graduation from Booker T.                     Benjamin Hooks in his 1941 high school graduation photograph. Hooks grew up in a middle-class family in South Memphis and worked in his father's Hooks Brothers photography studio. After graduation from Booker T. Washington High School in 1941, he attended LeMoyne College and Howard University in Washington. Drafted in 1943, he served during World War II with the Army in Italy. After the war, he went to law school at DePaul University in Chicago on the GI bill. He returned to Memphis in 1948 and began practicing law, later serving as an assistant public defender and criminal court judge. In 1972, President Nixon appointed him to the FCC. He moved to the NAACP as its national executive director in 1977, replacing Roy Wilkins, who had led the association since 1955. Love, collection specialist.</p><p>The largest and perhaps the most significant.</p><p>Most of the collection focuses on Hooks' tenure as NAACP leader, from 1977-92.</p><p>The photo of Hooks shaking Reagan's hand was taken at the White House in late 1980.</p><p>The photo of Hooks standing between Trump and his first wife, Ivana, was taken at Trump Tower in 1987.</p><p>Trump was chairman of the host committee for the NAACP's 1987 convention in New York City.</p><p>Jackson spoke to the convention that year as a Democratic candidate for president.</p><p>Jackson campaigned against Reaganomics in 1988, and won over enough white, blue-collar voters to win seven primaries and four caucuses.</p><p>\"Trump's budget would cut taxes on the rich and cut social programs, education, housing for the poor,\" Jackson said.</p><p>\"Trump has a $15 billion Cabinet, and its first mission is to turn down health care for 24 million Americans? That's vulgar.\"</p><p>If he were alive today, Hooks likely would use the same language.</p><p>The Hooks Papers includes Hooks' copy of Reagan's speech to the 1981 NAACP convention in Denver.</p><p>During the speech, Hooks wrote margin notes in blue ink.</p><p>Reagan said: \"I genuinely and deeply believe the economic package we have put forth will move us toward black economic freedom.\"</p><p>Hooks wrote: <strong>JOKE</strong></p><p>Reagan said: \"Americans have been very generous with good intentions and billions of dollars ... Yet in spite of the money and the hopes, the government has never lived up to the dreams of poor people.\"</p><p>Hooks wrote: <strong>WHY</strong></p><p>Reagan said: \"If productivity had not stopped growing and started downhill after 1965, the GNP today would be $850 billion bigger...And this all would have happened with the compliments of the private sector.\"</p><p>Hooks wrote: <strong>PRODUCTIVITY STOP GROWING BECAUSE OF <u>GREED</u></strong></p><p>\"Dr. Hooks's political and social criticism was informed by his religious training,\" said Daphene R. McFerren, executive director of the Hooks Institute.</p><p>In his 1981 speech to the NAACP, Reagan said he wanted to end the \"bondage\" of government programs by helping business and industry bring about an \"economic emancipation\" for African-Americans.</p><p>After the speech, Hooks said Reagan's economic policies \"are setting into motion forces that are bound to wreak additional hardship, havoc, despair, pain and suffering on that huge body of the poor and the working poor of which blacks and other minorities are a disproportionate share.\"</p><p>Hooks, a pastor by vocation, a lawyer and judge by training, knew how to discern the difference between what is preached and what is practiced.</p><p>Thanks to the Hooks Papers, it's a lesson he's still preaching and teaching.</p><div (Photo: Hooks Institute/University of Memphis Libraries)\n\nIt's difficult to say which photograph the late Dr. Benjamin L. Hooks would find more surreal today.\n\nThe 1980 photo of him shaking hands in the Oval Office with President Reagan, a man whose policies and politics he roundly condemned back then.\n\nOr the 1987 photo of him standing beside future president Donald Trump, a man whose policies and politics he would have roundly condemned today.\n\nBoth are part of the new Benjamin Lawson Hooks Papers, a massive collection that has been digitized and made available to the public.\n\nBoth photographs were taken when Hooks, born and raised in Memphis, was national executive director of the NAACP.\n\nBoth are eery reminders of efforts -- then and now -- to shift the movement from civil rights to economic justice.\n\n\"Today, we're free but not equal,\" said Rev. Jesse Jackson, a friend and colleague of Hooks.\n\nJackson was in Memphis Monday to commemorate the 49th anniversary of the death of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. The struggle for economic justice that began with King in Memphis continued with Hooks in the 1980s.\n\nHooks, who died in 2010, led the NAACP's battles against Reagan's economic policies -- so-called Reaganomics -- which included tax cuts for the rich and cuts in social programs for the poor.\n\nThe Hooks Papers is a joint project of the Hooks Institute for Social Change and the University of Memphis was years in the making.\n\nImagine reading and analyzing, scanning and cataloging 397 boxes of materials filled with more than 197,000 documents, photographs, audio recordings and miscellany.\n\n\"It look a little longer than we thought, but it's now the largest collection on a single topic at the university,\" said William C. Love, collection specialist.\n\nThe largest and perhaps the most significant.\n\nMost of the collection focuses on Hooks' tenure as NAACP leader, from 1977-92.\n\nThe photo of Hooks shaking Reagan's hand was taken at the White House in late 1980.\n\nThe photo of Hooks standing between Trump and his first wife, Ivana, was taken at Trump Tower in 1987.\n\nTrump was chairman of the host committee for the NAACP's 1987 convention in New York City.\n\nJackson spoke to the convention that year as a Democratic candidate for president.\n\nJackson campaigned against Reaganomics in 1988, and won over enough white, blue-collar voters to win seven primaries and four caucuses.\n\n\"Trump's budget would cut taxes on the rich and cut social programs, education, housing for the poor,\" Jackson said.\n\n\"Trump has a $15 billion Cabinet, and its first mission is to turn down health care for 24 million Americans? That's vulgar.\"\n\nIf he were alive today, Hooks likely would use the same language.\n\nThe Hooks Papers includes Hooks' copy of Reagan's speech to the 1981 NAACP convention in Denver.\n\nDuring the speech, Hooks wrote margin notes in blue ink.\n\nReagan said: \"I genuinely and deeply believe the economic package we have put forth will move us toward black economic freedom.\"\n\nHooks wrote: JOKE\n\nReagan said: \"Americans have been very generous with good intentions and billions of dollars ... Yet in spite of the money and the hopes, the government has never lived up to the dreams of poor people.\"\n\nHooks wrote: WHY\n\nReagan said: \"If productivity had not stopped growing and started downhill after 1965, the GNP today would be $850 billion bigger...And this all would have happened with the compliments of the private sector.\"\n\nHooks wrote: PRODUCTIVITY STOP GROWING BECAUSE OF GREED\n\n\"Dr. Hooks's political and social criticism was informed by his religious training,\" said Daphene R. McFerren, executive director of the Hooks Institute.\n\nIn his 1981 speech to the NAACP, Reagan said he wanted to end the \"bondage\" of government programs by helping business and industry bring about an \"economic emancipation\" for African-Americans.\n\nAfter the speech, Hooks said Reagan's economic policies \"are setting into motion forces that are bound to wreak additional hardship, havoc, despair, pain and suffering on that huge body of the poor and the working poor of which blacks and other minorities are a disproportionate share.\"\n\nHooks, a pastor by vocation, a lawyer and judge by training, knew how to discern the difference between what is preached and what is practiced.\n\nThanks to the Hooks Papers, it's a lesson he's still preaching and teaching.\n\nRead or Share
