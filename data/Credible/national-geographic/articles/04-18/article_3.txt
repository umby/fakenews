quintessential geologic feature of the island.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2><b>Flores: Flowers, Walking, and Waterfalls</b></h2></div>\n<div class=\"image parbase At <a href=\"http://www.petercafesport.com/\" target=\"_blank\">Peter Caf&eacute; Sport</a>, the salty set posts crew-wanted notices and drinks G&amp;T\u2019s beneath a ceiling dripping with yacht club burgees. In 1957, the Capelinhos volcano began erupting and didn\u2019t stop for 13 months, spewing gases, mushroom clouds of black ash, and occasional projectiles the size of cannonballs. The seismic unrest spawned numerous earthquakes and a largescale diaspora of Azorean refugees to the United States. At just 60 years old, the barren landscape feels not of this world, with the space-aged underground <a href=\"http://parquesnaturais.azores.gov.pt/en/faial-eng/what-visit/environmental-centres/capelinhos-volcano-interpretation-centre\" target=\"_blank\">Interpretation Centre</a> enhancing the effect.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2>Take this trip</h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p><b>Go: </b><a href=\"https://www.azoresairlines.pt/en/home\" target=\"_blank\">Azores Airlines SATA</a> is constantly adding new routes, making this cornucopia of adventure and pleasure more accessible than ever before.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p><b>Do: </b><a href=\"http://www.azoreanactiveblueberry.com/\" target=\"_blank\">Azorean Active Blueberry</a> offers canyoning and coasteering adventures on S&atilde;o Miguel (safety equipment and wetsuits included). <a href=\"http://www.gerbybirding.com/\" target=\"_blank\">Gerby Birding</a> leads fanatic \u201ctwitchers\u201d and nature-lovers along coastal breeding areas and deep into the island\u2019s forests in search of resident, migrant, and vagrant birds. Scrimshaw artist <a href=\"http://johnvanopstal.nl/\" target=\"_blank\">John von Opstal</a> receives visitors to his workshop in Horta, Faial, by appointment. <a href=\"http://www.westcanyon.pt/en/\" target=\"_blank\">West Canyon Turismo Adventura</a> on Flores leads half-day treks and canyoning excursions (with emphasis repelling down waterfalls).</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p><b>Sail: </b><a href=\"https://www.espacotalassa.com/\" target=\"_blank\">Espa&ccedil;o Talassa</a> offers dolphin and whale tours out of Pico\u2019s former whaling capital Lajes do Pico. The company donates 50 cents per customer to support planting trees to offset their carbon footprint. Charter-boat company <a href=\"http://www.sailazores.pt/\" target=\"_blank\">Sail Azores</a> on Faial offers daily and weekly rentals (skippered or bareboat) and itineraries for multi-island cruising.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p><b>Eat: </b>Outfitter <a href=\"http://tripixazores.com/\" target=\"_blank\">Tripix</a> leads ascents of Mount Pico and can also organize wine-tasting tours of Pico, cheese-tasting tours on S&atilde;o Miguel, and volcanism tours of Faial. Cheese cooperative <a href=\"http://lactacores.pt/cooperativas.php?pageid=4\" target=\"_blank\">Uniqueijo</a> on Terceira island offers biweekly tours and tastings.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p><i>Sophie Massie is a writer and researcher with a penchant for islands, sailing, and the Lusophone and Francophone worlds. islands rests at the intersection of the Eurasian, African, and North American tectonic plates. Portugal\u2019s sun splashed Azores Islands are a trailblazer in sustainable tourism and boast a robust outdoor culture. The archipelago is considered one of the greenest destinations in the world, protecting not just natural wonders, but also a way of life (wine, honey, pineapple, and various cheeses enjoy protected status here). Dip your toes into the warm Azorean waters and discover paradise on these islands:\n\nTerceira: Architecture and Caving\n\nIn the effusively cultured Angra do Hero\u00edsmo on Terceira island, even the sidewalks are works of art. When not admiring the intricate limestone and basalt mosaics at your feet, look up and behold the Mannerist cathedrals and Easter-egg colored mansions that fill the UNESCO World Heritage colonial city. Housed in a former Franciscan convent, the Museu de Angra do Hero\u00edsmo showcases the breadth of Terceirense culture, from naval and military artifacts to textiles and decorative arts.\n\nBeneath the island\u2019s refined surface lies a bizarre geological formation: the chimney and underground chambers of an extinct volcano. Climb down the 300-foot lava tube of Algar do Carv\u00e3o and see for yourself. Stalactites, stalagmites, and a rainwater lake are all beckoning.\n\nPico: Volcanoes, Whales, and Vinho\n\nView Images Mount Pico volcano on Pico Island\n\nPhotograph by Reinhard Dirscherl\n\nSoaring to 7,714 feet, the volcanic Mount Pico is the highest point in all of Portugal and the most challenging hike in the archipelago. Certified guides lead ascents and overnight camping in the crater just beneath the summit. On a clear day, five islands can be seen from the summit, though hikers should prepare for all types of weather on the exposed mountain face, including snow on the peak in winter and spring.\n\nDuring the industrial whaling boom of the 18th and 19th centuries, New England whalers sailed to the Azores to stock up on provisions and fresh crew. Since whaling formally ended in the 1980s, the camera has replaced the harpoon in capturing whales here. Get a history primer at the Whaling Museum in Lajes do Pico before casting off on a whale-watching adventure.\n\nMore terrestrial treasures lie on the western and northern coasts, including the UNESCO World Heritage Pico vineyards. Here grapevines course low to the ground between orderly basalt rock walls, which protect the precious vines from wind and sea spray. Drink up while you\u2019re here\u2014most Pico wines don\u2019t leave the archipelago, let alone Portugal. Sample acclaimed whites and fortified licoroso wines, at Pico Island Wine Cooperative in Madalena.\n\nS\u00e3o Miguel: Canyoning, Birding and the Thermal Lifestyle\n\nView Images Thermal pool in Caldeira Velha on S\u00e3o Miguel\n\nPhotograph by Gaspar Avila, Alamy\n\nFor nature-lovers, the island of S\u00e3o Miguel is a giant botanical playground. Miradouros (scenic overlooks) with manicured gardens sprinkle the coastal regions. Pink and blue hydrangeas hem in cow pastures and flank the rims of volcanic crater lakes.\n\nIn the densely vegetated canyons of the Nordeste region, canyoneers choose between rappelling or leaping off waterfall-laced cliffs into swimming holes below. Birders also flock here for a glimpse of the critically endangered Azores bullfinch, one of Europe\u2019s rarest birds, found only in the laurel forests of Nordeste.\n\nFor limbs weary of hiking, nothing beats a restorative soak in a natural thermal bath. In coastal Ponta da Ferraria, ocean waves crash over natural rock pools and commingle with thermal water. In fern-fringed Caldeira Velha, thermal waterfalls lend a romantic ambience to the occasion. By contrast, the boiled-egg stench and roiling steam of Furnas serve as a reminder that this region is a potent hot spot. Along the banks of Lagoa das Furnas, local restaurateurs let the Earth do the cooking by submerging giant Dutch ovens in the bubbling calderas. Try an elevated version of cozido, the region\u2019s traditional, thermally-cooked stew, at Terra Nostra Garden Hotel, where waiters pour umami-packed broth over the stewed meats and vegetables tableside.\n\nS\u00e3o Jorge: Cheese and Trekking\n\nView Images Traditional cheese manufacturer on S\u00e3o Jorge\n\nPhotograph by Mauricio Abreu, Aurora\n\nTo sink your teeth into a piece of sharp salty S\u00e3o Jorge cheese is to taste history. Early Flemish settlers of the island were thought to have brought cheese-making to the knife-shaped landmass. Today the famous cow\u2019s milk cheese can be found in Europe and North America, but the imported stuff pales in comparison to the holistic experience of tasting it with a view of the pastures and cows that made it possible.\n\nThe long and narrow orientation of the island, 34 miles long and four miles wide, invites lengthy treks down the island\u2019s spine. Look for trails that lead to faj\u00e3s, coastal flatlands created by lava flow or landslides, and a quintessential geologic feature of the island.\n\nFlores: Flowers, Walking, and Waterfalls\n\nView Images Lagoa das Patas on the island of Flores\n\nPhotograph by Luis Ascenso\n\nAs the westernmost island in the chain, Flores is also the wettest. But all that rain means Flores lives up to its flowery name, with emerald green highlands, crater lakes, and the most vigorous of flora. Waterfall-streaked cliffs and fields of azaleas and hydrangea rank up there with the most beautiful scenes in the entire archipelago. Numerous trails cross the island, making it a walkers\u2019 paradise. End a day of trekking on the west coast at Faj\u00e3 Grande for a spectacular sunset\u2014the last one in Europe.\n\nFaial: Sailing and Volcanoes\n\nView Images Whaling boats between Faial and Pico islands\n\nPhotograph by Mauricio Abreu, Aurora\n\nSailors crossing the Atlantic have rested in the Azores since the beginning of transatlantic voyages, and the cosmopolitan port town of Horta on Faial lives and breathes this maritime tradition. Stroll along the marina and admire the sailors \u201cgallery,\u201d where visiting crew paint emblems of their ships on the jetty. At Peter Caf\u00e9 Sport, the salty set posts crew-wanted notices and drinks G&T\u2019s beneath a ceiling dripping with yacht club burgees. The upstairs scrimshaw museum features a collection of carved and engraved whale teeth, a rarified art form still practiced today albeit using antique and salvaged ivory.\n\nAcross the island, a vastly different image of Faial emerges: one of raw, ferocious volcanism. In 1957, the Capelinhos volcano began erupting and didn\u2019t stop for 13 months, spewing gases, mushroom clouds of black ash, and occasional projectiles the size of cannonballs. The seismic unrest spawned numerous earthquakes and a largescale diaspora of Azorean refugees to the United States. At just 60 years old, the barren landscape feels not of this world, with the space-aged underground Interpretation Centre enhancing the effect.\n\nTake this trip\n\nGo: Azores Airlines SATA is constantly adding new routes, making this cornucopia of adventure and pleasure more accessible than ever before.\n\nDo: Azorean Active Blueberry offers canyoning and coasteering adventures on S\u00e3o Miguel (safety equipment and wetsuits included).
