Hornet on April 18, 1942.\n\nPhotograph by Getty Images\n\nIn the early weeks of 1942, America was outraged, humiliated, and demoralized. The battleships of its Pacific Fleet lay at the bottom of Pearl Harbor, victims of the Japanese surprise attack launched on December 7, 1941.\n\nIn the coming months, the U.S. and its allies could only stand by and watch as the Japanese juggernaut raced across the Pacific, crushing every British, Dutch, French, and American position in its path.\n\nFollowing the Pearl Harbor attack, President Franklin Roosevelt furiously pressed the chiefs of the armed services to find a way to retaliate against the Japanese homeland, but no one knew how to overcome the logistical challenges.\n\nWarplanes based on aircraft carriers were too small to inflict significant damage, and they didn\u2019t hold enough fuel to make the mission feasible. Bombers could rain down destruction but were thought to be too large to take off and land from a carrier.\n\nThen one cold January day someone got the idea that the B-25 \u201cMitchell,\u201d a relatively new, twin-engine, medium bomber, might be able to take off from a flattop deck. Landing one of the planes on a carrier was out of the question, but after bombing Japan it would have just enough fuel to make it to friendly fields in China.\n\nArmy Air Corps commander Gen. Henry \u201cHap\u201d Arnold sent for Lt. Col. James \u201cJimmy\u201d Doolittle, one of the era\u2019s most famous aviators and a man Arnold knew to be \u201cabsolutely fearless.\u201d\n\nView Images In 1922 pioneering aviator James \"Jimmy\" Doolittle became the first American pilot to fly coast to coast with just one stop for fuel.\n\nPhotograph courtesy Smithsonian Institution\n\nThe only pilots qualified to fly B-25s in combat were members of the Army Air Corps\u2019 17th Bombardment Group stationed at Fort Pendleton, Oregon. Doolittle assembled more than 100 of the group\u2019s officers and men and informed them that volunteers were needed for \u201ca very hazardous mission.\u201d Every man signed up.\n\nThe Army pilots underwent arduous training at a secret site on Florida\u2019s Gulf Coast. Accustomed to taking off from mile-long runways, the fliers learned terrifying tactics for launching their planes from a short carrier deck. Meanwhile, two dozen B-25s were equipped with extra fuel tanks and stripped of all unnecessary weight.\n\n\"Bound for Tokyo\"\n\nOn April 2, 1942, the carrier U.S.S. Hornet slipped out of San Francisco Bay with 16 bombers lashed to its flight deck. Cruisers and destroyers joined the carrier at sea, and the task force commander sent a message to all ships: \u201cThis force is bound for Tokyo.\u201d A chorus of cheers rose from five thousand throats into the darkening Pacific skies.\n\nView Images Flight deck crews prepare the B-25 bombers for their mission. It was so secret that even President Franklin Roosevelt wasn't told about it at first.\n\nPhotograph by Underwood Archives, Getty Images\n\nThe task force rendezvoused with the carrier Enterprise in mid-ocean, then continued steaming westward toward Japan. Onboard the ship, Doolittle\u2019s Raiders, as the fliers came to be called, were told to memorize and practice an important Chinese phrase: lusha hoo metwa fugi, \u201cI am an American.\u201d\n\nBecause Japanese troops were occupying large portions of the Chinese mainland, the airmen also learned how to distinguish a Chinese friend from a Japanese foe. They studied maps, gambled, watched movies, ate ice cream, and contemplated their fate. Doolittle estimated that their odds of returning alive were less than fifty-fifty.\n\nView Images Lt. Colonel Doolittle (fourth from right) and four fellow raiders pose with Chinese allies who helped the downed airmen elude capture.\n\nPhotograph by Underwood Archives, Getty Images\n\nAfter two weeks at sea, as the task force approached enemy territory, the weather began to deteriorate. Seas grew tall as a three story building. Then, at 3 a.m. on April 18, radar detected a Japanese picket boat. A Navy cruiser attacked and sank the vessel, but not before its crew had broadcast an alert.\n\nPlans called for Doolittle\u2019s squadron to take off about 300 miles from Japan, but when spotted the American ships were still 700 miles offshore. After a brief discussion with naval commanders, Doolittle got the okay to proceed anyway. The klaxon sounded immediately, and the captain of the U.S.S. Hornet gave the order: \u201cArmy pilots, man your planes!\"\n\nThe ship was rolling and pitching wildly as Doolittle became the first to attempt takeoff. The carrier\u2019s flight officer was timing the rise and fall of the vessel\u2019s bow to give the plane the benefit of the rising deck. \u201cIt was like riding a see-saw,\u201d Doolittle said.\n\nOn signal, he revved his engines until his crew feared he\u2019d burn them up, then lumbered down the deck. \u201cWe wondered what the wind would do to him,\u201d said pilot Ted Lawson, who was four planes back in Ruptured Duck. \u201cEveryone knew if he couldn\u2019t, we couldn\u2019t.\u201d\n\nView Images News of the daring air raid made front-page headlines and boosted American morale less than five months after the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor.\n\nPhotograph by Bettman-Corbis, Getty Images\n\nJust as the carrier lifted up on a swell, Doolittle became airborne with only yards to spare. \u201cHe hung his ship almost straight up on its props,\u201d Lawson said, so everyone could see the entire top of the plane, \u201cthen he leveled out.\u201d\n\nAll 16 planes made it into the sky, but not before a navy crewman slipped on the soaking deck and thrust his arm into the whirring propeller of the last plane to take off.\n\n\"We got an aircraft carrier!\"\n\nFive hours later, flying low, the squadron reached the coast of Japan. Fishermen and farmers looked up and waved, assuming the warplanes were Japanese. The weather had cleared.\n\nMost of the bombers homed in on Tokyo, which appeared immense and sprawling, like Los Angeles. Coming in at rooftop level gave the raiders a jump on the hundreds of anti-aircraft guns ringing the Japanese capital.\n\nDoolittle spotted his target\u2014a large munitions factory\u2014and pulled up to 1,200 feet to reach bombing altitude. The bombardier dropped four incendiaries, setting the factory ablaze.\n\nView Images Doolittle sits beside the wreckage of his B-25 after crash landing in China. He assumed he'd be court-martialed for losing all 16 planes under his command.\n\nPhotograph by Underwood Archive, Getty Images\n\nAnother pilot headed across Tokyo for a large naval base in Yokohama. As the bomb bay doors opened, anti-aircraft fire, called flak, jolted the plane, but the bombardier managed to pull the lever. After a few seconds he shouted jubilantly, \u201cWe got an aircraft carrier!\u201d\n\nAll the airmen half expected a cloud of Japanese fighters, called Zeros, to descend on them. But the raiders stormed in and out of their target cities so quickly that they largely eluded the enemy fighters.\n\nAt one point during the action the Japanese prime minister, Gen. Hideki Tojo, was approaching an airbase in a small official plane. As he descended to the runway, Doolittle\u2019s B-25 flashed by without firing a shot. The general\u2019s aide reported that the plane was \u201cqueer looking.\u201d\n\nEnterprise and Hornet , now speeding home, began picking up signals from Radio Tokyo telling of the raid. Great cheering broke out among the thousands of sailors aboard. They had done it! America had struck back!\n\nFlying on Empty\n\nDoolittle\u2019s squadron reassembled over the Sea of Japan and flew westward into the setting sun. Not one of the B-25s had been shot down. Now all they had to do was make it to the landing fields in friendly Chinese territory.\n\nBut the Americans would now be landing in China at night, greatly complicating matters. To make things even worse, the weather once more began to deteriorate.\n\nView Images American airmen are guided to safety by Chinese comrades after crashing near this village. Less fortunate fliers were captured or killed by Japanese troops.\n\nPhotograph by by Time Life Pictures, U.S. Air Force, The LIFE Picture Collection, Getty Images\n\nThe China coast was lined with tall mountains that ranged deep into the interior. Visibility was zero, and the bombers were running out of fuel. There was no sign of the homing signals that were supposed to mark the runways. Doolittle decided that the only option was to keep flying until they ran out of gas, then bail out into the dark, rainy night.\n\nAs each plane\u2019s engines began to choke and sputter, the pilots and crew dropped one by one through the open bomb bay into who knew what. Miraculously, only three men were killed in landing. Japanese soldiers captured eight others; three of these were executed, and the rest were horribly tortured and sentenced to life in prison.\n\nDoolittle himself plunged into in a rice paddy fertilized with human waste. He tried calling out lusha hoo metwa fugi at several huts, but nobody understood him.\n\nOne pilot landed in a tree and, in what may be the only instance of a cigarette saving a person\u2019s life, he lit up before cutting himself free and dropping to the ground. He finished smoking, tossed the still-glowing butt into the air, and watched in horror as it disappeared into the blackness far, far below. The tree that snagged his parachute stood on the edge of a thousand-foot cliff.\n\nView Images President Roosevelt awarded Doolittle the Congressional Medal of Honor for his role in planning and leading the air raid on Tokyo.\n\nPhotograph by Bettman-Corbis, Getty Images\n\nAnother American, seeking both to hide and escape from the cold, started to climb into a long wooden box resting on sawhorses in a barn, only to discovered that it was a casket containing \"a very elderly Chinese gentleman.\"\n\nMost of Doolittle\u2019s five-man crews were reunited the next day. Friendly Chinese sheltered and transported them\u2014by rickshaw, sedan chair, donkey, oxcart, bicycle, and piggyback\u2014to the safety of American lines, where they were greeted as heroes.\n\nNews of the daring attack was trumpeted from radios and newspapers around the world, putting fresh heart in demoralized Allied forces. More importantly, the hit-and-run raid helped military planners gain vital intelligence that would change the course of the war.\n\nBefore the Doolittle mission, the Japanese navy was observing near radio silence, frustrating the efforts of American cryptographers, who had deciphered only about 10 percent of the Imperial Navy's code book. The stunning airstrike at the heart of Japan unleashed a torrent of coded messages, so that within a few weeks American codebreakers had cracked nearly 90 percent of the Japanese naval code.\n\nView Images Last living member of the Doolittle Raiders, Richard E. Cole visits Doolittle's grave in Arlington National Cemetery. Now 101 years old, Lt. Colonel Cole served as Doolittle's co-pilot.\n\nPhotograph by Melina Mara, The Washington Post, Getty Images\n\nLittle more than a month later, in June 1942, Japan dispatched four of its six large aircraft carriers to attack a U.S. military outpost on Midway Island. The forewarned Americans were waiting in ambush and sank all four of the Japanese carriers. The devastating loss crippled the Japanese fleet and helped turn the tide of the war in the Allies' favor.\n\nWhen Jimmy Doolittle arrived back at Army Air Corps headquarters in Washington, he found Hap Arnold and Army Chief-of-Staff George Marshall waiting for him.
