With its gray coat and black spots, the big cat was nearly invisible against the rock of this arid landscape. But its prey had moved on and were now slowly foraging up a trail clearly carved into a peak on the other side of the windswept valley.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The snow leopard leapt down the slope and bounded over to a rusty red rock standing near the frozen watering hole. It circled it, sniffed for other snow leopards, and clawed four parallel white lines. Then it turned back towards the ibex.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cHere you can see the small scratch marks of the snow leopard,\u201d says Alexander Karnaukhov, a biologist and snow leopard specialist with the World Wildlife Fund Russia, dragging his fingers over the rock in the Sailyugem National Park. \u201cThis is how he makes it clear to other snow leopards that he lives here,\u201d says Karnaukhov, who goes by Sasha. seeing it from the point of view of the endangered cat; describing the animal\u2019s movements from its tracks.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cRight at this spot, we're going to put up a camera trap,\u201d he says, \u201cand get a better idea of who lives here.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Denis Malikov, a biologist and assistant director of the national park, pulled out a GPS unit and entered yet another data point for Russia\u2019s first ever full-range snow leopard census.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Just above the rock, Karnaukhov found snow leopard scat. Pulling out a small baggie, he picked it up and pocketed it. He will send this and other samples to Moscow for DNA analysis.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Using this combination of genetic material and camera traps, which biologists use to laboriously identify individual cats by their unique spot patterns, the Russian biologists hope to get a fuller understanding of the world\u2019s northern-most group of the endangered predator. Their findings will help clarify global numbers\u2014an integral part of a heated debate among scientists that will determine whether the cats remain on the endangered list of the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), or whether they are moved to being only \u201cthreatened.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Coming back down the slope, the two scientists debated where they will put the trap, a camouflaged, weatherproof plastic box crammed with camera, motion sensor, memory card, insulation, and enough AA batteries for a couple of months. Too high up the hill and they feared clouds and shadows would trigger a picture, filling the memory card with pretty, but useless, landscapes. Too low and they worried a bush might obscure shots of the cat. In a few days, Malikov would make the bone-jarring, two-hour drive on his ATV back up this remote, rocky riverbed, pound a stake into the frozen ground, and set up the trap. Then they have to wait.</p></div>\n<div class=\"UniversalVideo section\">      \t\t\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n    \n        \n            \n            \n                \n                \n                \n      \n    \n    \n\n</div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2>Counting Russian Leopards</h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Currently, in Russia, it is thought that there are between 70 and 90 snow leopards, or <i>Panthera uncia</i>, making up only one to two percent of the worldwide population. In the partial range survey carried out in 2016, researchers confirmed 41 snow leopards and eight litters of kittens, totaling 19 young, says Karnaukhov. This year they hope to find out how many survived. More importantly they are surveying the entire range\u2014including the Republic of Altai, Buryatia, and Tyva (commonly called Tuva)\u2014some of the most remote lands in Russia, all bordering Mongolia.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Fortunately for scientists like Karnaukhov and Malikov, the cats are \u201cvery conservative,\u201d frequently using the same \u201clinear landmarks\u201d\u2014game trails, riverbeds, and ridgelines through the mountains. Also, many cats will use the same marking stone\u2014like the one with the claw marks on it.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cIt\u2019s like nature\u2019s Facebook,\u201d says Karnaukhov. As the cats scratch, rub against, and spray urine on cliffs and exposed rock they communicate with one another. That makes it easier for scientists to collect photographs and videos of the animals, allowing them to identify and count the snow leopards.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>And the Sailyugem National Park is, more and more, a good place to go looking for the predator. \u201cIt\u2019s like the Russian Africa,\u201d says Mikhail Paltsyn, a Russian biologist and one of the founders of the park, currently working on his PhD at SUNY\u2019s College of Environmental Science and Forestry in Syracuse. \u201cIt\u2019s easy to see 300 ibex just from one spot,\u201d he says. \u201cIt's a very bad day if you haven't seen a hundred ibex in a day.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Indeed, driving up the dry, frozen, windswept valleys of the park in Soviet jeeps past the small log cabins of the impoverished shepherds who live here year around, it is common to see big herds of Siberian ibex (<i>Capra sibirica</i>), feeding on the grasses high in the hills and mountains. This ibex is the longest and heaviest wild goat, with great, upward arcing horns. Also common is the argali (<i>Ovis ammon ammon</i>), the giant cousin of the American bighorn sheep. It is the largest of all sheep, weighing as much as 328 pounds, with some sets of horns weighing up to 75 pounds.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The ibex is the favorite prey of the snow leopard, with a population of around 4,000 to 5,000 in the park. Paltsyn says a prey base of this size could naturally support a population of around 30 to 40 snow leopards. But from 2004 to 2012 there were only between two and five snow leopards left in the Argut River watershed, which includes the national park, and that not a single cat was found in the Sailyugem.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Today there are no less than 15 snow leopards in the watershed, \u201cso it seems the population is slowly restoring,\u201d says Paltsyn. \u201cI\u2019m very optimistic about the future,\u201d he says, \u201cbecause right now we have a very good level of protection.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cThey\u2019re more than rare, they\u2019re cryptic,\u201d says Peter Zahler, the regional director for Asia for the Wildlife Conservation Society.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Even today, little is known about snow leopards because they occur at such low densities and live in \u201csome of the most difficult terrain imaginable,\u201d he says. It is not for nothing that the snow leopard is known as the ghost of the mountains. The animal\u2019s home range reads like a list of fairytale lands beyond the edge of the known world. Living as high as 19,000 feet, they are restricted to the high mountains of the Altai, Tian Shan, Kun Lun, Pamir, Hindu Kush, Karakorum, and Himalaya ranges.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>But even if Paltsyn is optimistic, the snow leopard still faces an uphill battle for survival. In Russia the main threat is poaching of musk deer, whose glands are used for traditional medicine in China and Korea. One kilo of granulated musk can fetch around $45,000 on the international market, and it takes about 40 deer to make a kilo. Hunters use wire snares set in the undergrowth to catch and strangle the deer. Snow leopards die as \u201cby-catch\u201d\u2014just as dolphins die when tuna are netted\u2014as they follow game trails at the lower elevations.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The intersection of musk deer and snow leopard habitats \u201cleads to a dramatic erosion of the population of the snow leopard, and, in some places, to its almost complete extermination,\u201d says Karnaukhov. Indeed, over the past two years the population of snow leopards at a nature reserve 200 miles northeast of the park dropped from eight or nine to just one, after the new reserve director fired all the rangers hired to remove snares.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cIf you give up, the area will be covered in snares again,\u201d says Paltsyn, \u201cand snares very easily kill snow leopards.\u201d (<a href=\"http://news.nationalgeographic.com/2016/10/snow-leopards-kyrgyzstan-conservation-endangered/\">More snow leopards poached, despite bold plan</a>.)</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Besides snaring, snow leopards in Russia face the same problems they face everywhere, though some are of a particularly Russian flavor. At one point an oligarch was flying helicopters full of government officials and rich businessmen into the park to hunt argali and Siberian ibex. The hunting was small scale, but, when combined with <a href=\"https://urldefense.proofpoint.com/v2/url?u=http-3A__static1.1.sqspcdn.com_static_f_157301_27298294_1476983224237_Snow-2DLeopard-2DReport.pdf-3Ftoken-3DoSMUC-252F5hYNvwqovbnQs2Tmx88cw-253D&amp;d=DwMFaQ&amp;c=uw6TLu4hwhHdiGJOgwcWD4AjKQx6zvFcGEsbfiY9-EI&amp;r=YvQx9zgFl9iZpXAswLhVDVvmWPJIY7k_NZVquyWT-DI&amp;m=kHHrbGWs7XQPN-vPVodGQgFORzwIIGulOFbs3PFg_ko&amp;s=sC4dW2IwVyZYfnqXUEsAkE6L678d0WcJRseGhcHkek0&amp;e=\">global challenges</a> like habitat loss, <a href=\"http://news.nationalgeographic.com/2015/12/151202-mongolia-climate-change-snow-leopards-livestock-world-wildlife-fund/\">climate change</a>, a drop in prey numbers due to livestock overgrazing, and retaliatory killings by ranchers protecting those herds, snow leopards face serious challenges across their 11 range countries. The big cat was placed on the endangered species list by the IUCN in 1972.</p></div>\n<div class=\"UniversalVideo section\">      \t\t\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n    \n        \n            \n            \n \n\n</div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2>Changing the Animal\u2019s Status?</h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Currently, the IUCN is in the middle of an already multi-year process to reassess the status of the snow leopard, even though none of these threats have been eliminated. Indeed, not only are they are not going away, climate change could eliminate up to 30 percent of the snow leopard\u2019s territory in the Himalaya alone over the coming decades, putting this apex predator in the same category as the polar bear in terms of species that will dramatically lose habitat due to a warming world.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Some on the IUCN panel are recommending delisting the big cat. Others think that continuing and new threats, combined with a lack of accurate data on snow leopard numbers, means that the animal should retain its current classification. Those who support not reclassifying the cat recommend waiting until new technology, like camera traps, is able to provide a clearer answer on population size.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>To many, this is the crux of the argument. The prevailing range of global estimates varies between around 3,900 and 7,500 animals. But a <a href=\"https://urldefense.proofpoint.com/v2/url?u=https-3A__www.elsevier.com_books_snow-2Dleopards_nyhus_978-2D0-2D12-2D802213-2D9&amp;d=DwMFaQ&amp;c=uw6TLu4hwhHdiGJOgwcWD4AjKQx6zvFcGEsbfiY9-EI&amp;r=YvQx9zgFl9iZpXAswLhVDVvmWPJIY7k_NZVquyWT-DI&amp;m=kHHrbGWs7XQPN-vPVodGQgFORzwIIGulOFbs3PFg_ko&amp;s=S0gsHfGIFp8GiwfdCN2b-F5-LhXt8CIp7OMSQdQPWAA&amp;e=\">book</a> published in June last year\u2014the first comprehensive scientific volume on the animal, with around 200 scientists contributing\u2014caused a <a href=\"https://urldefense.proofpoint.com/v2/url?u=https-3A__www.snowleopard.org_statement-2Dof-2Dconcern_&amp;d=DwMFaQ&amp;c=uw6TLu4hwhHdiGJOgwcWD4AjKQx6zvFcGEsbfiY9-EI&amp;r=YvQx9zgFl9iZpXAswLhVDVvmWPJIY7k_NZVquyWT-DI&amp;m=kHHrbGWs7XQPN-vPVodGQgFORzwIIGulOFbs3PFg_ko&amp;s=epnWyP8LT6r6vEkTXWLhsJE_KNX9UKsQpPnM1mw_5uU&amp;e=\">firestorm</a> when contributors to one chapter claimed there may be between 4,678 and 8,745 cats in just 44 percent of their global range. The implication being that there may be up to twice as many in the total range.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Zahler, of the Wildlife Conservation Society, contributed to the chapter with the new numbers. He says snow leopard researchers have a good understanding of the range, but not of how many live in that territory.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cWithin that area we have a pretty good sense of where we know a lot and where we know a little and where we know almost nothing at all,\u201d he says.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Currently, the high and low estimates differ by a factor of nearly two. \u201cSo, they\u2019re not terribly accurate, regardless of which one you think is best,\u201d he says. \u201cWe're still, to a large extent, in the infancy of understanding them.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>But he feels that the broad application of camera traps, satellite collars, pattern recognition software, and other technology will soon begin to fill in the gaps. \u201cIt's very difficult to extrapolate from signs as to whether you're looking at one snow leopard or five,\u201d he says.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>If its status changes, the stakes for the future of the animal could be significant. Gustaf Samelius, a researcher at the Swedish University of Agricultural Sciences and the assistant director of science at the Snow Leopard Trust, says delisting could mean \u201cgovernments are not going to put any money towards conservation of snow leopards.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>He thinks those who put together the chapter with the higher numbers were \u201ca little uncritical\u201d in their use of expert \u201cguesstimates\u201d and sign tallies. Already, colleagues in Pakistan have told him that their government is shifting money away from snow leopard conservation programs, since numbers appear to be on the rise.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>And because of the recent improvements in technology, Samelius believes, \u201cit\u2019s not that the numbers have increased, it's just that our tools are getting better at detecting them.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Zahler agrees. \u201cI want to be very clear: we don't really know how many snow leopards there are or what the real trend is,\u201d he says. \u201cSnow leopards are probably declining. They just seem to be declining less rapidly than a lot of the other big cats.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2>Hope for the Future?</h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Even if the experts don\u2019t agree on snow leopard numbers, they all agree that the animals still face significant threats. \u201cThe scary thing is that the threats are increasing,\u201d says Samelius. Zahler agrees: \u201cThe last thing I would want is for people to think that there's a few thousand more snow leopards than we thought, so we don't need to do anything.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The Soviet jeep slid over the smooth ice of the river and then abruptly continued on its jouncing journey over large river stones, up the valley. At a newly built trailhead, half covered in drifted snow, the jeeps stopped. The scientists and a couple scouts got out. More than 800 feet above us, the wind howled over a bald ridge, dropping the temperature to -40F. A ranger had found a likely spot for a trap, with a rock next to a game trail. The bottom of the hill was already above 6,500 feet. We started up through loose scree and wind-hardened snow.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>For two years, when they started putting up camera traps in the area in 2010, Karnaukhov says they saw no snow leopards. Then they started to appear. This was possibly due to an increase in population, but also because the team became more experienced in putting the cameras up, in the right places.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>In the wind, the small team shouted, even at close range. The chosen rock would not work. The trap would face the sunrise and sunset and that might trigger the camera. They chose another rock. Tethering the camera to a big stone, they propped it up and wedged small stones around it. Then they tilted it forward so it aimed down at the game trail.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The valley stretched away into the distance, fading into a haze of dust and blown snow.
