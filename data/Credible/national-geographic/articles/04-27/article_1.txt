He was now, he said, \u201cthe exact age of Adolf Hitler, the most successful man in the world.\u201d Then he introduced himself as Douglas Chandler, a contributor to National Geographic magazine.\n\nView Images Douglas Chandler (left) arrives at his trial in Boston in 1947, where he would face charges of treason.\n\nPhotograph via AP\n\nAt National Geographic headquarters in Washington, D.C., a memo was circulating among top editors with a newspaper clipping from The Washington Post: \u201cNazi \u2018Paul Revere\u2019 Proves Former Baltimore Man.\u201d Letters began pouring in from magazine subscribers who had been listening to the disparaging broadcasts, in which Chandler had boasted about his employer while repeatedly railing against its leadership.\n\n\u201cI couldn\u2019t have been more surprised if it had been the Annual Report of the Ladies\u2019 Benevolent Society being ripped to ribbons!\u201d wrote one concerned reader, who had heard the magazine mentioned a dozen times. \u201cThis is what I am curious about\u2014how did the Nazis come to have such a spite against the \u2018National Geographic\u2019?\u201d\n\nBoth National Geographic and the FBI had already been investigating Chandler\u2019s Nazi ties. But now, with his radio reveal, the American public discovered that a National Geographic writer had\u2014loudly\u2014taken up the Nazi\u2019s cause.\n\nHow He Was Hooked\n\nBorn in Chicago in 1889, Chandler served in the U.S. Navy during World War I, then dabbled in sales\u2014mostly razors and skin creams. He found success as a stockbroker in the late 1920s, but his timing was unfortunate: The ensuing financial crisis tanked his savings, along with those of his wealthy wife, Laura.\n\nSo Chandler turned to journalism. He took a job at the Baltimore News-American writing a weekly news column, often in a poetic verse. He was known among his friends, editors, and fellow servicemen as charming but arrogant. In his writing and socializing he grew increasingly despondent about the state of America, and began to blame his failures on the Jews.\n\nIn 1931, the Chandlers and their two daughters set sail for France, where Douglas would discover two new passions: photography and Nazism. He soon purchased a Leica camera and began a career as a freelancer. Then he visited Germany for the first time, where\u2014according to historian John Carver Edwards\u2019 book Berlin Calling: American Broadcasters in Service to the Third Reich\u2014he was introduced to the Nazi Press Department attach\u00e9 and the general consul in Munich. The latter took him on a tour of Dachau, one of the Nazi\u2019s first concentration camps, where Chandler was impressed by the clean barracks. He left convinced that U.S. authorities were distorting conditions in Germany.\n\nIn 1936, Chandler sent photographs from a trip to North Africa to an acquaintance: John Oliver La Gorce, an associate editor at National Geographic. Chandler wrote that he was enjoying life abroad and planned to stay there, \u201cpre-supposing that no general catastrophe is going to overtake poor Europe.\u201d\n\nLa Gorce declined to publish the photos, but he proposed that Chandler write an article about Berlin. The magazine was looking for a story \u201cpicturing the life of the people, their amusements or hobbies, something of the caf\u00e9 life, recreational interests, the business side, recent developments in architecture, how traffic is handled \u2026\u201d\n\nThough political tensions were historically high, La Gorce urged Chandler to abide by National Geographic\u2019s founding editorial rules, which dictated that stories should be \u201cof a kindly nature\u201d and, most important, apolitical. \u201cIt is realized that present conditions in Germany are decidedly controversial, and it is difficult to write about it without impinging upon politics and religion,\u201d wrote La Gorce, \u201cbut we leave that to other publications to cover.\u201d\n\nChandler agreed, noting that he had already spent several days photographing the towns where Hitler was born, and where his parents had died, for \u201ca comprehensive series of photos of all that concerned his youth.\u201d\n\nA few months later, he sent a 9,000-word story titled \u201cBerlin, City of Rivers and Trees\u201d and a letter ensuring that he had \u201cfaithfully steered clear of those \u2018controversial\u2019 subjects against which your letter warned me.\u201d La Gorce praised Chandler and deposited $500 in his bank account.\n\nThe resulting article\u201447 pages of dramatic images showing swastika-draped buildings and reverential descriptions of a city under Nazi rule\u2014is among the most embarrassing in National Geographic\u2019s history. The proposed title was swapped for \u201cChanging Berlin,\u201d but it failed to depict a country seized by totalitarianism and religious persecution. Instead, photos show Hitler\u2019s birthday parade and a bunch of happy Berliners.\n\n\u201cTo develop boys and girls in body and mind,\u201d one caption reads, \u201cand thus insure a sturdy race to defend Germany in the future, is a policy of the present government.\u201d The only hint of trouble in Germany was a single line describing butchery practices: \u201cKosher killing is everywhere prohibited.\u201d\n\nGetting in Deep\n\nIn 1937, Chandler was introduced to Ulrich von Bulow, the man in charge of recruiting Americans and Brits for Joseph Goebbels\u2019 propaganda ministry.\n\nAt the same time, he continued writing for National Geographic. He reported on Turkish democracy, Baltic culture, Belgian architecture, and a Yugoslavian monarch.\n\nWhen Chandler returned to Germany, in 1938, he was pleased to see Nazism taking root. \u201cI find a thrilling and admirable new social order in the process of burgeoning here,\u201d he wrote to a friend.\n\nIn 1939, when the Chandlers were staying in an old castle on Korcula, an island off the Dalmatian Coast, they were visited by William Danforth, founder of the Purina pet-food company and a friend of La Gorce. When Danforth returned home, he wrote La Gorce to express his gratitude for the introduction, but noted that \u201c[t]he disturbing thing to me \u2026 was that Mr. Chandler was more Pro-Natzi [sic] and Anti-Jewish than any man of any nationality that I met anywhere on our trip.\u201d\n\nView Images Swastika flags are drapped across downtown Berlin, covering the armory (at right).\n\nPhotograph by Wilhelm Tobien, National Geographic Creative\n\n(La Gorce\u2019s suspicion may have already been aroused: The year before, Chandler wrote in a letter that the boycott of the Third Reich by American universities had brought him \u201cshame and embarrassment.\u201d)\n\nHe thanked Danforth for the tip and assured him that he\u2019d \u201cmade it very plain \u2026 that The Geographic did not concern itself with political or religious controversies and its published articles must be without propaganda or bias.\u201d\n\nHe then sent a staff member in Europe to visit the Chandlers and investigate the Nazi allegation. The Chandlers\u2019 German leanings were soon confirmed.\n\nLa Gorce ordered correspondence with Chandler to be cut off, canceled an upcoming assignment to Albania, and made a full report of Chandler\u2019s activities to the U.S. government.\n\nChandler, now living in Yugoslavia and Italy, was growing increasingly conspiratorial. When La Gorce stopped answering his cables and letters, he blamed the influence of Jews in the media. When he made his views too well known, neighbors on Korcula, where he was living, accused him of being a Nazi\u2014and he decried himself the victim of a witch hunt led by Jews.\n\nFatherland Calling\n\nIn the winter of 1940, the U.S. consul in Italy ordered all Americans to return home. Chandler instead procured a visa that would take him and his family to Germany. When they arrived, he immediately offered his services\u2014pro bono\u2014to Goebbels\u2019 propaganda recruiter, according to the book Berlin Calling.\n\nVon Bulow arranged for Chandler to get a time slot on the Reich Broadcasting Corporation\u2019s \u201cUSA Zone,\u201d a program piped into the U.S. via shortwave radio that sought to turn public sentiment against involvement in the war. Upon hearing the news of Chandler\u2019s hire, Laura wrote in her diary: \u201cThank God Douglas has this wonderful opportunity to serve the USA!\u201d\n\nChandler was joining an army of propagandists waging war over the airwaves. In Japan, Tokyo Rose taunted American soldiers. In an office near Chandler in Berlin, William Joyce, an American-born Irish broadcaster who called himself \u201cLord Haw-Haw,\u201d railed against Winston Churchill in a shrill upper-class accent. In April 1941, \u201cPaul Revere\u201d joined them.\n\nView Images Wealthy Berliners dine at an outdoor cafe. Chandler's writing and photographs gave no hint at the dark side of Nazism.\n\nPhotograph by Douglas Chandler, National Geographic Creative\n\nHitler and Goebbels had instructed Chandler to follow a five-point propaganda program\u2014a \u201cpsychological weapon of war\u201d that was later outlined at Chandler\u2019s treason trial. It stipulated: \u201c1) Bolshevism is public enemy No. 1 for the whole world. 2) Jews all over the world are supporting Bolshevism. 3) Germans are the happiest and best cared for people in the world. 4) Germany is invincible. And 5) England is economically and politically decadent.\u201d\n\nSpecific material for the broadcast came straight from daily meetings led by Goebbels.\n\nAround Berlin, Chandler was never seen without a swastika on his lapel. He drove a maroon Mercedes with an American flag painted on the side. A Time magazine reporter who caught up with him in Berlin described him as \u201ca tall, handsome, crisp-mannered, crisp-dressed person, with crisp iron-grey hair.\u201d\n\nA Traitor Unmasked\n\nA month into his program, Chandler revealed his identity, mentioning National Geographic nearly a dozen times in the process. Almost immediately, subscribers who listened began writing in to the magazine\u2019s headquarters in Washington, D.C. La Gorce personally wrote back to many of them.\n\n\u201cIt is a sickening commentary on human nature than an especially intelligent man would sell his birthright for Nazi gold,\u201d he replied to one, \u201cbut as history has recorded, such things happen now and then.\u201d\n\nTo another he wrote: \u201cIn his silly broadcasts he is probably trying to \u2018smear\u2019 us to build himself up in importance but isn\u2019t getting anywhere. If and when he ever tries to return to the USA this renegade will be properly dealt with.\u201d\n\nIn an editorial headlined \u201cAmerica\u2019s No. 1 Traitor,\u201d a former friend of Chandler\u2019s named Albert A. Brandt wrote: \u201cExtolling the righteousness, glory and invincibility of the Nazis, Douglas Chandler outdoes Goebbels.\u201d (But, he noted, Chandler was \u201cstill an exceptionally handsome man.\u201d)\n\nWhile he was making a stir stateside, Chandler was dissatisfied with his set-up in Berlin. He was a hypochondriac and an insomniac who drank copiously without showing signs of inebriation. Each recording session, he claimed, made him physically ill.\n\n\u201cWithin the first hour I would be seized by a violent nervous paroxysm which centered in my solar plexus and caused me during the time of writing a violent diarrhea each day,\u201d he later wrote.\n\nChandler also complained endlessly to his superiors. He was bitter that the propaganda department had neither helped him find an apartment nor given him a proper office. And he disliked everything: the show\u2019s music, the other announcers, being told what to say. His bosses suspended him and threatened him with a lawsuit.\n\nWar and Peacemakers\n\nOn December 7, 1941, the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor drew America into WWII.\n\nChandler\u2019s car, branded with the American flag, was confiscated by the Germans. His activities now had potentially graver repercussions, but after a three-month hiatus he decided to continue with the show, reasoning that \u201cat the end of the war when I go home, if the Democrats are still in power, I will definitely be persona non grata with officialdom but will be acclaimed by the masses of the people.\u201d\n\nHis broadcasts the following year show he knew about\u2014and supported\u2014Hitler\u2019s \u201cFinal Solution\u201d to eradicate Judaism, and hoped it would be adopted in America.\n\n\u201cYes, by all means let Pearl Harbor be avenged,\u201d he said on the air. \u201cBut not upon the Japanese ... upon the real authors of this war: the Jews. The day is not far off the horizon when the Yankee cry will be for a plentifully purging pogrom, and the measures employed by the Reich will seem child\u2019s play in comparison.\u201d\n\nView Images During Chandler's time in Berlin, Mutiny on the Bounty played at a theater. A year after this photo was published, violence broke out on an infamous night known as Kristallnacht, when Jewish stores and homes were torched and vandalized.\n\nPhotograph by Douglas Chandler, National Geographic Creative\n\nAs the U.S. fought German advances, Chandler\u2019s claim that he was working in the best interests of America by dissuading its involvement in the war became obsolete. \u201cMy only thought during this period had been for ... the continued existence of what we called the American way of life,\u201d he later told the FBI.\n\nIn 1942 the U.S. began prosecuting traitors. The next summer, Chandler and three others\u2014including the poet Ezra Pound\u2014were indicted. Chandler attributed this to the power of the Jews.\n\nHis broadcasts grew increasingly mild. He played classical music and launched a poetry series featuring the works of Walt Whitman and Alfred Lord Tennyson, noting that \u201cpoets and musicians were the ultimate peacemakers.\u201d\n\nPostwar Reckoning\n\nThe hunt for wartime traitors began soon after the U.S. declared victory in Europe. Authorities found Chandler and his family living in a Bavarian village. According to Berlin Calling, Chandler spent the first 15 minutes of his apprehension staring at the captain who\u2019d been sent to retrieve him, dumbfounded. Then he asked how the Americans had liked his program.\n\nChandler was taken to a U.S. Army detention center, where he wrote a 48-page autobiography. He was one of 11 Americans prosecuted for treason after WWII. His case went to trial in 1947 and captivated the country. The American government hadn\u2019t given much credit to the Nazi broadcast blitz, but it was later estimated that between 150,000 to 300,000 Americans had heard the programs.\n\n\u201cA verbal picture of Douglas Chandler as an eccentric insomniac who contemplated suicide, and imagined Jewish agents were following him was drawn on the witness stand in federal court here today,\u201d the Boston Traveler wrote during the trial\u2019s third week.\n\nWar had taken its toll on Chandler, who was then 58. An account in Berlin Calling read: \u201cAt first glance the shrunken, somewhat cadaverous man who stood before the court seemed like a startled rabbit. Those who had expected a satanic presence were disappointed ... If Chandler represented anything at all, he illustrated the poetic truth of the Platonic doctrine that evil is incompleteness of being.\u201d\n\nChandler\u2019s lawyers argued that he\u2019d exercised his First Amendment rights, and they defended him as \u201ca true paranoiac.\u201d Four psychiatrists testified that he had an \u201cobsessive fear\u201d of Jews. One doctor argued that Chandler was not \u201ccapable of resisting the opportunity of broadcasting his views on Jews.\u201d\n\nAs the trial wore on, National Geographic was still trying to separate its name from his. Chandler told the FBI that the magazine had requested he \u201csurvey\u201d various countries for articles, and its name was dropped frequently in the trial. Newspaper reports referred to him variously as a \u201cEuropean correspondent,\u201d a \u201cspecial representative,\u201d and an \u201ceditorial staff member.\u201d\n\nAt this point Theodore F. Koop, chief of the magazine\u2019s news bulletin service, sent a memo to Boston newspapers: \u201cThe facts are that Chandler never was any of these things, or had any other connection with the National Geographic magazine except that of a freelance contributor who was offered several articles before the war started.\u201d\n\nChandler\u2019s insanity defense didn\u2019t hold up against testimony from 20 witnesses, including 16 of his former German colleagues. Before the judge ruled, Chandler stood up and said: \u201cI am of course not insane, but I have permitted them to defend me on the grounds they chose. It is the tragedy of my life that the warnings I gave my country were not, and are not, yet accepted. Time, however, will vindicate me.\u201d\n\nHe was sentenced to life in prison and a $10,000 fine. Fifteen years later, Chandler was the only \u201cradio traitor\u201d still imprisoned. The U.S. attorney general announced that the 73-year-old would be released into the care of one of his daughters.\n\nThe only matter left was the fine. An Episcopal minister appealed directly to President John F. Kennedy, and five days later Chandler\u2019s sentence was commuted. He returned to Germany.\n\nA Ghost Returns\n\nBut National Geographic hadn\u2019t rid itself of this embarrassing episode. In 1970, Chandler wrote to Editor Melville Bell Grosvenor, requesting $200 to $300 for expenses incurred on an assignment that had been canceled after his Nazi sympathies were discovered.\n\n\u201cThis letter has been sitting on my desk for some days, and frankly I haven\u2019t the slightest idea how to answer it,\u201d Grosvenor wrote in a memo to a deputy. \u201cChandler must be an old man now, and it appears he is really desperate for funds.\u201d\n\nLittle is known of Chandler\u2019s life after this letter. But a few other remnants have resurfaced online.\n\nIn an early 2000s blog, two commenters shared their tales of meeting the then-forgotten propagandist. One of them claimed to have shared a train carriage with Chandler in Germany in the 1970s. By then Chandler was in his 80s and reportedly living in the Canary Islands with a younger German wife. They got to talking, the commenter said, but it appeared that prison hadn\u2019t changed Chandler\u2019s politics.\n\n\u201cHe was an unrepentant Nazi, to the end,\u201d the commenter wrote.
