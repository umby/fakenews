But it\u2019s not meth and it\u2019s not cocaine. In one case, according to a special agent, it was about 25 parrots destined to be sold as pets. Smugglers used tire inner tubes to keep the box of birds afloat during the river crossing.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The southern border of the U.S. is a hot zone for wildlife smuggling, and while President Trump has vowed to stop illegal immigration from Mexico, it\u2019s unclear what he intends to do to thwart the flow of illicit wildlife.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Of the nearly 50,000 illegal shipments of wildlife and wildlife products seized at ports of entry from 2005 through 2014, more than a quarter originated in Latin America, according to a <a href=\"http://www.defenders.org/sites/default/files/publications/trends_in_wildlife_trade_from_latin_america_to_the_us.pdf\">2016 fact sheet</a><u> </u>from Defenders of Wildlife, a<u> </u>Washington, D.C.-based conservation organization. This included nearly 55,000 live animals and three million pounds of wildlife products.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Alejandra Goyenechea, senior international counsel for Defenders of Wildlife and a co-author of a <a href=\"http://www.defenders.org/sites/default/files/publications/combating-wildlife-trafficking-from-latin-america-to-the-united-states-and-what-we-can-do-to-address-it.pdf\">comprehensive report</a> about the trade published the year before, says the findings represent \u201cjust the tip of the iceberg. We cannot imagine how much more is happening that we haven\u2019t caught,\u201d she says.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Latin America has the greatest wildlife diversity on Earth, but its animal populations have fallen by 83 percent since the 1970s\u2014the most drastic decline of wildlife in any global region\u2014according to the <a href=\"http://assets.worldwildlife.org/publications/723/files/original/WWF-LPR2014-low_res.pdf?1413912230&amp;_ga=1.196197612.1391626635.1488252309\">2014 Living Planet Report</a> by the Washington DC-based World Wildlife Fund. (<a href=\"http://news.nationalgeographic.com/2016/10/living-planet-index-world-lose-two-thirds-animals-2020-conservation-science/\">Read more: World to Lose Two-Thirds of Wild Animals by 2020?)</a></p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Nicholas Chavez, the southwest region special agent for the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, which is responsible for, among other things, enforcing the country\u2019s wildlife laws, oversees enforcement along most of the nearly 2,000-mile border with Mexico. The main entry points for smuggled wildlife are border cities such as El Paso, Texas, San Diego, California, Nogales, Arizona, and Laredo, Texas.</p></div>\n<div class=\"promo section   \n    \n\n</div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Chavez says the top confiscated live animals\u2014the majority destined for the pet trade\u2014are reptiles and parrots from countries throughout South America as well as Mexico. Small monkeys are also seized. Smuggled wildlife products are mostly leather wallets, boots, and belts made from the skins of caimans, alligators, and sea turtles. \u201cThere\u2019s a demand for exotic skin boots, and the more exotic, the more people want them,\u201d he says.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cComing from Mexico, there\u2019s a lot of vehicle traffic, and neither customs or us can catch everything.\u201d There aren\u2019t enough agents, Chavez adds, \u201cand it\u2019s all throughout the border.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Take Brownsville, Texas, a city with three bridges across the Rio Grande but only one full-time inspector and one agent based about 50 miles away who\u2019s responsible for about 325 miles of border between the Gulf of Mexico and Eagle Pass, Texas. El Paso has five inspectors and one agent covering the 450 miles of border that includes three bridges, a railroad, and the city\u2019s international airport.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>These few officers aren\u2019t solely responsible for policing the illegal trade, Chavez explains. In addition they must monitor all the animals and wildlife products coming in legally.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Most of the seized live animals are placed in rehabilitation facilities and zoos, and some are repatriated to their country of origin. The majority of confiscated animal products are sent to the <a href=\"https://www.fws.gov/wildliferepository/\">National Wildlife Property Repository</a> in Denver, but a number go to universities and museums for their educational value.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2><b>HOW THE LEGAL TRADE WORKS</b></h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Trade of animals, plants and wildlife products between countries is regulated by the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), a treaty joined by 183 governments around the world.</p></div>\n<div class=\"image parbase section\">\n      \t\t\n\n\n\n\n      \t\t\n\n\n\n\n\n      \t\t\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n</div>\n<div class=\"image parbase class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>According to Goyenechea, many of the popular animals and wildlife items\u2014the parrots, the caiman shoes, the crocodile boots\u2014coming legally into the U.S. from Latin America are classified as CITES Appendix II species. This means they require a permit from the country of export, and each country has a quota\u2014a set number of annual permits\u2014for each species to ensure its population remains sustainable.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Any trade outside this legal system has the potential to put wild populations in jeopardy, Goyenechea says.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>And, she adds, animals traded by smugglers are more likely to suffer, even die, during transport. \u201cBy hiding them, they put them in worse conditions. Of course there\u2019s always going to be a risk by trading live animals, but it\u2019s worse when they\u2019re traded illegally.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2><b>TO CATCH A SMUGGLER</b></h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>In 2016 Congress passed the Eliminate, Neutralize, and Disrupt (END) Wildlife Trafficking Act, which aims to combat trafficking and poaching globally. Among other things, the law elevates wildlife trafficking to the same offense level as drug and arms trafficking and gives prosecutors more tools to go after individuals involved in wildlife crimes.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>One commonly cited limitation of the act is that it doesn\u2019t restrict intrastate trade of illegal wildlife parts and products. So some states are taking matters into their own hands.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>New Mexico, which shares a border with Texas at El Paso, one of the top border cities for illegal wildlife shipments, has no legislation to address wildlife trafficking. So Sen. Mimi Stewart (D-Albuquerque) recently introduced the <a href=\"https://www.nmlegis.gov/Sessions/17%20Regular/bills/senate/SB0081JUS.pdf\">New Mexico Wildlife Trafficking Act</a>, which would impose both civil and criminal penalties. Offenders would be charged with a misdemeanor and subject to a maximum fine of $25,000, or three times the value of the smuggled item, whichever\u2019s greater.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Stewart says she wants to discourage the trade by hitting traffickers where it hurts\u2014their pocketbooks. \u201cIt\u2019s really become incumbent upon the states to do the enforcing. I think we need some added tools for our state and national law enforcement to deal with this,\u201d she says of the new legislation.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Arizona is one of nine states that have already introduced similar legislation in 2017. Most states, Stewart says, focus on rhino horn and elephant ivory, and California is the only border state with multispecies legislation against wildlife trafficking.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Chavez cautions against over-reliance on legislation to solve the problem. \u201cWe\u2019ve had the <a href=\"https://www.fws.gov/international/laws-treaties-agreements/us-conservation-laws/lacey-act.html\">Lacey Act</a> on the books for well over a hundred years, and there\u2019s always a smuggling case.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The Lacey Act, passed in 1900 as the first federal law to protect wildlife, prohibits the trade of illegally sourced wildlife, fish, and plants, and it allows the U.S. to enforce the wildlife laws of the country of origin.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>According to Chavez, the Lacey Act\u2019s main weakness is the difficulty of enforcing another country\u2019s laws with cooperation from its government. And foreign laws, or interpretation of those laws, may change. In addition, at times a foreign government may not be able to provide witnesses or other necessary support for a case.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Educating consumers is \u201cmaybe a better option than putting another law on the books,\u201d Chavez says.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2><b>RAISING CONSCIOUSNESS</b></h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service is partnering with the <a href=\"http://uswta.org/\">U.S. Wildlife Trafficking Alliance</a>\u2014a private-public partnership working with government entities\u2014to provide travelers and travel industry leaders with <a href=\"http://uswta.org/buyinformed/\">educational resources</a> that can be used to help fight the illegal wildlife trade. Resources include pamphlets, public service announcements, and infographics. In November 2016 U.S.-based JetBlue airlines, a member of the alliance, began airing a <a href=\"https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=awpRoOUbSLg\">short film</a> on its flights about \u201cbuying informed\u201d when visiting countries in the Caribbean. (<a href=\"http://news.nationalgeographic.com/2016/03/160303-wildlife-trafficking-internet-businesses/\">Read more: JetBlue, Etsy, and Others Want to Help Stop Wildlife Crime.</a>)</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cGovernments and organizations around the globe are collaborating in unprecedented ways to combat wildlife trafficking,\u201d said Bryan Arroyo, assistant director of the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service\u2019s International Affairs Program in a <a href=\"http://uswta.org/traveltraderelease2017/\">January press release</a>. \u201cPowerful as these efforts are, the real power lies in the hands of consumers. Educating travelers so they don\u2019t unwittingly contribute to the poaching and wildlife trafficking epidemic is vital to ending this grave threat to our planet\u2019s most precious legacy.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Goyenechea, of Defenders of Wildlife, agrees. \u201cAs consumers, we\u2019re the ones driving the demand,\u201d she says. \u201cIf [traffickers] see consumers aren\u2019t purchasing illegal items, the demand decreases. We\u2019re the only ones that can change that, and we need to be aware.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<h2><b>TRUMP ADMINISTRATION\u2019S ROLE UNCLEAR</b></h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cI think the government has realized that the United States is a major player as a consumer, has taken major steps, and has been an example to the rest of the world,\u201d Goyenechea says. \u201cI just hope the new administration will support the measures that have been adopted.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Sen. Chris Coons (D-Delaware) co-sponsored the END Wildlife Trafficking Act in the Senate and says he\u2019s hopeful that President Trump and his team will continue the fight against wildlife trafficking, which he calls a bipartisan issue.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>\u201cThe fact that President Trump\u2019s executive order on transnational organized crime included wildlife crime shows the issue is on the administration\u2019s radar, but it\u2019s up to Congress to hold the administration accountable and make sure the American people recognize that wildlife trafficking and other forms of natural resource exploitation are serious threats to international stability and security,\u201d he said in an e-mail.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Bob Dreher, Defenders of Wildlife\u2019s senior vice president for conservation programs, agrees, adding that it\u2019s still a bit early to speculate on how the administration may confront wildlife trafficking. But he thinks it\u2019s possible that the issue will carry weight with the administration in its fight to tighten border security.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Nevertheless, Dreher says proposed budget cuts across federal agencies such as the Department of Justice and the Department of the Interior\u2014the umbrella agency of the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service\u2014could have \u201ccrippling effects on our ability to fight wildlife crime. One of the things we have to say we\u2019re worried about is that they\u2019ll be starved for funding.\u201d (<a href=\"http://news.nationalgeographic.com/2016/11/wildlife-watch-trump-wildlife-trafficking-animal-conservation/\">Read more: How Wildlife May Fare Under Trump</a>.)</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Because the president has yet to speak out on the issue, Dreher says, \u201cI think this is really kind of uncharted territory.\u201d</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p><a href=\"http://www.tinadeines.com/\"><i>Tina Deines</i></a><i> is a freelance journalist based in Albuquerque, New Mexico.</i></p></div>\n\n</div>\n\n More than a quarter of the illegal wildlife products seized at U.S. ports from 2005 through 2014 originated in Latin America.\n\nPhotograph by Eric Gay, Getty Images\n\nTraffickers from Latin America are desperate to get their product into the United States\u2014so desperate they\u2019ll risk the dangers of swimming across the Rio Grande with their contraband. But it\u2019s not meth and it\u2019s not cocaine. In one case, according to a special agent, it was about 25 parrots destined to be sold as pets. Smugglers used tire inner tubes to keep the box of birds afloat during the river crossing.\n\nThe southern border of the U.S. is a hot zone for wildlife smuggling, and while President Trump has vowed to stop illegal immigration from Mexico, it\u2019s unclear what he intends to do to thwart the flow of illicit wildlife.\n\nOf the nearly 50,000 illegal shipments of wildlife and wildlife products seized at ports of entry from 2005 through 2014, more than a quarter originated in Latin America, according to a 2016 fact sheet from Defenders of Wildlife, a Washington, D.C.-based conservation organization. This included nearly 55,000 live animals and three million pounds of wildlife products.\n\nAlejandra Goyenechea, senior international counsel for Defenders of Wildlife and a co-author of a comprehensive report about the trade published the year before, says the findings represent \u201cjust the tip of the iceberg. We cannot imagine how much more is happening that we haven\u2019t caught,\u201d she says.\n\nLatin America has the greatest wildlife diversity on Earth, but its animal populations have fallen by 83 percent since the 1970s\u2014the most drastic decline of wildlife in any global region\u2014according to the 2014 Living Planet Report by the Washington DC-based World Wildlife Fund. (Read more: World to Lose Two-Thirds of Wild Animals by 2020?)\n\nNicholas Chavez, the southwest region special agent for the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, which is responsible for, among other things, enforcing the country\u2019s wildlife laws, oversees enforcement along most of the nearly 2,000-mile border with Mexico. The main entry points for smuggled wildlife are border cities such as El Paso, Texas, San Diego, California, Nogales, Arizona, and Laredo, Texas.\n\nChavez says the top confiscated live animals\u2014the majority destined for the pet trade\u2014are reptiles and parrots from countries throughout South America as well as Mexico. Small monkeys are also seized. Smuggled wildlife products are mostly leather wallets, boots, and belts made from the skins of caimans, alligators, and sea turtles. \u201cThere\u2019s a demand for exotic skin boots, and the more exotic, the more people want them,\u201d he says.\n\n\u201cComing from Mexico, there\u2019s a lot of vehicle traffic, and neither customs or us can catch everything.\u201d There aren\u2019t enough agents, Chavez adds, \u201cand it\u2019s all throughout the border.\u201d\n\nTake Brownsville, Texas, a city with three bridges across the Rio Grande but only one full-time inspector and one agent based about 50 miles away who\u2019s responsible for about 325 miles of border between the Gulf of Mexico and Eagle Pass, Texas. El Paso has five inspectors and one agent covering the 450 miles of border that includes three bridges, a railroad, and the city\u2019s international airport.\n\nThese few officers aren\u2019t solely responsible for policing the illegal trade, Chavez explains. In addition they must monitor all the animals and wildlife products coming in legally.\n\nMost of the seized live animals are placed in rehabilitation facilities and zoos, and some are repatriated to their country of origin. The majority of confiscated animal products are sent to the National Wildlife Property Repository in Denver, but a number go to universities and museums for their educational value.\n\nHOW THE LEGAL TRADE WORKS\n\nTrade of animals, plants and wildlife products between countries is regulated by the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), a treaty joined by 183 governments around the world.\n\nView Images Parrots from South America are among the most popular live animals smuggled into the U.S. from Mexico.\n\nPhotograph by Joel Sartore, National Geographic Photo Ark\n\nAccording to Goyenechea, many of the popular animals and wildlife items\u2014the parrots, the caiman shoes, the crocodile boots\u2014coming legally into the U.S. from Latin America are classified as CITES Appendix II species. This means they require a permit from the country of export, and each country has a quota\u2014a set number of annual permits\u2014for each species to ensure its population remains sustainable.\n\nAny trade outside this legal system has the potential to put wild populations in jeopardy, Goyenechea says.\n\nAnd, she adds, animals traded by smugglers are more likely to suffer, even die, during transport. \u201cBy hiding them, they put them in worse conditions. Of course there\u2019s always going to be a risk by trading live animals, but it\u2019s worse when they\u2019re traded illegally.\u201d\n\nTO CATCH A SMUGGLER\n\nIn 2016 Congress passed the Eliminate, Neutralize, and Disrupt (END) Wildlife Trafficking Act, which aims to combat trafficking and poaching globally. Among other things, the law elevates wildlife trafficking to the same offense level as drug and arms trafficking and gives prosecutors more tools to go after individuals involved in wildlife crimes.\n\nOne commonly cited limitation of the act is that it doesn\u2019t restrict intrastate trade of illegal wildlife parts and products. So some states are taking matters into their own hands.\n\nNew Mexico, which shares a border with Texas at El Paso, one of the top border cities for illegal wildlife shipments, has no legislation to address wildlife trafficking. So Sen. Mimi Stewart (D-Albuquerque) recently introduced the New Mexico Wildlife Trafficking Act, which would impose both civil and criminal penalties. Offenders would be charged with a misdemeanor and subject to a maximum fine of $25,000, or three times the value of the smuggled item, whichever\u2019s greater.\n\nStewart says she wants to discourage the trade by hitting traffickers where it hurts\u2014their pocketbooks. \u201cIt\u2019s really become incumbent upon the states to do the enforcing. I think we need some added tools for our state and national law enforcement to deal with this,\u201d she says of the new legislation.\n\nArizona is one of nine states that have already introduced similar legislation in 2017. Most states, Stewart says, focus on rhino horn and elephant ivory, and California is the only border state with multispecies legislation against wildlife trafficking.\n\nChavez cautions against over-reliance on legislation to solve the problem. \u201cWe\u2019ve had the Lacey Act on the books for well over a hundred years, and there\u2019s always a smuggling case.\u201d\n\nThe Lacey Act, passed in 1900 as the first federal law to protect wildlife, prohibits the trade of illegally sourced wildlife, fish, and plants, and it allows the U.S. to enforce the wildlife laws of the country of origin.\n\nAccording to Chavez, the Lacey Act\u2019s main weakness is the difficulty of enforcing another country\u2019s laws with cooperation from its government. And foreign laws, or interpretation of those laws, may change. In addition, at times a foreign government may not be able to provide witnesses or other necessary support for a case.\n\nEducating consumers is \u201cmaybe a better option than putting another law on the books,\u201d Chavez says.\n\nRAISING CONSCIOUSNESS\n\nThe U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service is partnering with the U.S. Wildlife Trafficking Alliance\u2014a private-public partnership working with government entities\u2014to provide travelers and travel industry leaders with educational resources that can be used to help fight the illegal wildlife trade. Resources include pamphlets, public service announcements, and infographics. In November 2016 U.S.-based JetBlue airlines, a member of the alliance, began airing a short film on its flights about \u201cbuying informed\u201d when visiting countries in the Caribbean. (Read more: JetBlue, Etsy, and Others Want to Help Stop Wildlife Crime.)\n\n\u201cGovernments and organizations around the globe are collaborating in unprecedented ways to combat wildlife trafficking,\u201d said Bryan Arroyo, assistant director of the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service\u2019s International Affairs Program in a January press release. \u201cPowerful as these efforts are, the real power lies in the hands of consumers. Educating travelers so they don\u2019t unwittingly contribute to the poaching and wildlife trafficking epidemic is vital to ending this grave threat to our planet\u2019s most precious legacy.\u201d\n\nGoyenechea, of Defenders of Wildlife, agrees. \u201cAs consumers, we\u2019re the ones driving the demand,\u201d she says. \u201cIf [traffickers] see consumers aren\u2019t purchasing illegal items, the demand decreases. We\u2019re the only ones that can change that, and we need to be aware.\u201d\n\nTRUMP ADMINISTRATION\u2019S ROLE UNCLEAR\n\n\u201cI think the government has realized that the United States is a major player as a consumer, has taken major steps, and has been an example to the rest of the world,\u201d Goyenechea says. \u201cI just hope the new administration will support the measures that have been adopted.\u201d\n\nSen. Chris Coons (D-Delaware) co-sponsored the END Wildlife Trafficking Act in the Senate and says he\u2019s hopeful that President Trump and his team will continue the fight against wildlife trafficking, which he calls a bipartisan issue.\n\n\u201cThe fact that President Trump\u2019s executive order on transnational organized crime included wildlife crime shows the issue is on the administration\u2019s radar, but it\u2019s up to Congress to hold the administration accountable and make sure the American people recognize that wildlife trafficking and other forms of natural resource exploitation are serious threats to international stability and security,\u201d he said in an e-mail.\n\nBob Dreher, Defenders of Wildlife\u2019s senior vice president for conservation programs, agrees, adding that it\u2019s still a bit early to speculate on how the administration may confront wildlife trafficking.
