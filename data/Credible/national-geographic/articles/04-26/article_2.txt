What\u2019s the new evidence being presented?</b></h2></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>To start, the wear features on the rocks match what one would expect from stone tools, specifically those used for smashing up bones, says research team member <a href=\"http://cas.uow.edu.au/members/UOW094304.html\">Richard Fullagar</a>, an archaeologist at Australia\u2019s University of Wollongong.</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>There\u2019s also the matter of the stones\u2019 placement. The site was entombed in siltstone, a type of sedimentary rock that forms from fine-grained sediments\u2014the sort that would settle out only in very slow-moving, low-energy water. But at least four sister species were living in East Asia around the time, and three would be contenders for crossing into the Americas. (The fourth is <i>Homo floresiensis</i>, the much-ballyhooed \u201chobbits\u201d of Indonesia\u2019s island of Flores\u2014but they\u2019re probably not involved.)</p></div>\n<div class=\"parbase smartbody section text\">\n<p>Might the tool users have been <i>Homo erectus</i>, our direct ancestors and the earliest known fire-starters? But as Dem\u00e9r\u00e9 and their colleagues tell it, their evidence\u2014a mastodon skeleton, bone flakes, and several large stones\u2014shows that the area was a \u201cbone quarry,\u201d where an unknown hominin allegedly smashed fresh mastodon bones with stone hammers, perhaps to extract marrow or to mine the skeleton for raw materials.\n\nHowever, many of the world\u2019s leading experts in American archaeology already have expressed some form of skepticism to the paper\u2019s claims. Some have rejected it outright.\n\n\u201cThe earliest occupation of the Americas is a highly contentious subject,\u201d says University of Southampton archaeologist John McNabb. \u201cThe date of the find at 130,000 years ago is a really big ask for archaeologists who are used to talking about 12, 13, 14,000 years ago. Richard Cerutti, the San Diego Natural History Museum field paleontologist who was on call at the time, flagged the site for follow-up.\n\nHe and Dem\u00e9r\u00e9 then blocked off the area and excavated it, digging down to material undisturbed by the heavy machinery overhead. (Dem\u00e9r\u00e9 received a grant from the National Geographic Society in January 1993 to support emergency excavations of the site.)\n\nThe site struck Dem\u00e9r\u00e9 as unusual from the beginning, given the large rocks associated with the site, apparent concentrations of bone flakes around the largest stones, and the mastodon remains\u2019 odd arrangement. One of its tusks, for example, was embedded vertically in the dirt.\n\nWait, the site was found in 1992 and excavated in 1993 ... why is it only being announced now?\n\nThe main delay came from the sheer difficulty in accurately dating the site. It wasn\u2019t until 2011 and 2012 that Jim Paces of the U.S. Geological Survey could provide state-of-the-art ages for the mastodon bones, based on the relative amounts of uranium and thorium within them.\n\n\n\nDem\u00e9r\u00e9, a paleontologist by training, also had a lot of other projects on his plate. After writing a brief report on the site in 1995 for the California Department of Transportation, he turned his attention to his other research projects, including the evolution of baleen whales.\n\nThe first Americans: Clues to an ancient migration By Nature Video\n\n\n\nIn the meantime, a small band of online commenters had seized upon his 1995 report as a sign that evidence on the peopling of the Americas was being \u201csuppressed.\u201d Dem\u00e9r\u00e9 appears surprised and confused by the charge. \u201cNobody ever said, Forget this ever happened,\u201d he says. \u201cThat\u2019s just baloney.\u201d\n\nOK. What\u2019s the new evidence being presented?\n\nTo start, the wear features on the rocks match what one would expect from stone tools, specifically those used for smashing up bones, says research team member Richard Fullagar, an archaeologist at Australia\u2019s University of Wollongong.\n\nThere\u2019s also the matter of the stones\u2019 placement. The site was entombed in siltstone, a type of sedimentary rock that forms from fine-grained sediments\u2014the sort that would settle out only in very slow-moving, low-energy water. If water didn\u2019t move the rocks there, then perhaps people carried them to the site.\n\nIn addition, fractures on the mastodon bones suggest they were broken while fresh\u2014and the researchers say they couldn\u2019t have been smashed by natural processes. The skeleton likely were not trampled by other large creatures, the team argues, since some of the mastodon\u2019s more fragile bones\u2014such as its ribs and vertebrae\u2014are less shattered than the sturdy limb bones. Nor could smaller animals have done it, the team argues, because scavenging carnivores can\u2019t chew their way through the middle of a fresh mastodon femur.\n\nThe team also provided experimental evidence that hammerstones and anvils make similar fracture patterns in fresh elephant bones, suggesting by analogy that such a process could have been at work at the Cerutti site.\n\nSounds intriguing! Nor does it fully rule out the possibility that the wear patterns on the stones were a result of rocks bumping against one another in a stream, he says.\n\n\n\n\u201cWhen you put the total package together, there\u2019s certainly more evidence to reject [the study] than accept it,\u201d Dillehay says.\n\nIt\u2019s not impossible that human history in the Americas is older than currently thought, says Southern Methodist University archaeologist and National Geographic grantee David Meltzer, an expert on early Americans.\n\n\u201cBut to prove it, you cannot take broken bones and nondescript stones to make the case, not without demonstrating that nature could not have broken those bones and modified those stones,\u201d he says.\n\nAndy Hemmings, the lead archaeologist at a site of ancient human habitation in Florida called Old Vero, agrees. \u201cWe just have to accept that the stones were carried in, [but] from where, and by whom? In particular, one of the mastodon\u2019s teeth is shattered for no obvious reason.\n\nView Images San Diego Natural History Museum paleontologist Don Swanson points at a rock fragment near a large mastodon tusk fragment.\n\nPhotograph by San Diego Natural History Museum\n\n\u201cEverything that\u2019s broken was still there, so it wasn\u2019t mined for tools, and you\u2019re certainly not getting marrow out of the bone of a mastodon tooth,\u201d he says. \u201cSo what exactly is supposed to have gone on?\u201d\n\nIf the stones are tools, though, does that cinch the case for humans?\n\nNot necessarily. Some Clovis-first holdouts still don\u2019t buy it.\n\nDramatically extending the timeline for humans in the Americas makes the Cerutti claim even more contentious.
