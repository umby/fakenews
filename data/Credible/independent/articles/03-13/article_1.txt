<div class=\"main-content-column\">\n      <div class=\"text-wrapper\" itemprop=\"articleBody\">\n        <p>Theresa May has been given the green light to start the formal Brexit process as MPs and Lords passed the Article 50 bill \u2013 but her victory was overshadowed by Nicola Sturgeon's shock demand for a second referendum on Scottish independence.</p>\n\n<p>The Prime Minister finally won her long Parliamentary battle to trigger the Article 50 exit notice on her terms, when a threatened Conservative revolt in the Commons melted away.</p>\n\n<p>Two Lords amendments \u2013 guaranteeing the rights of 3 million EU nationals in Britain, and giving MPs a \u2018meaningful vote\u2019 on the outcome of the negotiations \u2013 were thrown out with large majorities.</p>\n\n<div 50</a></li>\n</ul></div>\n</div>\n\n<p>It paved the way for the Article 50 Bill to be given Royal Assent early on Tuesday, after peers in the upper chamber also caved in, rather than deciding to \u2018ping\u2019 it back to the Commons in a series of late-night votes.</p>\n\n<p>However, earlier in the day Ms May was rocked by the SNP First Minister\u2019s referendum demands, signalling a fresh constitutional battle over Scotland\u2019s future.</p>\n\n<p>Accusing the Prime Minister of blocking Scotland\u2019s desire for a special deal with the EU, Ms Sturgeon said the referendum should be held between autumn 2018 and spring 2019 \u2013 without last-minute concessions.</p>\n\n<p>Within hours, No.10 announced Ms May would not, as widely expected, trigger Article 50 as early as Tuesday, instead suggesting it would take place in the final week of March.</p>\n\n<p>Downing Street denied any wobble, or that the Prime Minister did not want to appear to be immediately confronting the SNP, with the union so obviously at stake.</p>\n\n<p>Her spokesman insisted the stated policy had always been to start Brexit \u201cat the end of March\u201d, but many senior figures in Europe had also anticipated Article 50 to be invoked this week.</p>\n\n<p>There were also suggestions that Cabinet ministers were still yet to agree on the exact form of the letter to be sent to the European Commission.</p>\n\n<p>The day of drama began when Ms Sturgeon called a press conference to warn that the Prime Minister\u2019s Brexit strategy made a second referendum all but inevitable.</p>\n\n<div class=\"inline-block inline-block-related-single inline-block-left\" data-nid=\"/news/uk/politics/article-50-trigger-brexit-leave-eu-trade-talks-deal-customs-union-single-market-a7627971.html\"><a May of refusing to discuss full Scottish access to the single market, saying: \u201cThe UK government has not moved even an inch in pursuit of compromise and agreement.\u201d</p>\n\n<p>Buoyed by opinion polls putting the Yes vote almost neck and neck with No supporters, Ms Sturgeon added. \u201cOur efforts at compromise have instead been met with a brick wall of intransigence.\u201d</p>\n\n<p>Theresa May immediately accused the SNP First Minister of playing a \u201cgame\u201d, but \u2013 significantly \u2013 did not rule out allowing the referendum to go ahead.</p>\n\n<p>Under Section 30 of the Scotland Act, the UK Parliament must grant Scotland the power. Many observers believe Ms May cannot simply say no \u2013 but could try to prevent the referendum until after Brexit is completed.</p>\n\n<p>Meanwhile, in the Commons, just two Conservatives \u2013 Alex Chalk and Tania Mathias \u2013 rebelled in the vote on unilaterally guaranteeing the rights of EU nationals ahead of the Brexit talks.</p>\n\n<p>It allowed the Government to win that vote easily, by 335-287 votes, a majority of 48 \u2013 larger than when MPs first voted on the controversy.</p>\n\n<p>Just hours later the Lords then backed down on the Liberal Democrat amendment as peers voted by 274 to 135 to disregard the motion. One Labour peer Lord Lea described the Brexit Bill during the evening as the \u201cshortest suicide note in history\u201d.</p>\n\n<p>Brexit Secretary David Davis insisted the UK would reach a \u201cswift\u201d agreement over the one million UK nationals living in EU member states and the 3.2 million EU citizens living in Britain.</p>\n\n<p>Later, the Commons threw out the second amendment passed by Lords, designed to ensure Parliament has the final say over the nature of Britain\u2019s withdrawal from the EU.</p>\n\n<p>No Tories voted with the Opposition parties, despite Mr Davis failing to offer any concessions on the vote on any final deal \u2013 or, crucially, if Ms May fails to strike a deal.</p>\n\n<p>Dominic Grieve, the former Attorney General, was among a handful of
