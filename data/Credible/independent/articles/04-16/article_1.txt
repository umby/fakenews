<p>Thousands of asylum seekers and migrants have been wrongly denied NHS healthcare, in some cases being refused treatment despite suffering from life-threatening conditions such as cancer and heart disease, doctors have warned.</p>\n\n<p>Undocumented migrants with urgent care needs have been refused treatment by hospitals, despite NHS guidelines that state no one should be refused urgent or immediately necessary treatment because they cannot pay, <em>The Independent</em> has learned. A large number of migrants have also been wrongly turned away from registering at GP surgeries on the grounds that they did not provide identification documents.</p>\n\n<p>In other cases, <em>The Independent</em> has seen emails showing asylum seekers who have the right to free healthcare have been wrongly charged for hospital treatment, sometimes up to thousands of pounds each.</p>\n\n<div class=\"relatedlinkslist inline-block-related-list inline-block-left\">\n<h2 class=\"box-title\">Read more</h2>\n\n<div class=\"relatedlinkslist-content\" style=\"min-height: 10px;\">\n<ul class=\"related\"><li><a data-author=\"May Bulman...\" data-nid=\"7666491\" data-publish_date=\"04-04-2017\" href=\"/news/uk/home-news/asylum-seekers-uk-hiding-identities-to-avoid-discrimination-a7666491.html\">Asylum seekers in UK 'hiding their identities to avoid discrimination'</a></li>\n\t<li><a data-author=\"Will Gore...\" data-nid=\"7665161\" data-publish_date=\"03-04-2017\" href=\"/voices/croydon-attack-asylum-seeker-brexit-made-attacks-like-this-likely-a7665161.html\">Sadly, Brexit has made attacks like the one in Croydon more likely </a></li>\n\t<li><a data-author=\"Niamh McIntyre...\" data-nid=\"7637801\" data-publish_date=\"19-03-2017\" href=\"/news/uk/home-news/government-support-asylum-seekers-home-office-amber-rudd-section-95-immigration-refugees-poverty-a7637801.html\">Government keeps asylum seekers \u2018below subsistence levels\u2019 on \u00a35 a day</a></li>\n</ul></div>\n</div>\n\n<p>The erroneous refusals and charges come amid a crackdown on the cost of overseas visitors using the NHS. A memorandum of understanding published in January stated that NHS digital was required to share patient information with the Home Office, while new measures that came in at the start of April require hospitals to check whether patients are eligible for free care on the NHS before providing most kinds of care.</p>\n\n<p>Doctors and campaigners have warned these changes are increasingly discouraging people from accessing healthcare, with potentially fatal consequences.</p>\n\n<p>Doctors of the World, which runs clinics for undocumented migrants and asylum seekers in the UK, said more than a fifth (22 per cent) of its patients are wrongly turned away from an NHS GP or hospital because they did not have ID or address documents.<span style=\"line-height: 1.6;\">\u00a0</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">The charity, which saw 1,758\u00a0</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">people</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">\u00a0</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">at its UK clinics last year, warned that the cases it encounters\u00a0are\u00a0the \"tip of the iceberg\", with\u00a0</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\"></span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">thousands of</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">\u00a0vulnerable\u00a0</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">people</span><span style=\"line-height: 20.8px;\">\u00a0across the UK also being\u00a0affected\u00a0but unable to\u00a0access\u00a0secondary support.</span></p>\n\n<p>In one case seen by the charity, a man suffering from a heart condition was refused treatment by a hospital despite being in a \u201chigh risk\u201d condition. Another male asylum seeker, who had been in the country for more than 10 years, was refused treatment for kidney cancer. In both cases, Doctors of the World intervened and ultimately had to involved solicitors, at which point the hospitals agreed to treat the patients.</p>\n\n<p>Phil Murwill, Doctors of the World\u2019s UK programme manager of services, said: \u201cThe rules are very clear that these men should have been treated. Either the hospital somehow didn\u2019t consider it to be urgent or they just weren\u2019t applying to regulations that something that is urgent must be provided with care.</p>\n\n<p>\u201cIn the end we had to get some legal advice. Once the solicitors had written an enquiring letter to the hospital, the bills were written off. But there are probably lots of cases where no one is advocating for the person, and they accept it.</p>\n\n<p>\u201cWe\u2019ve seen quite a few cases like this where someone requires care that is urgent or immediately necessary and so should be provided, but the overseas team has had information from the Home Office saying this person isn\u2019t eligible for this care, and they\u2019ll pass it onto the clinician.</p>\n\n<div class=\"inline-block inline-block-related-single inline-block-left\" data-nid=\"/life-style/health-and-families/health-news/pregnant-women-nhs-care-maternity-immigration-illegal-childbirth-immigrant-customs-rights-eu-brexit-a7639426.html\"><a href=\"/life-style/health-and-families/health-news/pregnant-women-nhs-care-maternity-immigration-illegal-childbirth-immigrant-customs-rights-eu-brexit-a7639426.html\"><img src=\"https://static.independent.co.uk/s3fs-public/styles/readmore_card/public/thumbnails/image/2016/10/11/12/pregnant-woman.jpg\" /><div class=\"capsules\">\n<ul><li>\n\t<div class=\"label context-capsule\">Read more</div>\n\t</li>\n</ul></div>\n\n<div class=\"content\">\n<h2>Pregnant women 'too frightened' to seek NHS care due to legal status</h2>\n</div>\n</a></div>\n\n<p>\u201cAnd we know that this is the tip of hte iceberg and what we\u2019re seeing are not isolated cases, but situations that are happening across London and across the country. We are concerned that thousands of people across the UK are being affected by this.\u201d</p>\n\n<p>Clinical staff working in NHS hospitals warned \u00a0the new regulations that have come into force this month, which require hospitals to refuse treatment to undocumented patients unless their health needs are urgent or immediately necessary, could lead to an increase in these sorts of errors, with potentially fatal consequences.</p>\n\n<p>Erin Dexter, a fifth year medical student at UCL and a member of campaign group Docs Not Cops, who has been working in London hospitals, told <em>The Independent:</em> \u201cThere\u2019s going to be a lot of confusion about this. There will be misunderstandings about things or rules will be applied wrongly in some cases. Human bodies and illness are unpredictable things. You don\u2019t know when someone\u2019s relatively stable condition is going to rapidly deteriorate.\u00a0</p>\n\n<p>\u201cIf you don\u2019t treat someone\u2019s diabetes then they\u2019re not going to die in the next 24-hours, but there will be serious consequences of that in the longer term. Someone was recently told by a hospital in Sheffield that they weren\u2019t going to treat his stomach ulcer, but that he should come back when it bursts. Emergency services are so much more costly, and when people get sicker of course it\u2019s more expensive to then treat them. It doesn\u2019t make fiscal or moral sense.\u201d</p>\n\n<p><em>The Independent </em>has also found that hospital overseas departments have also charged asylum seekers up to thousands of pounds in bills for medical treatment NHS guidelines state they should be exempt from. One male asylum seeker received invoices for a bill amounting to \u00a32,205 for medical treatment he had on a broken knee cap, and it wasn't until an independent caseworker intervened that it transpired he was in the process of appealing his asylum claim, meaning he was exempt from the charges.</p>\n\n<p>An email exchange between the caseworker and the overseas department at Barking, Havering and Redbridge University Hospital Trust shows that after the caseworker said the man was in the process of appealing his asylum claim, the department conceded that he was exempt from the
