ahead of Labour in a new poll, giving the party their greatest lead while in government since 1983, shortly before Margaret Thatcher\u2019s second victory at the ballot box.</p>\n\n<p>The poll, conducted by ComRes for <em>The Independent</em>, gives the Tories 46 per cent of the vote share, 25 per cent for Labour, 11 per cent for Tim Farron\u2019s pro-EU Liberal Democrats and 9 per cent for the embattled Ukip under Paul Nuttall\u2019s leadership.</p>\n\n<p>According to historic data on the UK Polling Report, it is the highest lead for any party since May 2009, when three polls gave David Cameron a 22-point lead. Rarely has the party been fewer than 10 points behind their Conservative rivals in this period.</p>\n\n<p>But it also comes as further polling showed Mr Corbyn\u2019s policy blitz during the parliamentary recess \u2013 including free school meals for all children by forcing private schools to pay VAT \u2013 is supported by the majority of the British public. The poll also suggests that association with the leader has little, if any, impact on the public\u2019s perceptions of the Labour party\u2019s current policy agenda.</p>\n\n<p>Responding to the polls, the Labour leader told <em>The Independent</em>: \u201cOur policies are popular because to most people it\u2019s common sense that our Government should act in the interests of the overwhelming majority and that will mean taking on the powerful.</p>\n\n<p>\u201cThe people of\u00a0Britain have been held back too long, but we have the ideas and the will to change the country for the better. The poll suggests three in 10 of those who voted for Ukip at the 2015 general election would now vote for the Tories \u2013 a likely reflection of the Prime Minister\u2019s hard Brexit rhetoric. \u00a0</p>\n\n<p>A crucial test for Mr Corbyn\u2019s party will also come on 4 May, when voters will head to the polls in a series of local elections in England, Wales and Scotland. A party hoping to win power at Westminster should aim to gain significant ground in these contests. \u00a0</p>\n\n<p>But in an article for <em>The Independent</em>, John Curtice, a professor of politics at Strathclyde University who is considered one of Britain\u2019s top polling experts, said Labour and Ukip, not the Conservatives, appear most at risk of losing ground.</p>\n\n<p>Mr Curtice wrote: \u201cIf next month the Liberal Democrats succeed in repeating some of their more spectacular by-election advances, that might enable the party to begin to rebuild the local government base that five years of coalition managed to destroy. And any sign that its potential competitor on the centre-left of British politics is finally enjoying something of a revival could be the worst news of all for Jeremy Corbyn.\u201d</p>\n\n<p>The ComRes poll also discovered significant support for a new political party in Britain. Two in five of British adults, or 41 per cent, agree that there is space for a centre-ground party.
