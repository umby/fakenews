In a news conference Wednesday in New York City, the man who created the iconic bronze bull near the New York Stock Exchange said the statue of a young girl installed last month just a few feet away &mdash; staring boldly at his 7,100-pound beast &mdash; violates his artistic copyright.</p><p class=\"skip-nav article-more\">\n\t<a>Advertisement</a>\n\t</p>\n\n<div class=\"ad aside\" id=\"ad_bigbox1\"></div>  <a id=\"skip-target1\"></a>\n\n<p>\n<b class=\"b\"></b>&ldquo;The girl herself as a particular piece of sculpture is fine,&rsquo;&rsquo; Steven Hyman, a lawyer for Di Modica, said in a telephone interview. &ldquo;But using it with the bull changes the bull.&rdquo;</p><p>It&rsquo;s an odd notion &mdash; an artist outraged by a creative act. Yet the addition of &ldquo;Fearless Girl,&rdquo; a much-praised statue installed by a company promoting a fund that supports gender diversity, has turned into a legal battle pitting a 76-year-old artist&rsquo;s message, and ego, against public opinion and corporate interests.</p><div class=\"inline-newsletter\" data-exact-target-ID=\"20712964\" data-mailing-list-name=\"Talking Points\" data-is-amp=\"\" data-section=\"/business\">\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__network-error\"></div>\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__copy\">\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__title\">\n      Get <span class=\"inline-newsletter__bold\">Talking Points</span> in your inbox:\n    </div>\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__description\">\n        An afternoon recap of the day\u2019s most important business news, delivered weekdays.</div>\n    </div>\n  <form class=\"inline-newsletter__content\" method=\"post\">\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__error-text\"></div>\n    <input placeholder=\"Enter email address\" type=\"text\" id=\"inline-newsletter-email-input\" class=\"inline-newsletter__input\">\n\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__button-container\">\n      <button class=\"inline-newsletter__button\">\n        <span class=\"inline-newsletter__button-text\">Sign Up</span>\n      </button>\n      <div class=\"inline-newsletter__spinner\"></div>\n    </div>\n  </form>\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__thanks\">\n    Thank you for signing up!\n    <a href=\"/newsletters?p1=Article_inline-newsletterWidget_ThankYou_Link\" class=\"inline-newsletter__link\">Sign up for more newsletters here</a>\n    </div>\n</div>\n\n<p>Di Modica, an Italian who lives in New York and Sicily, created the bull after the 1987 stock market crash to show the &ldquo;strength and power of the American people,&rdquo; he has said. It was installed in the dead of night in 1989, in front of the stock exchange, and taken away that same day. It was later moved a few blocks away to a park, where it became an iconic piece, drawing tourists who flock to it for photos.</p><!-- total:0 -->\n\n<p>Last month, State Street Global Advisors, the $2.5 trillion investment arm of Boston&rsquo;s State Street Corp., took a page out of his book. The company installed the bronze statue by Kristen Visbal of &ldquo;Fearless Girl,&rdquo; hands on hips, under cover of night. Her arrival on March 7 was part of State Street&rsquo;s pressing companies to name more women directors, and to tout an investment fund that favors companies with diverse executive teams.</p><p>A day later, her image had gone viral on social media. The statue was supposed to be a temporary installation, but it will remain in place through February.</p><!--  working ad -->\n<p class=\"skip-nav article-more\">\n\t<a>Advertisement</a>\n\t<!-- <a href=\"#skip-target2\">Continue reading below</a> -->\n</p>\n<!-- adLayerPath: /Boston/Production/BostonGlobe.com/WebPages/Homepage/homepage.ad-layer.story-asset.dwp -->\n<div class=\"ad aside\" id=\"ad_bigbox2\"></div>  <a id=\"skip-target2\"></a>\n\n<p>&ldquo;They&rsquo;ve taken the bull and made it into their particular piece of art, and they can&rsquo;t do that without his permission,&rdquo; Hyman said.</p><p>He sent letters Wednesday to New York Mayor Bill de Blasio and to State Street and its New York advertising agency, urging removal of the statue and demanding financial damages.</p><figure class=\"figure\" itemtype=\"http://schema.org/ImageObject\" itemprop=\"associatedMedia\">\n<img alt=\"Arturo Di Modica holds a model of his Charging Bull sculpture during a news conference Wednesday, April 12, 2017, in New York. She suggested Di Modica would have done better to welcome &ldquo;Fearless Girl.&rdquo;</p><p>Laura Baring-Gould, a Somerville artist whose public artworks include the <a class=\"a\" href=\"http://www.publicartboston.com/content/dorchester-voices-dorchester-history\" shape=\"rect\">giant &ldquo;Clapp&rsquo;s Favorite Pear&rdquo;</a> in Dorchester, said she can understand Di Modica&rsquo;s frustration.</p><p>&ldquo;You put your heart and soul into something, then you have to let it be in the public and see what happens,&rsquo;&rsquo; she said.</p><p>A lot has happened since Di Modica felt the urge to shore up Wall Street&rsquo;s confidence in the late 1980s. We&rsquo;ve had the global financial crisis, followed by the Great Recession, and a divisive election. The statue of the girl facing the bull has clearly struck a chord, Baring-Gould said.</p><p>&ldquo;The public is experiencing a frustration with the political environment and the power structure,&rsquo;&rsquo; she said. &ldquo;It&rsquo;s exciting that the piece has unleashed that.&rdquo;</p><p>There&rsquo;s no question &ldquo;Fearless Girl&rdquo; has also been a public relations juggernaut for State Street Global. Twenty years after the firm was known for a macho culture in which <a class=\"a\" href=\"https://www.highbeam.com/doc/1P2-8485988.html\" shape=\"rect\">a pink shoe\n</a>\n was placed on the desks of men who wouldn&rsquo;t go out drinking with the guys, the investment manager won headlines for urging companies to hire more women executives and board members.</p><p>Its parent company, State Street Corp., has 11 directors, three of them women. But that doesn&rsquo;t impress Hyman, the lawyer for the sculptor who made the bull.  He accuses State Street of using the sculpture &ldquo;for commercial purposes.&rdquo; Indeed, the plaque near the statue at first cited the company&rsquo;s name and the SHE moniker for a State Street Global investment fund.</p><p>Both of those bits of promotional material have been removed, State Street spokeswoman Anne McNally said.</p><p>&ldquo;It was a brilliant ad campaign. We give them credit for that and wish them godspeed,&rsquo;&rsquo; Hyman said. &ldquo;Just leave the bull out.&rdquo;</p><p>State Street is reviewing the attorney&rsquo;s letter, McNally said.</p><p>&ldquo;We continue to be grateful to the City of New York and people around the world who have responded so enthusiastically to what the Fearless Girl represents,&rsquo;&rsquo; she said in a statement, &ldquo;the power and potential of having more women in leadership.&rdquo;</p><p>Di Modica, for his part, is not entirely against commercial pursuits. He has sold other versions of his bull celebrating capitalism, which are located in Amsterdam and Shanghai. He also has sued companies for allegedly using his bull image, including Walmart Stores Inc. and the publisher Random House.</p><p>So far, he has not filed suit against State Street or New York City. In a news conference Wednesday in New York City, the man who created the iconic bronze bull near the New York Stock Exchange said the statue of a young girl installed last month just a few feet away \u2014 staring boldly at his 7,100-pound beast \u2014 violates his artistic copyright.\n\nAdvertisement\n\n\u201cThe girl herself as a particular piece of sculpture is fine,\u2019\u2019 Steven Hyman, a lawyer for Di Modica, said in a telephone interview. \u201cBut using it with the bull changes the bull.\u201d\n\nIt\u2019s an odd notion \u2014 an artist outraged by a creative act. Yet the addition of \u201cFearless Girl,\u201d a much-praised statue installed by a company promoting a fund that supports gender diversity, has turned into a legal battle pitting a 76-year-old artist\u2019s message, and ego, against public opinion and corporate interests.\n\nGet Talking Points in your inbox: An afternoon recap of the day\u2019s most important business news, delivered weekdays. Sign up for more newsletters here\n\nDi Modica, an Italian who lives in New York and Sicily, created the bull after the 1987 stock market crash to show the \u201cstrength and power of the American people,\u201d he has said. It was installed in the dead of night in 1989, in front of the stock exchange, and taken away that same day. It was later moved a few blocks away to a park, where it became an iconic piece, drawing tourists who flock to it for photos.\n\nLast month, State Street Global Advisors, the $2.5 trillion investment arm of Boston\u2019s State Street Corp., took a page out of his book. The company installed the bronze statue by Kristen Visbal of \u201cFearless Girl,\u201d hands on hips, under cover of night. Her arrival on March 7 was part of State Street\u2019s pressing companies to name more women directors, and to tout an investment fund that favors companies with diverse executive teams.\n\nA day later, her image had gone viral on social media. The statue was supposed to be a temporary installation, but it will remain in place through February.\n\nAdvertisement\n\n\u201cThey\u2019ve taken the bull and made it into their particular piece of art, and they can\u2019t do that without his permission,\u201d Hyman said.\n\nHe sent letters Wednesday to New York Mayor Bill de Blasio and to State Street and its New York advertising agency, urging removal of the statue and demanding financial damages.\n\nAP Arturo Di Modica held a model of his \u201cCharging Bull\u201d sculpture.\n\nThe dueling statues have sparked passionate reactions, from hundreds of tweets defending \u201cFearless Girl\u201d to a New York art journal\u2019s calling the statue \u201cfake corporate feminism\u201d and \u201ceverything that\u2019s wrong with our society.\u201d\n\nDe Blasio, in a tweet on Wednesday, said, \u201cMen who don\u2019t like women taking up space are exactly why we need the Fearless Girl.\u201d\n\nNancy Sch\u00f6n, the West Newton artist who created the popular \u201cMake Way for Ducklings\u201d parade of ducks in Boston\u2019s Public Garden, is a big fan of \u201cFearless Girl.\u201d She said she can\u2019t relate to Di Modica\u2019s reaction.\n\n\u201cI\u2019m sorry he feels that way,\u2019\u2019 she said. \u201cThis is one of the most exciting pieces of sculpture out there, making a statement about what\u2019s happening today.\u201d\n\nSch\u00f6n was referring to the political climate, and the anger that inspired women\u2019s marches around the world after the US presidential election. She suggested Di Modica would have done better to welcome \u201cFearless Girl.\u201d\n\nLaura Baring-Gould, a Somerville artist whose public artworks include the giant \u201cClapp\u2019s Favorite Pear\u201d in Dorchester, said she can understand Di Modica\u2019s frustration.\n\n\u201cYou put your heart and soul into something, then you have to let it be in the public and see what happens,\u2019\u2019 she said.\n\nA lot has happened since Di Modica felt the urge to shore up Wall Street\u2019s confidence in the late 1980s. We\u2019ve had the global financial crisis, followed by the Great Recession, and a divisive election. The statue of the girl facing the bull has clearly struck a chord, Baring-Gould said.\n\n\u201cThe public is experiencing a frustration with the political environment and the power structure,\u2019\u2019 she said. \u201cIt\u2019s exciting that the piece has unleashed that.\u201d\n\nThere\u2019s no question \u201cFearless Girl\u201d has also been a public relations juggernaut for State Street Global. Twenty years after the firm was known for a macho culture in which a pink shoe was placed on the desks of men who wouldn\u2019t go out drinking with the guys, the investment manager won headlines for urging companies to hire more women executives and board members.\n\nIts parent company, State Street Corp., has 11 directors, three of them women. But that doesn\u2019t impress Hyman, the lawyer for the sculptor who made the bull. He accuses State Street of using the sculpture \u201cfor commercial purposes.\u201d Indeed, the plaque near the statue at first cited the company\u2019s name and the SHE moniker for a State Street Global investment fund.\n\nBoth of those bits of promotional material have been removed, State Street spokeswoman Anne McNally said.\n\n\u201cIt was a brilliant ad campaign. We give them credit for that and wish them godspeed,\u2019\u2019 Hyman said. \u201cJust leave the bull out.\u201d\n\nState Street is reviewing the attorney\u2019s letter, McNally said.\n\n\u201cWe continue to be grateful to the City of New York and people around the world who have responded so enthusiastically to what the Fearless Girl represents,\u2019\u2019 she said in a statement, \u201cthe power and potential of having more women in leadership.\u201d\n\nDi Modica, for his part, is not entirely against commercial pursuits. He has sold other versions of his bull celebrating capitalism, which are located in Amsterdam and Shanghai. He also has sued companies for allegedly using his bull image, including Walmart Stores Inc. and the publisher Random House.\n\nSo far, he has not filed suit against State Street or New York City.
