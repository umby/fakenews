Amjad Bahnassi, chairman of the society&rsquo;s Board of Trustees, said of the Worcester option.</p><p>The unexpected decision brings an abrupt end to a <a class=\"a\" href=\"https://www.bostonglobe.com/metro/2016/03/28/muslims-face-backlash-over-proposed-cemetery-dudley/awhGJrYq60mZGoDEbFWDJP/story.html\" shape=\"rect\">contentious, drawn-out process</a> in which Dudley officials and residents were accused of anti-Muslim bigotry, and the Islamic Society faced  allegations that the group did not negotiate in good faith.</p><div class=\"inline-newsletter\" data-exact-target-ID=\"20233035\" data-mailing-list-name=\"Fast Forward\" data-is-amp=\"\" data-section=\"/metro\">\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__network-error\"></div>\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__copy\">\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__title\">\n      Get <span class=\"inline-newsletter__bold\">Fast Forward</span> in your inbox:\n    </div>\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__description\">\n        Forget yesterday's news. Dudley zoning officials approved the cemetery in March while the Islamic Society continued behind-the-scenes discussions in Worcester to use part of Hope Cemetery there.</p><p>&ldquo;A few months ago, they approached us [in Worcester] and said would you be interested in having a piece,&rdquo; Bahnassi said. &ldquo;We said yes, and we started negotiations with them.&rdquo;</p><!--  working ad -->\n<p class=\"skip-nav article-more\">\n\t<a>Advertisement</a>\n\t<!-- <a href=\"#skip-target2\">Continue reading below</a> -->\n</p>\n<!-- adLayerPath: /Boston/Production/BostonGlobe.com/WebPages/Homepage/homepage.ad-layer.story-asset.dwp -->\n<div class=\"ad aside\" id=\"ad_bigbox2\"></div>  <a id=\"skip-target2\"></a>\n\n<p>Bahnassi said some logistics are being worked out, but that the society now intends to use the city-owned Hope Cemetery.</p><p>Bill Wallace, chairman of the Hope Cemetery Commission, confirmed the panel has been talking with the Islamic Society and has approved a preliminary agreement.</p><p>&ldquo;We&rsquo;re working on a master plan which does include appropriate burial space to meet the Islamic community&rsquo;s rituals and needs,&rdquo; Wallace said in a brief interview Thursday night.</p><p>The plan still would have to be approved by other city officials, Wallace added. </p><p>The Islamic Society had previously explored Dudley, in Central Massachusetts, as a more convenient option than its current burial ground in Enfield, Conn., more than 60 miles from Worcester. The project caused an uproar in the largely rural town, which seemed stunned by the prospect of a sprawling Muslim cemetery in its boundaries.</p><p>Bahnassi said that the society pushed forward there &mdash; despite ongoing talks in another community &mdash; partly to gain recognition of its constitutional rights to establish a religious cemetery in the town.</p><p id=\"U832212807868IAG\" style=\" letter-spacing: -26; ;\">Throughout the long road to approval, Dudley officials raised several bureaucratic hurdles to a 55-acre cemetery that some neighbors complained might contaminate their well water and bring traffic congestion.</p><p>&ldquo;We had to do this for our citizens, our children, and the next generation,&rdquo; Bahnassi said of the decision to fight on. &ldquo;We learned a lot. We learned that we have more to do to make people get to know us.&rdquo;</p><p>Bahnassi said another consideration was financial. Preparing 6 acres for burials, which the agreement allowed for the first decade, would cost $1.5 million, Bahnassi said. Even scaling back the cemetery to 2 acres would cost $600,000, he added. </p><p>In Worcester, costs will be incurred only for each burial, Bahnassi said.</p><p>The Islamic Society signed a purchase-and-sale agreement for about $300,000 for the Dudley property. But Bahnassi said local officials recently informed the society that the purchase must be completed before final approval, from the Board of Health, could be granted. </p><p>Given the convenience and affordability of Worcester, Bahnassi said, the society was not willing to take that risk. However, he thanked Dudley officials for the opportunity to apply for a cemetery.</p><p>Joseph said that the long process &mdash; which brought added legal expenses and often unflattering attention to Dudley &mdash; was &ldquo;unfortunate.&rdquo; However, he said he was grateful that the Islamic Society had found a site.</p><p>&ldquo;I said at the very first hearing that everyone has a right to bury their dead,&rdquo; Joseph said. &ldquo;I was not opposed to a cemetery of appropriate size. They are good, honest, and very likeable people.&rdquo;</p><p>The process, however, was often rancorous.</p><p>The US attorney&rsquo;s office in Boston, the state attorney general, and the American Civil Liberties Union all scrutinized the stops and starts on the bureaucratic trail that the Islamic Society found itself trying to navigate.</p><p> The agreement in March, apparently all but ensuring final approval by the Board of Health, seemed to bring closure.
