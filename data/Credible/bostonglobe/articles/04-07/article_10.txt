and choosing the suburbs over the city when they purchase a first home.</p><p>But the group of young adults born between the early 1980s and the mid-1990s aren&rsquo;t all taking the same path to the American dream.</p><p class=\"skip-nav article-more\">\n\t<a>Advertisement</a>\n\t</p>\n\n<div class=\"ad aside\" id=\"ad_bigbox1\"></div>  <a id=\"skip-target1\"></a>\n\n<p>Some move home after college, staying for several years &mdash; or longer &mdash; to launch careers, pay down debt, and save for a down payment. Others share apartments, deferring a bigger rite of passage for the more immediate gratification of living on their own.</p><p>But when they time comes, this generation does its homework and knows the drill. </p><div class=\"inline-newsletter\" data-exact-target-ID=\"18973750\" data-mailing-list-name=\"Today's Headlines\" data-is-amp=\"\" data-section=\"/metro/regionals/north\">\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__network-error\"></div>\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__copy\">\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__title\">\n      Get <span class=\"inline-newsletter__bold\">Today's Headlines</span> in your inbox:\n    </div>\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__description\">\n        The day's top stories delivered every morning</div>\n    </div>\n  <form class=\"inline-newsletter__content\" method=\"post\">\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__error-text\"></div>\n    <input placeholder=\"Enter email address\" type=\"text\" id=\"inline-newsletter-email-input\" class=\"inline-newsletter__input\">\n\n    <div class=\"inline-newsletter__button-container\">\n      <button class=\"inline-newsletter__button\">\n        <span class=\"inline-newsletter__button-text\">Sign Up</span>\n      </button>\n      <div class=\"inline-newsletter__spinner\"></div>\n    </div>\n  </form>\n  <div class=\"inline-newsletter__thanks\">\n    Thank you for signing up!\n    <a href=\"/newsletters?p1=Article_inline-newsletterWidget_ThankYou_Link\" class=\"inline-newsletter__link\">Sign up for more newsletters here</a>\n    </div>\n</div>\n\n<p>&ldquo;They know the market before they come through the door,&rdquo; says Nancy King, a realtor in Duxbury.</p><!-- total:0 -->\n\n<p>Meredith and Brian Bielecki, rented an apartment in Cambridge six years ago after moving to the city for Brian&rsquo;s job in financial services.</p><p>&ldquo;We didn&rsquo;t know the area and we wanted to save to buy a home in the suburbs,&rdquo; says Brian, 33. &ldquo;We got more serious after Meredith was pregnant with our first. We wanted to get out of the apartment into something bigger.&rdquo;</p><!--  working ad -->\n<p class=\"skip-nav article-more\">\n\t<a>Advertisement</a>\n\t<!-- <a href=\"#skip-target2\">Continue reading below</a> -->\n</p>\n<!-- adLayerPath: /Boston/Production/BostonGlobe.com/WebPages/Homepage/homepage.ad-layer.story-asset.dwp -->\n<div class=\"ad aside\" id=\"ad_bigbox2\"></div>  <a id=\"skip-target2\"></a>\n\n<p>What the couple wasn&rsquo;t expecting were the slim pickings and high prices, pushed even higher by bidding wars and cash offers. A home they fell in love with in Concord was sold to another buyer who was willing to up the ante beyond the Bieleckis&rsquo; $600,000 to $800,000 price range. Fixer-uppers with lower prices and less space did not appeal, even after Andrew Martini, their millennial realtor, had them meet with a contractor. Heartfelt letters to sellers explaining why they wanted the house had no effect.</p><p>The Bieleckis were looking for a home within walking distance of coffee shops, stores, and restaurants. But they soon realized they would need to compromise.</p><p>&ldquo;Location was most important,&rdquo; says Meredith, 34. &ldquo;We thought a minimum of three bedrooms would work, and we definitely wanted at least two bathrooms since we had always lived with just one. A good school district was always important as well. We only looked in a few towns.&rdquo;</p><p>Martini, their 30-year-old Concord realtor, suggested Carlisle, about 10 minutes by car from Concord but more rural, with a state park, an organic farm, and good schools. Then he helped them find the tan Colonial they moved into last June, a few months before the birth of their second child.</p><p>&ldquo;It&rsquo;s perfect for us,&rdquo; says Meredith.</p><p></p><div class=\"wide\">\n<img alt=\"\" data-fullsrc=\"//c.o0bg.com/rf/image_1920w/Boston/2011-2020/2017/03/27/BostonGlobe.com/Regional/Images/rathe_millennials_wew03.jpg\" src=\"//c.o0bg.com/rf/image_r/Boston/2011-2020/2017/03/27/BostonGlobe.com/Regional/Images/rathe_millennials_wew03.r.jpg\"><div class=\"figcaption\">\n<p class=\"credit\">Joanne Rathe/ Globe Staff </p>\n<p>Meredith and Brian Bielecki and their two children recently settled into a new home in Carlisle. </p>\n</div>\n</div><p></p><p>David Turcotte, a research professor and housing specialist in the department of economics at UMass Lowell, says while younger adults prefer the city, they can&rsquo;t afford to live there. So instead, they seek out surburbs that offer city amenities.</p><p>&ldquo;The interesting thing is millennials are the biggest category of homebuyers,&rdquo; Turcotte says. &ldquo;Once they&rsquo;re able and decide, they help drive the real estate markets.&rdquo;</p><div class=\"aside mod-a promo feat-pq\">\n<div class=\"box\">\n<blockquote>\n<p>&lsquo;The interesting thing is millennials are the biggest category of homebuyers. Once they&rsquo;re able and decide, they help drive the real estate markets.&rsquo;</p>\n</blockquote>\n<i class=\"cap\"><img alt=\"Quote Icon\" src=\"/rw/SysConfig/WebPortal/BostonGlobe/Framework/images/icon-promo-quote-red.gif\"></i>\n</div>\n</div><p>And when they find the house of their dreams, realtors say, millennials are realistic about how long they will stay.</p><p>&ldquo;When I first started out,&rdquo; says Concord realtor Carrie Hines, who has been selling houses for more than two decades, &ldquo;they&rsquo;d look at a house as if they were going to die there. Today, few buy and stay forever. They make more money and buy a bigger house. .&nbsp;.&nbsp;. They&rsquo;re more realistic about the evolution of our lives, that everything changes.&rdquo;</p><p>Lianna Kushi, 32, and her husband, Souvanna Pouv, 36, met in Lowell, where they are both executive directors of nonprofits. She runs a local incubator for entreprenuership; he is head of the Cambodian Mutual Assistance Association. They were married last year, are expecting their first child in the fall, and recently moved from a tiny apartment into a four-bedroom Victorian, purchased for $313,000 in the Highlands neighborhood in Lowell.</p><p>&ldquo;To be in our 30s and know folks in our generation are wanting to buy, but can&rsquo;t, we feel really lucky,&rdquo; says Kushi. &ldquo;We&rsquo;re happy and proud to have a home at what feels to us like a pretty young age.&rdquo;</p><p>Pouv, who was born in a refugee camp in Cambodia, and Kushi, a Smith College graduate who grew up in North Carolina, attended the <a class=\"a\" href=\"http://www.mvhp.org/\" shape=\"rect\" target=\"_blank\">Merrimack Valley Housing Partnership&rsquo;s</a> first-time homebuyers class. They found a mortgage program through the local credit union, and during an eight-month search, looked at 30 homes.</p><p>&ldquo;Souvanna and I are the oldest in our families. We have a sense of responsibility,&rdquo; says Kushi. &ldquo;We really want them to feel this is a place they can come to when it&rsquo;s needed. My dad used to joke that he didn&rsquo;t need a retirement plan. &lsquo;With six children,&rsquo; he said, &lsquo;One of them is bound to take you in,&rsquo; and I&rsquo;m the oldest.&rdquo;</p><p>Grace Harrington, a 28-year-old attorney who lives in Salem, bought her first home almost two years ago, a small condominium in the coastal city where she grew up. Now engaged and planning a wedding, Harrington says she and her fiance are talking about buying something bigger. </p><p>&ldquo;Because my mom is a realtor, she often talked about the value in &lsquo;paying to own&rsquo; versus &ldquo;paying to live.&rsquo; She stressed the importance of building equity, and both she and my dad encouraged me to stay home and save money in order to work towards buying,&rdquo; she says.</p><p>It took about five years for Harrington to save enough to finance her condo.</p><p>&ldquo;Four years of commuting in and out of Boston every day while working and going to [law] school was tiring, but I was also very lucky that I happened to live in a city that has proximity to Boston,&rdquo; she says. &ldquo;It was very feasible for me to live at home and commute.&rdquo;</p><p>Dracut resident Madeline Koufogazos, 24, moved home after graduating from UMass Lowell two years ago with a degree in English and a job with a Malden publishing company.</p><p>&ldquo;I&rsquo;m turning 25 in October, and I feel it&rsquo;s a good time to start looking,&rdquo; Koufogazos says. &ldquo;My goal now is to pay off student loans as quickly as possible, [plus a] car loan.&rdquo;</p><p>But living at home has given her a boost and reinforced what her parents taught her about managing her money. Meanwhile, she&rsquo;s considering her next move, possibly a shared apartment at <a class=\"a\" href=\"http://millno5.com/\" shape=\"rect\" target=\"_blank\">Mill No. 5</a> on Jackson Street in Lowell or a rental in Somerville, Allston, or Jamaica Plain.</p><p>&ldquo;I&rsquo;d like a place that&rsquo;s comfortable, a safe neighborhood, on the outskirts of Boston,&rdquo; she says.</p><p>In 2013, after graduating from Salem State University, Mary Bertrand, now 25, moved to Pennsylvania to work for <a class=\"a\" href=\"https://www.nationalservice.gov/programs/americorps\" shape=\"rect\" target=\"_blank\">AmeriCorps</a>, a one-year national service commitment. Afterward, she took a job with a nonprofit in Boston and moved into an apartment she shares in Quincy Center, not far from the train station.</p><p>&ldquo;About half of my friends live at home,&rdquo; said Bertrand. &ldquo;No one owns their own home or lives by themself.&rdquo;</p><p>Bertrand, who grew up in New Bedford, was impressed by the sea captains&rsquo; homes there and once imagined herself living in one.</p><p>But student debt and a modest salary have tempered that dream.</p><p>&ldquo;I&rsquo;d like to have a home that I can afford to live in,&rdquo; she says. &ldquo;Now, thinking about a house, I think a big house is a lot work, and I want just what I need, not a whole lot more.&rdquo;</p><p></p><h3 class=\"subhead\">\n<h2 class=\"subhead\">Who are the millennial homebuyers?</h2>\n</h3><p>\n<b class=\"b\">Median age:</b> 30 \n</p><p>Median income (2016): $77,400 \n</p><p>\n<b class=\"b\">Cost of typical home purchased:</b> $187,400 \n</p><p>\n<b class=\"b\">Buying in urban or central city areas: </b>17 percent \n</p><p>\n<b class=\"b\">Buying multifamily homes:</b> 10 percent \n</p><p>\n<b class=\"b\">Student debt with median loan balance of $25,000:</b> 46 percent</p><p>Source: National Association of Realtors <a class=\"a\" href=\"https://www.nar.realtor/sites/default/files/reports/2017/2017-home-buyer-and-seller-generational-trends-03-07-2017.pdf\" shape=\"rect\" target=\"_blank\">Home Buyer and Seller Generational Trends Report 2017</a>\n</p><p></p><div class=\"wide\">\n<img alt=\"\" data-fullsrc=\"//c.o0bg.com/rf/image_1920w/Boston/2011-2020/2017/03/24/BostonGlobe.com/Regional/Images/tlumacki_quincyapartment_regional081.jpg\" src=\"//c.o0bg.com/rf/image_r/Boston/2011-2020/2017/03/24/BostonGlobe.com/Regional/Images/tlumacki_quincyapartment_regional081.r.jpg\"><div class=\"figcaption\">\n<p class=\"credit\">John Tlumacki/Globe staff</p>\n<p>Mary Bertrand graduated from Salem State and shares an apartment in Quincy. Now engaged and planning a wedding, Harrington says she and her fiance are talking about buying something bigger.\n\n\u201cBecause my mom is a realtor, she often talked about the value in \u2018paying to own\u2019 versus \u201cpaying to live.\u2019 She stressed the importance of building equity, and both she and my dad encouraged me to stay home and save money in order to work towards buying,\u201d she says.\n\nIt took about five years for Harrington to save enough to finance her condo.\n\n\u201cFour years of commuting in and out of Boston every day while working and going to [law] school was tiring, but I was also very lucky that I happened to live in a city that has proximity to Boston,\u201d she says. \u201cIt was very feasible for me to live at home and commute.\u201d\n\nDracut resident Madeline Koufogazos, 24, moved home after graduating from UMass Lowell two years ago with a degree in English and a job with a Malden publishing company.\n\n\u201cI\u2019m turning 25 in October, and I feel it\u2019s a good time to start looking,\u201d Koufogazos says. \u201cMy goal now is to pay off student loans as quickly as possible, [plus a] car loan.\u201d\n\nBut living at home has given her a boost and reinforced what her parents taught her about managing her money. Meanwhile, she\u2019s considering her next move, possibly a shared apartment at Mill No. 5 on Jackson Street in Lowell or a rental in Somerville, Allston, or Jamaica Plain.\n\n\u201cI\u2019d like a place that\u2019s comfortable, a safe neighborhood, on the outskirts of Boston,\u201d she says.\n\nIn 2013, after graduating from Salem State University, Mary Bertrand, now 25, moved to Pennsylvania to work for AmeriCorps, a one-year national service commitment. Afterward, she took a job with a nonprofit in Boston and moved into an apartment she shares in Quincy Center, not far from the train station.\n\n\u201cAbout half of my friends live at home,\u201d said
