The U.S.&rsquo;s early assessment is that it involved the use of chlorine and sarin, according to two U.S. officials who weren&rsquo;t authorized to speak publicly on the matter and demanded anonymity.</p><p>In Khan Sheikhoun, the tragedy was compounded because so many victims were from a single extended family, the Alyousef clan.</p><p>Witnesses say four rockets hit around 6:30 a.m. Tuesday, smashing a crater in the ground, but causing minimal structural damage. It quickly became clear this was not a conventional attack.</p><p>Alaa Alyousef said his family was sleeping and woke to the sound of the impacts only a few hundred yards (meters) away. The first thing they saw was smoke. His father went outside then rushed back in. He had seen a woman walking near the strike suddenly collapse. The family frantically closed windows and dampened cloths with water and apple vinegar to put over their faces.</p><p>They were lucky, the wind went in the other direction, Alyousef said.</p><p>The rockets hit on the edge of North Harah, a district where much of the Alyousef clan lives. Frantically the clan&rsquo;s members and their neighbors fled, running from house to house trying to track down relatives.</p><p>Fadl remembered her panic when the rockets woke her.</p><p>&lsquo;&lsquo;My husband, where are you? Oh, where are you my lovely son,&rsquo;&rsquo; she recalled calling out. &lsquo;&lsquo;They were next to me but I couldn&rsquo;t see them.&rsquo;&rsquo; She said their eyes began hurting. &lsquo;&lsquo;The air became very heavy. There was no bad smell. But the air was so heavy to breathe.&rsquo;&rsquo;</p><p>The Alyousefs brought their dead to a family member&rsquo;s home that was outside the worst attack area. The courtyard was turned into a makeshift morgue where surviving relatives tried for hours to resuscitate loved ones already dead.</p><p>That&rsquo;s when Fadl finally collapsed, she said, only to wake up in a medical center.</p><p>While Fadl recovers with her son at her parents&rsquo; home in a town north of Khan Sheikoun, her husband is still looking for survivors from his extended family.</p><p>Alaa Alyousef said not all homes have been searched for survivors yet.</p><p>&lsquo;&lsquo;We are still in shock, a big shock. Our family is devastated,&rsquo;&rsquo; the 27-year-old said. &lsquo;&lsquo;Many are still missing. We are afraid to enter homes sometimes lest we find more people dead.&rsquo;&rsquo;</p><p>On Tuesday, he and other family members buried the clan&rsquo;s dead in the mass grave.</p><p>On the way to the grave, Abdel Hameed Alyousef asked a cousin to video his farewell to his children as he sat in the front seat of a van being loaded with bodies.</p><p>When the airstrikes hit, he was with his twin daughter and son. &lsquo;&lsquo;I carried them outside the house with their mother,&rsquo;&rsquo; the 29-year-old shop owner told the AP. &lsquo;&lsquo;They were conscious at first, but 10 minutes later we could smell the odor.&rsquo;&rsquo;</p><p>The twins and his wife, Dalal Ahmed, fell sick.</p><p>He brought them to paramedics and, thinking they would be OK, went to look for the rest of his family. They&rsquo;re all dead now,&rsquo;&rsquo; he said.</p><p>It was only later that his relatives could bring themselves to tell him that his children and wife had also died.</p><p>&lsquo;&lsquo;Abdel Hameed is in very bad shape,&rsquo;&rsquo; said his cousin, Alaa Alyousef.
